Five items in the roundup.

First, the headline event itself. The F-D-A's V-R-B-P-A-C voted nine to zero that the benefits of m-R-N-A-ten-ten outweigh the risks for adults aged fifty to sixty-four, and separately voted nine to zero that the same conclusion holds for adults aged sixty-five and older. Moderna is seeking standard approval in the fifty-to-sixty-four cohort and accelerated approval in the sixty-five-and-older cohort, and the F-D-A is expected to decide by August fifth. The Phase three trial, m-R-N-A-ten-ten-P-three-oh-four, enrolled forty-thousand eight-hundred-five adults across three-hundred-one sites in eleven countries, and showed a twenty-six-point-six percent relative vaccine efficacy improvement over a licensed standard-dose flu vaccine in adults aged fifty-plus, with twenty-seven-point-four percent relative efficacy in the sixty-five-plus subgroup — head-to-head superiority published in the New England Journal of Medicine. Against Fluzone High-Dose in adults sixty-five and older, m-R-N-A-ten-ten showed superior antibody responses at day twenty-nine and at six months. Second, Biogen and RayThera. On Wednesday Biogen announced an agreement to acquire RayThera for up to one billion dollars, structured as an upfront payment plus contingent milestones tied to clinical and regulatory progress, with closing expected in the third quarter. RayThera was co-founded in twenty twenty-three by C-E-O Qing Dong and chief scientific officer Gene Hung, raised a one-hundred-ten-million-dollar Series A in April twenty twenty-five, and is developing three anti-inflammatory small-molecule programs across immune-mediated conditions, none of which has yet entered the clinic. The lead program is expected to begin Phase one in the third quarter of this year. The strategic frame is straightforward — Biogen has been rebuilding around immunology since the HI-Bio acquisition in twenty twenty-four, and RayThera adds preclinical optionality to a pipeline that increasingly looks like a bet on chronic inflammatory disease as the next durable Biogen franchise after the multiple sclerosis declines. The financial structure — small upfront, large milestones — is the standard playbook for preclinical immunology acquisitions in twenty twenty-six. The price tag is at the upper end of preclinical-immunology valuations but well below the eight-hundred-million-to-one-point-eight-billion range that Biogen has paid for clinical-stage immunology assets in recent years.

Third, cAMPfield Therapeutics. Yesterday the I-B-D startup cAMPfield emerged with one-hundred-eighty million dollars in Series A funding led by Frazier Life Sciences with Deep Track Capital, Forbion, Abingworth, Venrock, Longitude Capital, Novo Holdings, and R-A Capital. The lead asset is prifemilast — a once-daily oral P-D-E-four-B-selective inhibitor — licensed from Chinese biotech Newsoara, which itself acquired the molecule from v-T-v Therapeutics in twenty eighteen. The drug is positioned as a best-in-class oral I-B-D candidate, and cAMPfield is launching a global Phase two-b in moderate-to-severe ulcerative colitis and a global Phase two in Crohn's disease. The founding team is notable — C-E-O Asit Parikh led Entyvio development at Takeda, Keith Usiskin advanced Otezla and Zeposia for I-B-D at Celgene and Bristol-Myers-Squibb, and Mark Stenhouse came from AbbVie commercialization on Humira. The strategic read is that I-B-D is now a three-layer competitive landscape — antibody combinations like Spyre's anti-TL1A plus anti-alpha-four-beta-seven we covered Tuesday, integrin and cytokine-pathway antibodies including Entyvio and Skyrizi, and now a wave of oral mechanisms led by Pfizer's Velsipity, Eli Lilly's Omvoh, and prifemilast as the next-generation P-D-E-four. The P-D-E-four-B-selective profile is the critical differentiation — broad P-D-E-four inhibition has historically tripped on gastrointestinal tolerability, and the B-isoform-selective profile is the bet that prifemilast can clear that bar.

Fourth, the F-D-A and Merck's Capvaxive. Yesterday the F-D-A approved an additional indication for Capvaxive — Merck's twenty-one-valent pneumococcal-conjugate vaccine — covering children and adolescents aged two through seventeen who have completed a primary pediatric pneumococcal vaccination series and have at least one chronic medical condition putting them at increased pneumococcal-disease risk. Conditions include chronic heart disease, chronic lung disease, diabetes, and chronic kidney disease. With this approval, Capvaxive becomes the only pneumococcal-conjugate vaccine specifically indicated and studied in the United States for this patient population, and Merck cites a twenty twenty-five C-D-C surveillance analysis showing Capvaxive's serotypes cover approximately seventy-nine percent of invasive pneumococcal disease cases in under-eighteens with at least one risk condition. Capvaxive's original adult approval in twenty twenty-four covered serotypes intentionally selected for adult invasive disease coverage, distinguishing it from Pfizer's competing Prevnar twenty. The pediatric-at-risk expansion is a meaningful market addition rather than a transformative one, but it does deepen Merck's hold on the high-risk pneumococcal segment ahead of the broader pediatric Prevnar replacement cycle.

Fifth and last, Eli Lilly and the three-forty-B fight. Yesterday Lilly began actually denying three-forty-B discounts to a group of large disproportionate-share hospitals that have refused to submit claims-level dispensing data under the company's expanded reporting policy. The mechanism — Lilly extended its long-standing contract-pharmacy claims-data requirement to in-house pharmacy dispenses effective February first, sent letters on June first warning non-compliant covered entities they had five business days to submit or lose access to three-forty-B pricing, and as of yesterday is enforcing those cutoffs. Lilly's own framing — approximately seventy percent of covered entities have complied, and a coordinated minority of large hospital systems is the holdout group. The American Hospital Association has called the policy unlawful and asked H-R-S-A to take enforcement action including civil monetary penalties. This is the clearest example yet of a major pharma using actual price denial — not just legal challenge or threat — as enforcement against the three-forty-B contract-pharmacy expansion, and it sets the precedent that other manufacturers in similar disputes including Johnson and Johnson, Sanofi, and Novo Nordisk are watching closely.
