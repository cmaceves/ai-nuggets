Good morning. It's Sunday, June twenty-first, twenty twenty-six, and this is your Calibr-Skaggs Daily Briefing. We're forty-eight hours into a quiet weekend news cycle, but the lead item is a platform-revival story I think will be more interesting than it first looks — and the broader week ahead is dominated by the B-I-O International Convention, which opens tomorrow morning in San Diego. Today's spotlight in a few hours is the launch of Nortiva Bio, the new Innoviva subsidiary built around the L-Y-N-X long-acting oral drug delivery platform that was salvaged from Lyndra Therapeutics when Lyndra wound down last spring. Five items in the roundup.

First, the spotlight item itself, in headline form. On Thursday, Innoviva publicly launched Nortiva Bio as a clinical-stage wholly-owned subsidiary built around the L-Y-N-X gastric-retention long-acting oral delivery platform invented in the labs of Robert Langer and Giovanni Traverso at M-I-T. Innoviva acquired the platform from Lyndra Therapeutics in twenty twenty-five for ten-point-two-million dollars upfront, plus up to twenty million in milestones and royalties on the first approved medicine, after Lyndra began winding down operations in March twenty twenty-five despite a positive Phase three pharmacokinetic readout on once-weekly oral risperidone in the back half of twenty twenty-four. The Nortiva advisory board is unusual for a launch this small — Robert Langer chairs it, with Giovanni Traverso, former acting F-D-A commissioner Janet Woodcock, Kallyope C-E-O Jay Galeota, Aisling Capital operating partner Scott Braunstein, and former Roche partnering head Sophie Kornowski. The lead asset is a once-monthly oral contraceptive funded by a five-million-dollar grant from the Gates Foundation, with first-in-human trials targeted for next year, and Nortiva intends to pursue partnered long-acting reformulations of approved and pipeline small-molecule therapies in parallel. Today's spotlight walks through the L-Y-N-X mechanism, the Lyndra arc and why a platform that delivered a clean Phase three pharmacokinetic readout still couldn't get to approval, the Innoviva royalty-vehicle thesis, the once-monthly contraceptive opportunity, and the read-through to Calibr's antibiotic and T-B drug development programs where adherence is the single biggest unsolved problem in delivering decade-old therapies to the patients who need them.

Second, cytisinicline. Yesterday — Saturday, June twentieth — was the P-D-U-F-A action date for Achieve Life Sciences's cytisinicline N-D-A in nicotine-dependence smoking cessation, the molecule we previewed on Friday's roundup as a candidate to become the first new prescription smoking-cessation drug in twenty years. Achieve has been telegraphing since mid-April that a complete response letter is the expected outcome — not on cytisinicline itself, but on the third-party manufacturing facility supplying the commercial product, which received an official-action-indicated classification from the F-D-A during a c-G-M-P inspection earlier this year. The company has already executed a technology transfer of cytisinicline supply to Adare Pharma Solutions and intends to resubmit the N-D-A naming Adare in the fourth quarter, with a U-S commercial launch now targeted for the first half of twenty twenty-seven. The C-R-L itself will likely be confirmed in a Monday press release if it came in on schedule. The pattern here — a small-molecule approval pushed by six to nine months purely because a contract manufacturer carries an O-A-I classification on an unrelated inspection — is one of the more underappreciated regulatory risks for partner-dependent assets, and a relevant scenario to keep in mind for any Calibr program planning a single-source small-molecule supply chain into commercial.

Third, Scribe Therapeutics. On Thursday, Scribe announced it has been awarded more than twenty-five million dollars from the California Institute for Regenerative Medicine across two preclinical engineered-C-R-I-S-P-R cardiometabolic programs — S-T-X-twelve-hundred targeting durable lowering of lipoprotein little-a, and S-T-X-fourteen-hundred targeting durable lowering of triglycerides — both designed as one-time genetic medicines using Scribe's in-vivo C-R-I-S-P-R platform. Scribe is preparing to take its first cardiometabolic program, the P-C-S-K-nine knockdown candidate S-T-X-eleven-fifty, into the clinic in the middle of this year. The strategic context is that the durable-cardiometabolic-risk thesis — replace lifelong daily oral statin or P-C-S-K-nine antibody therapy with a single-dose genetic intervention — is now competitive with multiple programs in clinic from Verve, C-R-I-S-P-R Therapeutics, Beam, and Lilly's twenty-twenty-four Verve acquisition, and the C-I-R-M funding model is a useful non-dilutive complement to the venture and pharma-partnership capital that has financed the rest of this category.

Fourth, Portal Biotechnologies. On Thursday, the cell-engineering and drug-discovery platform company Portal Biotechnologies announced the closing of an oversubscribed nine-million-dollar financing round led by N-F-X, with an expanded DARPA contract and reported customer-site adoption across the top ten global pharmas plus more than one hundred academic and biotech sites. Portal's platform uses mechanoporation — physical deformation of cell membranes — to deliver R-N-A, gene editors, probes, and other complex molecules into hard-to-transfect primary and pluripotent cells without compromising cell function, and the commercial argument is that delivery into target-discovery-relevant cell types has been a real bottleneck in cell therapy and functional-genomics screens. Nine million is a small follow-on financing, but the DARPA contract expansion plus broad pharma adoption is the substantive part of the story.

Roughly twenty thousand attendees across seventy countries are expected, with reported scheduled-partnering-meeting volume in the seventy-thousand range — a number that, if accurate, would set a record. The week ahead will be a dense bookkeeping cycle for biotech-pharma business development, especially in the partnered-asset and licensing categories given the current pipeline-replenishment posture across big pharma.
