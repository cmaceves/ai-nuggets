First, the plenary itself. Starting at one o'clock Chicago time this afternoon, ASCO walks through five late-breaking abstracts, in order: PROTEUS, the Johnson and Johnson perioperative apalutamide study in high-risk localized prostate cancer; SARC041, Eli Lilly's abemaciclib in dedifferentiated liposarcoma; LIBRETTO-432, Lilly's selpercatinib as adjuvant therapy in resected RET fusion–positive lung cancer; HARMONi-6, the Akeso and Summit Therapeutics overall-survival readout for the PD-1-by-VEGF bispecific ivonescimab against tislelizumab plus chemotherapy in first-line squamous lung cancer; and finally RASolute 302, Revolution Medicines' daraxonrasib in second-line pancreatic cancer, which we already covered in detail last week. The late-breaking abstracts dropped at eight a.m. Eastern this morning.

Second, the headliner of the headliners is HARMONi-6, and we'll spend the spotlight episode there in a few hours. The short version: this is the first China-developed asset ever to land a plenary slot at ASCO in the meeting's six-decade history. The PD-1-by-VEGF bispecific previously showed a forty percent reduction in progression risk versus a PD-1 inhibitor plus chemotherapy. The overall survival readout being presented today is what will determine whether ivonescimab actually displaces a PD-1 monotherapy partner in first-line lung cancer, and Summit Therapeutics, which holds rights outside Greater China, is the publicly traded proxy for that bet.

Third, PROTEUS. Mary-Ellen Taplin from Dana-Farber presents the final analysis as the plenary opener. The trial randomized about two thousand five hundred high-risk localized prostate cancer patients to six cycles of apalutamide plus androgen deprivation neoadjuvantly, then radical prostatectomy with pelvic node dissection, then six adjuvant cycles, against placebo plus ADT in the same schema. Dual primary endpoints are pathological complete response and metastasis-free survival. If apalutamide moves the needle on MFS in the curative-intent setting, it shifts Erleada from advanced disease into perioperative use, which is a meaningfully larger commercial opportunity for J&J.

Fourth, LIBRETTO-432. Lilly already reported positive topline in February, with statistically significant and clinically meaningful event-free survival in early-stage, surgically resected RET fusion–positive lung cancer. The plenary delivers the actual numbers. Overall survival was immature at topline. The relevance: selpercatinib has been a second- and third-line targeted-therapy asset, and adjuvant use would broaden its label into a curative-intent earlier setting, in parallel with how osimertinib's ADAURA result reshaped EGFR practice.

Fifth, SARC041. The investigator-initiated Phase 3 of Lilly's CDK4-and-6 inhibitor abemaciclib in dedifferentiated liposarcoma. This is a hundred-and-eight-patient study in a rare sarcoma where CDK4 is essentially universally amplified. The Phase 2 showed a seventy-six percent twelve-week progression-free rate, which is striking for a disease with almost no approved therapies. A positive Phase 3 here gives Verzenio a niche sarcoma indication and gives sarcoma patients their first targeted option in years.

Sixth, switching from oncology to deals. Eli Lilly announced on May 26th a three-company infectious-disease buying spree worth up to three-point-eight billion dollars: Curevo for its shingles vaccine amezosvatein, with synthetic adjuvant designed for better tolerability than Shingrix; LimmaTech, which is doing bacterial vaccines against Staph aureus, gonorrhea, and chlamydia, all of which are running out of antibiotic options; and Vaccine Company, focused on viral pathogens with long-term oncology and neurology risk. The breakdown is up to one-point-five billion for Curevo, up to one-point-five-five billion for Vaccine Company, and up to seven hundred eighty million for LimmaTech, all milestone-loaded. Lilly is essentially declaring it wants a vaccines franchise to sit alongside Mounjaro and Verzenio, and it's doing it through bolt-ons rather than a single megadeal.

Seventh, in breast cancer, the New England Journal published ASCENT-04 yesterday. Sacituzumab govitecan plus pembrolizumab cut the risk of progression or death by thirty-five percent versus standard first-line therapy in PD-L1-positive metastatic triple-negative breast cancer. Gilead now has the published evidence base to position the TROP2 antibody-drug conjugate plus Keytruda as a first-line standard of care in this setting, ahead of Daiichi and AstraZeneca's competing TROP2 ADC datopotamab deruxtecan, which got TNBC accelerated approval ten days ago.
