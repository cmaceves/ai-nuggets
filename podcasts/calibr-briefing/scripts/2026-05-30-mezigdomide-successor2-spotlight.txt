Today's spotlight is the Bristol Myers Squibb SUCCESSOR-Two readout for mezigdomide in relapsed or refractory multiple myeloma — the marquee late-breaking abstract from day one of ASCO twenty twenty-six in Chicago, presented yesterday and pushed to the trade press this morning. It's a big number on a flagship asset in a market that has been quietly reshaping itself around BCMA cellular therapies for the last three years, and it deserves a careful read.

Let me start with what was actually shown. SUCCESSOR-Two is a Phase three open-label randomized trial comparing the three-drug combination of mezigdomide plus carfilzomib plus dexamethasone — what BMS is calling MeziKd — against the two-drug control regimen of carfilzomib plus dexamethasone alone, in patients with relapsed or refractory multiple myeloma who had received one to three prior lines of therapy. The MeziKd arm hit the progression-free survival primary endpoint with a fifty-two percent reduction in the risk of disease progression or death. Median PFS came in at eighteen months on the mezigdomide arm versus eight-point-three months on control. That's not a delta — that's a doubling. Overall response rate was eighty-point-two percent versus fifty-three-point-four, and the complete-response-or-better rate was twenty-six-point-seven versus eight-point-nine, so the depth of response tracked with the duration. The benefit held across pre-specified subgroups including second-line patients, third-line patients, and patients with higher-risk cytogenetic disease. Safety was consistent with the known profile of mezigdomide plus the carfilzomib backbone — meaning hematologic toxicity, predictably — with no new safety signals. This is the first Phase three readout for mezigdomide, the molecule BMS has spent most of the last decade positioning as the next-generation successor to its Revlimid and Pomalyst franchise.

Now let me unpack the mechanism, because this is where the story gets interesting. Mezigdomide is a CELMoD — a cereblon E3 ligase modulator. It is a molecular glue. It binds the substrate-recognition pocket of cereblon, a substrate adapter for the cullin-four-A RING E3 ubiquitin ligase complex, and reshapes cereblon's surface so that the ligase recruits and ubiquitinates two specific transcription factors — Ikaros and Aiolos, encoded by IKZF1 and IKZF3 — that myeloma cells depend on for survival. The proteasome then degrades them, the cells die. Revlimid and Pomalyst — lenalidomide and pomalidomide — do the same fundamental thing. They are first-generation CELMoDs. What makes mezigdomide different is potency and substrate specificity. Mezigdomide induces the active closed conformation of cereblon in essentially one hundred percent of cereblon molecules at therapeutic exposures, compared to roughly fifty percent for iberdomide — the prior next-gen candidate — and roughly twenty percent for pomalidomide. The degradation kinetics for Ikaros and Aiolos are faster and deeper. The result is meaningful clinical activity in patients whose disease has become refractory to first-generation immunomodulatory drugs, which is the major unmet need in relapsed myeloma. Iberdomide, the earlier-generation CELMoD that BMS spent considerable resources developing, has shown more modest activity. Mezigdomide is the breakthrough.

The competitive context matters here. Multiple myeloma is the most contested hematology market in oncology. Over the last three years, BCMA-directed therapies have dominated the relapsed-or-refractory landscape — autologous CAR-Ts like Carvykti from Legend and Johnson and Johnson, Abecma from BMS and 2seventy, and BCMA T-cell engager bispecifics like teclistamab and elranatamab from J and J and Pfizer respectively. These therapies generate spectacular response rates but require either lymphodepleting chemotherapy followed by cell manufacturing — months of wait, hundreds of thousands of dollars, and concentrated delivery infrastructure — or weekly subcutaneous dosing with step-up titration and cytokine release syndrome management. They are extraordinary therapies, and they are commercially constrained by access. Mezigdomide is an oral pill. It can be combined with carfilzomib, lenalidomide, daratumumab — essentially every existing myeloma backbone. SUCCESSOR-Two is the carfilzomib combination. SUCCESSOR-One reads out the daratumumab combination, and BMS has additional Phase three programs in newly diagnosed and maintenance settings. If those readouts also deliver, mezigdomide is plausibly the largest oral myeloma franchise of the next decade.

For BMS, this matters strategically because Revlimid — the company's all-time top-selling oncology product — went off patent in twenty twenty-two and is in the back half of its erosion curve. Pomalyst, the second-generation CELMoD, faces loss of exclusivity in twenty twenty-eight. Mezigdomide is the franchise replacement. The SUCCESSOR-Two readout is not just a clinical win — it's the most important pipeline data point BMS has delivered in oncology since the Celgene acquisition. And it lands at a moment when BMS is under acute investor pressure on its growth profile.

The Calibr connection runs through three threads.

First, the molecular glue platform itself. CELMoDs are the most clinically validated molecular glue therapeutics in oncology, and they sit at the heart of the broader targeted protein degradation field that has expanded dramatically over the last five years. SUCCESSOR-Two is the strongest Phase three validation yet that a rationally designed next-generation molecular glue can substantially outperform first-generation analogs in the clinic. That has implications well beyond myeloma. Companies designing molecular glues against other E3 ligase-substrate pairs — Monte Rosa Therapeutics around GSPT1 and CDK2, Foghorn against BAF complex components, Plexium and C4 against multiple substrate classes — now have a Phase three benchmark they can plausibly aspire to. For Calibr, the cereblon and broader molecular-glue chemistry space is one of the more productive territories for novel small-molecule mechanism, particularly when paired with AI-guided cryptic-pocket and ternary-complex design.

Second, the artificial-intelligence-in-drug-discovery angle. Cereblon is one of the best-characterized E3 ligases in structural biology, with deep crystallography on cereblon-substrate-glue ternary complexes. That makes it an ideal proving ground for AI-driven ligand design tools — both for novel glues against cereblon's neo-substrate surface, and for AI models trained on cereblon-CELMoD ternary structures and transferred to other E3 ligases where structural data is sparser. Isomorphic Labs, Iambic Therapeutics, Xaira, and several quieter players have all published or signaled cereblon-glue programs. SUCCESSOR-Two raises the commercial ceiling for any next-next-generation CELMoD that improves further on mezigdomide's substrate selectivity or expands the neo-substrate repertoire — which is exactly the territory where computationally designed glues have the most plausible advantage.

Third, the cellular-therapy versus oral-small-molecule competitive dynamic. Calibr's in vivo CAR-T work and its broader interest in next-generation cellular therapies share an underlying assumption — that cell therapies will continue to deepen response in heavily pre-treated cancer where conventional small molecules cannot. SUCCESSOR-Two complicates that assumption modestly. It shows that a sufficiently potent next-generation small-molecule degrader can deliver durable activity in patients who have failed prior immunomodulatory therapy — without the manufacturing, cost, and infrastructure burden of CAR-T. The longer-term commercial picture in myeloma probably looks like co-existence rather than displacement — CAR-T and bispecifics deepening responses, oral CELMoDs extending duration and accessibility — but it's a useful reminder that the case for cellular therapy needs to clear an oral-small-molecule bar that just got meaningfully higher.

Three things to watch over the next twelve months. First, the SUCCESSOR-One daratumumab combination readout, expected later this year — if that hits with comparable magnitude, mezigdomide becomes the universal CELMoD backbone for relapsed myeloma. Second, the readouts in newly diagnosed disease, which would expand the addressable market by roughly threefold. Third, FDA filing strategy and the inevitable label-versus-pricing tension once approval lands. BMS will likely position mezigdomide as a foundation therapy with pricing reflecting the franchise replacement role.
