Welcome to the Calibr-Skaggs Daily Briefing spotlight for Sunday, May 31st, 2026. Today we go deep on HARMONi-6, the ivonescimab overall-survival readout that will be presented in the ASCO plenary session at one o'clock Chicago time this afternoon. This is the single most-watched data drop of the conference, and arguably the most consequential lung-cancer readout of the year. Let's unpack why.

Start with what ivonescimab actually is. It's a bispecific antibody co-developed by the Chinese biotech Akeso and licensed outside Greater China to Summit Therapeutics in a deal that closed in late 2022 for five hundred million dollars upfront and up to four-and-a-half billion in milestones. The molecule has two binding arms: one against PD-1, the same checkpoint that Keytruda and Opdivo target, and one against VEGF, the same growth factor that Avastin neutralizes. The biological logic is that VEGF in the tumor microenvironment is immunosuppressive in its own right, on top of feeding angiogenesis, so co-blocking PD-1 and VEGF on a single molecule should produce more than the sum of giving the two drugs separately. The geometry also matters: tethering the two arms together is supposed to concentrate VEGF blockade in tissues where PD-1 is engaged, which is the tumor, rather than spreading anti-VEGF activity systemically. That's the pitch. Whether the geometry actually delivers what the pitch promises is what the entire HARMONi program is designed to test.

Now to HARMONi-6 specifically. This is a Phase 3 trial conducted in China by Akeso, randomizing five hundred thirty-two patients with previously untreated, locally advanced or metastatic squamous non-small cell lung cancer, regardless of PD-L1 expression, to one of two arms. The experimental arm gets ivonescimab plus platinum doublet chemotherapy. The control arm gets tislelizumab, BeiGene's PD-1 inhibitor approved in China, plus the same platinum doublet. Note that this is not chemotherapy versus a checkpoint inhibitor; the control is already a modern standard, a PD-1 antibody combined with chemo. That makes HARMONi-6 a head-to-head between a bispecific and a checkpoint monotherapy on the same chemo backbone, in a setting where PD-1 plus chemo is already efficacious. That's a much harder bar.

The progression-free survival data already read out at ESMO last October, simultaneously published in The Lancet, and they were striking. Median PFS was eleven-point-one months on ivonescimab plus chemo versus six-point-nine months on tislelizumab plus chemo. The hazard ratio was zero-point-six-zero, p less than zero-point-zero-zero-zero-one. A forty percent reduction in progression risk against an active PD-1 comparator, with consistency across PD-L1 strata. That was the first time anyone had beaten a PD-1 plus chemo combination on PFS in first-line lung cancer in a randomized Phase 3. The question that hasn't been answered is whether that PFS benefit translates to longer life. PFS is a regulatory endpoint, but oncologists and payers want to see overall survival. That's what today's plenary delivers.

Here are the stakes. Analyst consensus going into today is that ivonescimab needs to land an OS hazard ratio below zero-point-eight to make the bull case work. Anything in the zero-point-six-five to zero-point-seven-zero range, matching the PFS magnitude, would be a near-unambiguous win and probably trigger an FDA filing in this indication. A hazard ratio at zero-point-eight-five or above, even if technically positive, would raise the question that has haunted earlier bispecific programs: PFS doesn't always translate. There's also a more cynical risk: the trial enrolled exclusively in China, on a Chinese chemo backbone, with a Chinese PD-1 comparator. The FDA's response to similar single-region Chinese data, most visibly with toripalimab and surufatinib, has been mixed. Summit will need to argue either that survival is so large the regional issue is moot, or that ongoing global trials, principally HARMONi-3 in nonsquamous, will close any geographic gap. HARMONi-3 had a disappointing interim PFS miss earlier this year, which raised the pressure on today's readout.

There's a third thing to track that doesn't fit on a forest plot. Today is the first time in ASCO's six-decade history that a China-developed asset has been selected for the plenary. That selection is itself a signal from the ASCO scientific committee that they think the data are practice-relevant. Whether that signal holds up under the kind of cross-examination that always follows a plenary presentation, especially around endpoint maturity and subgroup consistency, will define the narrative for Chinese biotech licensing for the rest of the year. Pfizer just paid more than twenty billion dollars in headline value to Innovent on Thursday for a similar bet on Chinese-originated oncology IP. The HARMONi-6 reception will calibrate how aggressively other large-cap pharmas chase those deals.

Now, what does this mean for Calibr's portfolio thinking. Calibr's biologics are not in the PD-1-by-VEGF lane, but two patterns from HARMONi-6 generalize. First, the bispecific format question. Calibr's MASH biologic is itself a dual-acting molecule engaging multiple receptors. If ivonescimab demonstrates that geometry-matters, that fixing two binding domains on one scaffold produces real clinical benefit over two separate antibodies, it strengthens the case that bispecific and multispecific formats deserve premium valuations. If the OS data are flat, it weakens that thesis across the field. Second, the head-to-head versus active comparator playbook. Akeso bet that the only way to get a bispecific taken seriously was to beat the dominant monotherapy on its own backbone. That's a high-risk, high-reward strategy that Calibr will face on its own assets at some point, particularly if FGF21 analogs need to start measuring up against resmetirom or against efruxifermin, both of which are moving to standard-of-care positions in their respective MASH segments. The reception today is a useful calibration of how the field rewards or punishes that strategy.

A final note on Summit's commercial position. Summit's market capitalization sits at around twenty-five billion dollars going into today, driven almost entirely by ivonescimab. The company is essentially a single-asset bet held outside one geography, with no approvals yet in the United States. A clean OS win plus a regulatory filing this fall puts Summit on a path to potential first FDA approval in 2027. A muddy OS readout, an HR above zero-point-eight or with subgroup heterogeneity, and Summit becomes a takeout candidate at a much lower price for whoever wants to gamble on the global rollout. Either way, the next twelve hours reshape one of the more interesting partnership stories in oncology.
