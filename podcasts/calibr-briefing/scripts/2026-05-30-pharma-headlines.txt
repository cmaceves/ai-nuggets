Good morning Travis. This is your Calibr-Skaggs Daily Briefing for Saturday, May thirtieth, twenty twenty-six. ASCO twenty twenty-six opened yesterday in Chicago and the first wave of late-breaking abstracts is hitting today. Six headlines.

First, Bristol Myers Squibb. The company announced positive late-breaking Phase three results from the SUCCESSOR-2 study of mezigdomide, its next-generation oral cereblon E3 ligase modulator, in combination with carfilzomib and dexamethasone for relapsed or refractory multiple myeloma. The mezigdomide combination reduced the risk of disease progression or death by fifty-two percent versus carfilzomib plus dex alone, with median progression-free survival of eighteen months versus eight-point-three months — a doubling of PFS. Overall response rate was eighty-point-two percent versus fifty-three-point-four percent, and complete-response-or-better was twenty-six-point-seven percent versus eight-point-nine percent. Benefit held across second- and third-line patients and across higher-risk disease. Safety was consistent with the known mezigdomide profile. This is the first Phase three readout for mezigdomide and the strongest validation yet for the CELMoD class — a next-generation molecular glue platform built around cereblon-driven Ikaros and Aiolos degradation. The data position mezigdomide as the heir apparent to the Revlimid and Pomalyst franchises and the centerpiece of BMS's defense against the encroaching BCMA CAR-T and bispecific wave in myeloma. We go deep in today's spotlight.

Second, Eli Lilly. At ASCO, Lilly presented Phase one Nexus-Oh-One data for its Nectin-4-targeted antibody-drug conjugate carrying a topoisomerase one inhibitor payload — positioned as a potential challenger to Pfizer-Astellas's Padcev in advanced urothelial carcinoma. In forty-eight patients, forty-two percent saw their tumors shrink, including twelve of thirty-six patients who had previously progressed on Padcev plus Keytruda, and eight of twelve who had never received Padcev. The strategic premise is that bladder cancers evolving resistance to Padcev's MMAE chemotherapy payload retain Nectin-Four as a viable target if the payload is swapped to a different chemical class. With Padcev plus Keytruda now the entrenched standard of care across muscle-invasive and metastatic urothelial settings, Lilly's wedge is post-Padcev salvage. The data are early but support advancement.

Third, Johnson and Johnson. J and J presented updated CHRYSALIS-2 data at ASCO for amivantamab plus lazertinib — its EGFR-MET bispecific antibody plus oral third-generation EGFR tyrosine kinase inhibitor combo, marketed as Rybrevant and Lazcluze — as first-line therapy for advanced non-small cell lung cancer carrying atypical EGFR mutations. In forty-nine treatment-naive patients followed for a median of thirty-one months, median overall survival reached forty-one months, with fifty-five percent of patients alive at three years and forty-six percent alive at four years. Atypical EGFR mutations are five to ten percent of EGFR-mutant lung cancer and respond poorly to standard EGFR TKIs, so a four-plus-year overall survival benchmark in this subgroup is meaningful. The data reinforce J and J's franchise in EGFR lung cancer against rising competition from Dizal's oral Zegfrovy in exon-twenty insertions.

Fourth, Wondercel Therapeutics and Allogene. Two CAR-T developments worth flagging together. Wondercel — the new cell-therapy company founded by Frank Fan, the former chief scientist at Legend Biotech who led discovery of cilta-cel, the BCMA CAR-T marketed as Carvykti by Legend and Johnson and Johnson — made its ASCO debut yesterday with an oral presentation on its lead universal CD19 CAR-T candidate in relapsed or refractory B-cell tumors. Wondercel's distinguishing claim is an off-the-shelf manufacturing approach that does not rely on the gene editing — TCR knockout and CD52 knockout — that has constrained the safety and persistence of conventional allogeneic CAR-T platforms from Allogene, Caribou, and CRISPR Therapeutics. The same day, Allogene announced its own leadership transition — chief executive David Chang will step down June thirtieth after eight years, with chief medical officer Zachary Roberts taking over July first. Chang remains on the board. Roberts has been the architect of the ALPHA3 large B-cell lymphoma program. Read these two items together: a generational shift in the off-the-shelf CAR-T category as the original players reorganize and a wave of next-generation entrants with differentiated manufacturing approaches enters the clinic.

Fifth, Replimune. The company announced it will resubmit its biologics license application for RP1 — an engineered oncolytic herpes simplex virus — in combination with Bristol Myers's Opdivo, for advanced melanoma after PD-1 progression. This is the third submission attempt. The first complete response letter came in July twenty twenty-five, the second rejection came in April twenty twenty-six under then-Commissioner Marty Makary and CBER director Vinay Prasad. Following the recent departures of both Makary and Prasad, Replimune held what it described as productive discussions with the agency, and the FDA indicated it would treat the resubmission as an urgent matter and prioritize its review. The third attempt follows a clear pattern of FDA leadership turnover unblocking previously stalled oncology applications.

Sixth, Agios Pharmaceuticals. The company announced it will not advance tebapivat — its next-generation oral pyruvate kinase activator — in lower-risk myelodysplastic syndromes, after Phase two-b data from sixty-five anemia patients failed to meet the company's predefined transfusion-independence threshold for further development. Tebapivat was well-tolerated with no new safety signals. The program continues in sickle cell disease, with Phase two topline data expected in the second half of this year. The MDS miss removes one growth lane from the next-generation PK-activator franchise that Agios built on the back of Pyrukynd in pyruvate kinase deficiency and thalassemia.
