Let's start with the biology. Alpha-one antitrypsin is a serine protease inhibitor made in the liver and secreted into circulation, where its main job is to restrain neutrophil elastase in the lung. The Pi-Z-Z genotype, which is the genetic background that causes severe alpha-one antitrypsin deficiency, has a single point mutation in the SERPINA-one gene, a guanine to adenine substitution that swaps a glutamic acid for a lysine at position three-forty-two. The result is two problems in one mutation. The Z-form misfolds, polymerizes, and gets stuck inside hepatocytes, which causes liver injury and eventually cirrhosis. And because the liver retains it, it never reaches the lung, which leaves elastase unopposed and drives early-onset emphysema. So Pi-Z-Z patients suffer from both gain-of-function liver toxicity and loss-of-function pulmonary disease, and any therapy you build has to address both arms.

This is the fundamental reason Alnylam's fazirsiran, the siRNA against SERPINA-one, was always going to be an incomplete answer. Fazirsiran knocks down the toxic Z-A-A-T and protects the liver, which is genuinely meaningful, but it does nothing to restore wild-type protein for the lung. Pi-Z-Z patients on fazirsiran still need augmentation therapy with pooled plasma-derived A-A-T to protect the airways. That is exactly the loss-of-function half of the disease that R-N-A editing, in principle, can solve. If you can edit the messenger R-N-A back to wild type before it's translated, you remove the toxic Z-protein at its source and you generate functional M-protein from the same allele. That is the M-Z-like phenotype Wave is targeting, and biologically it should be curative if you can sustain it.

Now the platform implications, because this is what I think matters most to your strategic thinking at Calibr.

First, A-D-A-R recruitment is finally working at protein-restoration scale in humans. A-D-A-R, the adenosine deaminase acting on R-N-A, is an endogenous enzyme that you don't have to deliver.
