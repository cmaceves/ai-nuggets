Number one. Merck and Kelun-Biotech's sacituzumab tirumotecan — known as sack T-M-T — hit both progression-free and overall survival in the pivotal Tro-Fuse oh-oh-five Phase 3 trial in pretreated advanced or recurrent endometrial cancer. Seven hundred seventy-six patients, all having progressed after platinum-based chemotherapy and PD-1 or PD-L1 immunotherapy, randomized to sack T-M-T versus physician's choice doxorubicin or paclitaxel. The interim analysis triggered early. Sack T-M-T is a TROP2-targeted antibody-drug conjugate with a topoisomerase-one payload — same target as Gilead's Trodelvy and AstraZeneca-Daiichi Sankyo's Datroway, but with a proprietary linker and payload from the Kelun platform. This is the first global Phase 3 win for the Merck-Kelun deal, which Merck signed in 2022 for one point four billion dollars upfront and which has been the high-water-mark case study for the China-licensing thesis ever since. It is also the first antibody-drug conjugate ever to hit both progression-free and overall survival as a monotherapy in pretreated endometrial cancer, an indication where standard chemotherapy is genuinely poor. Merck now has seventeen Phase 3 trials running with this molecule across multiple tumor types, including ten in women's cancers. This is the readout that vindicates the entire premise of paying real money for Chinese clinical assets, and you can expect every business development team in big pharma to point to it in the next set of board meetings.

Number two. BioMarin's BMN four-oh-one — the renamed I-N-Z seven-oh-one program it acquired with the two hundred seventy million dollar Inozyme deal in March 2025 — produced a mixed Phase 3 readout in children one to twelve years old with E-N-P-P-one deficiency, a rare genetic disorder of mineralization. The pivotal Energy three trial hit one of two co-primary endpoints — a statistically significant increase in plasma inorganic pyrophosphate at week fifty-two, which is the on-mechanism biomarker — but missed the other co-primary endpoint of skeletal healing as measured by X-ray scoring. BMN four-oh-one is a soluble Fc fusion of the E-N-P-P-one enzyme; without functional E-N-P-P-one, patients can't generate enough inorganic pyrophosphate to inhibit pathological vascular and skeletal mineralization, and they develop heart and bone disease that is often fatal in infancy. Hitting the biomarker but missing the imaging endpoint is a classic enzyme-replacement-therapy pattern — the mechanism works at the molecular level but the clinical sign takes longer than fifty-two weeks to manifest. The question is whether the FDA will accept the pyrophosphate biomarker as an accelerated approval surrogate the way it has for some other ultra-rare metabolic enzyme replacements. If not, BioMarin needs to follow patients longer at significant trial cost, and the two hundred seventy million looks expensive. Analyst notes were sharply more skeptical this morning.

Number three. Amgen is standing publicly behind Tavneos — generic name avacopan — its complement C5a-receptor antagonist for A-N-C-A-associated vasculitis. The controversy: roughly twenty deaths in Japan are now under review with a possible link to drug-induced liver injury, and Japanese distributors have issued formal safety warnings. European regulators are separately examining data-integrity concerns, and at least one influential FDA advisor has publicly called for the drug to be reviewed for U.S. withdrawal. Amgen says current global safety data still support the benefit-risk profile and that the company is engaging with regulators. Tavneos was acquired through the twenty-twenty-three ChemoCentryx purchase at three point seven billion dollars, and it has been a roughly four hundred million dollar a year asset for Amgen with significant room to grow in label expansion. A Japan-driven safety reset would be a real impairment. Worth watching whether the FDA's Office of New Drugs or the post-marketing safety side moves first.

Number four. The Supreme Court declined to take up the consolidated drug-pricing cases brought by Merck, Bristol Myers, Boehringer, Astellas, and others challenging the Inflation Reduction Act's Medicare drug-negotiation provisions on constitutional grounds. The denial of certiorari leaves the Third Circuit and Federal Circuit decisions in place, meaning the negotiation framework continues to operate and the next round of price-negotiated drugs takes effect on schedule. This is the end of the legal road for the industry's challenge. Strategic takeaway: any small-molecule asset entering the market post-twenty-twenty-eight needs to be modeled with the post-I-R-A net price curve baked in, including the nine-year exclusivity-from-launch trigger for small molecules versus thirteen years for biologics. That asymmetry continues to push pharma toward biologics and away from oral small molecules in indications where both are feasible — and is one of the unspoken structural reasons GLP-1 oral small molecules like orforglipron are getting priced and positioned the way they are.

Number five. Regeneron, after Friday's fianlimab Phase 3 failure in melanoma, pivoted hard yesterday to a strategic collaboration with Parabilis Medicines — the Mathai Mammen-led biotech formerly known as Fog Pharma — to develop a new class of therapeutics called antibody-helicon conjugates. Deal structure: fifty million upfront, seventy-five million committed to Parabilis's next equity round, and up to two point two billion in milestones plus tiered royalties across five undisclosed targets. The science is genuinely novel — Parabilis's helicons are stabilized, cell-penetrant alpha-helical peptides designed to hit intracellular targets that small molecules can't grip and antibodies can't reach. Conjugating them to antibodies for cell-type-specific delivery is the new platform play.
