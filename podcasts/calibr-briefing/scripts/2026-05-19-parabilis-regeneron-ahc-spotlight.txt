Welcome to today's spotlight on the Calibr-Skaggs Daily Briefing. I'm Nigel.

Today we are going deep on the Regeneron-Parabilis Medicines collaboration announced yesterday afternoon — fifty million dollars upfront, seventy-five million committed to Parabilis's next equity round, and up to two point two billion in milestone payments plus tiered royalties, all to develop a brand-new class of therapeutics called antibody-helicon conjugates, or A-H-Cs. This is a deal that matters for three reasons. First, it tries to crack the oldest problem in drug discovery — intracellular targets that small molecules can't grip and antibodies can't reach. Second, it puts a former Johnson and Johnson head of R&D, Mathai Mammen, in the chairman seat of an emerging platform company with a roadmap that runs through every modality category we are watching this year. Third, it is the cleanest example we have had so far of how Regeneron intends to refill its oncology pipeline after Friday's Phase 3 fianlimab failure in melanoma. Let's walk through it.

Start with the science. Parabilis Medicines is the company formerly known as Fog Pharma. It rebranded in January 2026 when it closed an oversubscribed three hundred five million dollar Series F. Its core technology is the helicon platform — stabilized, cell-penetrant alpha-helical peptides engineered to bind intracellular targets. The basic idea is straightforward but the chemistry took a decade. Many of the most disease-driving proteins inside our cells — transcription factors, scaffolding proteins, components of protein-protein interaction networks — have what biochemists call flat or shallow surfaces. They do not have the deep, well-formed binding pockets that small molecules need to anchor onto. So small molecules can't get a grip. Antibodies could in principle bind these surfaces, but antibodies are large folded proteins that cannot cross the cell membrane on their own, so they never reach the inside of the cell. Peptides have always been theoretically attractive — they are the right size to engage flat protein surfaces — but linear peptides are floppy, get degraded quickly, and do not cross membranes either. The helicon innovation is to lock peptides into a stable alpha-helical conformation using engineered staples and bridges, decorate them with chemistry that makes them cell-penetrant, and then evolve them through an A-I-enabled selection campaign against intracellular targets. The result is a peptide that holds its shape, gets inside the cell, finds its target, and binds with high affinity. It is, in plain language, the missing modality between small molecules and biologics.

Parabilis's lead asset, zolucatetide — until recently called fog oh-oh-one — is the proof point. It is the first and only direct inhibitor of the beta-catenin to T-C-F protein-protein interaction, a target that has been chased without success for thirty years. Beta-catenin is the central effector of the Wnt signaling pathway, which is dysregulated in colorectal cancer, desmoid tumors, hepatocellular carcinoma, familial adenomatous polyposis, and a long list of other cancers. The beta-catenin to T-C-F handshake is what turns on the downstream Wnt-responsive gene expression program. Multiple pharma teams have failed to drug it with small molecules because the interface is too flat. Zolucatetide is in a Phase 1/2 trial, has shown single-agent activity across five Wnt-driven tumor types, has FDA Orphan Drug and Fast Track designations in desmoid tumors, and is heading into a pivotal Phase 3 desmoid trial later this year. A separate clinical signal in familial adenomatous polyposis — where polyp burden in one patient was reduced — points to a much broader potential addressable population. That is the proof that the helicon modality actually works in humans.

Now the new deal. Yesterday's collaboration adds a second platform play on top of the standalone helicon program — antibody-helicon conjugates. The architectural logic mirrors antibody-drug conjugates but with a fundamentally different payload class. In an A-D-C, an antibody delivers a cytotoxic small molecule to a specific cell type by binding a tumor-surface antigen; the antibody-drug conjugate is internalized, the linker is cleaved inside the cell, and the cytotoxic payload — typically a topoisomerase or microtubule poison — is released to kill the cell. The cell specificity is the whole point. Now substitute the cytotoxic payload with a helicon peptide that engages an intracellular protein-protein interaction. You retain the cell-type-specific delivery of the antibody. You add intracellular targeting via the helicon. And critically, you have decoupled the question of cell entry — which the antibody handles via receptor-mediated uptake — from the question of intracellular target engagement, which the helicon handles. That is a real new modality, not a slide-deck modality. The deal covers five initial targets across multiple therapeutic areas. Regeneron has the antibody engineering muscle. Parabilis has the helicon library and the linker chemistry. It is a textbook platform-plus-platform structure.

The Mathai Mammen factor matters here, and it is worth pausing on. Mammen ran R&D at Johnson and Johnson for five years and was responsible for one of the largest and most modality-diverse pharmaceutical pipelines in the industry — small molecules, biologics, cell and gene therapies, vaccines. He left Johnson and Johnson in 2022 to head research at FogPharma, and over the last two years he has steered the rebrand to Parabilis, raised the Series F, advanced zolucatetide into pivotal positioning, signed a radioligand co-development deal with ARTBIO for alpha-particle therapies, and now landed the Regeneron deal. The pattern is very intentional — he is building Parabilis as a platform with three concurrent legs: the standalone helicon program with zolucatetide as its anchor, the radioligand chemistry leg with ARTBIO, and now the antibody-conjugate leg with Regeneron. Three legs because helicons are a chemical handle that can be plugged into multiple delivery and targeting paradigms. Mammen knows the big-pharma evaluation playbook because he used to be the evaluator. He is structuring the company to be acquired or partnered three different ways. From a BD-strategy standpoint, that is exactly the model a single-asset biotech should not be running.

Regeneron's side of the deal is the post-fianlimab story. Friday's Phase 3 melanoma readout — a five-month numeric progression-free survival benefit that did not reach statistical significance — was the second pipeline-defining loss for Regeneron in twelve months, and the analyst community was openly questioning where the next wave of oncology comes from. The Parabilis deal is the answer in miniature. Regeneron is augmenting an antibody franchise that already includes its REGN-five hundred-series of bispecifics and several A-D-Cs in development, by adding a non-payload modality category. Five targets at fifty million upfront is a cheap option in pharma terms — about ten million dollars per target risked. If even one of the five A-H-C programs makes it to Phase 2, the milestone payments start landing, and Regeneron has a differentiated story to tell beyond Eylea and Dupixent.

So what does this mean for Calibr's pipeline strategy? Five read-throughs to plant.

One. Intracellular protein-protein interactions are no longer the dead zone of drug discovery. The helicon platform has now produced a clinical-stage molecule against a target — beta-catenin T-C-F — that small molecule programs at every major pharma have failed to drug for three decades. Combined with the molecular glue and targeted protein degradation literature that has matured over the past five years, intracellular targets that used to be dismissed in pipeline reviews now have at least three different orthogonal modality options. Calibr's chemistry portfolio should be re-evaluating which Wnt, beta-catenin, transcription-factor, and scaffolding targets in adjacent pathway space deserve a fresh look now that the modality bottleneck is lifting.

Two. Conjugate platforms are not just antibody-drug conjugates anymore. The conjugate concept is generalizing. A-D-Cs were the first wave. Radioligand therapies — including the ARTBIO partnership Parabilis already has — are the second wave. Antibody-helicon conjugates are now the third. The unifying logic is targeted delivery of a payload that the body cannot otherwise safely tolerate at the doses needed for therapeutic effect. Any Calibr biologic with a unique payload class should be thinking about which conjugate partner — antibody, peptide, radioligand carrier — extends the molecule's reach the furthest. The payload-plus-delivery framing is now the dominant pharma modality conversation.

Three. The Mammen BD template is the new normal for platform deals. Fifty million dollars upfront plus seventy-five million in equity plus two point two billion in milestones across five targets is the structure to study. Upfront is small enough that big pharma legal can sign quickly. Equity ties the platform company's downside to the partner's success. Milestones are large enough to be meaningful but spread over time and outcomes. Five targets gives the platform partner enough breadth to mature the relationship into a multi-program collaboration. That structure — small upfront, meaningful equity, large back-loaded milestones, multi-target footprint — is the pattern that emerging platform companies should be pitching, and it is the pattern Calibr-spinout BD discussions should be benchmarked against.

Four. The Foresite and Foresite Labs portfolio question is worth raising even though Parabilis itself is not a Foresite company — its investors are led by Cormorant, Forbion, and General Catalyst. The relevant point is that Foresite's portfolio includes multiple platform companies attempting to address modalities adjacent to or competing with helicons — molecular glue platforms, induced-proximity programs, peptide chemistry plays. The Parabilis-Regeneron deal sets the price and structure benchmark that those Foresite portfolio companies will be measured against in their next BD conversations. Anyone in Foresite's network with a platform that has progressed to clinical-stage proof of mechanism — like zolucatetide — should be positioning for a similar structure within the next twelve to eighteen months.

Five. Multi-leg platforms get higher valuations than single-asset companies, even at equivalent stages. Parabilis has zolucatetide in clinic, ARTBIO for radioligands, and now Regeneron for A-H-Cs. None of those individual deals would have justified the three hundred five million Series F valuation on its own. The combination did. The strategic implication for any Calibr program contemplating spinout — whether it is the MASH biologic, the in vivo cell therapy platform, or the A-I-enabled discovery work — is to have at least two non-overlapping value drivers in flight at the same time. Single-asset spinouts get priced as single assets. Platform companies with multiple modality applications get priced as platforms.

Watchlist for the next twelve months. Zolucatetide Phase 3 desmoid initiation and the first pivotal data readout, which would be the first true validation of the helicon modality. The five undisclosed Regeneron A-H-C targets — how quickly the first I-N-D-enabling program is named, since that is the real signal about whether the technical handshake between the two companies works. Any follow-on deals Parabilis signs in 2026, since Mammen is clearly running an active business development cycle. And the broader question of whether any major pharma decides it wants the whole helicon platform — at which point the acquisition conversation goes from speculative to imminent.

Bottom line. The Parabilis-Regeneron deal is a real new modality category being created in real time, with structured economics that scale through milestones rather than upfront, run by a chairman who knows how to put platforms on the map. For a research institute like Calibr that is thinking about how next-generation conjugate biologics, intracellular target programs, and platform spinouts should be positioned and priced, today's announcement is the most useful single data point of the month. The era when intracellular protein-protein interactions could be dismissed as undruggable is closing fast.
