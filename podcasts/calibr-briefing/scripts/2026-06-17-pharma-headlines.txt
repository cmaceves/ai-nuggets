Good morning. It's Wednesday, June seventeenth, twenty twenty-six, and this is your Calibr-Skaggs Daily Briefing. Yesterday was a busy day for deal-making and platform-validation news — a half-billion-dollar A-I protein-design partnership from Merck, an Eli Lilly bolt-on in non-opioid pain, a private-equity take-private of one of the older A-I drug-development software companies, a positive Phase two cardiac-sarcomere readout that pushes Edgewise into Phase three competition with Bristol-Myers-Squibb and Cytokinetics, and a friendly F-D-A staff review for Moderna's messenger-R-N-A flu vaccine ahead of tomorrow's advisory committee. Today's spotlight is on the Merck-Protillion deal and the broader question of where the A-I-protein-design field is now relative to the antibody-platform-spinout model we discussed yesterday. Five items in the roundup.

First, Merck and Protillion. Yesterday Protillion Biosciences and Merck announced a drug-discovery collaboration potentially worth up to five-hundred-ten million dollars in milestones, plus an undisclosed upfront. Protillion is a Carlsbad California protein-engineering company spun out of Stanford in twenty-nineteen by Curtis Layton and Stanford genetics professor Will Greenleaf. The company's core platform is called Prot-MaP — Protein Display on a Massively Parallel Array — and the technology that distinguishes it is that they generate quantitative protein-binding data by displaying tens of millions of protein clusters directly on Illumina D-N-A sequencing flow cells, characterizing up to one million protein variants in a single forty-eight-hour experiment. The pitch is that the bottleneck for A-I protein design is not the model, it's the training data — and a massively-parallel display platform that produces million-variant quantitative binding datasets is the missing input. Protillion has raised eighteen million dollars in its Series A back in twenty twenty-two from ARCH Venture Partners and Illumina Ventures, and now sits with thirty employees and a Merck multi-program collaboration starting in inflammatory disease. Today's spotlight in a few hours unpacks the platform mechanics, the strategic logic for Merck, and the read-through to Calibr's antibody discovery and combination-medicine programs.

Second, Eli Lilly and 4E Therapeutics. Yesterday Lilly announced its acquisition of 4E Therapeutics, an Austin Texas neuroscience company developing non-opioid chronic-pain therapeutics. Terms were undisclosed. 4E's lead asset is 4-E-T-eleven-oh-three, the first M-N-K inhibitor for pain to enter human clinical trials. The target is the M-N-K-to-e-I-F-four-E signaling pathway in peripheral sensory neurons — a translation-initiation pathway that controls the synthesis of pain-mediator proteins in nociceptors. The pharmacological logic is that selectively blocking translation in pain-sensing neurons quiets the chronic-pain signal at its source without crossing into the central nervous system, sidestepping the addiction and sedation profile that has defined opioid pain control. 4-E-T-eleven-oh-three cleared its Phase one safety study and additional programs in the pipeline target migraines and acute pain. This is Lilly's second non-opioid-pain acquisition in two years, following the up-to-one-billion-dollar SiteOne Therapeutics deal in May twenty twenty-five, against the backdrop that Lilly has also discontinued two pain candidates in the same window. The Vertex Journavx approval last year set the regulatory precedent for non-opioid chronic pain, and the competitive race to build the next-generation non-opioid pain pipeline is now visibly on. 4E had raised under ten million dollars across private capital and N-I-H grants — a remarkably capital-efficient asset for what is now a high-profile pharma bolt-on.

Third, Altaris Capital and Simulations Plus. Yesterday Simulations Plus announced an agreement to be taken private by Altaris Capital for approximately three-hundred-seventy-five million dollars in cash at eighteen-dollars-fifty per share, a twenty-six-percent premium to the sixty-day average. Simulations Plus is one of the older A-I-accelerated drug-development software companies — physiologically-based pharmacokinetic modeling, A-I-driven A-D-M-E-T prediction, model-informed drug development tools used across most major pharmas — and the Altaris plan is to combine it with Chemical Computing Group, another Altaris portfolio holding, into a larger private A-I-drug-discovery software platform. The deal is expected to close in the fourth quarter of twenty twenty-six. The interesting strategic angle is the consolidation pattern. A-I drug-discovery software has gone through a decade of fragmentation — small focused tooling companies serving the pharma industry — and Altaris is now rolling those pieces up at the private-equity level. Watch for whether further P-E roll-ups of the cheminformatics and modeling layer follow. Public A-I drug-discovery companies — Recursion, Schrödinger, AbCellera — sit in a separate category since their business models include their own internal pipelines.

Fourth, Edgewise Therapeutics. Yesterday Edgewise reported positive top-line data from Part D of the Phase two CIRRUS-H-C-M trial of E-D-G seventy-five-hundred, the company's selective cardiac-sarcomere modulator, in fifty-three patients with hypertrophic cardiomyopathy — twenty obstructive and thirty-three nonobstructive. The mechanism is different from the cardiac-myosin inhibitor approach taken by Bristol-Myers-Squibb's Camzyos and Cytokinetics's aficamten. E-D-G seventy-five-hundred slows early contraction velocity and improves impaired cardiac relaxation without compromising systolic function — a sarcomere modulator rather than a pure myosin inhibitor. At twelve weeks, ninety percent of obstructive-H-C-M patients improved on hemodynamic left-ventricular-outflow-tract gradient measures, seventy-four percent achieved N-T-pro-B-N-P normalization or at least fifty-percent reduction, and in the nonobstructive arm — which the myosin inhibitors do not have a label for — about sixty-five-percent mean N-T-pro-B-N-P reduction with eighty-eight percent meeting the response definition. Kansas-City-Cardiomyopathy-Questionnaire overall summary scores improved by twenty-four points in obstructive H-C-M and thirteen points in nonobstructive. No patients dropped left-ventricular ejection fraction below fifty percent — meaningful, because the boxed-warning concern with the myosin inhibitors is systolic dysfunction. Edgewise plans to start Phase three in the fourth quarter. The shape of the H-C-M landscape now looks like this — Bristol-Myers-Squibb owns obstructive H-C-M, Cytokinetics is the cleaner-profile second entrant in obstructive, and Edgewise is positioning to be the first sarcomere modulator with a credible nonobstructive-H-C-M label. The nonobstructive market is roughly twice the size of the obstructive market and is currently unserved by any approved targeted therapy.

Fifth and last, Moderna's messenger-R-N-A flu vaccine. Yesterday the F-D-A released its briefing documents ahead of tomorrow's Vaccines and Related Biological Products Advisory Committee meeting on M-Flusiva, Moderna's messenger-R-N-A influenza vaccine for adults age fifty and older. The staff review found no myocarditis safety signal and no major efficacy deficiencies — a sharply different tone from the refuse-to-file letter the agency sent Moderna in February. The pivotal Phase three trial enrolled more than forty-thousand adults aged fifty and over and showed roughly twenty-seven percent better relative efficacy than the standard licensed comparator G-S-K Fluarix. The committee will vote on two questions tomorrow — risk-benefit for adults fifty-to-sixty-four on traditional approval logic, and for sixty-five-plus on accelerated-approval logic with a post-marketing confirmatory Phase four. Moderna stock moved meaningfully on the briefing-document release. The broader market read is that the messenger-R-N-A platform is rebuilding regulatory credibility for non-COVID indications after a difficult eighteen months — which matters for every messenger-R-N-A-platform follow-on program including the cancer-vaccine programs that several big pharmas now have in clinic.
