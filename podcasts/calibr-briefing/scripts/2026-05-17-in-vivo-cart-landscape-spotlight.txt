Welcome to today's spotlight on the Calibr-Skaggs Daily Briefing. I'm Nigel.

Today we're going deep on in vivo CAR-T after ASGCT 2026 — what Create Medicines' new one hundred twenty-two million dollar Series B actually says about the capital and platform race, what came out of the meeting in Boston that just wrapped Friday, and where the relevant Calibr read-throughs sit. This is the focus area Travis has flagged as the one to track most closely, so it's worth a careful pass on where the field is right now.

Let's start with the raise. Create Medicines closed one hundred twenty-two million dollars on Thursday last week, co-led by Arch Venture Partners, Newpath Partners, and Hatteras Venture Partners, with Alexandria Venture Investments and the existing syndicate joining. The company was founded in 2021 as Myeloid Therapeutics by Ron Vale — the UCSF and Howard Hughes cell biologist who co-founded Cytokinetics — and Siddhartha Mukherjee, the Columbia oncologist and Pulitzer winner. They rebranded in 2023 when they expanded beyond their original in vivo CAR macrophage focus into T cells and NK cells. The platform is an mRNA lipid-nanoparticle system designed to deliver chimeric antigen receptor messenger RNA directly to immune cells in the body, with targeting moieties on the LNP surface that bias delivery toward T, NK, or myeloid populations. The lead clinical asset is MT-304, an anti-HER2 multi-immune CAR currently in Phase 1/2 in HER2-positive breast cancer and other solid tumors. They also have CD19 and BCMA programs heading toward autoimmune indications. And — this is the part that matters competitively — Create now claims more than fifty patients dosed across its in vivo CAR programs. That makes their dataset, by their own reporting, the largest in clinical in vivo CAR.

Why does this raise matter? Three reasons.

First, the capital environment. One hundred twenty-two million for a private company that is not at a Phase 2 inflection is a real check in 2026 — most Series B rounds in cell therapy this year have been in the seventy-to-ninety million range. Arch leading is the relevant tell, because Arch was the founding investor in companies like Sana and Lyell — they price in vivo cell engineering as a platform play, not as a single-asset story. The implicit thesis is that whoever owns the targeted-LNP plus modular cargo chassis wins multiple indications.

Second, the patient count. Fifty patients dosed across multiple programs is more than the rest of the dedicated in vivo CAR field combined. Capstan, before AbbVie acquired it last summer, had dosed roughly a dozen in its autoimmune program. Umoja has dosed in the low single digits. Therorna's circular RNA CD19 candidate just entered a first-in-human investigator-initiated trial in autoimmune patients in China. Sana and Azalea are still preclinical. So Create's "largest clinical dataset in the field" framing is real — and it gives them an operational learning curve on LNP tolerability, transient versus persistent transgene expression, and adaptive immunity buildup that nobody else has yet.

Third, the platform diversification. Create's RetroT all-RNA gene writing system is an attempt to add site-specific genomic integration to the mRNA-LNP chassis — meaning they want to go from transient CAR expression to durable T-cell engraftment without leaving the LNP delivery substrate. That's the central technical question for the whole field right now. Transient CAR expression — which is what straight mRNA gives you — looks great in NHP B-cell depletion data because B cells take weeks to recover, but it almost certainly won't sustain the multi-year remission patterns that ex vivo CAR-T has shown in oncology. Durable engraftment requires either genomic integration or some other persistence mechanism.

Now zoom out to what came out of ASGCT in Boston this week. The Endpoints dispatch from the meeting put it well: in vivo CAR-T is everywhere, but it's still preclinical. The roster Friday morning alone tells the story. Sana presented its SG293 surrogate with cell-specific delivery and deep B-cell depletion in non-human primates, with no lymphodepleting chemotherapy, on May 12th. Azalea Therapeutics presented late-breaking first-in-primate data on its TRAC-CAR T platform on Friday — site-specific integration at the T-cell receptor alpha constant locus, done in vivo, in non-human primates. Therorna showed preclinical data supporting TI-0032, its circular RNA CD19-targeted in vivo CAR candidate now in that first-in-human investigator-initiated trial. And separately during the week, Tessera Therapeutics — the Flagship company we did a spotlight on earlier this week — disclosed that the same targeted-LNP chassis they use for in vivo gene editing in hematopoietic stem cells also generates functional CD19 and CD20 CAR-T cells in animal models, presented Friday morning by Alberto De Iaco. Every one of those is a platform claim. None of them is a human efficacy readout.

The closest thing the autoimmune wing of the field has to a clinical proof point is still Cabaletta's RESET-PV data in pemphigus vulgaris — drug-free clinical responses in roughly half of patients at the lowest dose of miv-cel, importantly without preconditioning chemotherapy. But miv-cel is still ex vivo CAR-T, not in vivo. So when we talk about "in vivo CAR-T clinical data," we are talking about a small handful of dosed patients across Create's MT-304 in solid tumors, Capstan's pre-acquisition autoimmune cohort, and now Therorna's IIT in China. The translational gap from NHP B-cell depletion to durable human autoimmune remission has not been crossed.

So the practical question for Calibr is, what does this all mean for the in vivo CAR-T program inside the campus pipeline? A few things to plant.

One. Tropism engineering is the discriminating axis, and the bar keeps rising. Sana's NHP work, Capstan's published mouse-to-NHP scaling, and Tessera's roughly fifty-two-fold marrow-to-liver biodistribution number — that's the kind of quantitative spec that IND-enabling packages are going to be judged against. Anyone moving an in vivo CAR-T program through CMC and IND-enabling tox should plan to report cell-type-specific delivery numbers, off-target liver and lymph node distribution, and a clean comparator to one of the published benchmark molecules. Vague "targeted LNP" claims won't survive review.

Two. The autoimmune indication path is the strategic wedge. Oncology in vivo CAR-T has to compete against approved ex vivo products with multi-year remission data — that's an uphill comparator. Autoimmune is where in vivo wins on accessibility — no chemo, no apheresis, broader patient pool — and where the bar is "comparable to high-dose biologics," which is a real but achievable target. Cabaletta's RESET-PV is the clinical reference point, and Cabaletta has the Eli Lilly relationship and the one hundred fifty million dollar registered direct from last week underwriting the next set of readouts. Any Calibr in vivo CAR-T positioning should be honest about where it sits in that landscape and which autoimmune indication has the cleanest biology.

Three. Durability is the unresolved central question, and platform choice determines how you answer it. Pure mRNA gives you transient expression and great safety but probably doesn't sustain disease modification. Lentiviral or virus-like-particle delivery — Umoja's VivoVec, for example — gives you integration but inherits some of the manufacturing and immunogenicity issues of viral vectors. Site-specific gene writing — Create's RetroT, Tessera's gene writer — is the elegant answer if it works at scale, but the first clinical durability readouts on those approaches are probably still twelve to eighteen months out. Internal program decisions should explicitly stake a position on the durability mechanism rather than punting it to the platform.

Four. Calibr's multi-modality story benefits from this moment. The deal market is consolidating around bundled-platform stories — the BMS-Hengrui thirteen-asset deal last week is the cleanest recent example. An institution with a MASH biologic going through IND-enabling and an in vivo CAR-T platform aimed at autoimmune sits exactly at the kind of bundle valuation premium that next-generation acquirers are paying. That positioning is worth being explicit about in any BD conversation.

Watchlist for the next twelve months. Whether Create Medicines pulls efficacy data from MT-304 or any of the autoimmune candidates that justifies the platform claim. Whether Capstan under AbbVie shows clinical data in 2026. Whether Sana or Azalea move from NHP into first-in-human. Whether Therorna's Chinese IIT generates clean tolerability and a B-cell depletion signal in autoimmune patients — that would be the first clinical autoimmune in vivo CAR readout. Whether any of these platforms shows durable T-cell engraftment, which would change the whole conversation. And whether Cabaletta confirms its preconditioning-free response signal at longer follow-up, because that's the autoimmune accessibility argument standing or falling.

Bottom line. In vivo CAR-T is now a fully capitalized field with platform diversity, real preclinical proof in non-human primates, and a small handful of dosed patients in oncology and autoimmune indications. The Create Medicines raise this week was the year's most concrete signal that the capital is staying in. But the field's central question — durable T-cell engraftment after in vivo delivery, in humans — is still unanswered. For Calibr, the strategic message is to pick a clear platform position on tropism, durability mechanism, and autoimmune indication, benchmark against the best published preclinical numbers, and move IND-enabling work with the understanding that the bar will keep rising through the end of 2026.
