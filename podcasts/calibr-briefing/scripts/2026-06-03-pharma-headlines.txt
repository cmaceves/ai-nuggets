Good morning. It's Wednesday, June third, twenty twenty-six, and this is your Calibr-Skaggs Daily Briefing. ASCO is in the rearview mirror. The post-conference news flow is shifting back to financings, licensing deals, and regulatory plumbing, and five items merit attention this morning.

First, NewLimit closed four hundred thirty-five million dollars in Series C funding led by Peter Thiel's Founders Fund. The cellular reprogramming biotech, founded in twenty twenty-one by Coinbase chief executive Brian Armstrong and former Google scientist Blake Byers, will use the proceeds to advance its lead liver-targeted messenger-RNA program into first-in-human trials next year. Existing investors Kleiner Perkins, Eli Lilly Ventures, and Human Capital all re-upped, joined by Thrive Capital, Greenoaks, and Quiet Capital. Post-money valuation triples to three-point-one billion dollars from roughly one billion fifteen months ago, which is a remarkable mark-up in a market that has otherwise been brutally compressing on private biotech values. The scientific premise is partial cellular reprogramming: pulsed delivery of transcription factors to reset cellular age markers without erasing tissue identity. Of the syndicate composition, the strategic name to watch is Lilly Ventures, given parent Lilly's recent dominance of the in vivo cell therapy field after the Orna and Kelonia transactions. We go deep on the NewLimit thesis in today's spotlight.

Second, Travere Therapeutics signed an exclusive licensing deal with Shanghai-based Everest Medicines for civorebrutinib, a covalent reversible Bruton's tyrosine kinase inhibitor in development for rare immune-mediated kidney diseases. Terms: one hundred twelve and a half million dollars upfront, up to one-point-zero-three billion in clinical, regulatory, and commercial milestones, and tiered high-single-digit to double-digit royalties on net sales in Travere's territory. The lead indication is primary membranous nephropathy, with planned expansion into focal segmental glomerulosclerosis and minimal change disease. Civorebrutinib's covalent-reversible chemistry is the differentiator: durable target engagement without the irreversible off-target liabilities that have constrained BTK inhibitors in autoimmune indications. Both parties signed a ten-year non-compete on competing BTK products in the rare kidney disease field. This is a real franchise extension for Travere from its sparsentan-anchored nephrology business into the immune-mediated kidney disease category that is becoming a real therapeutic lane.

Third, Vera Therapeutics announced alignment with the FDA on an earlier eGFR analysis from the ORIGIN Phase three trial of atacicept in IgA nephropathy. The revised plan moves the confirmatory eGFR readout to the third quarter of this year. Atacicept already has FDA Priority Review for accelerated approval based on a forty-six percent reduction in proteinuria from baseline at week thirty-six, with a PDUFA date of July seventh. The earlier-than-expected eGFR data, which is the gold-standard outcome for kidney function preservation, sets up a potential conversion from accelerated to full approval shortly after launch rather than years later. Atacicept's mechanism is dual blockade of B-cell activating factor and APRIL, both upstream drivers of pathogenic IgA-producing plasma cells. Put together with the Travere-Everest BTK transaction in the same morning's news, the rare immune-mediated kidney disease category is now a credible franchise opportunity with B-cell axis biologics, BTK chemistry, and complement modulators all converging.

Fourth, Roche's oral selective estrogen receptor degrader giredestrant missed its primary endpoint in the Phase three persevERA trial in first-line metastatic breast cancer. The combination of giredestrant plus palbociclib in hormone-receptor-positive, HER2-negative disease showed a numerical improvement in progression-free survival but no statistical separation versus the comparator. Roche has already filed and won Priority Review for giredestrant in early-stage breast cancer based on the positive evERA results, so the franchise is not dead, but the first-line metastatic setting was the larger commercial prize. This raises real questions about whether oral SERDs as a class can clear the bar in front-line combination settings, particularly against AstraZeneca's camizestrant, which is on a different regulatory trajectory after the PDUFA delay we covered last week, and Pfizer's vepdegestrant. The next-generation complete estrogen receptor antagonists, including the elacestrant followers, now have a more open competitive lane than they did last week.

Fifth, Takeda is moving toward an FDA filing later this fiscal year for zasocitinib, its oral TYK-two inhibitor in moderate-to-severe plaque psoriasis. Both pivotal Phase three trials met their co-primary endpoints. PASI-ninety rates were sixty-one and fifty-two percent at week sixteen across the two studies, against five and four percent for placebo and sixteen and fifteen percent for apremilast as the active comparator. The PASI-ninety differentiation against apremilast is the headline talking point, and the safety profile remained clean with upper respiratory infection and acne as the most common adverse events. Zasocitinib is Takeda's challenger to Bristol Myers Squibb's Sotyktu, with the same TYK-two pseudokinase-domain allosteric mechanism. Takeda is also running pivotals in psoriatic arthritis, Crohn's disease, and ulcerative colitis. That broader inflammatory bowel disease label is where the four-billion-dollar acquisition cost from the Nimbus deal starts to make sense.
