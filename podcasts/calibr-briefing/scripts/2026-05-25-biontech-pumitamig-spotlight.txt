Good morning Travis. This is your Calibr-Skaggs spotlight for Memorial Day Monday, May twenty-fifth, twenty twenty-six. Today we're going deep on BioNTech and Bristol Myers Squibb's pumitamig — B-N-T three-two-seven, also known as B-M-S nine-eight-six-five-four-five — heading into the ROSETTA Lung-zero-two readout at ASCO on May thirtieth. On the surface this is one more late-stage I-O readout in non-small-cell lung cancer. But pumitamig sits at the center of three converging stories — BioNTech's transformation from m-R-N-A vaccine company to broad oncology platform, BMS's eleven-point-one-billion-dollar bet on bispecific antibody chemistry as the next I-O backbone, and the Summit-Akeso ivonescimab competitive frame that is now four years into reshaping how the field thinks about P-D-axis-plus-V-E-G-F combinations. The Calibr read-throughs touch bispecific antibody platform thinking, partnership economics, and the question of how Chinese-origin assets continue to set the pace in I-O. Let me unpack it.

Start with the molecule. Pumitamig is a bispecific antibody — one molecule, two arms, each arm engineered to bind a different target. The first arm binds P-D-L-one, the ligand that tumor cells use to suppress T-cell anti-tumor activity through the P-D-one-P-D-L-one immune checkpoint. The second arm binds V-E-G-F-A, the vascular endothelial growth factor that drives tumor angiogenesis and creates the abnormal, leaky, immunosuppressive vasculature that tumors use to evade immune surveillance. The biological hypothesis is straightforward — block both at once. Checkpoint inhibition lifts the brake on tumor-killing T-cells. V-E-G-F neutralization normalizes the tumor vasculature, increases T-cell infiltration, and reduces V-E-G-F-driven recruitment of myeloid-derived suppressor cells and regulatory T-cells. The two mechanisms are not additive in the obvious cytotoxic sense. They are convergent, both targeting the immune-evasion phenotype, but from different cellular angles. A single bispecific molecule does both at once with one infusion, one pharmacokinetic profile, and a fixed stoichiometry between the two arms.

The bispecific format matters. The alternative — combining a P-D-one or P-D-L-one antibody with bevacizumab, the standard anti-V-E-G-F monoclonal — has been tried and has worked clinically. The IMpower-one-fifty trial in non-squamous N-S-C-L-C was the original validation, combining atezolizumab plus bevacizumab plus chemotherapy. But running two separate antibodies introduces independent pharmacokinetics, independent dose schedules, and unbalanced exposure to the two mechanisms across the dosing cycle. A bispecific solves that. It also opens the door to engineering — affinity tuning on each arm, F-c silencing or activation, half-life engineering — that you cannot do across two separate molecules.

Now the competitive frame. Ivonescimab — A-K one-oh-four — is Summit Therapeutics's bispecific antibody licensed from Akeso, a Chinese biotech, in late twenty twenty-two. Ivonescimab binds P-D-one, not P-D-L-one, on the checkpoint arm — and V-E-G-F-A on the other arm. So the closest competitive pairing for pumitamig is the same bispecific concept with the P-D-axis target reversed. In late twenty twenty-four, Akeso reported HARMONi-two in China — a Phase three trial of ivonescimab monotherapy versus pembrolizumab monotherapy in first-line P-D-L-one-positive non-small-cell lung cancer. The result was an eleven-month median progression-free survival for ivonescimab versus five-point-eight months for pembrolizumab, hazard ratio zero-point-five-one. Pembrolizumab is the global standard. A bispecific monotherapy beating Keytruda head-to-head was the read that detonated the field. It is the first credible argument in eight years that anything will replace Keytruda monotherapy in first-line N-S-C-L-C. Summit subsequently filed for U-S approval and is running the HARMONi-three global Phase three for confirmation.

Pumitamig sits in the same conceptual space. Different P-D-axis target — P-D-L-one rather than P-D-one — but the same V-E-G-F arm, the same biological premise, and the same indication strategy starting with first-line N-S-C-L-C. The ROSETTA Lung-zero-two trial that drops on May thirtieth is pumitamig plus chemotherapy versus chemotherapy in first-line non-small-cell lung cancer — squamous and non-squamous, all P-D-L-one expression levels, no actionable driver mutations. The interim Phase two dose-optimization data informs the ongoing pivotal Phase three, which is pumitamig plus chemotherapy versus pembrolizumab plus chemotherapy — a global head-to-head against the current standard of care.

The existing pumitamig dataset is encouraging. In extensive-stage small-cell lung cancer earlier this year, BioNTech and BMS reported a seventy-six-point-three percent confirmed overall response rate among thirty-eight evaluable treatment-naive patients in a Phase two trial of pumitamig plus chemotherapy. For S-C-L-C — a disease where standard chemo-immunotherapy combinations get response rates around sixty percent and where progression-free survival is measured in five to six months — a seventy-six percent O-R-R is a large mover. That dataset set the Phase three dose. The ROSETTA Lung-zero-two readout this week is the bigger commercial story because N-S-C-L-C is the dominant indication.

Now BioNTech's path to pumitamig. BioNTech acquired Biotheus — a Chinese biotech — in November twenty twenty-four for around eight-hundred million dollars upfront plus milestones. Biotheus was the originator of B-N-T three-two-seven. This was the deal that announced BioNTech's pivot from m-R-N-A vaccine company to broad oncology platform, post-COVID, with the cash hoard the company built during the pandemic. In June twenty twenty-five, BioNTech then partnered with Bristol Myers Squibb on pumitamig — fifteen-hundred million dollars upfront, two billion in equal annual non-contingent anniversary payments over five years, and up to seven-point-six billion in milestones, for a deal value of up to eleven-point-one billion dollars. Co-development, co-commercialization, split economics. That deal told the market two things — first, BMS believed bispecific antibody chemistry was the next I-O backbone and was willing to put eleven-billion behind a single asset; second, Chinese-origin assets continued to set the pace, and U-S and European large pharma would now systematically partner or acquire to access them rather than out-license from the legacy West-to-East direction. This is the largest single-asset I-O bispecific deal in industry history.

What does pumitamig mean for the broader BioNTech ASCO push? Three other assets share the meeting. Gotistobart — formerly O-N-C dash three-nine-two — is a next-generation C-T-L-A-four antibody that drops the PRESERVE-zero-zero-four Phase two overall survival data in platinum-resistant ovarian cancer on May thirtieth, eight A-M central. C-T-L-A-four is the original checkpoint target — Yervoy, the first checkpoint inhibitor, opened the field in twenty eleven — but C-T-L-A-four monotherapy has not had a real second life since. Gotistobart is engineered for p-H-dependent target binding to reduce systemic toxicity, the historical Achilles heel of C-T-L-A-four. If the ovarian survival data is real, gotistobart re-opens the C-T-L-A-four lane that the field had largely written off. BioNTech also presents multiple A-D-C combinations and novel-novel checkpoint pairings — more than twenty-five Phase two and Phase three studies across thirteen pivotal trials are now in the pipeline. The strategic message from this ASCO is that BioNTech is no longer an m-R-N-A vaccine company. It is an oncology platform with multiple late-stage molecules and the cash to fund all of them.

Now the Calibr read-throughs. Four of them.

First — bispecific antibody chemistry is the dominant late-stage I-O modality. The era of P-D-one and P-D-L-one monotherapy is closing. The next decade of immuno-oncology is built on bispecific platforms that combine checkpoint blockade with a second, mechanistically convergent target. Pumitamig and ivonescimab anchor the P-D-axis-plus-V-E-G-F bispecific class. Roche's R-O-seven-one-two-one-six-six-one is a P-D-one-T-I-M-three bispecific. AstraZeneca's volrustomig is P-D-one-C-T-L-A-four. There are now over fifty Phase two and three bispecific I-O assets in clinical development. The Calibr biologics function should be thinking in bispecific terms by default for any immuno-oncology asset, with single-mechanism antibodies as the fallback rather than the lead.

Second — partnership economics for bispecific platforms are large and frontloaded. The BMS-BioNTech pumitamig deal was eleven-point-one billion dollars in total value, one-point-five billion dollars upfront, two billion in non-contingent anniversaries. The Lilly-Insilico AI-platform deal earlier this year was two-point-seven-five billion. The Pfizer-Schrodinger deal for property-prediction A-I was a couple of billion. The relative scale tells you that platforms producing late-stage clinical bispecifics command premium economics. Calibr's small-molecule and biologics platforms have to be positioned as having that kind of late-stage productivity — which means the partnership posture, when Calibr brings late-stage bispecific data, should anchor on multi-billion deal expectations, not on five-hundred-million-dollar option agreements.

Third — Chinese-origin assets continue to set the pace. Pumitamig came from Biotheus. Ivonescimab came from Akeso. Merck and Kelun-Biotech's sac-T-M-T A-D-C came from Kelun, where the deal economics ran to over one-point-four billion in upfront and milestones for the lead asset. The pattern is the same as semiconductor and consumer-electronics maturation — global I-O drug development is now multipolar, with Chinese biotechs delivering competitive late-stage assets at a steady pace and U-S and European large pharma systematically buying access. For Calibr, the implication is the global competitive set is now wider than it used to be. Every program decision has to account for Chinese-origin programs in the same indication.

Fourth — the bispecific I-O backbone applies to combination strategies beyond I-O monotherapy. Pumitamig combines with chemotherapy in N-S-C-L-C. The next move will be pumitamig combining with antibody-drug conjugates, with bi-specific T-cell engagers, with mRNA cancer vaccines, with C-A-R-T. The bispecific is not a replacement for everything else. It is the new center of gravity around which combination regimens are built. Calibr's solid-tumor strategy — across ADCs, bispecifics, cell therapy, and small molecules — should anchor on the assumption that future combinations will include a P-D-axis-plus-V-E-G-F bispecific as the I-O leg, the way pembrolizumab is the I-O leg today. That changes which combination partners look attractive and which clinical-trial designs make sense for solid-tumor programs.

A quick word on what to watch this week. The ROSETTA Lung-zero-two data on May thirtieth will be evaluated on three axes — overall response rate against the seventy-six percent S-C-L-C bar BioNTech and BMS already set, progression-free survival relative to the ivonescimab HARMONi-two ceiling, and the P-D-L-one subgroup analysis. If response is restricted to P-D-L-one-positive patients, the commercial case narrows and the ivonescimab gap matters more.
