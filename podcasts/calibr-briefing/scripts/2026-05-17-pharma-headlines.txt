Number one. Create Medicines closed a one hundred twenty-two million dollar Series B, with Arch Venture Partners, Newpath Partners, and Hatteras Venture Partners co-leading, and Alexandria Venture Investments joining. The company you may remember as Myeloid Therapeutics until it rebranded — it was founded in 2021 by Ron Vale and Siddhartha Mukherjee around an mRNA lipid-nanoparticle platform for in vivo immune cell programming. The pitch is that a single intravenous infusion of targeted LNPs delivers messenger RNA encoding a chimeric antigen receptor directly into T cells, NK cells, or myeloid cells inside the body — no apheresis, no ex vivo manufacturing, redosable. The lead clinical asset is MT-304, an anti-HER2 multi-immune CAR in Phase 1/2 for HER2-positive breast cancer and other solid tumors. The company also has CD19 and BCMA programs heading toward autoimmune indications. Worth flagging: Create says it has now dosed more than fifty patients across its in vivo CAR programs, which it calls the largest clinical dataset in the field. That number matters because every in vivo CAR-T competitor — Capstan, Umoja, Orna, Sana, Therorna, Azalea, the rest — is still NHP-stage or in single-digit clinical dosing.

Number two. Biogen's diranersen, the tau-targeting antisense oligonucleotide it has been developing for early Alzheimer's, missed its Phase 2 primary endpoint in the CELIA study. The trial tested three escalating intrathecal doses against placebo over seventy-six weeks. The primary endpoint was a dose response on the Clinical Dementia Rating Sum of Boxes — and the dose response failed because, awkwardly, the lowest dose looked the best. Cerebrospinal fluid tau and tau pathology imaging dropped across all doses, and slowing of clinical decline tracked the tau biomarker, but only at the lowest dose did the cognitive separation reach a meaningful signal. Biogen is moving the lowest dose directly into Phase 3 anyway, citing the tau-to-cognition correlation and the consistent biomarker effect. The stock dropped roughly five percent on the readout. The takeaway is that the tau hypothesis is still alive but the dose-response story is uglier than the amyloid pivot a few years ago, and the field is going to watch the full data at the Alzheimer's Association International Conference this summer to judge whether Biogen is pushing on signal or pushing on hope.

Number three. BridgeBio's Attruby — acoramidis, the TTR stabilizer for transthyretin amyloid cardiomyopathy — keeps building separation from Pfizer's Vyndamax franchise. New data presented at Heart Failure 2026 in Barcelona, with Bayer presenting in Europe as the licensing partner, showed acoramidis reduced outpatient worsening heart failure events on top of the previously reported survival benefit. The long-term ATTRibute-CM extension is now showing a forty-four point seven percent reduction in all-cause mortality and a forty-nine point three percent reduction in cardiovascular mortality through fifty-four months. Real-world evidence circulating on medRxiv shows acoramidis reducing diuretic intensification by forty-three percent versus tafamidis. And the commercial side is tracking: Q1 revenue came in at one hundred eighty point six million, up twenty-four percent from the same quarter a year ago. The ATTR-CM story matters because it's one of the cleanest recent examples of a small biotech taking real market share from a multibillion-dollar incumbent on a head-to-head biomarker-and-outcomes argument — a useful template for how Calibr's next-generation MASH biologic could position against incumbent FGF21 and THR-beta agents at launch.

Number four. The ASGCT annual meeting wrapped in Boston on Friday, and the dominant theme across the week was in vivo CAR-T. Endpoints' dispatch from the meeting summed it up well — in vivo CAR-T is everywhere, but it's still preclinical. The data this week were almost entirely in non-human primates. Sana Biotechnology presented its SG293 surrogate with cell-specific delivery and deep B-cell depletion in NHPs without lymphodepleting chemotherapy. Azalea Therapeutics presented first-in-primate TRAC-CAR T engineering — site-specific knock-in at the T-cell receptor alpha constant locus in vivo, the late-breaking oral on Friday morning. Therorna showed preclinical data on TI-0032, its circular RNA CD19-targeted in vivo CAR-T candidate, which has now advanced into an investigator-initiated first-in-human trial in autoimmune patients in China. And Cabaletta's RESET-PV pemphigus vulgaris data from earlier in the week — drug-free clinical responses without preconditioning chemotherapy — remains the closest thing the autoimmune in vivo CAR-T space has to a clinical proof point, even though Cabaletta's miv-cel is technically still ex vivo. The Create Medicines raise on top of all this signals that capital is flowing into the platform race even though no in vivo CAR-T product has yet demonstrated durable T-cell engraftment in humans.
