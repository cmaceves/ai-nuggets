## Script

Good morning. This is Receptor and Reason for Sunday, June 8th, 2026. Ten items today, weighted toward neuropsychopharm with a couple of computational and agentic AI picks at the end. We open with a result that has been a long time coming.

A group at UCL Queen Square has the first direct electrophysiological evidence of reward processing in the human ventral tegmental area. The team piggybacked on deep brain stimulation surgery for chronic cluster headache — these are patients getting electrodes placed in the VTA itself for pain control, and the surgical window opens a once-in-a-decade opportunity for cognitive electrophysiology on the dopaminergic midbrain in awake humans. Thirteen patients did an instrumental learning task with two options paying off at different reward rates. VTA local field potentials selectively encoded rewarding outcomes — bigger than for losses or neutral outcomes — and this was independent of whether the patient learned to prefer the high-reward option. In the eight participants who did learn the contingency, the VTA response to unexpected rewards was larger than the response to expected rewards. In other words, a reward prediction error signal, in the human VTA, recorded directly, doing what the Schultz monkey-dopamine literature would predict. And in two participants who explored both options enough, the VTA encoded the expected value of the chosen option during decision-making. This is a small sample by design — these recordings are rare — but as a translational anchor between rodent reinforcement-learning circuit work and human reinforcement learning, it is about as direct a confirmation as you can get.

Item two stays in that same VTA-dopamine circuitry but moves to depression. Molecular Psychiatry published a paper from Wuhan University this week on the Nogo receptor, NgR, in the dorsal CA3 of the hippocampus and how it gates a dCA3 to VTA to nucleus accumbens dopamine pathway in stress-induced depressive behavior. Chronic unpredictable mild stress in male mice raises NgR expression specifically in glutamatergic neurons in dorsal CA3, and those neurons become less excitable. Knock down NgR in dCA3 or boost the excitability of those glutamatergic cells and depressive-like behavior is alleviated; do the opposite and stress-induced depressive behavior gets worse. The mechanism, traced with circuit-specific manipulations, is that dCA3 glutamatergic neurons project specifically to VTA dopamine neurons, which then project to nucleus accumbens, and inhibition of nucleus accumbens D1 and D2 receptors is required to produce the depressive behavior. NgR is the receptor for the myelin-associated growth inhibitors — Nogo, MAG, OMgp — so this puts a myelin-related signaling pathway upstream of the canonical hippocampal-VTA-NAc reward circuit and frames it as a candidate antidepressant target. Worth noting it is male-only and CUMS-only, so generalization across sex and across depression models is still open.

Sticking with addiction-relevant circuits, Neuropsychopharmacology published a Yale paper on the submedius thalamus and its reciprocal connections with orbitofrontal cortex in incubation of oxycodone craving. Incubation of craving — the well-replicated phenomenon where drug seeking actually intensifies during abstinence rather than decaying — is one of the cleaner behavioral models we have for the relapse problem. Submedius is a smaller thalamic nucleus with reciprocal OFC connections that has not gotten much attention in this literature. Bilateral pharmacological inactivation of submedius reduced oxycodone seeking on abstinence day 15, when craving has incubated, but not on day 1, when it hasn't. Retrograde tracing combined with Fos showed that both directions of the submedius-OFC connection were activated during incubated seeking. And contralateral disconnection — inactivating submedius on one side and OFC on the other, which forces the circuit to operate through the disconnected pathway — also blocked incubated seeking. So submedius and OFC are talking to each other during incubated oxycodone craving, and breaking that conversation blocks the behavior. A new node for opioid relapse circuits.

Fourth item, a comparative dopamine biology preprint from Rochester. The rodent literature has converged on the idea that midbrain dopamine neurons are not uniformly dopaminergic — many co-release glutamate or GABA, and the proportions matter for behavior. Whether that generalizes to primates has been less clear. This group did RNAscope in situ hybridization in five young macaques across the A10 VTA region, the A9 substantia nigra pars compacta, and the A8 retrorubral field, probing tyrosine hydroxylase, vesicular glutamate transporter 2, and glutamic acid decarboxylase 1. The headline number is that multiplexed transmitter neurons were more frequent than TH-only neurons — about 22 percent expressed TH plus VGluT2, another 23 percent expressed all three markers, and TH-only neurons were just 19 percent of the population. GAD1 co-expression mostly occurred in the triple-labeled cells. This holds across all three midbrain subregions. The takeaway is that fast synaptic co-transmission alongside dopaminergic neuromodulation is the rule, not the exception, in young primate midbrain. That has real consequences for how we read VTA activity in humans, including the UCL result we just talked about — those LFPs are not pure dopaminergic activity, and the glutamatergic component is a meaningful fraction of the signal.

Item five, also basal ganglia but a very different angle. A group at Nagoya City University looking at cholesterol homeostasis in substantia nigra pars reticulata parvalbumin neurons. SNr is the output station of the basal ganglia, and its parvalbumin GABAergic neurons provide the tonic inhibition that gates action selection downstream. The team had previously shown that mice lacking PDZD8, a tethering protein at endoplasmic reticulum membrane contact sites, accumulate cholesteryl esters in the brain and show ADHD-like behavior. This new paper localizes the lipid accumulation specifically to SNr parvalbumin neurons — they describe it as lipofuscin-like — and traces downstream consequences: reduced SNr projections to thalamus and to midbrain dopamine targets, increased striatonigral input from caudate-putamen. The proposed circuit interpretation is that lipid accumulation impairs SNr parvalbumin neuron function, which disinhibits SNr's targets, which disrupts the action-selection loop. This is correlative in this paper, not a causal demonstration that the lipid is doing the damage, but it puts cholesterol metabolism on the map as a basal-ganglia vulnerability and a candidate mechanism for ADHD-like phenotypes outside the usual catecholamine framing.

Item six bridges back to dopamine but in adolescent clinical neuroimaging. Another Neuropsychopharmacology paper, this one on adolescent cannabis use and dopamine neurophysiology indexed indirectly through subcortical brain iron. Tissue iron concentration correlates with dopaminergic activity, and you can index it noninvasively with MRI T2-star measurements. The team scanned 81 adolescents aged 14 to 17 — either with fewer than five lifetime cannabis episodes or more than eleven — with limited alcohol and nicotine use and no other illicit substance use. Lower tissue iron was associated with more daily concentrate hits, more hours high per day, higher use frequency, and higher cannabis use disorder severity. Post-hoc analyses highlighted the VTA as the key region driving the signal. The interpretation aligns with the adult and animal literature on chronic cannabis blunting dopaminergic neurophysiology, but here in adolescents and using an MRI proxy rather than PET. The caveats are real — this is cross-sectional, 81 participants, and tissue iron is a proxy for dopaminergic activity, not a direct measure. But as a scalable, radiation-free imaging strategy for adolescent substance use research, the 1/nT2-star approach is interesting.

Seventh item, multimodal brain-behavior mapping in adolescents using the ABCD study. A Yale-led preprint that pulls structural MRI, diffusion microstructure, and task-based functional MRI across corticostriatal, corticolimbic, and executive control networks in 5,408 youths aged 13 to 14. They run a regularized canonical correlation analysis to find modes of covariation between the brain measures and psychopathology domains, then ask whether those modes predict school-related impairment a year later. They find two distinct modes. The first is a broad-psychopathology mode where higher diffusivity, lower reward-task activation across all three networks, and lower corticostriatal surface area went with higher overall psychopathology, and higher psychopathology scores on this mode predicted more school impairment a year out. The second mode dissociated anxiety from externalizing — lower corticolimbic diffusivity, lower executive control volume and surface area, and lower cortical thickness with higher corticostriatal and corticolimbic volumes went with higher anxiety but lower externalizing — and on this mode the relationship to school impairment ran the other way. The honest reading is that ABCD-based rCCA finds robust but modest brain-behavior modes that have predictive validity at one-year follow-up. Effect sizes are not large. But the methodology of treating broad and dimension-specific psychopathology as separable canonical modes — rather than running diagnosis-by-diagnosis brain comparisons — is the right direction for adolescent computational psychiatry.

Item eight, a computational psychiatry paper that lands in the addiction space. Also Neuropsychopharmacology, also late May. The team built a smartphone-based ecological momentary assessment framework that delivers delay discounting and risk-ambiguity decision tasks using adaptive design optimization — a Bayesian method that lets you get reliable parameter estimates from 20 to 30 trials per task instead of the hundreds typical lab paradigms use. They deployed it in 79 people going through a 5-to-6-week smoking cessation program, with daily task delivery alongside ecological momentary surveys of craving, stress, mood, and medication adherence. At the day-to-day level, elevated craving, depressive symptoms, and ambiguity tolerance predicted more smoking the next day; lower discounting rates and reduced craving and stress predicted cessation success at the end of treatment. The notable result is that models trained on data from the first week alone reached an AUC of 0.76 for predicting cessation success, approaching the 0.83 AUC of the model trained on the full study period. That is exactly the right direction for personalized intervention — you don't have to wait six weeks to know who is going to relapse, you can learn a lot from week one. The methodology is generalizable to other substance use disorders.

Ninth item, an adolescent stress epigenome paper from Kanazawa University. They did ATAC-seq plus RNA-seq on hippocampal CA1 in an adolescent social isolation mouse model, with single-cell reference mapping and cell-type deconvolution layered on top. ATAC-seq picked up isolation-associated changes in chromatin accessibility — enhancers linked to calcium transport opened up, promoters linked to synaptic organization closed down. The AP-1 motif specifically lost accessibility, which is interesting because AP-1 is the immediate-early-gene transcription factor family that mediates a lot of activity-dependent transcription, and reduced AP-1 access could weaken activity-driven gene programs. RNA-seq found 106 differentially expressed genes including upregulation of Fosl2 and Hdac9 — both linked to chromatin regulation — and downregulation of Fbxw7. Pathway analysis pulled out myelination. Cell-type deconvolution found a relative increase in oligodendrocytes and a relative decrease in excitatory neurons in isolated mice. The interpretation is that adolescent social isolation drives coordinated chromatin and transcriptional remodeling in hippocampal CA1, with the oligodendrocyte response surfacing as a candidate cellular mechanism. Worth flagging that adolescent social isolation as a long-term psychiatric risk factor has been hot in this literature — see the Picciotto Shank3 paper we covered yesterday for a closer-to-circuit view of the same general theme.

And to close, a learning-rule paper that I think will appeal here. A group at the Astera Institute with O'Reilly at UC Davis — known for the Leabra family of cortical learning models — tested whether synaptic plasticity in mouse hippocampal slices behaves like a function of the temporal derivative of activity over a theta cycle. The motivation is that error backpropagation, the workhorse algorithm of deep learning, can be closely approximated by a learning rule that responds to differences in activity states over time — go from low to high activity and strengthen, go from high to low and weaken. They drove presynaptic activity across two 100-millisecond halves of a 200-millisecond window at either 25 hertz or 50 hertz, with matching low or high postsynaptic depolarization, and ran all four combinations. The prediction held cleanly. Positive temporal derivative — low to high — gave LTP. Negative temporal derivative — high to low — gave LTD. And both no-change conditions, including the one with high activity throughout, gave no net change. That last result is the important one, because it argues against pure-Hebbian or rate-based interpretations where high activity per se should drive potentiation. This is a slice experiment, hippocampal not cortical, with idealized stimulation, so it does not prove the cortex runs backprop. But as a constraint on what biologically plausible learning rules need to do, the temporal-derivative-shaped LTP/LTD profile is a clean result, and one of the more direct empirical hooks under the broader argument that biological circuits implement something approximating gradient-based credit assignment.
