## Script

This is Receptor and Reason for Monday, May twenty-fifth, twenty twenty-six. Ten items today, all fresh — six in the broad neuropsychopharmacology bucket, two more in neurodegeneration and pain pharmacology, one new computational schizophrenia method, and one mechanistic-interpretability piece on how large language models align with brain language regions. We open with what looks like the most consequential mechanism paper of the week, a targetable axis in syndromic autism with non-human-primate-validated reconstitution.

First, an ASXL3-thyroid hormone axis in parvalbumin interneurons controlling autism-like behaviors, out of Songjiang Research Institute. ASXL3 loss-of-function variants cause Bainbridge-Ropers syndrome and overlap with autism spectrum disorder, but until now it was unclear how loss of this chromatin regulator perturbs circuit development and whether the deficits are reversible. The group shows that Asxl3 haploinsufficiency in mice reduces cortical thickness, depletes upper-layer projection neurons, and — counterintuitively — increases parvalbumin interneuron density, with the expected autism-like behavioral readouts. The mechanism is a chromatin handoff: ASXL3 loss causes altered histone H2A monoubiquitination, which derepresses DIO3, the deiodinase that inactivates thyroid hormone. Brain thyroid hormone gets depleted; thyroid-hormone receptor alpha deletion in inhibitory progenitors phenocopies the parvalbumin expansion. Three things make this stand out. One, the rescue has a critical window — neonatal thyroid-hormone supplementation rescues behavior, adolescent supplementation does not. Two, the group built an intein-based AAV that splits and reconstitutes the full-length ASXL3 protein in vivo, normalizing both cortical architecture and behavior in heterozygous mice. Three, the same AAV system drives efficient ASXL3 expression in non-human primate brain, which is the translational signal you need before this kind of gene therapy enters serious development. For an audience tracking neurodevelopmental drug discovery, this is a clean three-modality target — chromatin, endocrine, and inhibitory-circuit — with a defined therapeutic window and a delivery platform already validated in primate brain.

Second, CGRP receptor signaling as the gate on an influenza-derived immune engram, out of Duke neurobiology. The premise — that neuronal ensembles encode functional aspects of an immune challenge in a so-called immune engram — is recent, and the mechanistic dissection here is the kind we have been waiting for. Acute non-neurotropic respiratory infection with the Puerto Rico eight strain drives a cellular response in the posterior insula peaking around three days post-infection. Using TRAP2 mice with chemogenetic capture, the group tags the active ensemble at that timepoint. Reactivating that insular ensemble later produces both anxiety behavior and elevated cytokine transcription in lung tissue but not spleen. Then the key pharmacology — they ablate calcitonin gene-related peptide producing cells in the airway, which abolishes synaptic engulfment by microglia in the insula during infection without changing the peripheral cellular response. And they replicate the effect with rimegepant, the small-molecule CGRP receptor antagonist already on the market for migraine. Crucially, rimegepant during engram formation prevents the anxiety phenotype on later reactivation but does not block the peripheral gene expression. That dissociation matters. It means CGRP signaling gates the behavioral arm of the engram but not the immunological arm, and it identifies an FDA-approved drug class as a pharmacological tool for breaking infection-derived anxiety phenotypes without disabling host defense. The translational read-through is obvious — post-acute COVID anxiety, post-viral anxiety more broadly — and the experiment is clean enough that the migraine class deserves a serious second look outside its current label.

Third, acid-sensing ion channel one-A in the basolateral amygdala and carbon-dioxide-evoked jumping, out of Iowa. Carbon-dioxide inhalation is one of the few stimuli that evokes panic-disorder-like behavior in both humans and mice, and acid-sensing ion channels are the obvious candidates because they open when extracellular pH drops. What the group shows is that global ASIC1A disruption increases CO2-evoked jumping while reducing freezing, with elevated c-Fos induction in the basolateral amygdala. Direct acidification of the basolateral amygdala recapitulates the same shift. The electrophysiology is where this gets interesting — CO2 inhalation normally suppresses firing in most basolateral amygdala neurons, but ASIC1A disruption enhances suppression of putative interneurons specifically, and facilitates excitation of putative principal neurons. So the model is that ASIC1A's pH-sensing function is concentrated in the inhibitory population, and losing it shifts the defensive response away from freezing and toward jumping — an active rather than passive defense. For panic disorder and PTSD, where defensive-response selection is the clinical problem, ASIC1A is a credible target even though early ASIC modulators have struggled in CNS development. The pH-amygdala axis as a panic substrate gives a non-monoamine mechanism that the SSRI- and benzodiazepine-saturated panic-disorder market actually needs.

Fourth, the RNA helicase MOV10 and a corticalized fear response, out of Illinois. MOV10 is highly expressed in the developing brain, sits at synapses, and is required for embryonic viability; in humans, MOV10 loci correlate with enhanced cortical brain volumes and with substance dependence by epigenetic profiling. The brain-specific MOV10 knockout shows enhanced fear memory, and the new finding is that the enhanced fear travels through an unexpected anatomical substrate. Diffusion tensor imaging reveals impaired structural connectivity of the canonical fear circuits — amygdala-hippocampal-prefrontal pathways — and yet the behavior is more fearful, not less. The model the group proposes is that MOV10 loss elevates GABRA2 expression in hippocampus, reduces canonical anatomical connectivity, and shifts the substrate for fear memory retrieval to a corticalized response — increased cortical fMRI activity on re-exposure that does not go through the standard subcortical circuit. This is a useful mechanistic frame for the intractability of certain PTSD memories, and it provides a molecular handle — GABRA2 hippocampal upregulation — that could in principle be modulated. It also reframes substance dependence comorbidity with PTSD as potentially sharing this corticalized-learning architecture, which would predict that conventional benzodiazepine-class GABAA modulators may be the wrong intervention for this subtype.

Fifth, a new computational fMRI framework, DESINE — Dynamic Estimation of Spatially Interactive Networks — applied to schizophrenia by the Calhoun group at Georgia State. Most dynamic functional-connectivity analyses summarize networks as temporally varying scalar relationships. DESINE goes spatial — it computes joint density distributions, essentially two-dimensional histograms, of voxel-wise activity across pairs of networks in sliding windows, and quantifies how those joint distributions deviate over time. Applied to ninety-one network pairs in five hundred and eight subjects, three hundred fifteen controls and one hundred ninety-three schizophrenia patients, the headline is that schizophrenia patients show a constrained dynamic repertoire — markedly lower variability of these spatial-interaction metrics, particularly in high-activity regions. The deviation metrics correlate with cognitive performance, with positive and negative symptom scale scores, and with chlorpromazine-equivalent antipsychotic dose. The conceptual reframing is that psychopathology in schizophrenia is partly a constraint on the brain's capacity to express a diverse range of spatial interactions — a rigidity in configuration space rather than a deficit in any single network. For the field this matters because it gives a candidate biomarker that is both global and voxel-agnostic, which is what you want for cross-site reproducibility, and because the chlorpromazine-equivalent correlation hints at an antipsychotic-response readout.

Sixth, direct RNA sequencing of brain cells and Alzheimer's disease pathology, out of Genentech. The methodology lift is that the group benchmarks two nanopore long-read formats — cDNA-PCR and direct RNA sequencing — and shows that direct RNA sequencing achieves higher accuracy and sensitivity for transcript quantification and de novo isoform construction across neuropathological gene sets. The substantive finding is more interesting. They build a multi-omic atlas in iPSC-derived neurons by integrating direct RNA sequencing for transcript abundance, N6-methyladenosine modification status, and poly-A tail length with ribosome profiling and mass spectrometry. Two results stand out. One, direct RNA sequencing combined with ribosome profiling predicts protein abundance synergistically — neither modality alone does as well, which has implications for how the field reads translation from sequencing data. Two, there is a significant inverse relationship between m6A modification and mRNA abundance that depends on ribosomal A-site engagement, providing a mechanistic link between RNA modification and translation efficiency. Applied to post-mortem Alzheimer brain, m6A profiles distinguish early-versus late-stage disease. The headline for the drug-discovery audience is that this is the right resolution to start asking which neurodegeneration-relevant transcripts have their function gated at the modification layer — a regulatory dimension drug discovery has barely touched compared to splicing and abundance.

Seventh, a calibrated high-throughput electrophysiology framework for clinical interpretation of CACNA1G missense variants, out of Wollongong. CACNA1G encodes the low-voltage-activated T-type calcium channel Cav-three-point-one, expressed broadly in thalamus, cerebellum, and parts of cortex; missense variants are increasingly identified in absence epilepsy, cerebellar ataxia, and early-onset neurodevelopmental phenotypes, but most come back from clinical sequencing as variants of uncertain significance. The group built an automated patch-clamp pipeline, calibrated against twenty-five benign-or-likely-benign and sixteen pathogenic-or-likely-pathogenic reference variants, and applied ACMG-AMP functional-evidence criteria conservatively. More than eighty percent of pathogenic variants showed reduced current density; deactivation kinetics added discriminatory information for variants with preserved current density. Applied to five variants of uncertain significance from Australian patients, two were reclassified as likely pathogenic based on voltage-sensor-perturbation signatures. The reason this matters for the audience is that automated patch-clamp finally gives ion-channel functional-evidence pipelines the throughput that ACMG-AMP guidelines have been waiting for. The framework is templated and explicitly portable to other channelopathy genes, which is the right shape for clinical-grade functional evidence at scale.

Eighth, TYK2 inhibition of hyper-IL-6 and Oncostatin M signaling in sensory neurons, out of Cambridge. Chronic visceral pain in inflammatory bowel disease has no good targeted pharmacology — opioids drive ileus, neuromodulators have limited efficacy. The IL-6 family cytokines are abundant in IBD pathogenesis, and Oncostatin M has been shown to sensitize nociceptors, but the downstream kinase pathway in sensory neurons was unclear. The group shows that hyper-IL-6 — IL-6 complexed with its soluble receptor — and Oncostatin M elicit calcium mobilization in a subset of murine colonic sensory neurons, and that the response can be attenuated by pan-JAK inhibition or, interestingly, by selective TYK2 inhibition alone. TRPV1 or TRPA1 inhibition also reduces the OSM-responsive population, indicating the cytokine signal is funneled through those transducer channels. For drug discovery this is the cleanest finding — TYK2 selectivity is a property the JAK-inhibitor class has been engineering for years to escape the JAK1-JAK2 cardiovascular and hematologic liabilities that gave the first generation black-box warnings. Deucravacitinib is the precedent for TYK2-selective allosteric inhibition in psoriasis. The Cambridge data argue for IBD pain as an indication-expansion candidate for that mechanism, working through a peripheral nociceptor-sensory mechanism distinct from the immune-suppression action that has driven existing TYK2 development.

Ninth, reduced VPS26B in primary motor cortex and altered glutamate receptor expression in a Parkinsonian mouse model, from the Korea Research Institute of Bioscience and Biotechnology. VPS26B is a retromer subunit that regulates the surface trafficking of GluA1 — the AMPA receptor subunit — and has been studied mostly in the trans-entorhinal cortex relevant to early Alzheimer pathology. The new claim is that cortical VPS26B is also relevant to Parkinson disease, specifically in the primary motor cortex. In the MPTP mouse model, VPS26B levels drop in M1, surface GluA1 drops alongside, and synaptic protein levels drop. VPS26B overexpression partially attenuates these losses, and VPS26B-deficient mice show unstable accelerating-rotarod performance after MPTP administration. VPS26B overexpression improves rotarod performance in both wild-type and knockout backgrounds. The story is consistent with a model in which the motor symptoms of Parkinson disease are not exclusively a striatal-dopaminergic problem but include a cortical glutamate-trafficking dysfunction that is correctable independent of the dopaminergic substrate. For drug discovery, retromer-stabilizing small molecules — the chaperone class explored by the Petsko and Small groups — get an additional rationale in PD motor symptoms beyond their original Alzheimer hypothesis.

Tenth and closing, sparse autoencoders mapping brain-LLM alignment onto cortical semantic topography, from the mechanistic-interpretability-meets-neurolinguistics literature. The longstanding observation that intermediate layers of large language models best predict human brain responses to language has lacked a mechanistic account of which features in those layers do the predicting. The group trains sparse autoencoders on GPT-2 XL and Llama-3 eight billion, decomposing each layer into sixteen to thirty-two thousand interpretable features, and then asks which feature taxonomy best predicts brain responses. Semantic features alone recover ninety-four percent of peak encoding performance, substantially above variance-matched baselines. They then test a sharper prediction — five semantic subcategories defined a priori from independent neuroscience programs should map to distinct cortical regions, and they do, with a Spearman rank correlation of zero-point-seven-two. The takeaway is that the residual mystery of LLM-brain alignment is largely a semantic-features story — the same semantic categories that organize human cortex also organize the latent space of language models, at a granularity that previous regression-based encoding could not resolve. For computational psychiatry this is the right substrate to build on. If you want to identify altered semantic processing in schizophrenia or autism from naturalistic language data, decomposing the language-model representation into sparse features lets you isolate which semantic categories are perturbed, rather than relying on opaque whole-layer alignment scores.
