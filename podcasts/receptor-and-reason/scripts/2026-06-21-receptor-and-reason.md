## Script

Welcome to Receptor and Reason for Sunday, June 21st, 2026. Nine items today — heavy AD lead, cannabis and opioid addiction in the middle, an HPA-axis arc through to GPCR pharmacology, and a couple of agentic and foundation-model pieces on the way out.

We open with a paper that tries to put APOE4 microglial biology on a unified map. The group combines spatially resolved proteomics with single-nucleus multiomics across APOE3/3 and APOE4/4 brains in Alzheimer's disease, and instead of carving microglia into discrete clusters they treat the data as a continuous manifold of states. What falls out is a directional shift: APOE4/4 cells lose homeostatic identity but also fail to fully reach the canonical disease-associated microglia program. They get stuck in an intermediate state — inflammatory signaling on, metabolic and phagocytic engagement off — and that state sits preferentially in spatial niches of astrogliosis and senescence. The framing is the useful part. A lot of recent microglia work has been pulling in opposite directions on whether DAM is protective or harmful in APOE4 carriers, and a manifold view makes the answer "neither, exactly — these cells can't get there in the first place." That is a different therapeutic target than tuning the DAM response. If the activation threshold itself is shifted by APOE4, then the relevant intervention is the transition, not the endpoint.

Pairing nicely with that is a preprint from the Trushina group on CP2, a brain-penetrant mitochondrial complex I modulator, dosed into APP/PS1 female mice starting at nineteen months — so deliberately advanced disease, well past the early-intervention window most therapeutic studies sit in. To analyze the response they built iMiceBrain, a brain-specific genome-scale metabolic model, and ran transcriptomics, lipidomics, and targeted metabolomics through it. The therapeutic signature is interesting: CP2 restores mitochondrial substrate flexibility, reactivates fatty acid oxidation, normalizes pyruvate dehydrogenase flux, and corrects cholesteryl ester and sphingolipid abnormalities — and these changes track with synaptic and cognitive recovery. The "mild" qualifier matters here. Complex I as a target has historically been toxicology-coded, because hard inhibition kills neurons. The case the authors are making is that a partial, reversible nudge at complex I drives an adaptive metabolic reprogramming that you cannot get from any single downstream metabolic intervention. Whether that survives translation to humans is the open question, but as a mechanistic proof that lipid and energy phenotypes in AD are still tractable late in disease, it's a useful counterweight to the dominant amyloid-and-tau frame.

Staying on mitochondria, but shifting circuits entirely. A new preprint from a U.S. group uses single-nucleus RNA-seq and single-nucleus ATAC-seq on rat nucleus accumbens after prenatal delta-9-THC exposure, looking specifically at medium spiny neurons at postnatal day 24. The dominant signal isn't endocannabinoid receptor downregulation, which is where you might expect to land — it's coordinated suppression of mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation gene programs, plus coupled changes in ribosomal and proteasomal machinery. The ATAC-seq side finds altered chromatin accessibility at promoters enriched for NRF1 and YY2 motifs, which are mitochondrial biogenesis regulators. And then an acute THC challenge at P24 in prenatally-exposed offspring further exacerbates the mitochondrial deficit and delays MSN maturation. The reframe is that prenatal cannabis exposure may not be acting primarily through cannabinoid receptor signaling per se, but through a mitochondrial-bioenergetic chokepoint that gates MSN maturation in reward circuitry. That is a different mechanistic story from "CB1 too high or too low," and it raises the obvious next question of whether postnatal mitochondrial support could rescue the MSN phenotype.

That sets up an addiction-genetics piece. Bachtell's group at Colorado ran a two-phase oxycodone self-administration paradigm — two-hour acquisition sessions and twelve-hour extended-access escalation — across seven inbred rat strains from the Hybrid Rat Diversity Panel. Acquisition timing, total intake, escalation slope, and within-session distribution were all strain-dependent, and crucially, the M520/N strain showed a distinctive burst-responding pattern that was stronger in females. The contribution here isn't a new gene — it's a behavioral phenotype taxonomy that decomposes oxycodone use into separable, heritable sub-trajectories. The escalation literature has been dominated by aggregate-intake measures, which lump rapid initiation, sustained taking, and burst-like loss of control into one number, and likely smear over distinct genetic architectures. Pulling those apart is the kind of methodological move that lets the next OUD GWAS in rats and humans target the right phenotype.

From substance use, into the HPA axis. A Tronson group paper looks at chronic combined oral contraceptive exposure — ethinyl estradiol plus levonorgestrel — in mice, focusing on glucocorticoid and mineralocorticoid receptor signaling across the dorsal and ventral hippocampus and the PVN. Basal corticosterone is unchanged, but the dexamethasone suppression test shows impaired GR-mediated negative feedback, and the molecular work shows region-specific divergence. Dorsal hippocampus: enhanced GR-dependent gene signaling, prolonged Fkbp5 induction. Ventral hippocampus: enhanced MR-dependent signaling, blunted stress-induced CRF. PVN: reduced MR expression and altered MR signaling. The intuition this paper supports is that the modest but real depression-risk signal in some OC users is not a global cortisol suppression effect — it's a redistribution of how stress signal is read across hippocampal subfields and the PVN. If that holds, then thinking about OC mood liability as a uniform effect is wrong, and the GR-MR balance in specific subregions becomes the actionable axis.

Adjacent to that, on the depression side, there's a paper from the Lerch lab on the hippocampal metabolic signature of insulin signaling in depression. In humans, they use multimodal neuroimaging — MR spectroscopy plus resting-state functional connectivity — and find that hippocampal GABA and lactate concentrations correlate with HbA1c, and that hippocampal-to-default-mode-network connectivity tracks mood severity independent of adiposity. The mechanistic causal probe is a mouse model with hippocampus-specific insulin receptor depletion, and here the result is the surprising one: knocking down hippocampal insulin signaling enhances neuronal metabolism and attenuates anxiety-like behavior. Same lactate-GABA signature, but pointing the other way behaviorally. The take is that brain insulin resistance in depression isn't a uniform deficit — there's a cell-type-and-context dependency that makes "fix brain insulin signaling" much too coarse as a therapeutic frame. The hippocampal metabolic signature itself is the more reliable readout. Note that bioRxiv hadn't rendered the body for this one yet, so the take is based on the abstract.

Staying with the HPA axis, structurally. A group out of Robinson's lab applies native mass spec and HDX mass spec to the MC2R–MRAP complex — that is, the ACTH receptor and its obligate accessory protein. Two clean findings. One, when MRAP is depleted, MC2R becomes heavily palmitoylated at the C-terminus — palmitoylation appears to substitute for the membrane-anchoring contribution that MRAP normally provides. Two, ACTH binds only the MC2R-MRAP complex and stabilizes both the receptor and the MRAP N-terminus, while antagonists shift the equilibrium toward MRAP-independent populations and destabilize transmembrane helix two. TM2 destabilization persists even when MRAP is removed. For an audience interested in GPCR pharmacology, the useful generalization is that MRAP-family accessory proteins aren't just trafficking chaperones — they remodel the conformational landscape of the receptor, and ligand efficacy can be redefined by what an antagonist does to the receptor-accessory protein interface. That's a pharmacological design axis that small-molecule programs targeting melanocortin receptors generally haven't engaged.

Two computational items to close. First, Tox21mer — a 43-million-parameter transformer that encodes Tox21 concentration-response curves together with assay metadata into a single 768-dimensional embedding. Pretraining is self-supervised: mask the response curve, reconstruct it, with light auxiliary supervision on outcome and AC50. The interesting result is what frozen-embedding probes can do — macro-F1 of 0.985 on agonist-versus-antagonist-versus-inactive, R-squared of 0.87 on log-AC50. And ablations show that masked-only pretraining alone gets you almost there, which means the representation is really learned from the curve shape and assay context, not from the supervision labels. That matters because most of the recent work on toxicology foundation models has leaned heavily on chemical structure encoders, with curves as labels. Tox21mer flips that — the curve becomes the primary modality. Whether downstream tasks benefit from fusing this with structure-based encoders is the obvious next move, and the authors gesture at distillation into chemistry-only student models for large-scale virtual screening.

Closing on agentic AI for science. ContinuumCellAgent is an autonomous agent that tries to do an entire research run — literature review, hypothesis, computational experiments, manuscript draft, adversarial peer review — as a single unattended invocation. The interesting design choice is that they ground protocols in curated research-method checklists that simultaneously define the reviewer rubric. So the agent's plan and the agent's critic are reading from the same spec. They also expose a diagnostics layer that records file-based artifacts, message traces, and state transitions, which is the part most other AI-scientist systems have ducked. The case studies are open-domain QA plus biomedical longevity, and the artifacts are checkable rather than just plausible-looking. For listeners building agentic systems in biology, the lesson is less about the specific results than about the architecture move: making the diagnostic layer first-class is what lets you debug long-horizon failures, and grounding plans in the same rubric the reviewer uses is what gives you any hope of catching them.
