# Receptor & Reason — 2026-05-31

## Script

Good morning, Alan. Ten items today, weighted toward Tier 1 with two computational-bio pieces and a lighter closer that picks up where yesterday's LSD story left off. The unifying thread across the first three picks is the same blunt point: summary-level molecular readouts hide the biology you actually want, and the new wave of tools is finally giving us the resolution we need — at the isoform level, at the systemic-by-sex level, and at the protein conformational level.

We open with what is probably the most useful resource paper of the week. The Breen lab at Mount Sinai dropped a long-read isoform atlas of purified human prefrontal and orbitofrontal cortical cell types. They sorted nuclei from adult cortex into GABAergic neurons, glutamatergic neurons, oligodendrocytes, astrocytes, and microglia, then ran long- and short-read RNA sequencing in parallel. The headline numbers are striking. They cataloged about 220,000 full-length isoforms, of which roughly 35 to 56 percent were previously unannotated, and the unannotated transcripts skewed longer, more exon-rich, and predominantly protein-coding. The more provocative finding is that glia, particularly oligodendrocytes and microglia, are the most isoform-diverse populations in the cortex — not neurons. This inverts the textbook framing. And about 59 to 62 percent of the transcripts that differentially distinguished cell identity were absent from current annotations. The clinical kicker: pathogenic variants are enriched more than two-fold at novel splice boundaries within disease genes, including POGZ, TARDBP, and PLP1. If you run a GWAS or burden test against canonical gene models, you are missing a layer of variation. This atlas is the new baseline for anyone doing transcriptomic work on a brain disorder. It is also a free downstream amplifier for psychiatric genetics — every hit you have already mapped now deserves a re-look against the long-read isoform landscape.

Staying on resources but pivoting to plasma. The Suryadevara group at Stanford ran proteomic profiling on plasma from 408 deeply phenotyped participants spanning Alzheimer's, Lewy body dementia, and Parkinson's. The framing is unusual and useful: they explicitly ask how circulating bone biology and cellular senescence pathways differ across the neurodegenerative spectrum, by sex. The findings are sex-divergent enough that pooling collapses the signal — inflammatory and bone-related pathways diverge by sex within disease, with shared mitochondrial and metabolic dysfunction sitting underneath. Several candidate proteins correlated with established neurodegeneration biomarkers, particularly pTau181. The takeaway for translational work is that systemic comorbidities — bone loss, peripheral senescence — are not nuisance variables to regress out. They are part of the disease phenotype and they segregate by sex. If you are designing a fluid-biomarker panel for early detection, you probably want sex-stratified models, not a unified one.

Third in this opening triad, structural proteomics from the Picotti group at ETH Zurich, addressing a long-standing gap: we have multiple in vitro conformational states for alpha-synuclein — disordered monomer, helical, oligomeric, fibrillar — but the actual conformational distribution in cells and tissue has stayed hotly debated because the standard structural methods don't deconvolve mixtures in situ. They built a structural-proteomics fingerprint approach. The idea is to measure structure-specific proteolytic patterns from each well-characterized in vitro conformation, then use those fingerprints to back out the conformational composition of complex biological samples quantitatively. They benchmarked it on a ground-truth in vitro time course of amyloid fibril formation and could simultaneously track a falling disordered monomer pool, a rising beta-sheet-rich oligomer pool, and a delayed fibril pool, in the same sample. They then took it into yeast and complex biological samples. For drug discovery, this matters because Parkinson's-disease therapeutic discussions about whether a compound stabilizes monomer, blocks oligomerization, or shifts the oligomer-to-fibril ratio have lived almost entirely in vitro. A readout that quantifies the conformational mixture in situ closes the translational loop. Expect the same technique to be repurposed for Tau and TDP-43.

Pivoting to mechanism and pharmacology. The Conant lab at Georgetown asked whether 4-methylumbelliferone — already approved in Europe for biliary spasm — could touch Alzheimer's pathology. The mechanistic hypothesis is interesting: 4-MU inhibits hyaluronic acid synthesis, which reduces perineuronal nets, the extracellular matrix sheaths around fast-spiking interneurons. Enzymatic degradation of perineuronal nets had already been shown to rescue memory in AD mouse models, but no one had asked whether the existing drug that does it pharmacologically would work. From three months of age, APP/PS1 mice went onto either a vehicle or 4-MU-supplemented diet, for either 70 days or 52 weeks. Both regimens reduced the soluble parenchymal A-beta 42 over 40 ratio, and the long arm reduced insoluble plaque, lowered perineuronal net intensity, and ameliorated spatial memory deficits. This is a repurposing candidate with a known human safety profile, a mechanistically coherent target, and a real functional readout. The caveat is that hyaluronic acid synthesis inhibition is not subtle systemically, and the cognitive effect size at 52 weeks deserves scrutiny. Worth tracking.

Now the stimulation mechanism story. The Vlachos lab at Freiburg has been pushing on cellular mechanisms of repeated rTMS for years; this week they nailed down the role of microglia. Using mouse organotypic brain cultures, they delivered ten sessions of intermittent theta burst stimulation over two weeks — basically a cumulative therapeutic regimen translated to a slice — and tracked what changed. Pyramidal neurons showed reduced AMPA-mediated mEPSC frequency and reduced dendritic spine density. Critically, when they depleted microglia and ran the same protocol, the response inverted: synaptic strength went up and spine density went up. Stimulation also enhanced microglial uptake and degradative capacity. So microglia are not bystanders. They are required mediators of the homeostatic structural remodeling that multi-session rTMS produces. For the field that has been arguing for years about whether iTBS strengthens or weakens synapses, the answer is "it depends on whether microglia are doing their job." That likely matters for patient stratification — neuroinflammatory state at baseline may predict who responds.

Sixth, a gene therapy story that crosses into psychiatry by way of muscular dystrophy. The Muntoni group at UCL Queen Square just posted work in the mdx52 mouse, a model of Duchenne that lacks the relevant brain dystrophin isoforms — the same Dp427 and Dp140 isoforms that are lost in about 90 percent of Duchenne patients. About 40 percent of Duchenne patients have significant neurobehavioral comorbidities: autism, ADHD, OCD, and intellectual disability. These have been treated as somewhat orphaned secondary phenotypes by the muscle-focused therapeutic pipeline. The new work delivered a neuronal-targeted AAV micro-dystrophin to neonatal mdx52 males by either intravenous or intracerebroventricular routes and ran an extensive behavioral battery. Both routes reduced anxiety-like and emotional reactivity phenotypes, but intravenous delivery was superior across the broader panel, also rescuing grip strength and motor coordination. The conceptual point is that for a multi-organ X-linked disorder where the brain isoforms are a known molecular cause of the neuropsych phenotype, a single systemic gene therapy can address both somatic and CNS comorbidities. This logic generalizes — any monogenic disorder where the brain isoform is mechanistically tied to a psychiatric phenotype is now a candidate for the same approach.

Seventh, a channelopathy paper that matters for refractory pediatric epilepsy. The Zamponi lab at Calgary did patch-clamp characterization of an L228P mutation in CACNA1E, the gene encoding the R-type calcium channel Cav2.3. CACNA1E mutations were only recently linked to developmental and epileptic encephalopathies. More than fifteen DEE-associated CACNA1E variants are known, but only a handful have been studied functionally. The L228P mutant did not change peak current density, inactivation kinetics, or recovery from inactivation, but it shifted activation toward hyperpolarized potentials — meaning the channel opens at less-depolarized membrane potentials than it should. This is consistent with a gain of function that lowers the threshold for cellular excitability. For the precision-medicine ambition in DEE, this kind of mutation-by-mutation biophysics is what lets you stratify patients toward channel blockers versus channel openers. R-type channels are not heavily drugged, but specific blockers like SNX-482 exist as tool compounds. The therapeutic translation is still distant, but the mechanistic foothold is real.

Eighth, a screening platform paper from inside Merck. The Cakir group has built a fully automated system for high-density extracellular recording from the interior of intact human cortical organoids. The setup uses a microfabricated probe and a robot that completes insertion within minutes while keeping the organoid structurally intact, and starts recording spontaneous spikes immediately. They extracted physiologically grounded spike features, then trained an LSTM classifier to distinguish organoids from healthy iPSC donors versus organoids from familial Alzheimer's donors carrying amyloid precursor protein mutations. The classifier hit 100 percent accuracy despite real intra-class variability, which is the expected red flag — these classifiers tend to overfit small batched datasets, so the number needs replication in larger cohorts. The more interesting result is mechanistic. They then treated the AD organoids with a drug candidate that reliably lowers amyloid-beta levels, and the classifier still called them AD-like electrophysiologically. The functional electrophysiological phenotype dissociated from the molecular biomarker. This is exactly the kind of disconnect that has dogged AD drug development — anti-amyloid agents hit the molecular target without correcting the network function that the patient cares about. Having a high-throughput functional readout that says so before you take a candidate into a mouse or a patient is the actual value here. It also says something uncomfortable about how much we should trust amyloid lowering as a surrogate endpoint.

Ninth, on the computational side, the Rose group at UC Irvine released CARIBOU — a multi-agent system for autonomous bioinformatics analysis, specifically designed for institutional HPC environments. The design is more interesting than the average agentic-bio paper because it acknowledges what has been killing these systems: stateless execution, broken reproducibility, restricted deployment, and an inability to look at intermediate results and adjust. CARIBOU organizes specialized agents through researcher-editable blueprints — analytical roles, workflow guidance, domain-specific reasoning — grounded in persistent executable computational environments built on Singularity or Apptainer containers. The agents share an evolving analytical state across workflow stages, which is what enables the iterative execute-observe-correct behavior during QC, batch integration, clustering, and cell-type annotation. They benchmarked on unit tasks, metadata reconstruction, and full single-cell RNA-seq analyses using Allen Brain Atlas hippocampus and Tabula Sapiens data. The HPC-native design is the thing to take seriously — most published agentic-bioinformatics systems assume Jupyter or a cloud sandbox, and that gap is what stops them from running where the data actually lives. Worth a look if you have lab data trapped on a Singularity-only cluster.

Finally, the lighter closer, picking up the LSD thread from yesterday. The Jerbi group at Montreal used source-resolved MEG to characterize what LSD actually does to cortical dynamics, in an LSD-versus-placebo design with and without music. The interpretive question they tackle: when people report widespread "cortical desynchronization" on psychedelics, is that a real weakening of oscillations or just a confound from shifting peak frequencies? Their answer is that both effects exist and are dissociable. LSD produces robust, spatially structured upward shifts in alpha and beta peak frequencies, alongside genuine attenuation of oscillatory power, in partly different cortical patterns. Beyond rhythmic activity, LSD flattens the aperiodic 1-over-f spectral slope and increases neural signal complexity and fractality, preferentially in sensory, language, emotion, and imagery-related networks — and sparing motor cortex. Music did not robustly amplify the neural signatures, which is a small but interesting set-and-setting finding worth holding alongside the clinical psychedelic literature that treats music as load-bearing. The methodological point — distinguish peak-frequency shift from power attenuation when you parameterize psychedelic EEG or MEG — should propagate into anyone doing oscillatory analysis of acute psychedelic data.

That's the package. Tomorrow.
