## Script

This is Receptor and Reason for Tuesday, May twenty-sixth, twenty twenty-six. Ten items today, all fresh. We open on what is probably the cleanest CNS receptor-pharmacology paper of the week — a heterodimer asymmetry in the metabotropic glutamate receptors that opens new allosteric design space — and we close on a knowledge-graph distillation showing that expert-level neuroscience reasoning can be induced in a small fine-tuned language model from a single textbook.

First, transmembrane domain dominance and allosteric inversion in metabotropic glutamate one-slash-five heterodimers, out of the Javitch lab at Columbia. Class C G-protein-coupled receptors function as obligate dimers, and metabotropic glutamate receptors one and five — both Gq-coupled — heterodimerize widely in CNS regions where the two are co-expressed. The basic mechanism puzzle has been that only one protomer can engage G-protein at a time, but the field has not had a clean answer to which protomer carries the signal in a heterodimer or how that decision is made. Using CODA-RET — a BRET assay that measures direct Gq recruitment to defined, full-length receptor pairs — the group shows that signaling at the metabotropic glutamate one-five heterodimer flows predominantly through the metabotropic glutamate one protomer. Domain-swap chimeras localize the dominance to the transmembrane domain. The consequence for drug discovery is the interesting part. Cis-acting positive and negative allosteric modulators of metabotropic glutamate five undergo what they call allosteric inversion in the heterodimer — a metabotropic glutamate five PAM at the homodimer effectively becomes a negative modulator at the heterodimer, because metabotropic glutamate five is not driving Gq there. The metabotropic glutamate five selective negative allosteric modulator MTEP is silent at the heterodimer entirely. The conceptual offer is that a trans-acting metabotropic glutamate five PAM, one that allosterically perturbs the opposite protomer, would in principle be selective for homomers — which is exactly the selectivity profile that has been hard to engineer pharmacologically, and which matters because mGlu five homomer pharmacology has spent two decades trying not to drag in mGlu one effects in cerebellum and elsewhere. This reframes how to read existing PAM and NAM datasets in tissues where heterodimers are abundant, and gives medicinal chemistry a coordinate — protomer identity plus cis-versus-trans engagement — to design selectivity across the homomer-heterodimer space.

Second, peptide rescue of synaptic AMPA receptor stabilization in a social isolation fear memory model, from the Bramham group in Bergen with the Greger lab at Cambridge. The premise is that synaptic AMPA receptor trapping — the activity-dependent stabilization of AMPA receptors at the postsynaptic density — depends on transmembrane AMPA receptor regulatory proteins, the TARPs, and that the immediate-early gene product ARC, or Arg three point one, binds the TARP carboxy terminal and destabilizes synaptic receptors. The group designed a twenty-one-amino-acid TAT-fused peptide — TARP-pep — that mimics the TARP carboxy-terminal motif and competitively occupies the ARC N-lobe binding pocket. In cultured hippocampal neurons, TARP-pep disrupts the TARP-ARC interaction and increases synaptic AMPA receptor surface stabilization. The in vivo experiment is the read. Acute intrahippocampal infusion of the peptide rescues the fear memory deficit induced by post-weaning social isolation — a behavioral phenotype that has been frustratingly resistant to pharmacological intervention because the underlying receptor-trafficking lesion has not been directly targetable. The reason this matters for the audience: destabilization of synaptic AMPA receptors is implicated transdiagnostically in cognitive dysfunction across schizophrenia, depression, and neurodevelopmental syndromes. Most existing AMPA pharmacology — ampakines, positive allosteric modulators — works on receptor gating, not surface stabilization. A protein-protein interaction inhibitor at the TARP-ARC interface is a different lane, and the in vivo proof of concept moves the strategy from speculative to something a chemistry program could plausibly evolve into a small-molecule or stapled-peptide therapeutic.

Third, ANKS1B in the nucleus accumbens controlling escalated cocaine self-administration through a CBP-FoxO3 chromatin complex, out of the Peking University National Institute on Drug Dependence. The translational anchor is a prior GWAS from the same group identifying ANKS1B as a shared genetic risk locus across heroin, methamphetamine, and alcohol dependence — a broad addiction-vulnerability signal that lacked mechanistic follow-through. The new study fills that in. ANKS1B expression in the nucleus accumbens drops after extended cocaine access in rats, and selective manipulation of ANKS1B in that region modulates the escalation of cocaine intake in a long-access self-administration paradigm — a model that is taken as the closest preclinical correlate of the loss-of-control phenotype that defines a substance use disorder rather than recreational use. Mechanism: ANKS1B interacts with the histone acetyltransferase CBP, controls H3 lysine twenty-seven acetylation at FoxO3 target loci, and represses FoxO3 transcriptional output during escalated intake. The chromatin-to-behavior chain is what makes this useful. ANKS1B itself is hard to drug directly, but CBP and FoxO3 each have tractable chemical biology, and the specificity of the effect to escalation rather than acquisition is the right specificity profile for a substance-use-disorder indication that has been historically dominated by replacement therapies and aversive agents. The cross-substance GWAS signal also makes ANKS1B-pathway pharmacology a candidate for the polysubstance phenotypes that current treatments do not address well.

Fourth, sex-specific cannabinoid addiction-like behavior and prefrontal microRNA signatures, from the Maldonado group at Universitat Pompeu Fabra. The clinical motivation is that women progress from initial cannabis use to cannabis use disorder faster than men, with higher relapse vulnerability, but preclinical models are male-dominated and the neurobiological substrate of the female vulnerability is poorly mapped. The group used a synthetic cannabinoid agonist self-administration model — WIN fifty-five thousand two hundred twelve dash two, a full agonist at cannabinoid receptors one and two — in male and female mice across the operant addiction-like criteria framework: persistence during drug-free periods, compulsive responding under punishment, and motivation by progressive ratio. Female mice met more criteria and at higher severity. The substrate piece is the microRNA profiling in medial prefrontal cortex, where they identify a sex-specific signature in the susceptible females that converges on synaptic plasticity and glutamatergic signaling targets. Two reasons to flag this. One, the methodological caveat — WIN fifty-five thousand two hundred twelve dash two is not delta-nine-THC, the partial agonist that does the work in human cannabis exposure, and the field has been criticized for over-interpreting full-agonist findings. The group acknowledges that. Two, even with that caveat, the convergence of female-vulnerable microRNA signatures on glutamatergic plasticity genes is consistent with the broader case that cannabis use disorder may share more biology with stimulant-class addictions than with opioid-class addictions, which has direct implications for the pharmacology lane — cannabinoid receptor one neutral antagonists and allosteric modulators rather than agonist replacement.

Fifth, Alzheimer disease-associated presenilin two N141I impairs neuronal lipid homeostasis and mitochondrial dynamics through Gbf1 downregulation, from UCLouvain. Presenilins are the catalytic core of gamma-secretase and produce amyloid-beta, but the field has been increasingly skeptical that all the disease biology in familial Alzheimer presenilin mutations runs through amyloid alone. The group dissected presenilin two by comparing knockout neurons to neurons expressing the N141I familial mutation. Both reduce neuronal lipid content, but exogenous lipid supplementation rescues only the N141I phenotype — a partial loss-of-function signature. N141I neurons also have reduced expression of OPA1, the mitochondrial fusion regulator, and that too is rescued by lipid supplementation. RNA sequencing identifies Gbf1 — a Golgi-specific guanine nucleotide exchange factor — as selectively downregulated in N141I but not in knockout tissue, confirmed at the protein level in mouse brain. Knockdown of Gbf1 in fibroblasts recapitulates the N141I lipid profile. The proposed axis — presenilin two to Gbf1 to lipid homeostasis to mitochondrial dynamics — sits outside amyloid processing entirely. For drug discovery this is the kind of presenilin-two-specific liability that the upcoming gamma-secretase modulator wave needs to be aware of, and it argues that targeting Gbf1 trafficking or its downstream lipid axis could be a separable disease-modifying lever in the familial population, with read-through to sporadic cases where presenilin two function is altered.

Sixth, a high-throughput cellular thermal shift assay platform that identifies small-molecule modulators of LILRB4 — also called ILT3 — with functional activity in human iPSC-derived microglia, from Weill Cornell. LILRB4 is an inhibitory immune receptor whose signaling on microglia restrains amyloid clearance and amplifies neuroinflammation; the field has been interested in disinhibiting microglia in Alzheimer disease for years but has had essentially no small-molecule tools — most work has been antibody-based. The group built a cellular thermal shift assay platform — CETSA reads target engagement by measuring drug-induced thermal stabilization of the protein in cells, which is the right format for membrane-receptor target validation that is hard to do biochemically. They screened approximately forty thousand compounds and identified IB15C, a submicromolar binder with structure-activity-relationship support. Orthogonal validation — microscale thermophoresis, surface plasmon resonance, docking, site-directed mutagenesis — confirms direct LILRB4 engagement at the predicted site. Functionally, in human iPSC-derived microglia, IB15C disrupts the LILRB4-ApoE interaction, reduces SHP1 and SHP2 phosphorylation, suppresses inflammatory cytokine secretion, and enhances amyloid uptake. This is the right read-through for the AD microglia thesis — small molecule, target engagement-validated, restores phagocytic function in the relevant human cell type. Whether it survives in vivo pharmacokinetics is the next question, but the chemistry is now in hand.

Seventh, region-specific Per1 induction dissociates circadian and mood responses to light, out of Fribourg. Light is well established as an antidepressant intervention — bright light therapy is one of the few non-pharmacological interventions that genuinely works in major depressive disorder with seasonal pattern — but the mechanism has never been cleanly dissociated from the circadian-phase-shifting action of light. The group used floxed Per1 mice with viral Cre delivery to selectively delete Per1 in either the suprachiasmatic nucleus, the master circadian pacemaker, or the lateral habenula, the structure that has emerged in the last decade as central to depressive behaviors and antidepressant response. Loss of Per1 in the suprachiasmatic nucleus blunted light-induced phase advances at circadian time twenty-two but did not affect light-induced improvement in despair-like behavior. Loss of Per1 in the lateral habenula did the opposite — light-induced improvement in despair-like behavior was lost, but circadian phase shifting was intact. So the two effects of light run through anatomically separable Per1-dependent mechanisms. For psychopharmacology this is important because it means antidepressant strategies that engage habenular Per1 induction or its downstream signaling — independent of any chronobiological intervention — could capture the mood benefit of light therapy without the constraints of timed exposure. It also predicts that the antidepressant response to light should be partly orthogonal to its sleep-and-circadian effects, which has been suspected clinically but not mechanistically grounded.

Eighth, attention-enabled PocketMiner — an artificial-intelligence method that simultaneously predicts cryptic pockets and their allosteric coupling — from the Bowman lab at Penn. Cryptic pockets are druggable binding sites that are not visible in the apo crystal structure and only open transiently, which is why they have been historically hard to discover; methods to find them have been computationally expensive, typically requiring long molecular dynamics simulations, and they have not generally told you whether the pocket is functionally important — whether opening that pocket would actually allosterically perturb the protein's activity. AE-PocketMiner is a graph neural network with an attention mechanism that takes a single input structure and produces both a cryptic-pocket-location prediction and an allosteric-coupling map to the rest of the protein. The paper outperforms previous cryptic-pocket prediction methods on benchmark datasets, recapitulates known allosteric interactions, and — the experimental piece — newly predicted cryptic pockets and the mutations that allosterically control their opening are confirmed in wet-lab experiments. For CNS drug discovery, where most of the validated targets are large multi-domain proteins — voltage-gated ion channels, transporters, class C GPCRs — and where allosteric modulation is the dominant strategy for achieving selectivity over closely related family members, having a single-structure method that predicts both the pocket and the allosteric link in one pass collapses what was a multi-step computational pipeline. It also gives medicinal chemistry a basis for prioritizing among predicted pockets — the ones that are mechanistically coupled to function rather than the ones that just happen to open transiently.

Ninth, LipoAgent — a safety-aware multi-agent LLM framework for lipid nanoparticle design, from NYU. Lipid nanoparticles are the dominant clinical platform for messenger RNA and small-RNA delivery, and despite the COVID-vaccine validation of the chemistry, designing new lipids that are both effective at transfection and not cytotoxic remains a screening bottleneck. The group's design insight is that toxicity should be treated as a decision-level prerequisite, not a parallel objective — if a lipid is toxic, its transfection-efficiency prediction is clinically irrelevant. LipoAgent encodes that with a conditional prediction objective: the model has to clear a toxicity gate before it produces an efficiency prediction. Multiple fine-tuned LLM agents propose and verify candidates, with lightweight human oversight invoked when the agents disagree. Across multiple foundation models, the framework delivers about a thirty-two percent relative improvement in messenger RNA transfection efficiency prediction over previously reported lipid-design models, and wet-lab validation shows that virtual screening rankings translate to real biological transfection outcomes. For an audience interested in CNS-relevant therapeutic delivery — where blood-brain-barrier-crossing lipid nanoparticles are an active area for siRNA and antisense oligonucleotide programs against neurodegenerative targets — the agentic architecture is the practical advance. Safety as a gating constraint, not a co-objective, is closer to how medicinal chemistry actually triages candidates, and the multi-agent disagreement-flagging gives a tractable human-in-the-loop interface that single-model screening pipelines tend to skip.

Tenth and closing, knowledge-graph-driven expert-level reasoning for neuroscience, out of Princeton. The setup is whether the deep, mechanistic reasoning that an expert neuroscientist applies to a question can be induced in a small fine-tuned language model using only the knowledge contained in a single authoritative neuroscience textbook — without scraping the open web. The pipeline is a dual-LLM validation step to construct a high-quality knowledge graph from the textbook, masked-language-model expansion of the graph over its topology, multi-hop question-answer item generation from graph traversal, supervised fine-tuning on those items, and finally reinforcement learning where path-derived knowledge-graph signals act as implicit rewards. The resulting fine-tuned model surpasses the underlying large language models on neuroscience expert-level reasoning while using orders of magnitude fewer parameters. Two reasons this lands as a closing item. One, it is a methodological argument that domain superintelligence in a specialist subject does not require web-scale heterogeneous training — the structured-knowledge-distillation route is competitive, and that has implications for cost, auditability, and avoidance of confounding contamination from low-quality sources. Two, the artifact the group releases — a knowledge-graph-derived synthetic neuroscience curriculum that a reader can quiz themselves on — is a clean demonstration that the knowledge-graph itself, not just the model, is a useful pedagogical object. For a field that has been waiting for a neuroscience-specific clinical-reasoning tutor that does not hallucinate around drug mechanisms, this is the kind of bottom-up superintelligence approach that could plausibly produce one.

That is the docket for May twenty-sixth. The week opens with a heterodimer-asymmetry pharmacology that should change how the field reads existing metabotropic glutamate PAM and NAM data, and closes on a textbook-to-superintelligence argument that specialist neuroscience reasoning is buildable without web-scale corpora. Heading into the rest of the week.
