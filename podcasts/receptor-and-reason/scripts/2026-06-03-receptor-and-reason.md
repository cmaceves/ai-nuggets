# Receptor & Reason — 2026-06-03

## Script

Good morning, Alan. Ten items today, opening on the molecular biology of mood disorders — the first transdiagnostic transcriptomic profile of the human habenula, an EEG brain-state biomarker for transcranial magnetic stimulation in depression, an endocannabinoid mechanism for the non-opioid component of placebo analgesia, and a single-nucleus multiome paper on chronic-stress disruption of hippocampal myelination. Then a motivation-circuit dissection of androgen depletion, three Parkinson's items — glial adenosine, levodopa-induced iron, and Netrin-one-driven graft plasticity — a computational neurotransmitter-cycling model for ketone administration, and closing on a benchmark for large-language-model bioinformatics agents.

First, from the Newell group at the University of Wollongong, the first transdiagnostic bulk RNA sequencing of the human habenula. The habenula is the small epithalamic structure that gates monoaminergic and reward output and that preclinical work has linked tightly to depression — habenula hyperactivity drives depressive-like behaviour in animals, and both ketamine and habenula-targeted deep brain stimulation reverse it. But the molecular architecture of the human habenula has been almost entirely opaque. They ran bulk RNA-seq on postmortem habenula-enriched tissue from six controls, six major depressive disorder cases, and six bipolar disorder cases from the Netherlands Brain Bank. Single-diagnosis comparisons found modest numbers of differentially expressed genes — about sixty per disorder. But combining major depressive disorder and bipolar disorder cases as one affective-illness group and contrasting against controls pulled out three hundred seventy-eight differentially expressed genes — a robust transdiagnostic signature. Up-regulated genes were enriched for potassium-channel activity, calcium homeostasis, and Wnt signalling — consistent with the preclinical story of altered habenula excitability driving depressive states. The clean methodological point is the transdiagnostic framing. Subtle disorder-specific effects get buried by sample size, but a shared affective-illness signature emerges cleanly when you collapse the diagnostic boundary that may not carve nature at its joints in this structure. The treatment implication: potassium-channel modulators and Wnt-pathway tools become a logical second-pass after the ketamine signal.

Next on depression treatment mechanism, from the Cocchi lab at the Queensland Institute of Medical Research, an EEG dynamics paper on transcranial magnetic stimulation for depression. Seventy people were recorded longitudinally during a course of TMS targeted at the left dorsolateral prefrontal cortex — the standard antidepressant site — and a hidden Markov-style dynamic network model was fit to their resting-state EEG to extract recurrent brain states. Higher baseline depressive symptom severity tracked with reduced occupancy of and fewer transitions into an anterior default mode brain state, paired with elevated posterior default mode activity. During treatment, the patients whose anterior default mode engagement dropped following each session were the ones whose symptoms improved in the second half of the intervention. The slower the cyclical brain-state dynamics at baseline, the worse the baseline severity. This is a useful brick to add to the TMS-response biomarker literature because it is mechanistic — not just a connectivity correlate but a dynamic, transition-rate biomarker that tracks treatment response over time on a per-session basis. The actionable angle for closed-loop TMS is that the anterior default mode signal could be the read-out you titrate stimulation against.

Switching from depression treatment to pain and placebo, from the Moayedi group at the University of Toronto in collaboration with the Atlas group at the National Institutes of Health. The known story on placebo analgesia is that mu-opioid receptor blockade with naloxone attenuates but does not abolish placebo pain relief, which has long implied a non-opioid component. They tested whether the endocannabinoid system carries that residual signal. In forty-eight healthy adults using a validated thermal placebo paradigm with blood sampling at baseline and after placebo and control, they measured circulating endocannabinoid ligands and beta-endorphin. Individual differences in placebo analgesia tracked with increases in fatty-acid-amide-hydrolase substrates — a composite of anandamide, palmitoylethanolamide, and oleoylethanolamide — but not with two-arachidonoylglycerol or beta-endorphin alone. The key interaction: beta-endorphin moderated the relationship. The fatty-acid-amide-hydrolase-substrate signal was strongly associated with pain reduction only when beta-endorphin was low. So endocannabinoid and opioid systems do not just both contribute — they substitute for each other in a state-dependent way. The clinical implication is that the variable that predicts who responds to a placebo or to an open-label, expectation-based intervention may be the baseline tone of the endocannabinoid system, and that opens an obvious experimental medicine path through fatty-acid-amide-hydrolase inhibitors as placebo-amplifiers in chronic pain.

Back to stress and depression, but at the molecular level. From the Wang lab at Augusta University, a single-nucleus multiome paper on chronic-stress damage to hippocampal myelination. Myelination deficits are one of the more reproducible pathological features of stress-related disorders — the white-matter signature shows up in human depression imaging and in postmortem oligodendrocyte counts — but the molecular link from stress to oligodendrocyte dysfunction has not been worked out. They put mice through chronic unpredictable mild stress, then ran joint single-nucleus RNA sequencing and chromatin-accessibility profiling on the hippocampus. Stress-responsive gene networks were enriched for depression-associated genes, with the oligodendrocyte lineage showing the heaviest vulnerability — particularly the immature oligodendrocyte-progenitor-cell and intermediate states. Stress disrupted progenitor migration, progression toward mature oligodendrocytes, intercellular communication, and myelination itself. The multiomic integration converged on SOXD transcription factors — SOX-five and SOX-six — as the key regulators of progenitor dysfunction. SOX-six chromatin immunoprecipitation sequencing confirmed direct SOX-six binding at regulatory elements for genes that control progenitor morphogenesis, migration, and glutamatergic signalling. Chronic stress reduced SOX-six protein levels in progenitors, and selectively restoring SOX-six expression in oligodendrocyte progenitors via an enhancer-driven approach rescued the stress-induced defects in migration and myelination. The therapeutic angle is that a cell-type-specific transcription factor that is both necessary and sufficient for the stress-myelination phenotype is exactly the kind of regulatory node where small-molecule or RNA-targeting approaches could plausibly act, and where the existing remyelination drug-development infrastructure built for multiple sclerosis becomes potentially relevant for depression.

Continuing on motivation and dopamine. From the Delevich lab at Washington State University, a paper that connects androgen deficiency in males to the mesoaccumbal circuit. The clinical observation is well established — testosterone loss is associated with reduced motivation, fatigue, and decreased goal-directed behaviour — but the neural mechanism has been hand-waved. They orchiectomized male mice either before puberty or in adulthood and ran a progressive-ratio closed-economy task that separates effort cost from food intake. Both timings of orchiectomy increased sensitivity to effort costs while leaving overall food consumption alone — a shift toward energy-efficient responding. The behavioural effects were similar across timing, but the neural adaptations were not. Adult orchiectomy increased dopamine content and reduced metabolite-to-dopamine ratios in the nucleus accumbens — meaning reduced dopamine turnover — and altered the excitability of both D-one-receptor-expressing and D-one-receptor-negative spiny projection neurons. Pre-pubertal orchiectomy left bulk dopamine measures unchanged but selectively reduced excitability of D-one spiny projection neurons in the nucleus accumbens core. So the same behavioural phenotype emerges from circuit-level adaptations that differ depending on whether testosterone is removed before or after the developmental window. The actionable point: motivation-deficit symptoms in androgen-deficient males may not be uniformly responsive to dopamine-targeting agents because the underlying mesoaccumbal change is timing-dependent, and that argues for stratifying clinical-trial design by age of onset.

Pivoting to Parkinson's, three items in succession. First, from the Olsen lab at the University of Pittsburgh in collaboration with the Broad metabolomics platform, a Drosophila alpha-synuclein-glia genetic screen that converges on adenosine metabolism. The model: human alpha-synuclein is expressed in fly neurons, and Drosophila genes are knocked down independently in glia. They screened the entire Drosophila kinome — every kinase — and the top hits were five genes involved in adenosine metabolism, including the adenylate kinases AK-one and AK-six and the adenosine kinases ADK-one and ADK-two. Knocking each of them down in glia increased brain adenosine, improved locomotion, reduced alpha-synuclein oligomers, and rescued neurodegeneration. The protective signal required adenosine signalling through the neuronal adenosine receptor in the fly. They then went to human astrocytes — knocking down AK-one decreased astrocyte reactivity markers and increased extracellular adenosine. Metabolomics in flies and in patient samples confirmed converging purine-metabolism changes. The translational lift here is the existing adenosine A-two-A receptor antagonist class — istradefylline is already approved for Parkinson's motor fluctuations — but the mechanism this paper supports is opposite in sign. The argument is that increasing glial adenosine release through pharmacological inhibition of adenosine kinase, rather than blocking the post-synaptic receptor, would be neuroprotective. That is a different molecular target from istradefylline and a different therapeutic logic — disease-modifying rather than symptomatic.

Continuing on Parkinson's, from the Mailman lab at Penn State College of Medicine, a paper that should restart a conversation about levodopa and substantia nigra iron. Iron accumulation in the substantia nigra is one of the most reproducible imaging findings in Parkinson's, and the dominant hypothesis has been that it is etiological — that iron-driven ferroptosis is part of what kills dopamine neurons. The authors tested the alternative — that the iron accumulation is a consequence of levodopa treatment, not a cause of disease. They used the unilaterally lesioned six-O-H-D-A rat model alongside unlesioned rats. Fifteen days of levodopa at allometrically scaled mid-stage Parkinson's doses produced significant increases in substantia nigra iron on the lesioned side and also on the unlesioned side — meaning the iron accumulation does not depend on the presence of dopamine neurons. A second four-month experiment in unlesioned rats showed iron rising progressively from baseline through one, two, and four months of daily levodopa. If this replicates in human longitudinal imaging cohorts, it reframes a sizable fraction of the substantia nigra iron literature from etiology to iatrogenesis. The clinical handle would be revisiting iron chelation as adjunct therapy and re-examining levodopa dose-finding with iron MRI as a safety endpoint.

Third Parkinson's item, from the Parish lab at the Florey Institute of Neuroscience and Mental Health in Melbourne, a cell-therapy paper that addresses the biggest practical problem with human pluripotent stem-cell-derived dopamine neuron grafts — they do not project as robustly as fetal donor tissue. The standard trophic intervention is glial-cell-derived neurotrophic factor, which works but is non-selective and produces off-target plasticity of non-dopaminergic neurons in the graft. Using single-nuclei RNA sequencing of grafts, they identified axon-guidance pathways — Semaphorin, Netrin, and Wnt — preferentially active in graft dopamine neurons. Overexpressing Semaphorin-three-A, Netrin-one, or Wnt-five-A in the host striatum selectively boosted A-nine dopamine neuron specification and striatal innervation without the off-target extrastriatal sprouting seen with glial-cell-derived neurotrophic factor. In Parkinsonian rats, Netrin-one overexpression in particular drove motor recovery and selective dopamine plasticity, with single-nucleus sequencing confirming the cell-type specificity. This is exactly the iteration the regenerative dopamine-cell-therapy field has needed — a way to support the graft that is more biology-aware than a broad-spectrum trophic factor. The next test worth seeing is in non-human primates with longer follow-up for off-target effects.

Two computational picks to close. First, from the Mujica-Parodi lab at Stony Brook, a biophysical model of human brain glutamate-GABA cycling that predicts the magnetic-resonance-spectroscopy response to administered ketones. The clinical premise is that ketones are used in epilepsy, in mood disorders, and increasingly in brain-aging interventions, and the leading mechanism-of-action hypothesis is modulation of excitatory-inhibitory balance through glutamate and GABA. But the actual biochemistry — how ketone bodies entering the brain shift glutamate and GABA pools — has been mostly hand-waved. They formalized it. The model centers on the pseudo-malate-aspartate shuttle and simulates the temporal dynamics and steady-state concentrations of glutamate and GABA as a function of ketone metabolism, then compares the predictions against magnetic-resonance-spectroscopy data from human ketone administration. The fit was good, and metabolic control analysis pulled out partial displacement of glucose metabolism through the pseudo-malate-aspartate shuttle as the dominant lever, with enzyme-specific knobs that selectively modulate one neurotransmitter or the other. The reason this matters for receptor pharmacology is that ketogenic interventions in psychiatry and neurology have been advancing clinically without a mechanism model that lets you predict which neurotransmitter the treatment is moving in which direction in which patient. This is the kind of quantitative-systems-pharmacology model that can be plugged into individual brain measurements to make ketone dosing patient-specific.

Closing on the agentic-AI thread. A bioRxiv paper from PromptBio Incorporated introducing PromptBio-Bench, a benchmark for large-language-model-based bioinformatics agents. Two hundred forty-four expert-curated tasks spanning bioinformatics and data-science workflows at three difficulty levels, with structured file-comparison scoring against expert reference outputs rather than free-text grading. They benchmarked three current bioinformatics agents — Biomni, ToolsGenie, and an in-house system — and reported comparable performance between Biomni and ToolsGenie, with all systems showing the expected sharp drop-off as task difficulty rose. Two reasons this is worth noting. One, it is structured-output scoring rather than judge-LLM scoring — the field has been moving in this direction because LLM-as-judge introduces a second model's blind spots into the eval, and file-comparison sidesteps that. Two, the bioinformatics-agent space has fragmented into Biomni and a handful of competitors, each evaluated on a different ad-hoc benchmark, which makes it nearly impossible to track real progress. A shared evaluation infrastructure with versioned tasks and reference files is exactly the dependency this work needed. Worth tracking how the leaderboard moves over the next two quarters.
