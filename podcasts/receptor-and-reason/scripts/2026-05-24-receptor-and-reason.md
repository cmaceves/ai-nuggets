## Script

Welcome back to Receptor and Reason. Ten items today, opening on a Northwestern paper that resolves a long-standing controversy about how striatal dopamine actually gets released during natural learning, then a clean cell-type map of mu-opioid receptors across the inputs to the ventral tegmental area, a brain-shuttle paper from VECT-HORUS that delivers small interfering RNA into deep brain regions across mice and non-human primates at clinically credible doses, a human-brain-organoid platform that predicts clinical seizure liability from calcium-network activity with serious discrimination, a Lieber Institute schizophrenia paper that resolves the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex at three spatial scales and converges on BDNF-TrkB, a novel amyloid-precursor-protein-derived peptide that may be a sex-biased driver of synapse loss in Alzheimer's, in-vivo evidence that temporal lobe epilepsy accumulates tau in patterns that track brain connectivity, a Drosophila glial-Alzheimer model that shows gut microbiome depletion accelerates tau and amyloid pathology, a transdiagnostic computational-psychiatry paper that splits failures of inference into two distinct phenotypes with opposite symptom profiles, and a closer from Google DeepMind on automatically discovering interpretable computational models of human and animal learning. Let's go.

Lead item — thalamus orchestrates local acetylcholine-dependent dopamine release in the learning striatum, from the Talia Lerner lab at Northwestern. The striatal dopamine field has had two competing accounts of how dopamine gets released during behavior. The textbook account is that midbrain dopamine neurons fire action potentials and release dopamine from their terminals, with the firing pattern carrying the reward-prediction-error signal. The alternative account, supported by ex-vivo slice work and optogenetic stimulation, is that cholinergic interneurons inside the striatum can also trigger dopamine release locally, by activating presynaptic nicotinic acetylcholine receptors on dopamine axons — release without a soma action potential. Whether that local mechanism actually operates during natural behavior, and what drives it physiologically, has been unclear. The Lerner group recorded simultaneous fiber photometry of striatal dopamine using a genetically-encoded dopamine sensor, and thalamic axon activity using a calcium indicator, in mice learning the accelerating rotarod — a striatal-dependent motor task. They tracked dopamine transients across days of learning, on-task and off-task, in both the dorsomedial striatum and the dorsolateral striatum. Three results. Dopamine transients in dorsomedial striatum, but not dorsolateral striatum, were frequently coupled to thalamic axon activity peaks, and that coupling was acetylcholine-dependent — so the thalamus drives cholinergic interneurons, which drive local dopamine release. Second, these thalamic-evoked dopamine events emerged with learning and depended on task engagement, not just movement. Third, and the most interesting piece, the responsiveness of the striatum to this local release mechanism was dynamically gated by the recent history of dopamine activity itself — a homeostatic-like regulation of how much weight the local pathway carries at any given moment. The pharmacological read-through is significant. Nicotinic agonists and positive allosteric modulators have struggled to find traction in cognitive disorders, and one reason has been the assumption that any effect would be diffuse and circuit-non-specific. The Northwestern data say the relevant nicotinic-driven dopamine release is regionally targeted, learning-dependent, and history-gated. A nicotinic modulator that selectively engages this pathway has a defined physiological niche — early goal-directed learning in dorsomedial striatum — not a generic dopamine boost. That is the kind of mechanistic anchor that the cognitive-enhancer side of the field has been missing.

Item two stays in the dopamine system, but shifts to opioid circuitry — mu-opioid receptor expression across cell-type specific afferents to the ventral tegmental area in male mice, from the Tom Jhou lab at the University of Maryland Baltimore. Opioid addiction biology is built on the observation that mu-opioid agonists modify ventral-tegmental-area dopamine activity through actions on both the dopamine neurons themselves and on the inputs that drive them. The textbook story emphasizes disinhibition — mu-opioids on GABAergic inputs reduce GABA release onto dopamine neurons, increasing dopamine firing. But the field has had only patchy data on which specific afferent populations actually express mu-opioid receptors at relevant levels. The Maryland group ran the question systematically — retrograde labeling from the ventral tegmental area to a range of distal brain regions, combined with RNAscope detection of oprm1 messenger RNA, which encodes the mu-opioid receptor, with co-staining for GABAergic markers. Two findings. Oprm1 is expressed in both GABAergic and non-GABAergic ventral-tegmental-area-projecting neurons across multiple input regions, so the disinhibition story is not the whole story — direct mu-opioid actions on glutamatergic and peptidergic inputs are real. But the GABAergic afferents do express higher levels of the receptor on average, especially from subcortical sources, while cortical inputs show low oprm1 expression regardless of GABAergic identity. Two implications. The relative weight of opioid-induced disinhibition is going to be input-source-dependent in ways that population-level pharmacology does not capture, and a next-generation opioid antagonist that biases away from particular afferents is a more credible engineering target than it would have been a year ago. Second, the cortical-input data argue that prefrontal top-down control of ventral-tegmental-area dopamine is largely opioid-insensitive at the synaptic level — relevant for how cognitive-behavioral interventions in addiction interact with opioid pharmacology, and for understanding why prefrontal-projecting circuit-level addiction therapeutics have been a separable design problem.

Item three, a serious advance on the brain-delivery side that follows directly from yesterday's brain-shuttle variability paper — harnessing transferrin receptor one for cross-species systemic delivery of small interfering RNAs to deep brain regions using single-domain antibodies, from the team at VECT-HORUS in Marseille. The brain-shuttle paradigm has been validated mostly for protein payloads — enzyme replacements, monoclonal antibodies, antibody-drug-conjugate-style biologics. Small interfering RNA — the RNAi modality — has been a much harder ask because the chemistry is different, the receptor-mediated transcytosis machinery has to handle a charged oligonucleotide, and the brain-distribution requirement for RNAi is stricter than for most protein modalities. The Marseille group used camelid-derived single-domain antibodies — anchor them as the heavy-chain-only mini-antibodies you find in llamas and alpacas, often called VHHs, about a tenth the size of a conventional immunoglobulin — directed against transferrin receptor one, the same shuttle target trontinemab uses. They conjugated the VHH one-to-one to tool small interfering RNAs and tested systemic dosing. Three results that matter. In neural cell cultures, the VHH conjugates produced potent RNA-interference activity at low nanomolar concentrations — receptor-mediated uptake is functional, not just trafficking. In wild-type mice, human-transferrin-receptor-transgenic mice, and non-human primates, the conjugates showed rapid transferrin-receptor-dependent clearance from circulation, and meaningful distribution into deep brain structures. And functional knockdown of target messenger RNA and protein reached fifty to eighty percent in deep brain regions, with half-maximal effective doses below one milligram per kilogram. That last number is what makes this a translational data point — it is in the range where antisense-oligonucleotide and small-interfering-RNA programs have moved into the clinic. The reason this matters in light of yesterday's Wyss-Institute paper is precise — the Wyss data say transferrin-receptor expression varies up to ten-fold across individuals, so even with a delivery platform this good, the brain-exposure variability across patients is going to be the dose-limiting variable, not the molecule. A peripheral diagnostic for shuttle-receptor expression and an RNAi-conjugate platform are two halves of the same product.

Item four, on the safety-pharmacology side — human central-nervous-system three-dimensional organoids predict clinical seizure liability from calcium network activity, from the 28bio team in Maple Grove, Minnesota. Drug-induced seizures remain one of the harder failure modes in central-nervous-system drug development. The preclinical models — high-dose rodent dosing, in-vitro multi-electrode arrays of dissociated neurons, hERG-style channel panels — collectively give you something, but their concordance with human clinical seizure events is weaker than the field wants. The 28bio group ran a head-to-head test. Sixty-six small-molecule drugs assembled from human clinical evidence, with thirty drugs documented to cause seizures clinically, and thirty-six without documented seizure liability. The drugs were tested in human induced-pluripotent-stem-cell-derived central-nervous-system three-dimensional organoids, with neuronal and astrocytic populations, expressing the right neuroactive-receptor and ion-channel gene programs and showing reproducible spontaneous calcium oscillations across production batches. Drugs were tested across concentration ranges anchored to reported clinical maximum plasma concentrations — so the predictions are pharmacokinetically realistic — and the calcium time-series responses were fed into a machine-learning workflow alongside chemical-structure features. The headline result is an area-under-the-receiver-operating-characteristic curve of zero-point-eight-seven-two for predicting clinical seizure liability, with eighty-three-percent sensitivity and eighty-nine-percent specificity at the drug level under cross-validation. Higher balanced performance than published in-vitro and preclinical seizure-liability models, and the model scores stratified the seizure-associated drugs by clinical prevalence — not just present-versus-absent, but a graded risk read-out. The reason this matters beyond seizures — pharmacokinetically-anchored functional human-tissue platforms with order-of-magnitude discrimination are exactly what the central-nervous-system safety-pharmacology gap has been waiting for. Expect the same architecture extended to cardiovascular liability, attention and arousal effects, and idiosyncratic neuropsychiatric adverse events. The bottleneck moves from "do we have a model" to "do we have a labeled clinical dataset to train against."

Item five, a serious schizophrenia paper from the Lieber Institute for Brain Development — mapping spatially organized molecular and genetic signatures of schizophrenia across multiple scales in human prefrontal cortex. Schizophrenia gene-expression studies have produced a long inventory of altered transcripts, but the field has struggled to pin down where in the cortex those changes live — which laminar domain, which cell type, which local microenvironment. The Lieber group did the spatial transcriptomics version of the question at three nested resolutions in postmortem human dorsolateral prefrontal cortex. At the laminar level, schizophrenia-associated transcriptional changes were strongest not in the cortical layers usually emphasized in the disease — layer three pyramidal neurons — but in glia-enriched domains, layer one and the meninges above, and the white matter below, with prominent downregulation of microglia-associated genes. Genetic risk localized differently — neuronal-rich gray matter — meaning the transcriptional disease signal and the inherited genetic-risk signal are anatomically dissociable. At the microenvironment level, neuronal and synaptic transcriptional changes were most prominent in neuropil — the space between cell bodies, the actual synaptic compartment — with downregulation of activity-dependent synaptic genes and inhibitory-neuron markers. At the cellular level, those neuropil-level signals reflected intrinsic alterations localized to specific cell types rather than diffuse field effects. And across all three scales, the convergent pathway pulled out is brain-derived-neurotrophic-factor — TrkB signaling, alongside inhibitory-circuit dysfunction. Two implications. The targeting story for schizophrenia therapeutics shifts further from "fix layer-three pyramidal neurons" toward "engage TrkB-pathway and inhibitory-interneuron biology." Several TrkB-targeted programs are already in early development for treatment-resistant depression — the schizophrenia data argue for indication expansion of those programs into negative-symptom and cognitive-symptom schizophrenia. And the dissociation between genetic-risk locus and transcriptional-disease locus says polygenic risk scores will not be sufficient as biomarkers — you want a spatial-transcriptomic or surrogate signature of the disease-state compartment.

Item six, a novel-target paper for Alzheimer's that the field is going to need to take seriously — AETA peptide contributes to Alzheimer's disease signature of synapse dysfunction, a multi-year project from the Bethus and Mari groups at the Institut de Pharmacologie Moléculaire et Cellulaire in Valbonne. The dominant explanatory framework for early synapse loss in Alzheimer's leans on amyloid-beta oligomers and on tau, both with extensive cell biology behind them but with therapeutic results that have been modest at best. The Valbonne group identified, in earlier work, a previously uncharacterized peptide called AETA — derived from the amyloid precursor protein but distinct from amyloid-beta — that modulates NMDA-receptor activity in healthy brain. The current paper asks whether AETA actually contributes to the disease. Post-mortem analyses of human hippocampal and prefrontal-cortex tissue showed significantly elevated AETA levels in Alzheimer's patients, with the elevation more pronounced in females. They then built a mouse model with chronically increased brain AETA expression. The mouse phenotype, again female-biased — increased astrocyte and microglia numbers without overt neuroinflammation, hippocampal transcriptional changes that closely parallel those in vulnerable human Alzheimer's brain regions, impaired NMDA-receptor signaling, dendritic spine loss, and memory deficits. Males in the same model were largely spared. Two reasons this matters. AETA enters the conversation as a third amyloid-precursor-protein-derived peptide that may be acting in parallel to amyloid-beta, with its own receptor pharmacology and its own therapeutic angle — antibody capture, secretase modulation, or NMDA-receptor-axis modulation are all on the table as starting points. And the female-biased phenotype lines up with the female-biased prevalence of Alzheimer's in human populations in a more direct way than amyloid-beta-centric models have managed. Worth watching whether the female-specificity replicates in independent cohorts and in a different mouse genetic background.

Item seven — in-vivo evidence for increased tau deposition in temporal lobe epilepsy, from the Montreal Neurological Institute at McGill. Temporal lobe epilepsy is the most common pharmaco-resistant adult epilepsy and has been categorized as a non-degenerative disease. Ex-vivo studies over the past few years have hinted that misfolded tau protein accumulates in temporal-lobe-epilepsy tissue, but the in-vivo human evidence has been limited. The McGill group used the tau positron-emission-tomography tracer MK-six-two-four-zero — anchor it as the second-generation tau ligand that selectively binds paired helical filaments in Alzheimer-spectrum tauopathy and has good signal-to-noise in temporal cortex — in twenty-eight temporal-lobe-epilepsy patients against twenty-eight healthy controls. They cross-referenced tau uptake against functional and structural brain connectivity, cognitive performance, and post-surgical tissue analysis from a subset of patients. Three results that matter. Temporal-lobe-epilepsy patients showed markedly increased tau tracer uptake in bilateral superior and medial temporal regions and parietal cortex — extending well beyond the seizure focus. The spatial distribution of tau accumulation followed regional functional and structural connectivity, suggesting that tau is propagating along network paths the way it does in Alzheimer's spectrum disease. Phosphorylated-tau immunohistochemistry confirmed deposition in five of six operated cases with available specimens, validating the imaging signal. The implication is that temporal lobe epilepsy may need to be reframed as a tauopathy-overlap syndrome with a non-Alzheimer driver — chronic seizure activity itself — and that the cognitive decline observed in long-standing temporal-lobe-epilepsy patients has a tau-pathology substrate, not just a hippocampal-sclerosis or network-dysfunction substrate. Anti-tau immunotherapies and tau-aggregation inhibitors that have been struggling to show benefit in mild Alzheimer's may have a cleaner test population in temporal-lobe-epilepsy cognitive decline — a smaller, more homogeneous, more clearly progressing cohort with a measurable imaging biomarker.

Item eight stays in the tau and amyloid space but lands on a different node — microbiome integrity protects against glial-mediated tau and amyloid pathology through circadian and autophagy homeostasis, from the Melkani lab at the University of Alabama at Birmingham. The gut-brain conversation in Alzheimer's has been long on association data and short on mechanism, which is partly a mouse-model problem — the inflammation and microbiome perturbations needed to get effect sizes in rodent Alzheimer models are large. The Birmingham group used Drosophila models with glial-specific expression of human tau and amyloid-associated transgenes — a clean genetic platform where you can perturb the microbiome cleanly and read out behavior, neurodegeneration, and proteostasis. The disease models showed shifts in gut microbial composition, particularly in Lactobacillus and Acetobacter species, suggesting an adaptive microbial response to glial-driven pathology — informative on its own. The functional experiment is microbiome depletion under axenic conditions — flies raised without gut bacteria. The axenic condition markedly worsened behavioral outcomes — disrupted sleep-circadian rhythms, impaired memory, reduced locomotor function — alongside amplified neuroinflammatory signaling, increased apoptotic gene expression, lipid dysregulation, and altered synaptic markers. The mechanistic finding is the cleanest part. Microbiome depletion induced an energy-stress signature — elevated phosphorylated AMP-activated protein kinase — but failed to restore proteostasis. Ubiquitinated proteins accumulated, the autophagy adaptor Ref2p built up, and autophagic flux was impaired. Without functional autophagy, tau, phosphorylated tau, and amyloid-beta-forty-two accumulated. The implication for the field is that the gut-microbiome axis is not just an immunomodulator in Alzheimer's — it is gated through autophagy and circadian biology, both of which are tractable therapeutically. Time-restricted-feeding interventions, autophagy enhancers like trehalose or spermidine, and circadian-tuned dosing of existing Alzheimer's drugs all become more biologically motivated than they were when the gut-Alzheimer link was mostly correlational. Drosophila is not human, but the mechanism is concrete enough to drive the next mouse experiment.

Item nine, a computational-psychiatry paper that should reshape how the field thinks about transdiagnostic dimensions — dissociating volatility and stochasticity reveals transdiagnostic computational signatures of psychopathology, from Payam Piray's group at the University of Southern California. The setup is a clean one. When you are trying to learn from a noisy environment, the optimal learning rate depends on whether the world is actually changing — volatility — or whether your observations are noisy reads of a stable world — stochasticity. The two demand opposite adjustments. Faster updates when the world changes, slower updates when the noise is high. People who confuse the two go wrong in opposite directions — under-update when they should update, over-update when they should hold. The USC group built a Bayesian model that decomposes individual learners into three computational phenotypes — intact learners; stochasticity-blind learners, who treat noise as change and over-update; and volatility-blind learners, who treat change as noise and under-update. In two large online samples across three tasks, they found a clean double dissociation against transdiagnostic psychiatric dimensions. Stochasticity-blind learners scored higher on the internalizing dimension — anxiety and depression. Volatility-blind learners scored higher on the externalizing dimension — behavioral addiction, compulsivity. Distinct symptom dimensions correspond to distinct, opposite failures of inference about uncertainty. The implication for psychiatric pharmacology is more concrete than most transdiagnostic-computational results. Drugs that modulate dopamine, norepinephrine, or serotonin shift the brain's effective learning rate in measurable ways — and you would expect a drug that flattens learning rate to help stochasticity-blind learners and harm volatility-blind learners, and vice versa. Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors slowing update rates is mechanistically aligned with their efficacy in anxious-depressed populations. Stimulants speeding update rates aligns with their efficacy in compulsive-behavioral disorders only in subgroups. Computational-phenotype stratification before randomization in psychiatric trials is the move the paper supports — testing whether the inferred phenotype predicts which arm a patient responds to.

Closing item, on the computational-modeling-meets-AI side — AI-discovered cognitive models reveal novel insights into human and animal learning, from a cross-institutional team led by Daniel Kasenberg at Google DeepMind with Nathaniel Daw and Kim Stachenfeld among the senior authors. The mechanistic-modeling tradition in psychology and computational neuroscience has been hand-crafted — researchers propose models from theory, fit them to behavior, and iterate. That works but it is slow, the model space is limited by the modeler's imagination, and the resulting models systematically fall short of capturing the richness of real behavior. The DeepMind team built DataDIVER — anchor it as an automated discovery pipeline that searches over short computer programs, jointly optimized to fit behavioral data and to be interpretable. The output is not a black-box neural network — it is a readable program connecting to existing reinforcement-learning and cognitive-modeling frameworks, that a human scientist can inspect, modify, and use to generate predictions. The validation strategy is the part that earns it a serious-paper rating. The discovered models predicted previously unreported behavioral patterns in re-analyses of existing human and animal learning datasets — predictions that came out of the automated discovery, not the human modeler, and that hold up empirically. The implication for the computational-neuropsychiatry field is straightforward. The hand-crafted family of reinforcement-learning models — Rescorla-Wagner, Q-learning variants, hierarchical-Bayesian extensions — has been the lingua franca for relating circuit-level biology to behavior, but the family has been narrow. An automated discovery tool that surfaces new model classes from data, in interpretable form, broadens what is testable. Combine it with the volatility-stochasticity dissociation from item nine — DataDIVER could plausibly discover the same dissociation from raw behavior, without the modeler imposing the Bayesian structure up front. That is the kind of methodological win the field has been waiting for. That's the slate. Back tomorrow.
