## Script

Welcome back to Receptor and Reason. Ten items today, centered on causal human neuromodulation and the modalities that surround it — a microstimulation study at the human substantia nigra that reshapes loss processing and momentary happiness in awake Parkinson's surgery, a wireless magnetoelectric nanodisc deep-brain-stimulation paper from MIT, and two pipeline updates from Psychiatric Times — a first-in-class endocannabinoid reuptake inhibitor entering Phase 2 in generalized anxiety, and a review of the GLP-1 wave moving into alcohol use disorder. From there we pivot to mechanism — a postnatal chemogenetic early-adversity model from Mumbai that produces durable adult glial dysfunction, and a Familial-Alzheimer's mechanism paper from Mount Sinai that suggests why gamma-secretase inhibitors went wrong. Two computational-pharmacology items follow — a sobering AlphaFold 3 evaluation on D-peptides, and a Model-Context-Protocol-driven natural-language interface to sixty life-science knowledge graphs. We close with a neuropeptide-pharmacology atlas of melanin-concentrating-hormone neurons in the maternal preoptic area, and a tool-compound paper on insulin-like-growth-factor sensitization for traumatic brain injury. Let's go.

Lead item, and the rarest kind of paper this corpus produces — direct causal human evidence at the substantia nigra. From a Yebra-and-Rutishauser collaboration spanning Cedars-Sinai, Caltech, and the Universidad Politecnica de Madrid — microstimulation of the human dopaminergic midbrain causally reshapes value-based decision-making and happiness. The setup is the increasingly productive awake-DBS-surgery paradigm. Parkinson's patients undergoing deep-brain-stimulation electrode implantation in the substantia nigra pars compacta agreed to a brief intraoperative behavioral task. They played a gambling task with interleaved happiness ratings while the surgical team delivered focal microstimulation at outcome. The findings are precise rather than diffuse. Stimulation at the moment of outcome reduced risk-seeking specifically in the loss domain — patients gambled less aggressively when chasing losses — without globally changing the rate of gambling or the gain-domain behavior. Choice efficiency improved. Patients guided their behavior toward gambles with higher expected value, and they earned more across the session. Computational modeling localized the change to loss-utility weighting — reduced loss aversion, more linear loss utility, gain-domain processing left intact. And in parallel, momentary happiness ratings showed that stimulation didn't tonically elevate mood. What it did was selectively amplify outcome-related happiness changes — happiness tracked decision value and reward prediction error more sharply under stimulation than without. Causal evidence that the human substantia nigra carves loss-and-gain valuation asymmetrically, and that hedonic experience is bound to phasic reward computations rather than to a tonic dopamine set-point. The pharmacological read-through is exactly the kind that informs how you think about dopaminergic interventions in depression and addiction. If loss processing and momentary happiness can be selectively reshaped at this circuit node, then drugs and stimulation strategies that engage the substantia nigra differentially across the loss-and-gain axis become a real design space. The next experiments — does this carry to the depression population, do the effects survive outside the operating room — are the obvious ones.

Item two, staying with deep brain stimulation but pivoting from the patient on the table to the implant of the future — magnetoelectric nanodiscs diminish motor deficits in a model of Parkinson's disease, from the Anikeeva group at MIT. Conventional DBS is a clinically transformative therapy that requires a craniotomy, a chronically implanted electrode array, and a stimulator under the skin — invasive, expensive, hardware-dependent, with the inflammation and gliosis you'd expect from any long-term implant. The magnetoelectric nanodisc, abbreviated MEND, is the most aggressive minimally-invasive reformulation the field has produced. Nanoscale discs are injected into the target structure — here, the subthalamic nucleus — and they convert an externally applied weak magnetic field into local electric polarization at the disc surface. No leads, no battery, no chronically implanted hardware. The therapeutic test is in a mouse Parkinson's model, with motor outcomes quantified through a computational gait-analysis pipeline they call GaitPattern that extracts fine-grained features induced by stimulation. MEND-mediated subthalamic-nucleus DBS produced motor improvements comparable to conventional electrode-based DBS in the same model — that's the headline. Two ancillary findings raise the stakes. The local inflammatory response was substantially smaller than with implanted electrodes, and the brain showed reduced oxidative stress after MEND-mediated stimulation — oxidative stress being a known driver of dopaminergic-neuron loss in Parkinson's. The latter point hints that a less-traumatic stimulation modality could carry a disease-modifying signature in addition to its symptomatic benefit, though that's a hypothesis the paper doesn't formally test. The translational distance from mouse to human DBS is real — discs need to be safely deliverable, magnetically addressable through skull, and stable in the brain for years. But the modality direction is the right one, and this is the most credible therapeutic demonstration the platform has produced so far.

Item three, a pipeline update from Psychiatric Times that's strategically interesting because of the receptor class — SYT-510 enters Phase 2 dosing for generalized anxiety disorder, from Synendos Therapeutics out of Switzerland. SYT-510 is a first-in-class selective endocannabinoid reuptake inhibitor — abbreviated by the company as an SERI — that elevates endogenous endocannabinoid tone by blocking endocannabinoid uptake into the cell, rather than directly engaging CB1 or CB2. The mechanistic argument is that direct CB1 agonism has been undruggable for anxiety because it produces dose-limiting cognitive and motor side effects — the cannabinoid receptors are too widely expressed and the modulatory dynamic range is too narrow. An endocannabinoid reuptake inhibitor, in principle, amplifies activity at synapses where the endocannabinoid tone is already engaged, which is a context-conditional way to recruit the system without globally activating it. Phase 1 in sixty healthy volunteers, ascending single and multiple doses, showed plasma and central-nervous-system exposure at pharmacologically relevant levels, no drug-related safety concerns, and exploratory electroencephalography readouts consistent with established anxiolytic activity. Phase 2 is now dosing in DSM-5-criteria generalized-anxiety-disorder patients. The reason this is worth flagging — the endocannabinoid system has been the second-most-promising-yet-undruggable pharmacology target in psychiatry, after the muscarinic story KarXT made tractable last year. If a selective reuptake-inhibition strategy turns out to be the right indirect entry, then the cannabinoid axis becomes a real therapeutic surface for anxiety, post-traumatic stress, and the affective comorbidities of chronic pain. The Phase 2 efficacy readout is the one to watch — the Phase 1 EEG signal is encouraging but doesn't yet say anything about clinical anxiolysis.

Item four, the other Psychiatric Times piece worth the time — GLP-1s for treatment of alcohol use disorder, a critical-review piece that consolidates a year of accelerating evidence. The narrative the field has been writing — glucagon-like-peptide-1 agonism extends beyond glycemia and weight to brain reward, and specifically to substance reinforcement — has been moving from observational signals to randomized trials over the last twelve months. The piece names what's now on the table. A randomized controlled trial from a National Institutes of Health group enrolled one hundred and eight treatment-seeking patients with alcohol use disorder and comorbid obesity. Twenty-six weeks of weekly semaglutide on top of cognitive behavioral therapy reduced heavy-drinking days, reduced laboratory self-administered alcohol consumption, reduced weekly alcohol craving, and as a bonus reduced cigarettes per day in active smokers. A multi-target trial emulation across more than one hundred and twenty million electronic-health-record patients found that tirzepatide was associated with a significant reduction in new alcohol-use-disorder incidence in type-2-diabetes patients relative to dipeptidyl-peptidase-4 inhibitors as a comparator — a real-world signal that survives in a population that's not a trial cohort. The mechanism the field is converging on is that GLP-1 receptors are expressed in the mesolimbic dopamine system — ventral tegmental area, nucleus accumbens, mesocortical projections — and the receptor agonism modulates reward signaling in a way that dampens the reinforcing value of alcohol without producing the aversive profile of older anti-craving agents. The interaction risks the piece flags — gastrointestinal tolerability remains the rate-limiter; the population that needs anti-craving therapy is not the same as the population that needs glycemic control, and the safety profile in lean treatment-seeking patients is still being characterized. Watch the larger randomized trials reading out over the next year — this is now a real pipeline rather than a hypothesis.

Item five, a mechanistic early-adversity paper from the Vaidya group at the Tata Institute of Fundamental Research in Mumbai, that completes the loop from circuit perturbation to durable glial pathology — chronic postnatal chemogenetic activation of forebrain excitatory neurons modulates adult glial function and metabolism in male mice. The conceptual setup is one of the most useful frameworks the early-adversity field has developed. Across multiple preclinical models — maternal separation, limited bedding, social isolation — adult phenotypes converge on a recognizable pattern: anxio-depressive behavior, glial dysfunction, disrupted amino-acid neurotransmission, and dysregulated G-protein-coupled-receptor signaling in cortical and hippocampal circuits. The Vaidya group has been arguing for years that the early-life perturbation can be reverse-engineered — that selectively driving excessive Gq signaling in forebrain excitatory neurons during early postnatal life, using the hM3Dq designer-receptor approach, recapitulates the adult phenotype without needing the upstream environmental insult. This paper is the glial-arm extension. Postnatal hM3Dq activation of CaMKII-positive forebrain excitatory neurons not only produces adult anxiety-like behavior — it produces bidirectional transcriptional regulation of glial-associated genes in cortex and hippocampus. Glial-fibrillary-acidic-protein, the astrocyte intermediate-filament marker, and the excitatory-amino-acid-transporter glutamate uptake machinery — GFAP, Aldh1l1, S100β, Eaat1, Eaat2, Eaat3 — go down in neocortex and up in hippocampus in adults whose excitatory neurons were transiently activated in the postnatal window. The astrocyte density doesn't change — the transcriptional and functional state of the astrocytes does. Nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy shows that astrocyte-specific glutamate and gamma-amino-butyric-acid turnover is reduced in the adult animals. Two implications. First, the early-life perturbation produces durable astrocyte transcriptional reprogramming, not just neuronal change — and the astrocyte state is now a candidate biomarker and a candidate intervention surface for adult psychopathology with developmental etiology. Second, the model is reverse-genetic — you can produce a chemogenetic version of the adult phenotype without environmental stressors, which is a cleaner platform for testing whether astrocyte-targeted therapeutics can reverse the established adult state.

Item six, a mechanism paper out of the Georgakopoulos and Robakis groups at the Icahn School of Medicine at Mount Sinai that gives the failed gamma-secretase-inhibitor Alzheimer's trials a clean post-mortem — Presenilin-1 Familial Alzheimer's mutants and gamma-secretase inhibition accumulate a VEGFR2-derived peptide called VCTF1, suppressing brain vascular-endothelial-growth-factor-receptor-2 dimerization, angiogenesis, and neuroprotection. The story knits two threads the field had treated as separate. Thread one — gamma-secretase inhibitors in Alzheimer's clinical trials a decade ago produced not just lack of efficacy but actual cognitive worsening, and the field has been blaming Notch signaling and other gamma-secretase substrates without a satisfying alternative mechanism. Thread two — most Alzheimer's patients have cerebrovascular pathology, and the vascular-endothelial-growth-factor-receptor-2 system, the principal angiogenic and neuroprotective receptor in brain vasculature, has been quietly implicated but not mechanistically integrated with the gamma-secretase story. The Mount Sinai group shows that the receptor undergoes a sheddase-driven cleavage by the metalloprotease ADAM17 to produce a membrane-embedded gamma-secretase substrate they call VCTF1 — a transmembrane and intracellular fragment of the receptor. Normally, gamma-secretase clears VCTF1 by cleaving it further. Presenilin-1 Familial Alzheimer's mutants and pharmacological gamma-secretase inhibitors both block this clearance, VCTF1 accumulates, and the accumulated fragment binds full-length receptor monomers and prevents the productive dimerization that VEGF-A signaling requires. Brain angiogenesis is suppressed. Neuroprotection is suppressed. The trial-failure story now has a clean mechanism. Gamma-secretase inhibition didn't just fail — it actively suppressed an angiogenic neuroprotective program that Familial-Alzheimer-mutant patients are already losing at baseline. The therapeutic read-through cuts both directions. Direct gamma-secretase inhibition is off the table for cognitive indications. But selective inhibition of the upstream ADAM17 cleavage that generates VCTF1, or pharmacological prevention of the VCTF1-monomer interaction with full-length receptor, could plausibly restore VEGF-A-dependent angiogenesis in the cerebrovasculature of Familial-Alzheimer patients. The next experiment is the proof-of-concept rescue in the mouse model.

Item seven, a sharp methodological audit on the computational drug-design side — AlphaFold 3 fails to predict D-peptide chirality, fold, and binding pose in heterochiral complexes, from the Donald lab at Duke. Background first. D-peptides — peptides built from D-amino-acid stereoisomers rather than the biological L-form — are a small but pharmacologically important therapeutic class. They resist protease degradation, often have improved bioavailability and membrane permeability, and several first-in-class D-peptide drugs have been approved. The computational design pipeline for D-peptide binders to biological L-protein targets has been improving, and AlphaFold 3, the highest-profile sequence-to-structure model in current circulation, was reported in its launch paper to have a low chirality-violation rate of about four-and-a-half percent on a diverse-chiral-molecule benchmark. Donald's group ran the targeted evaluation that the original benchmark didn't run. Three thousand two hundred and fifty-five black-box predictions of D-peptide binders in heterochiral complexes with L-protein targets, with explicit D-stereocenter inputs. The chirality-violation rate is fifty-one percent — eleven-fold higher than the headline number. On average, the model is no better than random chirality assignment at each peptide residue position. Increasing the number of seeds doesn't rescue it. The folds and binding poses are systematically wrong. And the model's own confidence metrics fail to distinguish the correct predictions from the chirality-violating ones — which is the bit that matters most for any computational-design workflow that uses confidence as a triage filter. Two takeaways. First, AlphaFold 3 is not the workflow for D-peptide design today, regardless of what its overall benchmark suggests — the model has a structural blind spot in heterochiral complexes that an L-protein-trained backbone almost has to have. Second, methodology-aware benchmark design matters. A general benchmark with broad chiral-molecule coverage hides the failure mode that a targeted D-peptide benchmark exposes. If you're running structure-based computational design at all, this is the kind of evaluation that needs to be in your stack.

Item eight, an agentic-AI paper from the Database Center for Life Science in Japan with the most concrete demonstration this year of why the Model Context Protocol is the right abstraction for life-science data infrastructure — TogoMCP — natural-language querying of life-science knowledge graphs via schema-guided large language models and the Model Context Protocol. Context. The Database Center for Life Science maintains an RDF Portal that aggregates approximately sixty life-science databases — UniProt, ChEMBL, Ensembl, and dozens of smaller specialized resources — into a unified knowledge-graph layer. Querying it requires fluency in SPARQL and in each database's specific RDF schema, which is approximately the union of skills that no working biologist has time to maintain. Large language models can in principle translate natural-language questions into SPARQL, but without schema-level context they hallucinate predicates and fail to resolve entity names. The TogoMCP system treats the language model as a tool-calling agent inside the Model Context Protocol, and adds two specific mechanisms. First, a YAML-format file they call the Metadata-Interoperability-Exchange file — abbreviated MIE — that dynamically supplies the language model with the target database's structural and semantic context at query time, so the schema lives outside the model weights. Second, a two-stage workflow that separates entity resolution via external REST APIs from schema-guided SPARQL generation, so name disambiguation and query construction don't entangle. The benchmark is fifty biologically-grounded questions across twenty-three databases. TogoMCP improves over an unaided baseline with a Cohen's effect size of one-point-eight-two — large, by the standard interpretation — with eighty-percent-plus win rates on the question types that have precise verifiable answers. The architectural argument is the more durable contribution. As life-science data infrastructure scales past what any one model can absorb into its weights, the path forward is to externalize schemas into protocol-accessible context files and to compose specialized tools through a standard interface — which is exactly what the Model Context Protocol gives you. The biomedical-LLM-agent corpus has been gravitating in this direction over the last six months. This is the cleanest demonstration so far.

Item nine, a neuropeptide-pharmacology paper from the Bittencourt lab at the Universidade de São Paulo with collaborators across Connecticut and Michigan — transcriptional plasticity of melanin-concentrating-hormone neurons in the medial preoptic area of lactating mouse dams. The setup engages a neglected corner of pharmacological neuroscience. Melanin-concentrating-hormone, a neuropeptide and one of the canonical orexigenic transmitters, is well-characterized in the lateral hypothalamus where it regulates feeding and arousal. A smaller, less-studied population sits in the medial preoptic area, the hypothalamic hub that coordinates maternal behavior. The functional and pharmacological identity of these preoptic melanin-concentrating-hormone neurons has been unclear. The group integrated single-cell-RNA-sequencing reanalysis of a published mouse-preoptic-area dataset with new single-cell profiling of fluorescence-sorted GABAergic neurons. Two transcriptionally distinct Pmch-expressing GABAergic populations emerge in the medial preoptic area, and both are dynamically engaged during mid-to-late lactation. The lactating-state transcriptional program is the interesting bit. Pmch is upregulated, and the upregulation comes with coordinated enrichment of a neuropeptide and hormone-responsive gene set — prodynorphin, the precursor of dynorphin and the endogenous kappa-opioid ligand; islet amyloid polypeptide, classically a beta-cell hormone but also a CNS neuromodulator; and the prolactin receptor. NeuroEstimator activity inference and pseudotime trajectory analysis support a lactation-driven shift in neuronal state, and weighted-gene-co-expression analysis identifies modules selectively enriched during lactation. Two implications for pharmacology. First, melanin-concentrating-hormone receptor 1 antagonists — which have been pursued and largely abandoned for obesity over the last decade — may have postpartum and maternal-behavior pharmacology that the original metabolic programs never tested. Second, the kappa-opioid signal embedded in this population is consistent with the broader story that kappa-opioid antagonists may have indications in postpartum mood disorders, which several industry programs are now actively exploring. Atlas-style work like this often quietly seeds the next generation of CNS pipeline targets — worth keeping the maternal-preoptic kappa-opioid-and-prodynorphin signal in the back of your mind.

Closing item, a tool-compound paper for an underserved indication — therapeutic effects of an insulin-like-growth-factor sensitizer in traumatic brain injury, from Zegarra-Valdivia and Torres Aleman at the Achucarro Basque Center for Neuroscience. Background. Traumatic brain injury is a high-incidence indication with essentially no disease-modifying pharmacology — supportive care, neurosurgical management when needed, and rehabilitation. Insulin-like-growth-factor-1, abbreviated IGF-1, has long been a candidate neuroprotective signal in brain injury, but its therapeutic use has been hamstrung by a recognized pharmacological problem — brain injury induces a loss-of-sensitivity to circulating IGF-1, so direct IGF-1 supplementation produces diminishing returns. The compound here, called AIK3a305, is a small-molecule IGF-1 sensitizer — it doesn't increase IGF-1 levels, it restores the brain's responsiveness to the IGF-1 that's already there. Mild traumatic brain injury in mice, induced by controlled cortical impact, was followed twenty-four hours later by daily intraperitoneal AIK3a305 dosing for four weeks. Sensorimotor disturbances measured on the adhesive-removal test were reversed. Neurological severity score and Y-maze cognitive performance were ameliorated. Anxiety-like behavior was normalized. Circulating IGF-1 went up — consistent with the sensitization-driven feedback mechanism — and the inflammatory biomarker interleukin-6 stayed at control levels rather than rising as TBI usually drives it. Bulk transcriptomics shows that the sensitizer treatment doesn't induce a large new gene program — it dampens both the upregulated and downregulated injury-response gene sets toward baseline, which is the right pharmacological signature for a sensitizer rather than a direct agonist. The class is novel — sensitizers, as a pharmacological category for protein-deficiency-of-response diseases, have been a quieter design space than direct agonists and antagonists. If AIK3a305 holds together through additional models and dose-response work, the IGF-1-sensitizer category becomes a credible candidate for traumatic brain injury and, by extension, the broader neurodegenerative indications where IGF-1 sensitivity is known to decline.
