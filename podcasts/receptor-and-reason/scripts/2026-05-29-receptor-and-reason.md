## Script

This is Receptor and Reason for Friday, May twenty-ninth, twenty twenty-six. Ten items today, all fresh. We open on Phase Two postpartum-depression data for a short-acting psychedelic prodrug, and we close on an agentic system for spatial-omics analysis with a persistent memory architecture.

First, full Phase Two results for luvesilocin in postpartum depression, presented this week at the American Society of Clinical Psychopharmacology and the American Psychiatric Association annual meetings by Reunion Neuroscience. Luvesilocin, formerly known as RE-one-zero-four, is a serotonin two-A agonist — a short-acting prodrug of four-hydroxy-di-isopropyl-tryptamine, with a roughly three-to-four-hour psychoactive duration designed to fit a single-day clinic workflow rather than the eight-hour window of psilocybin. The RECONNECT trial randomized women with moderate-to-severe postpartum depression to a single thirty-milligram dose or a one-point-five-milligram active-placebo control alongside protocolized psychological support. The primary endpoint — change from baseline on the Montgomery-Asberg depression rating scale at day seven — was met with a roughly twenty-three-point reduction from baseline in the thirty-milligram arm and a between-group difference at p equals zero-point-zero-zero-nine-four. Response was seventy-seven percent and remission was seventy-one percent in the active arm, with effects emerging by day one and durable through day twenty-eight. Safety was consistent with the known psychedelic class profile. The FDA had already granted breakthrough therapy designation back in February on the basis of the topline; this readout is the full trial. The company intends to advance into a single pivotal Phase Three trial in postpartum depression on a path where, if positive, that trial would complete the registrational data package. Two things make this worth the time. One — postpartum depression is currently served by brexanolone and zuranolone, both gamma-amino-butyric-acid-A allosteric modulators, so a serotonin two-A agonist would be the first mechanistically distinct rapid-acting option in the indication. Two — a short psychoactive window changes the clinic logistics, and that is what will determine real-world adoption more than the day-seven response rate ever will.

Second, lapatinib suppresses pro-inflammatory cytokines and alleviates pain behavior in a mouse model of postoperative pain, from the University of Kentucky. Lapatinib — a dual epidermal-growth-factor-receptor and human-epidermal-growth-factor-receptor-two tyrosine kinase inhibitor approved for HER-two-positive breast cancer — has shown analgesic signals in chronic pain models, and importantly, it does not engage central reward pathways the way opioids do. The group tested it in acute postoperative pain. In vitro, lapatinib suppresses tumor-necrosis-factor, interleukin-one-beta, interleukin-six, cyclo-oxygenase-two, matrix metalloproteinases, and prostaglandin-E-two synthesis in lipopolysaccharide-stimulated macrophages. In a mouse plantar-incision model, preoperative dosing reduces both mechanical allodynia and thermal hyperalgesia, with parallel decreases in pro-inflammatory mediator expression in the spinal cord and dorsal root ganglia. The data are not new in the sense that lapatinib's analgesic potential has been on the radar for a while, but acute postoperative pain — where opioid-induced hyperalgesia is the driver of the transition to chronic pain — is a setting where a non-opioid, non-reward-engaging mechanism has obvious clinical pull. The repurposing story has the usual caveats: lapatinib's hepatic and cardiac toxicity profile was tolerable in oncology, and tolerating it in postoperative pain is a different conversation. But the mechanistic logic — a kinase inhibitor that flattens the inflammatory cytokine surge driving acute-to-chronic pain transitions — is worth tracking.

Third, adrenomedullin restores mitochondrial bioenergetics and rescues interneuron phenotypes in human models of twenty-two-q-eleven-point-two deletion syndrome, from the Pasca lab at Stanford. Twenty-two-q-eleven-point-two deletion syndrome is the strongest known genetic risk factor for schizophrenia, with about a twenty-five-fold elevated risk relative to the general population, but the cellular mechanisms have remained underspecified. The group used induced-pluripotent-stem-cell-derived subpallial organoids and forebrain assembloids, the latter generated by fusing cortical and subpallial organoids so that migrating interneurons can be tracked into a developing cortical field. Patient-derived interneurons show impaired migration, excessive mitochondrial fragmentation, reduced oxidative phosphorylation, and dysregulated calcium signaling — a glycolytic shift consistent with the known position of mitochondrial genes in the twenty-two-q-eleven-point-two deletion interval. Adrenomedullin, a fifty-two amino-acid peptide hormone signaling through the calcitonin-receptor-like-receptor coupled with receptor-activity-modifying-protein-two, rescued essentially the full phenotype — mitochondrial morphology, membrane potential, respiration, calcium handling, actin retrograde flow, and interneuron migration. Mechanistically the rescue operates through two parallel pathways: protein-kinase-A-mediated phosphorylation of DRP-one at serine six-three-seven, which promotes mitochondrial fusion, and phospholipase-C-mediated calcium signaling, which normalizes actin dynamics. The same phenotypes and the same rescue replicate in primary developing human cortical tissue carrying the deletion. The pharmacological implication is that a G-protein-coupled receptor agonist already studied in cardiopulmonary indications could in principle be repositioned as a developmental intervention in the population at highest genetic risk for schizophrenia, with a window plausibly extending into early symptomatic phases — though the obvious caveat is that the brain-penetrance and tolerability profile of adrenomedullin as a chronic CNS therapeutic is unsolved.

Fourth, an antibody targeting nitrated alpha-synuclein counteracts protein-spreading pathology, from the Ulusoy lab at the German Center for Neurodegenerative Diseases. Tyrosine-nitration on alpha-synuclein is a well-documented post-translational modification in Parkinson's disease, but whether nitrated alpha-synuclein actively drives pathology or merely flags oxidative stress has been ambiguous. The group generated six-G-six, a monoclonal antibody selective for the tyrosine-thirty-nine nitrated alpha-synuclein species, and tested it in three mouse models. The first two are vagal-overexpression paradigms — one with paraquat-induced oxidative stress, one on a glucocerebrosidase L-four-four-four-P heterozygous background — and both produce robust caudal-to-rostral alpha-synuclein spreading that six-G-six markedly counteracts. The third model is striatal pre-formed fibril injection in alpha-synuclein-overexpressing mice, where six-G-six protects against fibril-induced aggregate pathology and against the ensuing nigral dopaminergic-neuron loss. A small pilot human study finds tyrosine-thirty-nine nitrated alpha-synuclein elevated in Parkinson cerebrospinal fluid relative to controls. This pairs naturally with last week's Wade-Martins humanized glucocerebrosidase L-four-four-four-P transgene paper — that work argued the L-four-four-four-P enzyme drives a release-machinery dopamine deficit independent of alpha-synuclein accumulation; this work argues that the alpha-synuclein arm of the same genetic background can be neutralized by selectively clearing a specific post-translationally modified species. The implication for the active-immunotherapy and passive-antibody programs in Parkinson's is that selectivity for pathogenic species, rather than total alpha-synuclein, is plausibly where the therapeutic window lives.

Fifth, induced-pluripotent-stem-cell-derived extracellular vesicles rescue deficits in human and mouse models of Parkinson's disease, from the Stern lab at the University of Haifa. The group derived dopaminergic neurons from patients carrying the familial A-fifty-three-T mutation in the alpha-synuclein gene and tracked their physiology over time. They identified a biphasic trajectory — an early hyperexcitable phase with elevated spontaneous firing, followed by progressive transition into hypoexcitability as the cells mature, accompanied by network dysfunction and alpha-synuclein accumulation. Transcriptomic profiling at the transition point shows simultaneous upregulation of stress-and-inflammatory pathways including p-fifty-three and Janus-kinase signal-transducer-and-activator-of-transcription, and downregulation of metabolic and synaptic-maintenance genes — a molecular signature that precedes the functional collapse. Treatment with extracellular vesicles derived from healthy induced-pluripotent-stem-cells — small membrane-bound particles carrying messenger-RNA, micro-RNA, and protein cargo — rescued both the electrophysiological deficits and the cellular pathology in the human neurons, and produced corresponding improvements in a mouse model. The reframe worth absorbing is that early dopaminergic dysfunction in Parkinson's may be a hyperactivity-driven metabolic-stress problem before it is an alpha-synuclein problem, and an intercellular signaling cargo from healthy cells can rebalance it — which positions extracellular-vesicle therapeutics as a cell-free alternative to cell-replacement strategies, with a different regulatory and manufacturing profile than transplanted dopaminergic precursors.

Sixth, conserved inhibitory motifs yield distinct hippocampal functions through regional circuit embedding, from the Siwani group at Uppsala University. Oriens-lacunosum-moleculare interneurons expressing the alpha-two subunit of the nicotinic acetylcholine receptor — known in the literature as OLM-alpha-two cells — exist along the entire longitudinal axis of the hippocampus, but whether the same cell type contributes the same computation in intermediate versus ventral hippocampus has been unclear. Using cell-type-selective optogenetics, behavioral assays, and monosynaptic plus trans-synaptic viral tracing, the group dissociates them functionally. Manipulation of intermediate-hippocampus OLM-alpha-two cells selectively influences object-directed exploration and novel-object processing without touching anxiety-like behavior; manipulation of ventral-hippocampus OLM-alpha-two cells modulates arousal and emotionally driven avoidance without contributing to object memory. Circuit tracing reveals the dissociation is structural — intermediate hippocampus is embedded in sensory cortical networks, ventral hippocampus is wired to limbic structures including the basolateral amygdala and nucleus accumbens, and the OLM-alpha-two cells themselves receive only sparse direct amygdala input, meaning the emotional modulation is happening through their downstream targets rather than at the cell. For pharmacology this argues that nicotinic-receptor or interneuron-targeted interventions cannot be evaluated as if the hippocampus is a single compartment; the same molecular handle has different behavioral readouts depending on which longitudinal subdivision is engaged.

Seventh, a distinct adolescent profile for activity and dopamine release in the nucleus accumbens during Pavlovian conditioning, from the Morrison lab at the University of Pittsburgh. Sign tracking — the tendency to approach and engage with reward-predictive cues, conventionally interpreted as transfer of incentive salience from the reward to its predictor — is a behavioral signature linked to addiction vulnerability and impulsivity in adults. Adolescents are paradoxically less likely to sign-track and more likely to goal-track, despite being the population with the heaviest behavioral burden of impulsivity. The group recorded individual neuron activity in the nucleus accumbens of male and female rats during Pavlovian conditioning at both ages, and in a separate cohort measured dopamine release with the genetically encoded sensor GRAB-DA. Cue-evoked nucleus-accumbens activity increased with training in adolescents and continued to develop into adulthood. The majority of adolescents were goal-trackers or intermediates, for whom reward-evoked activity peaked during adolescence and declined into adulthood, correlating with the eventual emergence of sign-tracking. Cue-evoked dopamine release was markedly higher in sign-trackers than goal-trackers at every age. The interpretation is that the adolescent-to-adult transition is a shift in which nucleus-accumbens signals dominate behavior — adolescents track outcomes through reward-evoked activity, adults shift toward cue-evoked dopamine signaling — and the developmental trajectory of that shift may be the mechanism that converts incentive-salience attribution into adult-like reward-seeking. For pediatric and adolescent pharmacology in attention-deficit-hyperactivity disorder and substance-use risk, this points at developmentally distinct dopamine substrates that should not be treated as a single adult-like system.

Eighth, attention-enabled PocketMiner uses attention to simultaneously predict cryptic pockets and their allosteric coupling, from the Bowman lab at the University of Pennsylvania. Cryptic pockets — binding sites that are present only transiently in a protein's conformational ensemble — could dramatically expand the druggable proteome if we could identify them reliably and, more importantly, if we could predict whether they are allosterically coupled to functional sites. PocketMiner, the group's previous graph-neural-network method, identified cryptic sites from single structures. The new version, attention-enabled PocketMiner, replaces a sub-component with a graph attention mechanism that, in a single forward pass, predicts both cryptic-pocket locations and which residues elsewhere in the protein are allosterically coupled to pocket opening. The method outperforms prior cryptic-pocket predictors and reproduces known allosteric interactions. They go further than benchmarking — they experimentally confirm newly predicted cryptic pockets and predicted mutations that allosterically gate pocket opening. The relevance for receptor-targeted drug discovery is direct: many G-protein-coupled-receptor and ion-channel pharmacophores depend on transient conformations or allosteric sites that conventional pocket-finding misses, and a single-shot predictor that returns both the site and its coupling map is the right input shape for downstream design.

Ninth, a perspective from the May issue of Pharmacometrics and Systems Pharmacology — quantitative systems pharmacology modeling amid the rise of agentic artificial intelligence, by James Lu and Rajat Desikan. The piece is short and worth flagging because it threads a needle the field has been ducking. Quantitative systems pharmacology — mechanistic, multi-scale modeling of pharmacokinetics, pharmacodynamics, and biology in a single framework — has long lived under a tension between fidelity, which is what makes a model trustworthy, and timeliness, which is what makes a model actually used in drug-development decisions. The argument is that agentic artificial intelligence collapses that tradeoff in both directions. On one side, autonomous agents can absorb routine modeling tasks — parameter calibration, sensitivity analysis, scenario simulation, report generation — releasing modeler time toward problem framing and validation. On the other side, quantitative-systems-pharmacology models themselves become first-class tools that multi-agent systems can call into for mechanistic reasoning, anchoring the language-model agent's outputs to interpretable biology rather than free-form chain-of-thought. The piece is editorial rather than empirical, so treat it as an organizing framework, but the practical implication is real — the right team structure for the next generation of clinical-pharmacology programs is one where mechanistic models and language-model agents are operationally fused, not run in parallel silos.

Tenth and closing, SpatialClaw — a memory-augmented autonomous ecosystem for spatial-omics analysis, from the Shang group at Northwestern Polytechnical University. Spatial-omics analysis pipelines have proliferated to the point where each lab essentially writes a bespoke chain of incompatible tools, and reproducibility has degraded — the same issue that the bench community spent the late twenty-tens fixing for single-cell analysis. SpatialClaw bundles thirty specialized analysis skills under a single natural-language interface and adds something the existing wave of bioinformatics agents has been missing — a graph-based persistent memory architecture with three hierarchical layers, session, episodic, and semantic, where dataset metadata, analysis lineage, biological insights, and user preferences are stored as versioned nodes and edges, with a deterministic promotion policy moving high-confidence findings up the hierarchy. A memory-augmented reasoning operator sits between the memory store and the planning agent, synthesizing retrieved experiences into task-specific guidance for each new query. Benchmarks across ten spatial-omics skills and three memory-sensitive scenarios show consistent improvement over conventional conversational agents and over a non-memory ablation. This is the same architectural pattern that yesterday's AutoScientists paper made the case for at the multi-agent level — that decentralized memory and explicit lineage are what convert one-shot reasoning into accumulating institutional knowledge — applied here at the single-analyst level. For neuroinformatics work involving spatial transcriptomics of brain tissue, this is the kind of substrate worth watching.

That is the docket for May twenty-ninth. The week closes on the first new-mechanism rapid-acting postpartum-depression contender in years, a Parkinson's antibody story that nicely interlocks with last week's glucocerebrosidase work, and a perspective piece arguing that quantitative systems pharmacology and agentic artificial intelligence have to fuse rather than compete. Heading into the weekend.
