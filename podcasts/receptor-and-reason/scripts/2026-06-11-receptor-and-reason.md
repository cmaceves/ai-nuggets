## Script

Welcome to Receptor and Reason. Thursday, June eleventh, twenty twenty-six. Eleven items today. We open with two pieces of clinical psychiatry pipeline news, then move through bipolar and autism pathophysiology, a dopamine prediction-error story from the fly, two pieces of fairly tight receptor pharmacology, a Parkinson's organoid platform, a new patient-tissue atlas for neurodegeneration, a small-molecule rescue across two very different mitochondrial diseases, and an agentic AI close. Let's start with the most striking clinical readout of the week.

That's Autobahn Therapeutics reporting positive Phase 2 topline data for elunetirom in bipolar depression. Elunetirom is a brain-penetrant CNS thyroid hormone receptor agonist, designed to engage central thyroid hormone receptors without driving the peripheral thyroid axis. The mechanistic premise is that depression involves impaired neuronal bioenergetics, and that selectively boosting mitochondrial function and neuroplasticity in CNS through thyroid hormone receptor agonism can produce a meaningfully different antidepressant effect than monoaminergic drugs. The trial, called AMPLIFY-BD, was an open-label Phase 2 in twenty-one adults with bipolar one or bipolar two disorder in a moderate-to-severe depressive episode, dosed adjunctively for six weeks. Primary endpoint was change in the Hamilton Depression Rating Scale, seventeen-item version. They report a sixteen-point-eight-point reduction at week six, with a P value below zero-point-zero-zero-one. Response rate, defined as at least fifty percent reduction in the Hamilton score, was seventy-five percent. Remission rate, defined as Hamilton score of seven or below, was fifty percent. Onset was rapid: a nine-point-seven-point reduction by week two. Safety profile was mild-to-moderate adverse events with no serious treatment-related events. They also report functional connectivity changes in depression-relevant regions on resting-state fMRI, which is a useful objective bolt-on. The caveats are real. Open-label, no comparator, twenty-one patients. Bipolar depression has historically generated impressive open-label signals that don't survive blinding, and even when they do, the regulatory bar in this indication is high — the FDA wants both depression efficacy and a clean signal on treatment-emergent mania, which a six-week study doesn't capture. But the mechanism is genuinely differentiated from anything else in the bipolar depression pipeline, FDA Fast Track is already granted from last month, and the company has a Phase 2 in adjunctive MDD called AMPLIFY-MDD reading out in Q3. If the BD signal holds, this is a candidate to watch closely.

Staying in clinical psychiatry pipeline news, Psychiatric Times this week ran an interview with Daniel Karlin, the CMO of Definium Therapeutics, on the consequences of the April executive order accelerating FDA action on psychedelics. The headline asset for Definium is DT120 ODT, lysergide tartrate as an orally disintegrating tablet — essentially a pharmaceutical-grade LSD program. They are currently in Phase 2 in major depressive disorder, the Emerge study, with topline readout planned for this quarter, and the company is now expanding into a Phase 3 program in PTSD called Haven on top of an existing GAD program. Karlin's framing in the interview is that the executive order doesn't change Definium's regulatory strategy — they were already engaged with FDA on the design challenges specific to 5-HT2A agonist trials. Those design challenges are the real story here. Blinding when the active drug produces a six-to-twelve-hour clearly identifiable psychedelic experience is essentially impossible. Functional unblinding rates approach one hundred percent. FDA is reportedly issuing guidance on trial design for serotonin 2A agonists later this year, and the practical question for the field is whether that guidance treats functional unblinding as a design constraint to mitigate or as an inherent feature to characterize. For the audience that has been watching the MDMA program collapse on similar issues, the next year of psychedelic clinical work is going to turn substantially on what that guidance contains.

Moving into pathophysiology, a paper from Bruna Panizzutti's group at Deakin University on whole-transcriptome sequencing of iPSC-derived cortical networks from people with bipolar disorder. Twelve participants with BD and twelve unaffected controls. They reprogrammed peripheral blood cells to iPSCs, differentiated to neural progenitors, and matured cortical co-cultures of neurons and astrocytes. Differential expression analysis with gene set enrichment flagged one hundred ninety-one enriched pathways, and the headline finding is consistent downregulation of toll-like receptor signaling in BD cortical networks. The interpretation the authors prefer is profound immune dysregulation as part of the BD signature. The caveats: n equals twelve per group is small, iPSC-derived cortical networks vary widely between lines, and TLR pathway enrichment is a fairly broad readout. But the convergence with the broader literature on innate immune signaling in mood disorders — IL-6, IL-1-beta, NLRP3 inflammasome — is consistent enough that it's worth flagging. The clinical translation question is whether innate immune signatures stratify BD patients for response to existing treatments, which is the kind of pharmacogenomic angle that would give this finding teeth.

Next, an autism paper from Sergio Osorio's group at Massachusetts General Hospital on cortical excitation-inhibition balance across development. The excitation-inhibition imbalance hypothesis for autism is twenty years old at this point, and the empirical evidence has been all over the place. This study uses resting-state MEG in one hundred seventy-two participants — eighty with autism, ninety-two typically developing — across ages six to thirty-two. They compute a functional E/I metric called fEI, derived from critical brain dynamics, across five hundred cortical parcels. The headline result is that fEI is not statically altered in autism. When you split by age, autism participants show elevated fEI in childhood, decreased fEI in adolescence, and no group difference in adulthood. One regional effect survives independent of age: right dorsolateral prefrontal cortex has lower fEI in autism across the full age range. A group-by-age interaction in left inferior parietal cortex shows fEI rising with age in typically developing participants but not in autism participants. The implication is that previous studies of E/I in autism have been mixed largely because they were collapsing across developmental stages where the imbalance flips sign. For pharmacological intervention, the obvious read is that GABAergic or glutamatergic modulators aimed at correcting E/I imbalance need an age stratification, and possibly a regional one, to even be evaluable.

Picking up the dopamine thread, Dhruv Grover's group at UC San Diego dissects multi-timescale learning signals in Drosophila dopaminergic neurons. The PPM3 dopamine neurons, monitored by calcium imaging trial-by-trial during aversive conditioning, show two distinct dynamics on the same cells. The first is classical prediction-error: activity shifts from the unconditioned stimulus to the conditioned stimulus as learning proceeds, suppresses when an expected outcome is omitted, and increases when the outcome exceeds expectations. That's the standard reward-prediction-error signal mammalian midbrain dopamine neurons are famous for. The new finding is a slower state-like dynamic on the same neurons: tonic activity transitions that emerge with learning and track the acquisition of learned behavior. When task demands increase by inserting a temporal gap between cue and outcome — trace conditioning — both fast and slow dynamics shift to later trials, accompanied by delayed behavior acquisition, and the neurons develop an anticipatory response that tracks the expected timing of the outcome during the gap. Why this matters: the dominant model of dopamine has been that prediction error and slower contextual signals live on different neuron populations. Showing that a single dopaminergic neuron type carries both moment-to-moment errors and longer-timescale learning-stabilization signals reopens the question of how dopamine is multiplexed in mammals. For computational psychiatry that uses dopamine prediction error as a target — addiction, schizophrenia, Parkinson's — the slow signal is a layer that current reinforcement learning models of these disorders mostly ignore.

A precision pharmacology piece from Jingqiong Kang's group at Vanderbilt on SLC6A1, the gene encoding the GABA transporter GAT-1. Pathogenic SLC6A1 variants cause developmental and epileptic encephalopathies. The new variant they characterize is a de novo missense, alanine three hundred five to valine, in a patient with myoclonic-atonic epilepsy. Across nine stability-prediction algorithms run on the cryo-EM-derived GAT-1 structure, the variant is uniformly destabilizing. Functionally, GABA uptake is reduced in HEK293T cells and in iPSC-derived astrocytes and cortical neurons. ER colocalization rises from about thirty percent in wildtype to about eighty percent in the variant, consistent with a trafficking defect. Then the pharmacology. Four-phenylbutyrate, an FDA-approved drug used for urea cycle disorders that doubles as a pharmacological chaperone, reduces ER retention to about forty percent and restores total GAT-1 fluorescence toward wildtype. It also restores GABA uptake. Independently, wildtype GAT-1 gene augmentation also improves uptake. The combination of 4-PBA plus gene augmentation produces rescue greater than either alone. So you have a two-axis precision-medicine framework for SLC6A1 DEEs: pharmacochaperone the misfolded protein to get it to the plasma membrane, and augment the gene dose. There are roughly forty distinct SLC6A1 variants in the patient population, the trafficking defect doesn't apply to all of them, but for the trafficking-defective subset this is exactly the kind of mechanism-stratified small-molecule plus gene therapy combination that's going to be the template for the next decade of monogenic CNS disease.

Speaking of precision receptor pharmacology, a methods paper from Alexandre Mourot's group at ESPCI in Paris on photoswitchable nanobodies for alpha-7 nicotinic acetylcholine receptors. The standard tool kit for optical control of receptors has been photochromic small-molecule ligands, which have decent temporal precision but limited subtype specificity, or genetically encoded photoreceptors, which require receptor engineering and a transgenic line. The new approach combines a high-affinity nanobody — single-domain antibody fragment — that selectively binds alpha-7 nicotinic, covalently coupled to an azobenzene photoswitch carrying an acetylcholine analog as the warhead. In Xenopus oocytes, the construct, called MalAzoCh-C4, enables wavelength-controlled activation of recombinant alpha-7. In acute hippocampal slices, it produces robust photocontrol of endogenous alpha-7 receptors on interneurons, sufficient to modulate action potential firing. Why this matters: alpha-7 nicotinic is a long-standing target across schizophrenia cognitive symptoms and Alzheimer's, with a graveyard of compounds that lacked the necessary selectivity. A platform that gets selectivity from the nanobody and temporal control from the photoswitch, without requiring receptor engineering, is one of the more genuinely useful additions to the photopharmacology toolkit in the last few years. The translational path is obviously limited by the need for light delivery, but for the basic-pharmacology question of what alpha-7 specifically does in intact circuits, this is a substantial improvement.

Now to disease modeling. Michela Deleidi's group at the Imagine Institute in Paris reports a midbrain organoid protocol that produces substantially more substantia-nigra-pars-compacta-like dopamine neurons. Existing human midbrain organoid protocols generate dopamine neurons that are mostly ventral tegmental area-like in identity, missing the SNpc-like signature that defines the Parkinson's-vulnerable population. The new protocol combines triphasic WNT modulation with dynamic bioreactor culture and produces organoids enriched in TH-positive, GIRK2-positive, ALDH1A1-positive neurons — the standard SNpc markers — with single-cell transcriptomics confirming emergence of SOX6-positive, GIRK2-positive SNpc-like neurons. Synaptic maturation, electrophysiological activity, and dopamine release are all improved. Critically, when they expose these organoids to alpha-synuclein preformed fibrils, they get aggregate formation and dopamine neuron degeneration. That's the assay that's been hard to recapitulate in earlier midbrain organoid systems — the Parkinson's-relevant vulnerability that current induced-pluripotent dopamine neuron protocols largely fail to model. The translational implication is that drug screens for Parkinson's neuroprotection that have been running on VTA-like dopamine neurons may have systematically missed the SNpc biology. Whether the WNT-and-bioreactor system scales for medium-throughput phenotypic screening is the next question.

Adjacent in neurodegeneration, a platform paper from Crownlands called Gateway, which is a clinical workflow for sampling and profiling olfactory sensory neurons from patients with neurodegenerative disease. The motivation is well known: forty-two percent of Americans over fifty-five will develop dementia, but the living human brain can't be routinely sampled. Olfactory sensory neurons are part of the CNS but accessible through nasal biopsy. They combine device-guided olfactory epithelium biopsy with on-site fixation and 10x Genomics FLEX RNA profiling. The dataset they release is a four-million-cell atlas from two hundred two human donors, healthy controls plus Alzheimer's and Parkinson's patients, on the CELLxGENE platform. The validation argument is that Gateway captures more brain-enriched genes than other clinically accessible tissue sources. In their exploratory disease analyses, they identify dysregulation of neuroinflammation, endolysosomal biology, proteostasis, and synaptic maintenance — all canonical neurodegeneration pathways — in olfactory neurons from patients. The promise is the scale: a clinically accessible neuronal tissue that you can serially biopsy across disease progression, in numbers that conventional brain banks never reach. The caveat is that olfactory sensory neurons are sensory neurons, not the cortical or subcortical populations that bear the brunt of AD and PD pathology, and the disease-relevance argument depends on signaling and transcriptional programs being preserved across these populations. The atlas itself is the contribution worth taking seriously regardless.

A small-molecule rescue piece from Heather Mortiboys's group at the University of Sheffield, looking for convergent mitochondrial phenotypes across primary mitochondrial disease and neurodegeneration. Leigh syndrome is caused by mutations in mitochondrial respiratory chain proteins, with severe pediatric encephalopathy. Huntington's is a CAG-expansion neurodegenerative disease where mitochondrial dysfunction is secondary. Patient fibroblasts from both diseases were profiled with high-content imaging across multiple mitochondrial parameters. Despite the very different underlying causes, both converge on an imbalance between functional and dysfunctional mitochondria — fewer healthy mitochondria, more dysfunctional ones. They screened for compounds that rescue this imbalance in both backgrounds, and identified A769662, an AMPK activator, as effective in both. Interestingly, the rescue runs through different pathways in Leigh syndrome versus Huntington's, but the net mitochondrial-quality outcome converges. For drug discovery, this is the kind of phenotypic screen design that captures convergent biology across genetically distinct CNS diseases, and AMPK activators are an existing pharmacological class with development experience, including direct activators that have moved through clinical phases for metabolic disease. The Huntington's neuroprotection space has accumulated a long list of failed monotherapies, and a mitochondrial-quality-restoration angle backed by a class with known pharmacology is a useful addition.

To close, an agentic AI paper, on arXiv, called OmniBioTwin from Allison and colleagues. The pitch is a system-of-twinned-systems framework for health digital twins. The existing landscape is fragmented: organ-specific or task-specific models that don't compose, and system-level twin frameworks that lack architectural rigor. OmniBioTwin organizes the components as modular computational entities coupled through explicit interaction operators, in a seven-layer architecture spanning data integration, autonomous twin modeling, cross-scale coupling, temporal synchronization, and human-in-the-loop decision support. What makes this relevant to the audience here is the demonstration: a multiscale twin for GLP-1 signaling pathways in Alzheimer's disease, composing molecular, cellular, and organ-level twins within the unified system. GLP-1 agonists in Alzheimer's are an active clinical question — Novo Nordisk's evoke trials for semaglutide read out negative on cognition last year, but the metabolic-CNS interface story isn't closed. A digital-twin framework that can integrate molecular pharmacology with population-scale clinical data is a class of tool that, if it works, is going to shape how the next round of GLP-1-in-CNS trials are designed and interpreted. The paper is more architectural than empirical at this stage. The interesting question, like with most agentic framework papers, is whether the system is portable to a problem the authors didn't pre-select to look good on. That said, it's a serious attempt at the cross-scale composition problem, and the GLP-1-in-AD instantiation is well chosen.
