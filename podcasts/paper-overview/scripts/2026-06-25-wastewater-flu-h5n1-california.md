## Script

Welcome to Paper Overview. Today we're walking through a recent medRxiv preprint titled "Genomic wastewater surveillance of human and animal influenza A viruses in California during the 2024 to 2025 flu season." It's a collaboration anchored at the University of California, Berkeley, with the California Department of Public Health, the Los Angeles County Sanitation Districts, and Lawrence Livermore National Lab. It was posted in mid-June 2026.

Here's the headline up front. The authors built a wastewater surveillance pipeline that can recover the whole genome of influenza A virus from sewage, across all eight viral segments and across many different subtypes simultaneously — including the seasonal human strains and the H5N1 bird flu that's been spreading in U.S. dairy cattle. They sampled three California wastewater treatment plants weekly for an entire flu season, sequenced the influenza they captured, and compared what showed up in sewage to what showed up in clinical testing. The big finding: wastewater consistently detected H5N1 across all three sites all season long, while clinical detections of H5N1 in people were sporadic and rare. And second, they built a new analysis algorithm that can take a soup of mixed influenza subtypes in one wastewater sample and untangle the relative proportions of each clade — something existing tools could not do.

Let me back up and lay out the background, because the framing matters here.

Influenza A virus is the version of flu we worry about for pandemics. It has a segmented genome — eight separate pieces of RNA that code for different viral proteins. Two of those segments code for the surface proteins that the immune system recognizes: hemagglutinin, which is the H in names like H5N1, and neuraminidase, which is the N. The numbers refer to different versions of those proteins. So H1N1, H3N2, and H5N1 are all influenza A, but with different surface proteins, and they tend to circulate in different host species. H1N1 and H3N2 are the seasonal human flu strains. H5N1 is primarily a bird flu.

In March of 2024, H5N1 — specifically a clade called two point three point four point four B — was reported for the first time in U.S. dairy cattle. That was a surprising jump. By the second half of 2024, California in particular had a huge outbreak in dairy herds, and the state accounted for more than half of the human H5N1 cases in the United States at the time. None of those cases led to sustained human-to-human transmission, but the situation is exactly the kind of cross-species spillover that public health surveillance is supposed to catch early.

The question is: how do you watch for it? Clinical surveillance — testing sick people and sequencing the virus — is the obvious answer, but it's limited. Most people with mild flu never get tested, and even fewer get sequenced. And clinical sampling won't see what's happening in cows or birds.

Wastewater surveillance is the alternative. People shed influenza virus in respiratory secretions and feces, and so do dairy cattle, and so do birds whose waste enters water systems. So a sewage sample at a treatment plant integrates infections across a whole sewershed — the population that drains into that plant — without needing anyone to walk into a clinic.

The catch is that getting useful information out of wastewater is hard. The CDC's existing wastewater system uses PCR to detect the influenza M gene — that's the matrix protein gene, one of the conserved internal segments. PCR will tell you that there's influenza A in the water, and roughly how much, but it can't tell you which subtype, which clade, or whether it came from a human or a cow. To get that, you need to sequence the genome.

And sequencing influenza from wastewater is itself difficult, for two reasons. First, the virus is at very low concentrations relative to all the other genetic material floating around in sewage, so you have to enrich for it before sequencing. There are a few ways to do that: tiled amplicons, universal amplicons, or probe capture. Probe capture uses synthetic DNA baits that hybridize to the influenza genome and pull it out — and unlike amplicon methods, it tolerates sequence mismatches well, which matters when the virus is degraded or when you're trying to catch a novel variant. The downside has been that commercial probe panels target many viruses at once, which dilutes the influenza signal.

Second, even once you have sequence data, the analysis is hard. A single wastewater sample can contain multiple subtypes of influenza mixed together — H1N1 and H3N2 and possibly H5N1 all at once. You need a tool that can take those mixed reads and figure out the proportions of each clade. The available tools, like Freyja and LolliPop, were built for SARS-CoV-2 — which has a single, unsegmented genome — and they generally can only handle one influenza subtype at a time when applied to flu.

So this paper sets out to solve both problems. They use a custom probe capture panel designed specifically for influenza A, targeting eleven different subtypes that span human, avian, dairy cow, swine, and other mammalian hosts. And they adapt an expectation maximization algorithm — a statistical method for inferring the most likely mixture of components given the observed reads — to handle the segmented genome and multiple subtypes simultaneously.

Now let's walk through what they actually did.

They picked three wastewater treatment plants in California. One in East Bakersfield in the Central Valley, one in Central Contra Costa County in the Bay Area, and one in Los Angeles County. Different population sizes, different geographies, but all in California. They sampled each plant weekly from November 2024 through February 2025 — basically a full flu season.

For each sample, they ran two parallel workflows. One was PCR-based quantification — they measured the total influenza A concentration using the M gene, and a separate assay specifically targeting the H5 gene. That gave them an overall picture of how much flu was in the water and how much of it was H5. The other workflow was sequencing. They prepared sequencing libraries, applied the influenza-specific probe enrichment to pull out viral reads, and sequenced deeply on an Illumina NovaSeq.

To validate the method, they did two controls. First, they took one wastewater sample and processed it both with and without probe enrichment. Without enrichment, they got zero influenza reads. With enrichment, they got nearly six thousand. So the probe panel works. Second, they made a synthetic mixture of three influenza subtypes — H5N1, H3N2, and H1N1 — at a known ratio of fifty, thirty, and twenty percent, and ran it through the whole pipeline. The pipeline recovered the proportions accurately, deviating by less than one percent from the expected mixture.

They also benchmarked their EM algorithm against an existing tool called EsViritu, which is a more general viral metagenomics tool that can take multiple references. When both tools had access to the whole influenza genome, both were accurate. But when forced to use only the M gene — the conserved internal segment that PCR-based assays target — EsViritu became badly biased, overestimating H5N1 to more than ninety-six percent of reads. Their EM algorithm stayed accurate. That bias matters for real data, where coverage is often patchy and you may not have good information across the whole genome.

Once they had a working pipeline, they compared what they found in wastewater to what was found in clinical sequencing. The clinical data came from samples that the California Department of Public Health had sequenced and deposited in GISAID, the public influenza database, and they pulled clinical test positivity rates from the state's respiratory virus dashboard.

Here's what the comparison showed.

For the seasonal human subtypes — H1N1 and H3N2 — wastewater and clinical data agreed well. H1N1 came in two co-circulating subclades, both within the broader pandemic 2009 H1N1 lineage, and the proportions of those subclades in wastewater tracked the proportions in clinical sequences. H3N2 was almost entirely one subclade — somewhere between ninety-nine and a hundred percent of reads — both in wastewater and in clinical data. So when human seasonal flu dominated the signal, the wastewater story matched the clinical story.

The H5N1 picture was different and more interesting. H5N1 clade two point three point four point four B — the dairy cattle clade — was consistently detected in wastewater across all three treatment plants throughout the whole study period. In clinical samples, by contrast, H5N1 detections were sporadic and rare. Just a handful of cases, scattered through the season.

The wastewater also picked up something the clinical data missed entirely. The Los Angeles plant showed an early peak in influenza concentration in mid-November 2024 — a peak that the PCR data showed was driven by H5N1, not by seasonal flu. There was no corresponding peak in clinical test positivity. Whatever was driving that H5N1 signal at LA County in mid-November simply wasn't showing up in sick people's clinical samples. A similar but smaller H5N1-driven divergence happened at the Bakersfield plant in late December. By contrast, when wastewater peaks at the Contra Costa plant were driven by seasonal human flu rather than H5N1, they aligned cleanly with the clinical peaks.

The authors then asked: what's the source of the H5N1 in the wastewater? Is it coming from people, from dairy runoff, from birds? To get at that, they built a maximum likelihood phylogenetic tree using over two thousand H5N1 sequences from GISAID, collected from humans, avian hosts, dairy cows, and swine between late 2022 and late 2025. They then placed the wastewater and clinical H5N1 sequences on that tree.

The wastewater H5N1 sequences mostly clustered with reference sequences from dairy cattle infections, with a smaller subset clustering with avian strains, particularly at the Los Angeles and Bakersfield plants. All nine of the human clinical H5N1 sequences from California clustered with dairy cow strains, consistent with the known cattle-to-human spillover.

That said, the authors are careful about this. Because the H5N1 clade two point three four four B sequences are so similar across host species — only a few nucleotide differences separate cattle-derived from avian-derived virus — you can't definitively say from a wastewater read which host the virus came from. Especially when wastewater coverage doesn't span the specific signature regions that distinguish hosts.

They also did a variant analysis, looking for amino acid substitutions in the viral protein-coding regions. The most important thing they were looking for is mutations associated with mammalian adaptation — changes that would suggest H5N1 is getting better at replicating in humans. The classic one is a substitution called E six two seven K in the polymerase basic two segment, which is associated with improved mammalian transmission. They did not find that mutation in any of their wastewater samples. They did see other amino acid substitutions across various segments, including more substitutions in neuraminidase and the polymerase basic two segment for H5N1, consistent with selective pressure during host switching, but nothing that signaled an alarming adaptation event.

One technical caveat is worth flagging because the authors are very honest about it. During the study, they discovered that some of their sequencing reads showed contamination from a previous experiment — specifically from hemagglutinin tiled amplicons that had been used in earlier work in the lab. They could see the contamination because the read coverage pattern on the HA segment had the wrong shape — a tiled amplicon pattern rather than the smoother probe capture pattern — and the HA read count was anomalously high. They couldn't reliably clean it up, so they made the decision to drop the HA segment entirely from their analysis and work with the remaining seven segments. This is actually a nice illustration of the advantage of their EM approach: because the algorithm doesn't require any single segment, it can still estimate clade composition from whatever genome you have. They essentially turned a contamination problem into a stress test, and the method passed.

So what does this all mean?

The big-picture significance is that wastewater sequencing can complement clinical surveillance in ways that PCR-based wastewater monitoring cannot. PCR tells you that flu is in the water; sequencing tells you which flu. That distinction matters enormously when an animal-origin influenza is circulating alongside seasonal human flu. The Los Angeles November peak is the cleanest demonstration in this paper — a substantial wastewater signal that clinical data did not catch and that PCR alone would have been misinterpreted as a regular seasonal wave.

For pandemic preparedness, the value proposition is being able to track novel or animal-associated influenza in places where active animal surveillance is sparse, and to watch for mammalian adaptation mutations as they arise. It's not a replacement for clinical sequencing, but it fills in gaps that clinical sampling will always have.

The methodological contribution is the analysis pipeline — the influenza-specific probe panel and the EM-based deconvolution that can handle multiple subtypes and incomplete genomes simultaneously. Existing tools couldn't do this, and that gap was a real bottleneck for using wastewater to monitor segmented viruses.

The honest limitations are several. Coverage is still patchy at low viral concentrations, and the genome regions you most want — the antigenic surface proteins — are also the regions most prone to sequencing dropout and, in this case, to laboratory contamination. The sequence similarity between cattle and avian H5N1 means you cannot definitively assign a host to a wastewater detection. There aren't enough human H5N1 clinical samples from the same sewersheds to do a really clean comparison. And the proportions the EM algorithm reports need further validation against orthogonal quantitative methods like RT-PCR.

But the framework is in place. The authors have shown that whole-genome influenza sequencing from wastewater, with subtype and clade resolution across multiple co-circulating strains, is feasible at scale during a real flu season — including one where a novel animal-origin influenza was on the move. That's a meaningful step forward for the kind of biosurveillance public health agencies will need as zoonotic threats become more frequent.

That's the paper. Thanks for listening.
