# The 17% Problem — Stanford's AI Index 2026 Lands a Sobering Bioinformatics Reality Check

Paper link: https://hai.stanford.edu/ai-index/2026-ai-index-report
Coverage: https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01199-z

## Summary

Stanford HAI dropped the 2026 AI Index Report last week, and tucked inside the new science chapter is the most calibrated thing anyone has published about agents in biology this year. On SWE-bench Verified, coding agents went from 60% to nearly 100% in twelve months. On Terminal-Bench, real-world task success rate jumped from 20% to 77.3%. And on BixBench — Future House's benchmark of real-world bioinformatics analyses with actual data and actual notebooks — frontier models score 17% in the open-answer setting, no better than random in multiple choice. PaperArena, the new tool-augmented scientific reasoning benchmark, lands the best multi-agent system at 38.8% versus 83.5% for a PhD expert. The "jagged frontier" framing the report uses is doing a lot of work: agents look superhuman on benchmarks designed by AI engineers, and remedial on benchmarks designed by working scientists.

## Script

The Stanford AI Index Report 2026 just landed. Four hundred and twenty three pages, and most of the press coverage is going to the same things every year — investment is up, benchmark scores are up, regulation is lagging. But the part of the report worth your attention if you do computational biology is the brand-new science chapter, and the headline finding inside it is the kind of number that should reset the conversation about agentic AI in our field.

Here's the setup. Over the last twelve months, every general-purpose AI agent benchmark you can name has been on a tear. SWE-bench Verified, the standard test of whether a coding agent can resolve real GitHub issues, went from sixty percent to nearly a hundred. Terminal-Bench, which measures real-world task success in a shell environment, jumped from twenty percent to seventy-seven point three. OSWorld, the desktop computer-use benchmark, climbed from twelve percent to sixty-six. The cybersecurity agents went from fifteen percent to ninety-three on standard CTF-style tasks in two years. If you read only those numbers, you would walk away convinced that we are months, not years, away from agents doing your job, my job, and probably your PI's job.

Now read the science chapter.

On BixBench — and BixBench is the relevant one if you actually work in bioinformatics — frontier models score seventeen percent. BixBench is the benchmark Future House and Science Machine put out a year ago. It contains roughly fifty real-world scenarios drawn from actual published bioinformatics analyses, with the original Jupyter notebooks, the original data, and around two hundred open-answer questions about what those analyses found. The agent gets a docker container preloaded with normal bioinformatics packages, an empty notebook, and the data. It has to reason its way to the answer the way you or I would. GPT-4o and Claude 3.5 Sonnet — the frontier models tested at the time — landed at seventeen percent open-answer accuracy. In the multiple-choice version of the same benchmark, no better than random. Read that again. On real bioinformatics analyses, multiple choice, frontier agents are no better than random.

On PaperArena, a benchmark released last fall that tests tool-augmented agentic reasoning across multiple papers — exactly the kind of task you'd want an AI assistant for if you were doing a literature review for a grant — Gemini 2.5 Pro running in a multi-agent configuration tops out at thirty-eight point eight percent. The PhD expert baseline is eighty-three point five. That's a forty-five point gap, on a benchmark explicitly designed to evaluate the tasks agents are being marketed for.

ReplicationBench, which tests whether agents can replicate full astrophysics papers, sits below twenty percent. UnivEarth, an earth observation benchmark, has agent-generated code failing fifty-eight percent of the time.

Now, ChemBench is the one chemistry-flavored exception — frontier models there exceed average human expert performance on multiple-choice questions across twenty-seven hundred chemistry items, while still flubbing tasks a chemistry undergraduate would nail. So the picture isn't "AI agents are bad at science" — it's that the gap between performance on synthetic, well-scoped benchmarks and performance on real scientific work is vast, domain-dependent, and nowhere more vast than in biology.

The Stanford report has a phrase for this. They call it the jagged frontier. AI excels at some scientific tasks, performs at random on others, and the boundary between those two regions is not where intuition would put it. ChemBench works. BixBench doesn't. SWE-bench works. PaperArena doesn't. The benchmark is not the science, and it turns out that designing a benchmark that captures the actual labor of doing biology — picking the right covariates, knowing which QC failures matter, distinguishing a real signal from a batch effect, recognizing that this differential expression result is contaminated by the cell cycle — is itself a research problem. We don't have one yet. BixBench is the closest thing, and the score is seventeen.

Here's what I take away as someone who builds and maintains biomedical infrastructure for a living. First, be appropriately skeptical of agentic biology demos. If a paper claims its agent solved a biology problem and the evaluation is on a curated, derisked, well-scoped task, the BixBench number is what you should mentally compare against. The honest comparison is "does this work on real notebooks with real data" not "does this work on the cleaned-up subset of tasks that fit in a paper."

Second, the things that have to get better are not, primarily, model weights. The bottleneck on BixBench isn't reasoning — it's the long-context, multi-step, noisy data manipulation that bioinformatics actually consists of. That's why the success rate on coding benchmarks doesn't transfer. SWE-bench is well-defined: the test passes or it doesn't. BixBench has no test. The agent has to know what the right answer would even look like, and that's a domain skill, not a coding skill.

Third — and this is the part that matters most for anyone whose work intersects with knowledge graphs, ontologies, or curated biomedical data — the current weakness of agents on real bioinformatics analysis is exactly the gap that better-structured biomedical resources can close. If the agent has to reason from scratch about what tissue this gene is expressed in, what pathway it's in, what its synonyms are, what the orthologs look like, it will be wrong. If it can look those up in a structured, deterministic, well-curated resource, it has a chance. Every percentage point on BixBench that comes from "the agent got the right answer because it queried a clean external knowledge source" is a percentage point that argues for the value of the boring, unsexy, well-maintained data infrastructure that holds up the entire field.

The headline of the Stanford report this year is going to be "agents are racing toward human performance." That's true on the benchmarks AI engineers built. The footnote, which is much more interesting and much more important if you actually work in this domain, is that the closer the benchmark gets to real biology, the larger the gap. Seventeen percent on BixBench. Thirty-eight on PaperArena versus eighty-three for a human PhD. That is the calibration check, and the calibration check is sobering.

Two papers worth reading directly. BixBench, by the Future House team, on arXiv from March 2025. PaperArena, October 2025, also on arXiv. And the Stanford AI Index 2026 itself is free at hai.stanford.edu, with the science chapter being the part nobody's talking about and everybody in computational biology should read.
