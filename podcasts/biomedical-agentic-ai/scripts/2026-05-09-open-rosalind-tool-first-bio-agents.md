# 2026-05-09 — Open-Rosalind: tool-first biomedical agents and the case for auditability

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.05.06.722404v1

## Script

Welcome back. This is a second nugget for Saturday, May 9, 2026 — a complement to this morning's MP2D piece on inference-time steering. We're staying with agentic AI but pivoting from protein design back to the broader question of what a biomedical agent should look like.

The paper went up on bioRxiv yesterday, May 8th. It's called "Open-Rosalind: Tool-First Biomedical LLM Agents with Process-Aware Benchmarking," and it's a single-author preprint by Liang Wang at Huazhong University of Science and Technology. The phrase to pause on is "process-aware benchmarking" — that's the load-bearing one. The argument is that the way we currently grade biomedical agents — task accuracy on a fixed benchmark — actively rewards behaviors we should be punishing, and that grading needs to look at the process, not just the answer.

Here's the thesis. Large language models are increasingly used as scientific agents, and the flexibility that makes them attractive in general-purpose settings actively conflicts with what biomedical research demands, which is accountability. The paper proposes four operational principles for a biomedical agent. First, evidence-grounded outputs. Second, complete execution traces. Third, workflow-constrained execution rather than free-form reasoning. Fourth, explicit tool mediation for factual claims — meaning the agent does not retrieve a fact from its weights when there is a tool that would surface the same fact with a citation. The implementation is called Open-Rosalind, and the matching benchmark is called Open-Rosalind BioBench, which scores not just whether the answer is right but whether the tool calls were correct, whether citations are present, whether the trace is complete, and how often the run fails outright.

The numbers are where this paper gets honest in a way most agent papers do not. On the in-house benchmark, the reference pipeline hits 81.4 percent accuracy with complete traces — a respectable headline number. The author then runs the obvious ablation: strip out the tool layer and force the language model to answer from its weights. Accuracy drops by nineteen to twenty-six percentage points depending on the model. So far, expected. But then comes a thirty-task author-independent hold-out, and the deployment model crashes to 17.8 percent. That is severe external-validity collapse, and most agent papers either do not run that experiment or quietly bury it. Wang reports it, diagnoses five specific routing and normalization failures, applies targeted fixes, and gets the hold-out back to 53.3 percent — at which point the scariest finding, that the no-tool baseline was beating the agent, also disappears. The paper closes by explicitly framing itself not as a claim that protocol constraints guarantee good performance, but as an empirical study of what auditable agents actually look like under stress.

Three things worth taking from this. First, the philosophical point matches what BioThings Explorer has been doing for years — flexibility is a liability when answers feed into downstream decisions, and structured workflows that produce auditable traces are not a step backwards from agentic reasoning, they are what it should look like in this domain. Second, the ablation result — that pulling tools costs you twenty points of accuracy — is now the third or fourth paper in two months reaching the same conclusion from different angles, and at this point it has converged into a stable empirical claim. Tool-mediated retrieval beats parametric retrieval for biomedical work, and the size of the gap does not depend much on which frontier model you pick. Third, the hold-out collapse is the part everyone in this space should be reading. An eighty-one percent in-house score and an eighteen percent hold-out score is exactly the gap that destroys deployment when nobody runs a hold-out. The diagnose-and-fix loop the paper documents is more useful than the headline accuracy.

One thing the paper is not. It is not a multi-agent system, it is not a co-scientist, it is not trying to autonomously generate hypotheses. The whole pitch is that we should stop conflating those ambitions with what is actually needed in biomedical pipelines, which is a tool-first, trace-complete, citation-anchored agent that does narrow things reliably. Single author, bioRxiv preprint, no funder listed — it reads like someone wrote the paper they wished existed. Link in the show notes if you want to see how the BioBench is structured.
