# 2026-05-08 — Bota Bioscience BPL: compiler-verified protocols and a 30-billion-parameter LLM in a closed loop

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.05.05.720956v1

## Script

Welcome back to Biomedical Agentic AI for Friday, May 8, 2026 — this is the second nugget of the day. The morning episode, in case you missed it, was the Cedars-Sinai robotic proteomics paper from Jesse Meyer's group, where the agent sits on the analysis side of the loop. Today's afternoon pick is the architectural complement: the same kind of system, but on the execution side. The paper is "Towards autonomous biology: Compiler-Verified Protocols as a Foundation for Real World AI Execution," from Chang-Ting Chen's group at Bota Bioscience, posted on bioRxiv yesterday.

Here's the central observation, and it's an embarrassingly good one. Every other engineering discipline — software, hardware, civil — abandoned ambiguous natural-language specifications decades ago. You don't ship a circuit by typing "wire the resistor near the capacitor" into an email. You write a netlist that a compiler can check. Biology never made that move. Biology ships protocols as natural-language paragraphs in supplementary methods sections, where "vortex briefly" and "let stand at room temperature" mean whatever the postdoc on the day decides they mean. That's why protocols don't reproduce, why robots can't execute Methods sections, and why the agentic-AI dream of an LLM that hands a finished plan to a wet lab keeps stubbing its toe — the format the LLM has to write into is a format that can't be checked.

What Bota built is two things, and the pairing is the point. The first is BPL, the Biology Protocol Language. It's a domain-specific language with what they call a biology-native type system. Every quantity carries physical units, so you can't accidentally pipette milligrams into a volume. Every reagent declares its physical form — solid, liquid, suspension. Every container maintains compiler-tracked state, so the compiler knows that after step three the tube is at four degrees and contains buffer plus enzyme, and step four trying to add a heat-labile reagent into a still-warm vessel is a compile-time error rather than a Tuesday-afternoon mystery. Implicit assumptions are forced to be explicit; physically impossible operations are rejected before anyone touches a pipette. If you've spent any time staring at a published Methods section trying to reverse-engineer what the authors actually meant by "appropriate volume," you know how big a deal this is in principle. The hard part has always been getting from prose to the typed representation — and that's where the second piece comes in.

The second piece is BPL-COGEN, which is the agentic loop. They take a thirty-billion-parameter language model, fine-tune it, and put it inside a generate-validate-repair loop with the BPL compiler. The model reads a natural-language standard operating procedure, emits BPL, the compiler checks it, throws diagnostics on every type error and dimensional mismatch and impossible state transition, and the model rewrites until the compiler is happy. This is exactly the architectural pattern the field has been converging on for biomedical agents this year — a powerful but unreliable language model paired with a deterministic verifier that won't let bad output through. It's the same shape as the schema-constrained metadata agent from BMIR a couple of weeks back, the same shape as PNNL's PTM pipeline last week, the same shape as the multi-agent spatial transcriptomics paper from HKUST. The pattern is clearly working, and Bota's contribution is to apply it to the protocol-authoring problem.

The headline number — and this is the part I want you to take with appropriate skepticism — is a fidelity score of ninety-five point one percent against three hundred published Nature Protocols papers, where the source protocols are taken as ground truth. That is a striking number for a benchmark that hard. The skepticism caveat is that "fidelity to the source protocol" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. A high fidelity score against a written protocol is not the same as a high fidelity score against the experiment the authors actually ran in their lab — published Methods are themselves a translation, and often a lossy one. So the right reading is: the LLM-plus-compiler pipeline can faithfully encode what the paper says, which is necessary but not sufficient.

What rescues the paper from being purely a formalism exercise is the wet-lab piece. They actually ran two of their compiler-verified protocols. The first was a GFP expression library construction; the second was an HPLC-to-UHPLC method translation. In both cases, the same BPL source ran reproducibly across manual execution and liquid-handler-assisted execution. That cross-platform portability claim is what makes this more than a pretty type system — it's the demonstration that compiler-verified protocols actually carry the information needed for a robot and a human to do the same thing.

Where I land on this, for an Andrew-Su-listening-on-the-drive-home audience: the BPL-plus-LLM stack is the cleanest expression I've seen this year of where agentic biology is actually going. The agent doesn't write English. The agent writes into a structured language, and a non-AI compiler is the thing that decides whether the output is good. That is exactly the architectural commitment that knowledge-graph and ontology people have been arguing for, transposed into the protocol world. It is also, candidly, a research vehicle from a company — Bota Bioscience — and I'd want to see the BPL specification, the fine-tuning recipe, and the compiler open-sourced before I'd start building on top of it. But as a statement of where the field is heading, it is the right idea, executed end to end, with a wet-lab demo and a benchmark to anchor it. That's the bar.
