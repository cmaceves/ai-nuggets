# AutoScientists — self-organizing AI agent teams for long-running biomedical experimentation

Paper link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.28655

## Script

Today's nugget is an arXiv preprint called AutoScientists, and it's the most substantive piece of agentic AI architecture work I've seen this month aimed squarely at scientific discovery — including a chunk of explicitly biomedical evaluation. The headline contribution is a design decision, not a model: a decentralized team of AI agents for long running computational experimentation, with no central planner driving the search. And the headline result is that this design measurably beats the strongest prior AI agents across biomedical machine learning, language model training optimization, and protein fitness prediction, on matched compute budgets.

Let me unpack why the architectural choice is the interesting part, before I get to the numbers.

Most of the AI scientist systems we've talked about on this show — and there have been a lot of them this year — share a structural pattern. There's a central planner, often an L L M, that maintains a tree of hypotheses and parcels out work to sub agents that go off and run experiments, come back with results, and the planner updates its plan. That works fine for short horizon problems, the kind that resolve in one or two iterations. It struggles badly in the regime that scientific research actually lives in: long horizon, parallel exploration where most branches fail and the failures are themselves load bearing information. The classic failure modes are well known. The central planner forgets why it ruled out a direction three days ago. Sub agents redo the same dead end work because the shared state isn't propagating. And the system can't sustain genuine parallel exploration because every branch has to go through the planner's bottleneck.

AutoScientists rebuilds the loop around four mechanisms that all push in the other direction. First, there's a shared experimental state that all agents read from and write to — so when one agent finds out that a particular feature transformation doesn't help, that fact becomes visible to everyone. Second, the agents self organize into teams around promising hypotheses rather than being assigned. Third, before any agent spends experimental compute on a proposal, other agents critique it. The critique step matters because compute is the real budget constraint, and a lot of agent failures upstream are catchable by another agent reading the proposal carefully. And fourth, agents broadcast both successes and failures into the shared state, so failed directions persist as institutional memory rather than getting lost.

Now the numbers. On Bio M L dash Bench, which is a twenty four task benchmark spanning biomedical imaging, protein engineering, single cell omics, and drug discovery, AutoScientists hits a mean leaderboard percentile of seventy four point four percent, beating the strongest prior AI agent by eight point three three percentage points. That's a meaningful jump in a setting where the baselines are themselves fairly capable G P T five and Gemini three based agents. On G P T training optimization, which is a totally different domain from biomedicine but tests the long horizon claim cleanly, AutoScientists reaches the same target validation loss as the prior best system, Autoresearch, in roughly half the wall clock, and — this is the more striking number — continues to find improvements from a strong starting checkpoint where a single agent system finds zero. Seven accepted improvements versus zero. That's the long running, parallel exploration regime paying off.

The Protein Gym result is the one I'd flag for the biomedical audience specifically. They use the system to discover a method for A C E 2 spike binding fitness prediction that beats the current state of the art by twelve point five percent in Spearman correlation. Then — and this is the test that actually matters for whether the agent has found something general — they apply the exact same method, without modification, to all two hundred seventeen Protein Gym assays. It still improves over the prior state of the art by six and a half percent. That's a generalization claim, not a hill climbing artifact.

A few caveats worth being honest about. The benchmark wins are all on tasks where the ground truth is reasonably checkable — leaderboard scores, validation loss, Spearman correlations against held out fitness measurements. That's the regime where agentic systems can self correct because they can see whether they're improving. The harder regime, where the experimental signal is noisy, expensive to acquire, or only weakly tied to the hypothesis under test — wet lab biology, in other words — is not what this paper evaluates. Whether self organizing agent teams hold up there is the open question, and probably the question that determines whether this architectural pattern is the future of autonomous biomedical research or just a clean win on a particular class of dry lab benchmarks.

The broader point, and the reason I'm picking this paper, is that the field of agentic AI for science is finally moving past the "wrap an L L M in a planner" template that defined the last eighteen months. Decentralized, critique driven, shared memory architectures are a real architectural shift, and AutoScientists is the first paper I've read that shows that shift paying off across multiple biomedical task families with the same code.

That's the nugget. Back tomorrow.
