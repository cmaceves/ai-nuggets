## Summary

Geraint Rees and James Wilsdon published a Comment in Nature on April 27 that's the cleanest data-backed argument so far that agentic AI is about to break peer-reviewed grant funding. They pulled application volumes from twelve multidisciplinary funders — Wellcome, the ERC, the Australian Research Council, the British Academy, Marie Skłodowska-Curie, others — and the trajectory since ChatGPT shipped is unambiguous. Total volume is up four percent in 2023 versus 2022, seventeen percent in 2024, and fifty-seven percent in 2025. The Marie Skłodowska-Curie postdoctoral fellowship is up one hundred and forty-two percent. And quality is up too — only five percent of those Marie Curie applications fell below the European Commission threshold in 2025, versus twenty percent in 2018. Their key conceptual claim is the line "an LLM can improve craft, but an agent optimizes for outcomes" — once researchers run agents trained on previously funded grants against the funder's brief, panels are no longer evaluating ideas, they're evaluating how well each side's agents have learned to mimic what funders rewarded last cycle.

## Script

I covered the Stanford AI Index report this morning. The benchmarks chapter, the agents-versus-real-bioinformatics gap, BixBench at seventeen percent. That's one face of the agentic AI moment. Now here's the other one, and it's the one that sits closer to what every PI in this audience actually does on a Tuesday afternoon.

A Nature Comment dropped on April twenty-seventh. Authors, Geraint Rees, vice-provost of research at University College London, and James Wilsdon, who runs the Research on Research Institute. The title is "Could agentic AI topple grant-funding systems?" The answer, with data, is yes, possibly soon. And the data is the part that's worth your time.

Rees and Wilsdon pulled application volumes from twelve multidisciplinary funders. Wellcome. The European Research Council. The Australian Research Council. The British Academy. Marie Skłodowska-Curie. Funders across Australia, Belgium, Canada, China, Spain, the UK, and the EU. Hundreds of thousands of applications.

Versus the 2022 baseline, before ChatGPT shipped, application volumes are up four percent in twenty-twenty-three. Up seventeen percent in twenty-twenty-four. Up fifty-seven percent in twenty-twenty-five. The European Marie Skłodowska-Curie postdoctoral fellowship volume is up one hundred and forty-two percent. The British Academy postdoctoral application is up fourteen percent. Every single funder they looked at was up.

That alone is suggestive. Some of it is funding-environment shifts, sure — fewer NIH dollars chasing more applicants, that kind of thing. But the second number is the smoking one. On Marie Skłodowska-Curie postdoc applications, the share of submissions that fell below the European Commission's quality threshold dropped from twenty percent in twenty-eighteen to five percent in twenty-twenty-five. Volume is way up, and the bar is getting cleared at a much higher rate. Reviewers are looking at floods of high-quality proposals and being asked to pick winners that increasingly look indistinguishable.

Other corroborating numbers in the Comment. Elsevier ran a survey of three thousand two hundred and thirty-four researchers across one hundred and thirteen countries in twenty-twenty-five. Fifty-eight percent reported using AI tools in their work, up from thirty-seven percent the year before. Forty-one percent of those used AI specifically to draft grant proposals. A separate analysis of LLM-suggestive text patterns in computer-science abstracts hit twenty-two percent of sentences in twenty-twenty-four. A preprint study of NIH and NSF grants since twenty-twenty-three saw a ten-to-fifteen percent upswing in similar patterns. The Frontiers survey on peer review found more than half of researchers using AI to help review papers, frequently against funder guidance.

Now here's the conceptual move that makes this Comment matter. Rees and Wilsdon write — and this is the line — "an LLM can improve craft, but an agent optimizes for outcomes." That's not a small distinction. An LLM polishes a draft you wrote. An agent, given a goal — write the strongest possible application for this specific call — searches the web for the funder's previously funded grants, reads them, learns the rhetorical and structural patterns, ingests your CV and your published papers, plans a sequence of drafting steps, executes them, evaluates against the funder's brief, iterates, and submits. The proposal that emerges is not your argument shaped by AI. It is a fully AI-generated proposal optimized to a brief, with you in the credit field.

Then run the same logic on the reviewer side, because Rees and Wilsdon are explicit about this — agents read and review the proposal too. If both proposals and reviews are mediated by agents trained on the same body of previously funded work, what the funding panel is actually evaluating is no longer the quality of an idea. It's how well the applicant's agent has simulated the patterns of past winners. That's a closed loop that converges to a horrible local optimum.

The current response from funders, and Rees and Wilsdon dismantle this in two paragraphs, is bans. The NIH announced in July twenty-twenty-five that applications "substantially developed" by AI are ineligible. UKRI prohibits reviewers from uploading proposals to GenAI tools, citing confidentiality. The authors point out, correctly, that bans are unenforceable, that the incentive to use AI is high, that the probability of detection is low, and that prohibition disadvantages non-native English speakers. Bans don't work.

So what they propose. Three categories. First, shift the emphasis of evaluation away from the written proposal toward the principal investigator, the team, and their track record. Bring back interviews — and they note the UK Medical Research Council announced in March it's reinstating interviews for all shortlisted grant applicants, which they read as a counter-AI move. Portfolio assessments. Track-record verification over a sustained period. The trade-off is well-known — these methods amplify the Matthew effect, they advantage incumbents, they punish early-career researchers and underrepresented groups, and they're expensive. So they recommend ring-fencing for early-career and anonymization at the first round.

Second, demand management. The NIH already capped per-PI submissions at six per year after finding that some researchers were submitting more than forty in a single round. Forty. Larger institutional or network grants instead of individual ones. More radical proposals — a lifetime ration of grant submissions, dividing funding pots equally across eligible researchers — which they note are politically infeasible.

Third, and this is the part that should make every computational biologist sit up. They call it fight fire with fire. Build AI-native funding systems. Profile applicants on multiple structured dimensions — publication impact, collaborative reach, methodological diversity, career trajectory. Use AI shortlisting to compare candidates against the dimensions of their record, not just the polish of their narrative. They cite work showing deep-learning models trained on publication abstracts and metadata can predict real-world impact better than citation counts and can identify Nobel-prize-preceding work before it gets recognized.

Read that last paragraph in the context of the work that gets done in places like Su Lab. Building structured profiles of researchers — multi-dimensional, queryable, interoperable — is, in effect, building a knowledge graph for people. ORCID identifiers, ROR institutional records, PubMed metadata, grant award histories from NIH RePORTER and ERC and UKRI, citation networks, software contribution graphs. The infrastructure to do what Rees and Wilsdon are calling for already partially exists, in pieces, scattered across open-data communities. The reason it isn't deployed against funding decisions is partly political and partly because nobody has stitched it together yet.

If their argument lands, that gap closes fast, because the alternative — humans manually evaluating fifty-seven percent more applications every year, all of them well above the quality bar, all of them written by agents — isn't tenable.

Two things I take away. First, the next twelve months in funding policy are going to be more interesting than the last ten years. Watch the European Research Council, the MRC, and the Wellcome Leap programs especially — they've all signaled openness to structural reform. Second, the people who maintain biomedical knowledge graphs and structured author and institution metadata are about to find their work in a far more politically charged conversation than they signed up for. The same identifiers and the same provenance trails that solve drug-target curation also potentially decide who gets funded. That's a different stakeholder set, and it's coming.

A bioRxiv preprint from April twenty-second on human-supervised agentic AI for drug repurposing, with a supervisor agent coordinating four sub-agents through mechanism-of-action elucidation. And Pipette, from April twelfth, which encodes scientific literature into an executable skill graph that multi-agent bioinformatics workflows can call against — exactly the structured-resource-as-tool-source pattern that yesterday's episode argued for.
