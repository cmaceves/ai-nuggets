# TxBench-PP — AI agents cap out at 59% on real preclinical pharmacology decisions, and the benchmark tests reasoning not memorization

Paper link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19245

## Script

Today's pick is TxBench-PP, an arXiv preprint posted yesterday by Hannah Le, Ramesh Ramasamy, Alex Urrutia, and colleagues. Full name: TherapeuticsBench Preclinical Pharmacology. It was yesterday's strong runner-up — passed because MetaHarmonizer's contamination protocol had broader read-through across biomedical AI. Today it ships.

The setup. The conventional wisdom in AI-accelerated drug discovery is that agents are getting remarkably capable: they search the literature, run molecular dynamics simulations, predict protein structure, design novel compounds. What has been conspicuously absent is a benchmark that checks whether an agent can do what a preclinical pharmacologist does every day — read an assay result, understand what it means for a compound's mechanism of action, decide whether the data supports moving the program forward. TxBench-PP fills that gap.

The benchmark. One hundred evaluations drawn from real-world assay data — not textbooks, not literature, not memorized facts. Coverage spans five preclinical domains: mechanism-of-action and pharmacodynamic reasoning, compound-target engagement, causal target validation, developability and safety, and translational efficacy. The word verifiable matters here. Agents don't answer multiple-choice questions about drug concepts. They receive realistic workflow snapshots — the kind of data package a scientist actually opens in the morning — and they inspect those files in a coding environment and return structured answers graded deterministically against ground truth. No LLM-as-judge, no partial credit ambiguity. Either you recovered the correct preclinical pharmacology decision or you didn't.

The results. Sixteen model-harness configurations were evaluated — eleven distinct models, four thousand eight hundred trajectories. No system reliably recovered preclinical pharmacology decisions. The strongest configuration, Claude Opus 4.8 paired with a Pi harness, passed 59.3% of endpoint attempts, with a confidence interval running from 51 to 68 percent. GPT-5.5 paired with Pi came in second at 55.3%, 47 to 64 percent. Every other configuration fell below that, often considerably. Fifty-nine percent on a benchmark where the wrong answer means a failed drug program, or worse, a safety signal you missed — that's the calibration number the field needed to see.

The methodological contribution. The key distinction the paper draws is between memorized and reasoned answers. Existing benchmarks for AI in drug discovery can be gamed by a model that simply recalls facts from its training data: if the benchmark asks about a canonical mechanism of action, the answer is probably already in the weights. TxBench-PP specifically sidesteps this by grounding evaluations in actual assay data. The agent has to read the numbers in the file and derive the conclusion from the evidence in front of it, not retrieve a fact it memorized. That's the gap between current AI capabilities and what a functioning drug discovery agent actually needs.

The broader picture. TxBench-PP is framed as the first slice of a planned TherapeuticsBench family — benchmarks covering drug discovery across stages and therapeutic modalities. Preclinical pharmacology is the pilot; lead optimization, toxicology, translational modeling, and clinical pharmacology presumably follow. The implication is that 59% in preclinical pharmacology is a data point in a longer story about where the field actually is versus where it's being marketed as being.
