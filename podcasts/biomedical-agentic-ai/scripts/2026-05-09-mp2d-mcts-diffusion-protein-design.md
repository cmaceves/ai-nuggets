# 2026-05-09 — MP2D: Pareto-guided MCTS over a discrete diffusion model for protein design

Paper link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.05829

## Script

Welcome back to Biomedical Agentic AI for Saturday, May 9, 2026.

Today's nugget is a deliberate step away from the recent run of agent-platform papers, and toward the generative-model side of AI for biology. The paper went up on arXiv on the seventh, and is going to IJCAI this year. The title is "MP2D — Multi-Property Protein Diffusion: Constrained Monte Carlo Tree-Guided Diffusion for Multi-Objective Protein Sequence Design," from Zitai Kong, Yifan Dong, and colleagues at Zhejiang University. It's the kind of method paper that quietly matters, Andrew, because it addresses a problem every protein engineer eventually runs into and almost nobody solves cleanly — multi-objective design.

Here's the setup. Suppose you want an antimicrobial peptide. You don't just want it to kill bacteria. You want it potent, but also non-hemolytic, and soluble, and stable, and ideally novel — far enough from known sequences that it might dodge resistance and earn IP. Five conflicting properties at once. The same picture applies to protein binders: affinity, specificity, expressibility, stability, developability. The instinct in this field over the last two years has been to take a generative model — a protein language model or a discrete diffusion model — and condition it on the properties you want. That works well for one or two objectives. As soon as the objectives multiply and start to conflict, conditional fine-tuning either fails to balance them, or requires retraining from scratch every time a sponsor changes the brief. That is the gap MP2D goes after.

The mechanism is worth understanding because it's a clean idea. Start with an off-the-shelf discrete diffusion model — that's the now-standard recipe of taking a sequence, masking some fraction of the residues, and learning to denoise back. MP2D leaves that model untouched. What it changes is the inference procedure. Instead of running the diffusion model forward as a single denoising trajectory, MP2D treats each denoising step as a decision in a search tree, and runs constrained Monte Carlo Tree Search across that tree. The reward signal isn't a scalar — it's Pareto-based. So at each node, the algorithm asks: does this partial sequence push the candidate toward the Pareto front across all five properties, or does it dominate something we already had? Branches that look like they'll land on the front get expanded; branches that look dominated get pruned. Then there's a second loop on top of that, which the authors call global iterative refinement — once you have a candidate, you remask portions of it and re-denoise, with a dynamic Pareto constraint that stops the candidate population from bloating into a meaningless cloud. So you get exploration via MCTS at the residue level, and exploitation via remasking at the sequence level, both governed by multi-objective rewards.

For computational biologists, the part of this that should land is that nothing about the generative model is being retrained. You can plug MP2D onto a diffusion model that someone else trained — including one trained on antibodies, or enzymes, or whatever your domain is — and steer it through inference alone. They evaluate on two tasks. Antimicrobial peptide design with four conflicting properties. Protein binder optimization with five. They report consistent improvement over multi-objective baselines across all axes — and that is the hard claim, because the easy way to win on a multi-objective benchmark is to quietly trade off one property for another. The dynamic Pareto constraint is precisely the thing meant to prevent that.

Let me say what I think is significant and what I think is still open. On the significant side: this matches how lab teams actually triage. Nobody picks the single highest-affinity hit. They want a Pareto front and then domain expertise picks from the front. A method that natively produces a Pareto-balanced batch, without retraining for each new combination of objectives, is much more useful than yet another conditional generator. On the open side: the abstract is light on which specific properties were used in the four- and five-objective settings, which means we don't yet know whether the harder objectives — say, expressibility, or non-immunogenicity — were scored by cheap surrogate predictors or by something more honest. And MCTS at inference time is not free. The compute story will matter when you're scaling to libraries of thousands of candidates. The full paper, sixteen pages, four figures, seven tables, is where you'll find both of those answers — link in the show notes.

The pattern worth flagging is that this is the second paper this season — after the Lilly–Profluent recombinase work in late April — where the headline contribution is not a new generative model, but a smarter way to steer one. I think we're going to see a lot more of that. Training-time conditioning is expensive. Inference-time steering is cheap, modular, and increasingly competitive.
