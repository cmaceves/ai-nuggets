# MedMisBench — measuring how badly medical LLMs fold under misleading context

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.05.25.727671v2

## Script

Today's nugget is a bioRxiv preprint from David Clifton's group at Oxford called MedMisBench, and it does the most thorough job I've seen yet of pressure testing what actually happens when a medical L L M meets a patient or a clinician who is, intentionally or not, feeding it bad context. The headline result is the kind of number that should change how we report medical L L M evaluations. Mean accuracy across eleven leading model configurations drops from seventy one point one percent on the original medical questions to thirty eight point zero percent when focused misleading context is injected into the prompt. That's a fifty one and a half percent attack success rate, on questions the same models had previously answered correctly.

Let me unpack why this paper matters before I get into the structure of the benchmark itself.

For about three years now the standard talking point about medical L L Ms has been some variant of, "they're hitting expert level scores on the U S M L E, on the Indian medical licensing exam, on board style questions in specialty after specialty." That framing has done a lot of work. It has been used to justify deploying L L Ms in patient facing health advice, in clinical decision support, in triage. And the implicit assumption is that high benchmark accuracy carries safety. MedMisBench's contribution is that it isolates the variable that separates a benchmark question from a real clinical encounter — which is that the real encounter contains context, and the context is not always correct, and the L L M is supposed to push back when it isn't.

The benchmark itself is ten thousand nine hundred thirty two medical question items, paired with forty eight thousand eight hundred eighty nine misleading context option pairs. The misleading context is the experimental knob. Same question, same answer choices, but now there's a sentence or two of plausible looking but false context glued to the prompt — a fabricated clinical guideline, an invented authority claim, a manufactured exception. The benchmark spans three evaluation dimensions, which are worth naming because they're not symmetric. The first is medical reasoning, which is the closest analog to existing exam style benchmarks. The second is agentic capability, which is the one most directly relevant to anyone building autonomous medical agents — can the system maintain correct judgment while operating in a multi step loop where intermediate context can be poisoned. The third is patient journey evaluation, which is the realistic clinician or patient interaction setting, multi turn, with the misleading context arriving naturally inside a conversation.

The shape of the failures is the part I'd most want clinicians and developers to internalize. Not all misleading injections are equally damaging. The two most effective attack categories are both about epistemic packaging. Authority framed falsehoods, where the misleading claim is dressed up as coming from a guideline body or a regulatory authority, reach sixty nine point five percent attack success. Exception poisoning, where the misleading context introduces a fictional carve out from a real medical rule, reaches sixty four point one percent. In other words, the more the false context looks like the kind of formal rule based knowledge that medical reasoning ought to lean on, the more reliably the L L M abandons its previously correct answer. This is not a model getting confused by noise. This is a model deferring to fluent fabricated authority.

The clinical review piece is where this stops being just a benchmarking exercise. A fourteen member clinical panel from seven countries reviewed a sample of the model's misled answers and judged thirty eight point two percent of them to carry serious potential harm. Not just wrong, but the kind of wrong that, deployed in a real patient encounter, would plausibly hurt someone. That number is the one I'd put in front of anyone arguing that the existing licensing exam scores constitute adequate evidence of safety.

A few honest caveats. The eleven model configurations include both open and closed models but the paper does not, as far as I can tell from this revision, fully separate out whether reasoning specialized models or models given explicit safety training do measurably better. It would also be useful to see how much of the brittleness is attributable to instruction following pressure — the models being too eager to incorporate any provided context — versus a deeper failure to maintain epistemic state. And the patient journey evaluation, while a meaningful step toward realism, is still based on scripted conversations, not real clinical encounters.

But the structural finding is the one that I think holds. Existing medical L L M benchmarks measure what models know. They do not measure whether models preserve correct judgment when the context around the question is adversarial. MedMisBench is a serious attempt to measure that second thing, at scale, with a clinical panel anchoring the harm calibration. For anyone working on agentic medical systems — and that's a lot of what this show covers — the agentic capability subset is the immediately relevant slice. Any agent that calls tools, reads chart data, ingests patient narrative, is going to encounter misleading context on a regular basis. The question is no longer whether your agent passes the U S M L E. The question is whether it holds the line when the prompt tries to make it not.
