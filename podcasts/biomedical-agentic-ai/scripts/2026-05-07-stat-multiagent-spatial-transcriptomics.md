## Summary

Today's third nugget is a bioRxiv preprint from Can Yang's group at HKUST — first authors Y. Chen, S. Han, Z. Chao, with co-authors across HKUST and collaborators. STAT is a multi-agent framework for spatial transcriptomics that makes an explicit architectural bet against the two dominant patterns in agentic biomedical tooling — naïve single-LLM front ends that flatten spatial data into generic single-cell tables, and fully autonomous agents that run end-to-end without giving the analyst a window to intervene. STAT instead pairs multi-agent decomposition with three concrete commitments: a persistent session that carries state across turns, a shared interactive tissue viewer the agents and the human both write to, and a staged skill-aware pipeline that routes each subtask to the agent best equipped for it. On an eleven-category benchmark across three spatial platforms at both cell and spot resolution, STAT beats both a baseline large language model and existing autonomous spatial agents on task completion, analytical quality, and token efficiency. The headline demo: STAT reproduces the key findings of a published Visium HD colorectal cancer study on a mixed-resolution breast cancer cohort using only natural language prompts. As tonight's bonus episode after the morning's PNNL PTM-LLM nugget and the mid-day Payne Lab Text-to-Target piece, STAT lands on the same architectural through-line — agents-plus-curated-infrastructure — but takes the design bet in a different direction. Where the earlier two picks pushed schema-constrained retrieval and provenance closure, STAT pushes shared-state interactivity, and that distinction is exactly the kind of design knob biomedical-AI builders should be tuning.

## Script

Today's third nugget is a fresh bioRxiv preprint from Can Yang's group at the Hong Kong University of Science and Technology, posted Tuesday and revised today. The title is, quote, S-T-A-T, A multi-agent framework for integrated and interactive spatial transcriptomics analysis, end quote. The first authors are Yangchao Chen and Songqi Han with collaborators at HKUST.

Spatial transcriptomics has an analyst problem. The technology is fantastic — you get gene expression measurements with tissue-coordinate metadata, so you can see not just which genes are expressed but where in a tissue section they are expressed, how they organize into neighborhoods, how they line up against histology. But the analysis pipeline has not kept up. There are dozens of computational methods scattered across diverse platforms — Visium, Xenium, MERFISH, Stereo-seq, Slide-seq, Visium HD, you name it — and they each have their own data formats, their own preprocessing assumptions, and their own ergonomic quirks. So an analyst sits down with a new dataset and spends most of their time on data assembly, format wrangling, method selection, and visualization plumbing rather than on the biology.

The natural reach is to throw an LLM at it. And the existing AI tools for spatial transcriptomics tend to fall into one of two failure modes. The first failure mode is the naïve mode — feed the spatial data into a chat interface, treat it as a flat single-cell table, lose all the spatial structure, give the analyst back a generic plot. That's not good enough; spatial biology is fundamentally about geometry, and flattening it loses what made the data valuable in the first place. The second failure mode is the autonomous mode — hand the data to a fully autonomous agent that runs an end-to-end analysis and hands you back a final report. That sounds good in a demo, but in a production research workflow it's the wrong default. Domain experts need to intervene. They need to look at intermediate visualizations, push back on a method choice, redirect the analysis, see what went into a downstream call. An agent that hides those moments behind a black-box pipeline forecloses on the very thing makes spatial biology interesting.

What Yang's group built is a different design point. STAT is multi-agent — it decomposes the analysis pipeline across specialized agents — but it pairs that with three architectural commitments that I want to spell out because each of them is a genuine design choice that other biomedical agentic systems should be considering.

First, a persistent session. State carries across turns. When you intervene at step seven, the agent at step eight knows what happened at step seven. This is not the default for LLM-driven pipelines, where each tool call resets context and the analyst has to keep reminding the system what is going on.

Second, a shared interactive tissue viewer. The agents and the human are both writing to the same visualization. When the agent annotates a region, the human sees it. When the human zooms or selects, the agents know. The viewer is the shared workspace, not a screenshot the agent dumps at the end.

Third, a staged skill-aware pipeline. Each subtask gets routed to the agent that has the right tools and the right reasoning for it. Cell-type annotation goes to a cell-type-annotation specialist. Niche detection goes to a niche-detection specialist. The orchestrator does not just hand the whole spatial analysis to a single generalist LLM and hope.

The benchmark numbers are concrete. STAT was evaluated on eleven analytical task categories across three spatial platforms, at both cell-resolution and spot-resolution data. Against a baseline large language model and against existing autonomous spatial-analysis agents, STAT wins on three axes — task completion, analytical quality, and token efficiency. The token efficiency point matters because in production these systems get expensive fast, and a multi-agent design that wastes tokens shuttling redundant context between agents will not survive contact with a real research budget.

The headline demo is the one that should land for this audience. STAT was given a mixed-resolution breast cancer cohort, with both cell- and spot-resolution data, and asked to do a multi-task spatial analysis using only natural language prompts. The system reproduced the key findings of a previously published Visium HD colorectal cancer study, on a different cohort, on a different cancer, just from prompts. That is a real reproducibility argument — not a benchmark on simulated tasks but a reach-back to a published result the analyst would have spent weeks reproducing by hand.

For an audience that thinks about agentic AI for biomedical research, STAT is worth your attention because of the design bet it makes. Today's morning episode covered a single-pipeline LLM-as-analyst story for proteomics PTM annotation. Today's mid-day episode covered an LLM retrieval layer with provenance closure for disease target discovery. Both of those are agents-as-analyst designs where the analyst hands a problem to the system and gets back a result. STAT is making a different bet — agents-as-collaborators, with shared state and shared visualization and a human in the loop by design. That is, in my opinion, the more honest design for biomedical agentic AI in 2026, because the biology really does demand intermediate review. STAT is one of the cleaner demonstrations of what that design looks like in practice.

If you build agentic biomedical tools, the take-away question is — what is your equivalent of STAT's persistent session, shared viewer, and staged skill-aware pipeline? Because the design point STAT is staking out — interactive over autonomous — is going to be a real axis of competition over the next year.
