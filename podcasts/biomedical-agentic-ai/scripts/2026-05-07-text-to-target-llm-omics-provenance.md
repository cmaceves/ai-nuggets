## Script

Today's second nugget is a bioRxiv preprint that posted on Tuesday from Phil Payne and Fuhai Li's group at Washington University in St. Louis. The title is "Interpreting Omics Data Analysis with Large Language Models for Disease Target and Drug Discovery." It pairs nicely with the morning episode — the morning episode was P-T-M proteomics, language models doing the annotation and interpretation step. This one is at the disease-target-prioritisation step, language models doing the literature retrieval step, and bolted onto numeric omics analysis for the cohort-specific quantitative side.

The paper's framing is a sentence I want to read back almost verbatim. Quote — large language models alone can rapidly retrieve disease mechanisms from biomedical text, but text-only outputs are general and unreliable for target and drug prioritisation without cohort-specific quantitative evidence. End quote. That sentence is the entire methodological argument of this paper, and it is also the methodological argument of half of what this show has been covering in the past two weeks. The paper's name for the resulting design is "Text-to-Target." Schema-constrained, multi-model, large-language-model retrieval, fused with numeric omics data analysis, with provenance closure at every step.

I want to walk through three pieces. The architecture, the evaluation, and what this audience should take away.

The architecture, first. Most "L-L-M plus omics" pipelines you have seen this year do the obvious thing — they treat the language model as a free-text knowledge base, ask it for candidate genes, and then post-hoc validate against omics. Payne and Li's group reject that pattern. They run the language model under a schema. That is, the retrieval is constrained — outputs have to fit a defined typing of entities and relationships, which is the same architectural commitment you saw in the Hardi-Musen ontology-constrained agent we covered on Sunday. They run multiple language models in parallel, not just one, and they fuse the model outputs against numeric omics signals from the cohort itself. The fusion step is the move that gives the paper its name and that I want to highlight. Candidates are partitioned into three categories. First, overlap-supported anchors — genes where the literature and the cohort omics agree. Second, retrieval-only hidden hubs — genes that the literature names but that the cohort omics did not surface. And third, network-emergent novelty nodes — genes that fall out of the cohort topology that the literature has not yet synthesised. Each category is treated differently in the downstream hypothesis and strategy generation, and only after that staged propagation does the system commit to a final candidate universe and strategy portfolio. The provenance closure is the property the paper is most explicit about — every final strategy traces back to a candidate, every candidate traces back to its retrieval artifacts and its numeric support. You can audit any conclusion the system reaches, all the way down to the source.

The evaluation. Two diseases. Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma — a 75-gene candidate universe and a 23-strategy portfolio, with significant DepMap support at both the target level and the strategy level. The DepMap signal there is the right external benchmark to pick, because the dependency-screen evidence in DepMap is broadly orthogonal to whatever the cohort-specific omics is telling you about expression and mutation. Alzheimer's disease, a stricter run — a tighter 34-gene universe and 14 strategies. Both target-level axes were significant under an expanded CRISPRbrain registry, with strong strategy-level enrichment. The numbers in this paper are smaller than the typical pharma-target-discovery pipeline, and that is the point — a tighter, audited, provenance-closed candidate set is the deliverable, not a one-thousand-gene long-list.

The takeaway for this audience. Three pieces. First, this is the same week-long through-line landing one architectural layer deeper. We have been saying for ten days, agents-plus-curated-resources, not agents alone. Today's paper says, agents-plus-curated-resources, with the audit trail itself as a curated artifact. The provenance closure is curated infrastructure. Every retrieval that a downstream auditor inspects, every numeric quantity that gets cited by a strategy — those are objects the system has to keep, structure, and serve. That is identifier infrastructure. That is data-integration infrastructure. That is the territory this audience has been building.

Second, the schema-constrained, multi-model retrieval pattern is now showing up in three different shows in the same week. Hardi-Musen on Sunday at the metadata layer. The PaperQA-three validation of OptimusKG on Monday at the knowledge-graph layer. Text-to-Target this week at the disease-target layer. The pattern is becoming the default. Free-text retrieval where the model just emits prose is leaving the field at the high end.

Third, a real caveat — this is a methods paper on two cohorts. It has not been compared head-to-head against PandaOmics or Open Targets' large-language-model layer or PageRank-style network methods or any of the other multi-modal target-discovery pipelines that have been published this year. The provenance closure is the honest argument, not the accuracy delta. If you read this paper, read it for the architectural commitment, not for a horse-race benchmark.
