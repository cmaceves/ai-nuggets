# IID-KG — ontology-aligned literature-derived KG for infectious and immune-mediated disease

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.05.21.727015v1

## Script

Today's nugget is a biomedical knowledge graph paper out of Insilicom that's worth dwelling on, because it threads several needles at once. The system is called IID dash KG, an ontology aligned, literature derived knowledge graph for infectious and immune mediated diseases. And the part that should grab a knowledge graph person's attention is not that they built a KG from the literature — plenty of groups do that — it's the combination of scale, the disease scoped corpus, and the way the ontology was actually constructed, which is a much more disciplined story than the usual relation extraction dump that gets called a KG.

Here's the shape of the pipeline. They start with thirty million PubMed abstracts and another one point four million full text articles from PMC, the open access subset that is enriched for I I D content — infectious disease, autoimmunity, immune mediated conditions broadly. They hand annotate a gold standard of five hundred abstracts plus eight full text papers for nested biomedical entities across six entity types, which is the painful but unavoidable step you have to do if you want the downstream models to behave. Nested means you handle the case where, say, a chemical mention sits inside a drug class mention sits inside a treatment regimen — flat span tagging would miss the structure. Then they run that across the full corpus, and on top of the entity layer they do ontology guided identifier assignment, full text relation extraction, and a relation resolution pass that collapses synonymous edges.

The piece I'd flag for our audience is what they did with disease ontology specifically. They started from four hundred eleven thousand disease surface forms extracted from the corpus — the long tail of how diseases actually get written about in papers, which is messier than M E S H or DO would have you believe. They ran hierarchical text classification to bucket them, then used a large language model as a refinement layer to clean and reconcile candidate term clusters, then cross referenced against existing ontologies, then put it through expert review. They land at one hundred seventy nine thousand confirmed M E S H mappings. That's the kind of pipeline you build when you actually want the resulting graph to be queryable against existing biomedical infrastructure, not just internally consistent. The L L M sits in the place where it's most useful — as a disambiguation and refinement assistant under expert oversight — rather than being the whole show.

The final graph is about one point eight million unique entities and sixteen point three million unique relations across eight relation types. They've released it publicly along with what they call repurposing workflows — pipelines built on top of the KG aimed at drug repurposing hypothesis generation, mechanism analysis, and ontology aligned literature mining. So it's pitched not just as an artifact but as a resource other groups can plug into.

A few caveats worth being honest about. The name I I D dash KG is going to collide in the literature with the existing Integrated Interactions Database, which has been around for years and is also abbreviated I I D — anyone searching is going to have to disambiguate. Disease scoped knowledge graphs have an inherent tension: deeper coverage of the target domain at the cost of cross domain edges that the big general purpose biomedical KGs, Hetionet, PrimeKG, S P O K E, the Wikidata health knowledge graph, give you for free. Whether that tradeoff is the right one depends on whether you're doing infectious disease drug repurposing specifically, in which case this is probably the highest signal corpus you can get, or whether you're doing cross indication target discovery, in which case you still want one of the broader graphs as your backbone. And the relation extraction is automated, which always means the precision on any individual edge is going to be lower than a hand curated database — these graphs are best read at the level of subgraph patterns, not at the level of any single triple.

The bigger pattern this fits into is the one we keep coming back to on this show: as the literature scales past what any human curator can keep up with, the only way disease specific knowledge graphs stay current is automated construction with an L L M assisted ontology refinement loop. The interesting question for the next few years isn't whether to build them this way — at thirty million abstracts there isn't another option — it's how to make the resulting graphs compose, so that an agent reasoning about, say, an autoimmune comorbidity in a cancer patient can pull from the I I D graph and the cancer graph and the Wikidata layer at the same time without the entity identifiers fragmenting. The repurposing workflows shipped with this release are a useful first answer on the agent facing side.
