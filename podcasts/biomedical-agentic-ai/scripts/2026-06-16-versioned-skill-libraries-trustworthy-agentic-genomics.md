# Versioned skill libraries — trustworthy agentic genomics with correctness in code, not the model

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.11.731523v1

## Script

Today's pick is a bioRxiv preprint from Manuel Corpas and collaborators across the University of Westminster, GENEQ Global, King's College London, and the African Computational Genomics group. Title: Trustworthy agentic genomics through versioned skill libraries. Quick caveat up front — bioRxiv's full text is locked behind a Cloudflare challenge from my host, so I'm working off the abstract. But the abstract is unusually concrete, and the architectural claim is the kind that's worth talking about even before the figures.

Here's the setup. Genomics is racing to adopt agentic AI — systems where you point a language model at a genome and a natural-language question and let it interpret. That's seductive, because pharmacogenomic interpretation, for example, is exactly the kind of structured-but-fiddly task where a competent agent could in principle save a clinical pharmacist hours per patient. The problem is that the field is deploying these agents faster than it's working out where, in the pipeline, you should actually trust them. This paper is the first large-scale controlled answer to that question.

They picked pharmacogenomics deliberately, because in that domain errors aren't aesthetic — they're measurable, and some of them are lethal-class. You can grade a star-allele-to-phenotype call against published guidelines and you can put a severity on getting it wrong. The benchmark is forty-four-and-a-half thousand scored evaluations across nine frontier large language models on a hundred and ten pharmacogenomic cases, plus a downstream test on real diplotypes from over seven thousand individuals drawn from three ancestrally diverse populations.

The three conditions they compare are the three architectures everyone's actually shipping right now. Condition one — let the model reason from its weights. Condition two — give it the same job but ground its reasoning by retrieving the correct, validated pharmacogenomic guidelines and letting it work from those. Condition three — take the validated decision logic out of the model's head entirely, encode it as a versioned skill, and have the agent invoke that skill as code instead of reasoning the answer out.

And the finding is sharp. Letting the model reason was stochastic — same input, different answers — and unsafe. Adding retrieval, the move everyone reaches for when an LLM is wrong, paradoxically made things worse on the lethal-error class. You handed the model the correct guideline and it still botched the mapping, just with more confidence. Encoding the validated decision logic as a versioned, executable skill made the mapping exact, made it auditable, and — this is the elegant part — made it identical across all nine models. The model is no longer in the loop for the decision; it's only in the loop for interpreting the input. All the residual error collapses to that single input-interpretation step, which is the part you can actually monitor.

The ancestry result is the one that I'd put on the slide. When unguarded model interpretation hit real diplotypes from the three populations, performance degraded along an ancestry gradient — the agent was worse for under-represented ancestries, the way every bias paper since two thousand and fourteen has warned. Pushing the decision logic into a deterministic skill execution removed that gradient from the clinical mapping. The disparity didn't disappear from the world — it relocated. It moved upstream, to whether the variant caller produced a complete star-allele input in the first place, which is at least a problem you can see, and audit, and budget against. That's a much better problem than a hidden disparity in model reasoning.

The thing I want listeners to take away is the architectural claim, because it generalizes well past pharmacogenomics. The default mental model for an agentic biomedical pipeline is: smart model in the middle, knowledge in retrieval around it, tools on the outside. This paper says the smart model in the middle is exactly where you don't want the critical decision to live. The validated logic — the part a regulator or a tumor board would actually sign off on — belongs in a versioned skill, executed as code, with the model relegated to parsing the input. That's a sharper version of what the agentic-coding community has been converging on with tool-use and structured outputs, applied to a domain where the stakes are clinical.

It also lines up with the recurring thread on this show — MedCTA on brittle tool-planning, EpiBench on verifiable evaluation, OmicsNavigator on auditable blueprints. The field is steadily learning that, for biomedicine, "agentic" without "auditable" is a non-starter. Versioned skill libraries are, basically, the executable analog of versioned ontologies. If you've been building knowledge graphs and APIs for biomedical agents, this is the architecture pattern to steal. That's it for today — talk to you tomorrow.
