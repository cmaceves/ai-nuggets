# M3A — evaluating agentic AI for biological discovery in autonomous and copilot settings

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.04.729919v1

## Script

Welcome back to Biomedical Agentic AI. Today's pick comes from the Van Allen lab at Dana-Farber, and it's the cleanest attempt yet to actually measure what current agentic AI can and can't do when you point it at a real computational-biology workflow. The framework is called M3A — the Multistep Multimodal Multiomic Agentic framework — and the paper title is "Evaluating agentic AI for biological discovery in autonomous and copilot settings." It went up on bioRxiv yesterday.

Here's the setup. A lot of agentic-AI papers in our space demo a single end-to-end pipeline that succeeds on a tidy benchmark, and call it discovery. The Dana-Farber team's argument is that real computational biology almost never looks like that. The data are heterogeneous, noisy, multimodal — gene expression here, chromatin accessibility there, paired clinical annotations that are inconsistent across cohorts. The actual scientific work isn't running a fixed pipeline; it's open-ended hypothesis generation and iterative reasoning over evidence that doesn't line up. So they built M3A specifically to put an agent into that setting and watch what happens — across eleven cancer types, with paired multi-omic single-cell data.

What's novel architecturally is that M3A holds a persistent multimodal data state across steps. So the agent isn't being re-prompted from scratch each turn — it carries forward the dataframes, the QC summaries, the embeddings, the intermediate plots, and a structured trace of every reasoning step. That lets the framework capture the agent's actual reasoning behavior, not just its final answer. And then they run the same framework in two modes — fully autonomous, where the agent just goes, and copilot, where a human domain expert is in the loop providing guidance.

They evaluate three complementary tasks. First, autonomous cell-type annotation across the eleven cancer datasets — a task that has a defensible ground truth and tests whether agents can do structured analysis end-to-end. Second, generating falsifiable biological hypotheses from gene programs — open-ended, no fixed ground truth, the kind of task where you really see what an agent's reasoning is worth. And third, a copilot experiment that systematically varies who's at the keyboard alongside the agent — including the effect of whether the human has actual domain expertise versus general analytical skill.

The headline finding is the interesting one. Current agents are genuinely good at broad systemic exploration — they'll churn through QC, marker-gene scans, embedding visualizations, and surface candidate signals across datasets faster than a graduate student would. But where they fall short is exactly where you'd expect: methodological judgment about which analysis to actually trust, and biological synthesis across analyses to land on a finding. The domain experts in the copilot arm remain critical — not because they execute more competently than the agent, but because they steer the agent away from confident-but-wrong analytical choices and tie the threads together at the end.

A few things I want to flag for why this matters for the broader agentic-biomedical-AI program. One — this is the right unit of evaluation. Most agentic-AI evals in our space score the final answer on a benchmark. M3A is scoring the trajectory, on tasks where the trajectory is the science. That's a methodological upgrade we badly need across the field. Two — the autonomous-versus-copilot framing is something almost every group is going to face in the next year, and we've had remarkably little empirical comparison. The finding that autonomy buys you broad coverage and a human buys you depth and judgment is intuitive, but having it pinned down with this kind of cross-cancer-type breadth gives the field something concrete to argue about. And three — releasing M3A as a framework means other groups can plug their own agents in and see where they land on the same tasks. That moves the conversation from "look at my demo" to "here's how my agent compares on a shared evaluation."

The honest caveats. Eleven cancer types is breadth, but single-cell multi-omics is still a narrow slice of the discovery work biomedical agents will eventually need to do. And the hypothesis-generation task is judged by experts, which is the right call given there's no ground truth, but expert judgment carries its own variance. Worth reading the methods carefully for how they handle inter-rater agreement.

But this paper is the kind of artifact this field has needed for at least a year — somebody actually measuring whether the agentic story holds up on real biological discovery, with enough scope that the conclusions mean something, and with the framework released so the next round of agents can be compared head-to-head. That's the move. Paper link is in the show notes. See you tomorrow.
