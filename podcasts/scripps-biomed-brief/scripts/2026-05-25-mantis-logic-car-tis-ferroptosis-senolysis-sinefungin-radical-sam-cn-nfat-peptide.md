# Scripps Biomedical Brief — 2026-05-25

## Episode

## Script

Good morning. Today's briefing covers four moves spanning a new logic-gated CAR architecture, a senolytic strategy that exploits therapy-induced senescence, a radical chemical-biology reframing of nucleoside antimetabolite biosynthesis, and a selective alternative to calcineurin inhibition for T cell immunosuppression.

First, Tushar Nichakawade and colleagues in the Kinzler, Papadopoulos, and Pardoll groups at Johns Hopkins introduce MANTIS — short for Multi-ANtigen Triggered Immune Synapse — a fundamentally new AND-gated chimeric antigen receptor architecture for T cell therapy. The standard problem with CAR T cell therapy is that the target antigen is rarely truly tumor-specific, so on-target off-tumor toxicity limits which antigens can be addressed. AND-gating, in principle, lets the cell discriminate cancer from normal tissue by requiring two antigens to co-occur, but every prior approach has tried to integrate two ligand-binding events through intracellular signaling chimeras that are leaky, slow, or hard to tune. MANTIS solves the problem geometrically rather than electronically. The CAR carries a bulky extracellular domain that physically blocks the receptor from reaching its second-antigen ligand at the immune synapse. When the first antigen engages, that blocking domain is shed; a free, properly sized CAR is unveiled, and only then can it engage the second antigen and trigger T cell activation. The mechanism leverages the well-known spatial constraints of the kinetic-segregation model of T cell receptor triggering — small receptor-ligand pairs cluster inside, big ones are excluded — and turns it into a programmable input gate. This is a clean, mechanism-first cell-therapy chassis that expands the set of targetable antigen pairs and gives engineers a tunable physical knob — receptor dimensions — alongside the chemical and signaling knobs already in use.

Second, Zhe Wang and Yulin Li at Houston Methodist with collaborators at Stanford and Baylor identify lysosomal ferrous iron accumulation as a conserved hallmark and an actionable vulnerability of therapy-induced senescence — the residual-disease state that fuels recurrence after chemotherapy or radiation. Across diverse tumor models the senescent tumor cell population is markedly hypersensitive to ferroptosis induction. Mechanistically, the transcription factor TFEB activates the heme-degrading enzyme heme oxygenase one, free ferrous iron piles up in the lysosome, and the cell becomes primed for iron-dependent lipid peroxidation death. In breast cancer patient-derived xenografts, sequentially following chemotherapy with a ferroptosis inducer significantly delays recurrence, and dual inhibition of GPX4 and FSP1 — the two parallel ferroptosis defense arms — produces often-complete eradication of residual tumors with no overt toxicity. This positions ferroptosis induction as a mechanism-specific senolytic strategy and gives a structurally rational dual-target drug-development thesis — a GPX4 inhibitor plus an FSP1 inhibitor, deployed after the cytotoxic chemotherapy cycle, rather than as a standalone agent.

Third, Chen-Fan Lee, Tianyu Zhou and Michael Freeman at the University of Minnesota with Wilfred van der Donk resolve the biosynthesis of sinefungin — a long-known SAM-mimetic nucleoside antimetabolite that has resisted mechanistic characterization for decades. The team identified the complete biosynthetic gene cluster from Streptomyces incarnatus and showed that the defining carbon-carbon bond at the heart of sinefungin is forged not by a pyridoxal-phosphate enzyme — the standard hypothesis for thirty years — but by a vitamin B-twelve-dependent radical SAM enzyme. Isotope tracing shows that the adenosyl group of sinefungin is transferred whole from adenosylcobalamin via a rare homolytic substitution, with the cobalamin cofactor consumed catalytically. Downstream, the pathway uses a cryptic phosphorylation-dephosphorylation strategy to gate substrate recognition, and two peptide aminoacyl-tRNA ligases — PEARLs — install alanines onto the small-molecule scaffold, one of them processively. PEARLs were known as peptide-modifying enzymes; the discovery that they act on small molecules iteratively expands the biocatalytic toolkit for nucleoside-amino-acid conjugates substantially. The team rebuilt the pathway as a defined enzyme cascade.

Finally, Adi Cohen, Maya Gabay and Maayan Gal at Tel Aviv University report cell-penetrating peptide conjugates of a C16orf74-derived peptide that selectively disrupt the calcineurin-NFAT protein-protein interaction in human T cells, without inhibiting calcineurin's broader catalytic activity. Cyclosporine A and tacrolimus block calcineurin's catalytic site directly, which gives them their potency but also the bulk of their off-target toxicity. The C16orf74 peptide had been characterized as a high-affinity inhibitor of the specific calcineurin-NFAT docking interaction but lacked membrane permeability. Conjugating it to a TAT or polyarginine cell-penetrating peptide gave intracellular delivery in mammalian cells, inhibited NFAT nuclear translocation, suppressed downstream IL-two transcription in human T cells at low-micromolar concentrations, and produced no measurable cytotoxicity up to fifty micromolar — establishing a therapeutic window that cyclosporine does not have. This is a clean proof-of-concept that the PPI-selective interface can substitute for catalytic-site inhibition in clinical immunosuppression.

That is the brief for today. Four moves — a geometrically AND-gated CAR architecture, a ferroptosis-based senolytic strategy for chemotherapy-residual tumor cells, a B-twelve-radical-SAM-plus-PEARL biocatalytic logic for nucleoside antimetabolite biosynthesis, and a calcineurin-NFAT PPI-selective peptide that sidesteps cyclosporine's off-target liability. Have a productive day.
