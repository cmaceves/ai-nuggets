## Script

Welcome to the Scripps Biomedical Brief for Saturday, June twentieth. Four very fresh bioRxiv preprints, all posted in the last forty-eight hours — a new chemistry-first approach to cell-type-selective drug delivery in the brain, the first full-length structure of a major cancer mutagenesis driver, label-free membrane-potential imaging that uses lipids themselves as the sensor, and a translatome-wide map of co-translational quality control. Because these are all v1 preprints from the last day or two, I am summarizing from the abstracts — bioRxiv has not yet rendered the bodies.

First, from Jaime Grutzendler's group at Yale, a platform called ExACT — transporter-guided therapeutics — that reframes cell-type-selective delivery as a chemistry problem instead of a receptor or antibody problem. The team screened combinatorial libraries of fluorescent small molecules directly in mouse brain in vivo and asked which chemical scaffolds get into which cells. The answer turned out to be controlled by the expression pattern of endogenous membrane transporters. Different chemistries preferentially entered neurons, astrocytes, pericytes, or endothelial cells, and one series was strongly selective for brain and retinal endothelium via Slco1a4. Selectivity transferred to the human orthologue, SLCO1A2, which is heavily expressed on brain endothelium and oligodendrocytes — confirmed in a humanized mouse and in human iPSC-derived oligodendrocytes. Two leverage points follow. You can ectopically express SLCO1A2 in neurons by gene therapy to create a synthetic entry port, turning previously inaccessible cells into addressable ones. And bifunctional compounds linking these transporter-targeting motifs to antisense oligonucleotides or small-molecule drugs retain their pharmacology while gaining transporter-dependent cell selectivity. For programs trying to deliver oligonucleotide or small-molecule payloads into specific brain cell types without leaning on AAV tropism or transcytosis-receptor antibodies, this is a different category of solution.

Second, from Hideki Aihara's lab at Minnesota with Reuben Harris, a cryo-EM structure of full-length human APOBEC3B captured in complex with its natural viral antagonist BORF2 — the large subunit of the Epstein-Barr virus ribonucleotide reductase. APOBEC3B is a cytidine deaminase that mutates single-stranded DNA, and it is one of the principal endogenous mutagens driving tumor evolution, therapy resistance, and metastasis across many cancer types. Individual domain structures have existed for a while, but the full-length protein had been elusive. The new structure shows the amino-terminal and catalytic carboxy-terminal domains bridging a novel BORF2 dimer interface in a geometry that is meaningfully distinct from the related restriction factor APOBEC3G. Mutational work suggests that this unique inter-domain contact regulates deaminase activity intrinsically, and that BORF2 neutralization works by capturing A3B into stable sequestered aggregates through a matrix of contacts to both domains plus an additional CTD-CTD interaction. For cancer drug discovery this is the key prize — selective inhibition of A3B over A3G has been the unmet specification, and the new structure gives a platform for designing molecules that exploit the A3B-specific inter-domain interface or that mimic BORF2-style sequestration.

Third, from Vasilis Ntziachristos's group at TU Munich with Miguel Pleitez, an imaging modality they call MIRACLES that reads cell membrane potential with no dye, no electrode, and no genetic indicator. The idea is to use the lipid acyl chains themselves as the sensor. Under the membrane's transient electric field, lipid conformations shift, and that shift changes their mid-infrared vibrational signature — detected by optoacoustic sensing in living cells. Proof of concept is single-cell tracking of glucose-stimulated insulin secretion in beta cells in real time. The implication for drug discovery is concrete. Phenotypic screens against electrogenic cell types — beta cells for diabetes, cardiomyocytes, neurons — currently lean on voltage-sensitive dyes that perturb membrane biology, genetically encoded indicators that perturb the cell, or patch clamp that is low-throughput. A label-free, vibrational readout of membrane potential at single-cell resolution clears all three constraints in one shot.

Fourth, from Lars Steinmetz at EMBL with Judith Frydman at Stanford, Martin Beck, and Mikhail Savitski, a method called UbSeRP that pairs ubiquitin-linkage-specific selection with ribosome profiling to map co-translational ubiquitination across the entire yeast translatome. They take the endogenous substrates of ribosome-associated quality control from a small handful of known cases to thousands. Two findings are worth taking forward. First, only a subset of stalled ribosome disomes actually engages quality control, and biophysical features of the nascent chain predict which ones do — a real codebook for which translation problems get resolved and which slip through. Second, there is widespread RQC-independent co-translational ubiquitination implicated in protein complex assembly, which would mean nascent-chain ubiquitination is not just about catching mistakes but also about organizing how chains assemble together. In a chronological aging model, RQC engagement drops and proteasomal handling of nascent proteins shifts to the RQC-independent pathways — a translation-step explanation for some of how proteostasis erodes with age.

That is the brief. Links in the show notes. Have a productive weekend.
