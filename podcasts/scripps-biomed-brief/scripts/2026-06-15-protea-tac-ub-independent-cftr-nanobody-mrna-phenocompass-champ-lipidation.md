# Scripps Biomedical Brief — 2026-06-15

## Script

Good morning. Today's brief is four bioRxiv preprints — a ubiquitin-independent proteasome-targeting chimera that bypasses the E3 ligase landscape entirely, an mRNA-delivered nanobody chaperone that rescues F508del-CFTR through a previously unseen open-channel conformation, a billion-scale phenotypic virtual screen at Genentech that recovers novel kinase-pathway scaffolds, and a chemical-biology assay showing that lipid-driven membrane permeation follows opposite rules in mammalian and Gram-negative cells.

First, from Min Jae Lee's group at Seoul National University, a fundamentally new mode for targeted protein degradation. The current generation of PROTACs and molecular glues works by hijacking E3 ligases to ubiquitinate the target — a strategy whose accessible substrate universe is bounded by the tissue distribution, intrinsic activity, and chemical tractability of the E3 set. Protea-Tac sidesteps that constraint. The chimera is a heterobifunctional protein, not a small molecule — one half is a ubiquitin receptor that docks the assembly directly into the 26S proteasome, the other half is an intracellular antibody specific to the substrate of interest. Recruitment alone — proximity to the proteasome — is sufficient to drive degradation without any ubiquitin chemistry on the target. The team demonstrates this with c-Fos, BRD4, TDP-43, tau, and a GFP-ODC reporter, and shows in vivo anti-tumor efficacy from c-Fos degradation in a mouse model. The take is that the proteasome itself becomes the recruitment endpoint, and intrabody specificity opens the substrate space well beyond what classical E3-recruiting degraders can cover. The cost is a protein modality with attendant delivery challenges — but for targets that the E3-based platform has visibly failed to reach, this redraws the map. Version three posted Saturday.

Second, from Cédric Govaerts at the Université Libre de Bruxelles, with John Hunt at Columbia, Marcus Mall at the Charité, and David Sheppard at Bristol, a clean demonstration that an intracellular nanobody can serve as a protein-specific chaperone for a misfolded ion channel — and that it can be delivered as mRNA in a lipid nanoparticle. The nanobody T2a binds nucleotide-binding domain one of CFTR with high affinity and thermally stabilizes it. Co-administered with currently approved correctors, T2a substantially improves F508del maturation, plasma-membrane expression, and channel activity, with single-channel recordings showing T2a promotes both full-open and sub-conductance states and protects against thermal deactivation. The mechanistic surprise comes from cryo-electron microscopy. Because T2a engages NBD1 in a way that blocks the canonical ATP-driven NBD1-to-NBD2 dimerization, the open-channel state observed in its presence cannot be the textbook conformation — and the structure shows NBD1 instead binding an alternative site on the channel altogether. So the nanobody both rescues trafficking and reveals a non-canonical active conformation. For audiences working at the intersection of chaperone biology, RNA delivery, and structure-guided design of correctors, this is the most concrete preview yet of an intrabody-as-drug platform for protein-misfolding disease, with an unexpected structural insight along the way. Version three posted yesterday with a revised title.

Third, from David Richmond's group at Genentech, PhenoCompass — a multimodal co-embedding model that aligns chemical structures and Cell Painting phenotypic profiles in a single representation, trained on the JUMP morphology consortium dataset across more than one hundred thousand compounds. The point is to take high-content phenotypic screening — which historically scales poorly compared to target-based screens — and apply it virtually at compound-library scale. Retrospective validation on historical biochemical screens confirms that PhenoCompass ranks compounds by biochemical target engagement, not merely visual similarity. Prospectively, the team searched 3.8 billion Enamine REAL compounds for inhibitors of the PI3K-mTOR pathway. Eleven novel compounds were nominated, structurally diverse, a fifty-four-fold enrichment over training-set hits. Orthogonal validation with a FOXO3A reporter assay and direct kinase inhibition confirmed seven structurally novel inhibitors with distinct mechanisms of action. The result is concrete: morphology-grounded virtual screening can reach scaffolds beyond the chemical neighborhoods of the training compounds, and the cellular morphological signature aggregates over diverse molecular targets converging onto the same downstream pathway state. A real prospective wet-lab result rather than the usual retrospective benchmark, and a credible response to the longstanding throughput ceiling on phenotypic discovery.

Fourth, from Marcos Pires's group at the University of Virginia, a chemical-biology study using their CHAMP assay — a chloroalkane azide tag with a cytosolically anchored HaloTag — to quantitatively measure cytosolic accumulation of a systematically designed library of lipid conjugates in both HeLa cells and E. coli. The two systems gave divergent verdicts. In mammalian cells, larger and more hydrophobic lipid conjugates — medium-chain, cyclized, heteroatom-containing — were preferentially internalized, consistent with the classical lipidation playbook. In E. coli, the same modifications usually reduced accumulation. Lipophilicity that helps mammalian uptake actively impedes diderm-envelope entry. The work attributes this divergence to charge, scaffold composition, and the specific barriers of the Gram-negative envelope, and frames it as a quantitative explanation for the long-standing scarcity of lipidated Gram-negative antimicrobials. For medicinal chemists optimizing across these two contexts — and for antibiotic-discovery programs that have repeatedly hit the diderm permeability wall — it is the kind of empirical design rule that translates directly into go-no-go criteria for hydrophobic modifications.

That is the brief for June fifteenth. Detailed links and references are in today's show notes. Until tomorrow.
