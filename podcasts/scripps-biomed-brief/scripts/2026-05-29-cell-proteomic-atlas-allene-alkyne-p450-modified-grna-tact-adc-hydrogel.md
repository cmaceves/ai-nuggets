# Scripps Biomedical Brief — 2026-05-29

## Script

Good morning. Today's brief: a cell-type-resolved proteomic atlas of the human body from Matthias Mann's group, a pair of fungal cytochrome P-four-fifties that biosynthetically install allenes and alkynes from a prenyl group, the first guide RNAs with essentially every nucleotide chemically modified that still drive robust Cas9 editing in vivo, and an injectable chitosan hydrogel that makes vesicant antibody-drug conjugates subcutaneously deliverable.

First, a landmark resource from Matthias Mann's lab at the Max Planck Institute of Biochemistry, with collaborators across the Human Protein Atlas. Using Deep Visual Proteomics on tissue from a single healthy female donor, the team built a cell-type-resolved proteomic atlas covering twenty-seven distinct cell populations across fourteen tissues, quantifying roughly two-thirds of all human protein-coding genes, with up to eighty-five hundred proteins per population. The proteome partitions bimodally — a universal housekeeping core, and highly specialized cell-type programs layered on top. By integrating this with the Human Protein Atlas single-cell RNA resource, they show that messenger-RNA-to-protein concordance is governed by pathway, not by cell identity, which is a cleaner organizing principle than the field has been working with. As a working example, they pull cancer-testis antigens out of oocytes that are completely invisible to bulk profiling — the kind of finding that immediately seeds target discovery programs in immuno-oncology. The atlas is openly accessible and is going to become a default reference for cell-type-resolved target nomination across drug discovery.

Second, from Yi Tang's group at UCLA with computational support from Ken Houk, a beautiful piece of biosynthetic chemistry. Allenes and alkynes are versatile functional groups in medicinal chemistry, total synthesis, and bioorthogonal conjugation — the alkyne being the cornerstone of click chemistry — but how Nature actually installs them, especially allenes, has been mechanistically unclear. The team characterized two fungal cytochrome P-four-fifty monooxygenases, PpnB and NseB, from the penipratynolene and sinuxylamide biosynthetic pathways. Both enzymes accept O-prenyl tyrosine substrates and catalyze oxidative removal of an sp-two methyl group, yielding an O-homoallenyl tyrosine and an O-but-two-ynyl tyrosine, respectively. Density functional theory calculations combined with isotope labeling and heterologous expression establish the mechanism: a selective carbon-carbon bond cleavage followed by a product-determining hydrogen atom abstraction step. A third pathway P-four-fifty, PpnD, then acts as an oxidative isomerase that converts the four-carbon terminal allene into a terminal alkyne. This is a genuinely new enzymatic editing strategy — the cytochrome P-four-fifty repertoire just gained two unusual product classes, both highly relevant to medicinal chemistry and to bioorthogonal probe design.

Third, from Jonathan Watts and Erik Sontheimer at UMass Chan, with the Khvorova lab next door, a chemistry tour-de-force on Cas9 guide RNAs. Therapeutic CRISPR editing in vivo wants guide RNAs that are exhaustively chemically modified — for nuclease stability, for delivery flexibility beyond lipid nanoparticles, and for reduced innate immune activation — but previous attempts at fully modified guides systematically lost Cas9 activity. Using an iterative structure-guided design strategy, the team systematically introduced modifications at each position of SpyCas9 guide RNAs, going beyond the usual two-prime-fluoro and two-prime-O-methyl repertoire to include two-prime-amino RNA, four-prime-thio RNA, and extended nucleic acid. The result is a growing library of guides where ninety to one hundred percent of nucleotides are sugar or backbone modified, and which consistently maintain or even improve editing efficacy in cells and in vivo. They show this works for nuclease editing, base editing, and other Cas9-based tools. For therapeutic CRISPR programs trying to push beyond the liver, the constraints around guide RNA delivery and stability are precisely what this chemistry attacks.

And fourth, from Alexandre Detappe at Strasbourg, a deceptively simple solution to a real ADC delivery problem. Every approved antibody-drug conjugate is given by intravenous infusion, even though both patients and clinics would much prefer subcutaneous dosing. The block has been that ADC payloads — auristatins, maytansinoids, camptothecin warheads — are vesicants that cause tissue necrosis when locally released, and the recombinant hyaluronidase excipients used to disperse subcutaneous antibodies actively make that worse. The team developed an injectable diacetyl-L-tartaric-anhydride-functionalized chitosan hydrogel, called TACT, that confines the ADC inside a protective depot at the injection site. TACT is compatible with already-approved ADC formulations off the shelf, and release kinetics scale with drug-to-antibody ratio. In direct head-to-head studies, the hyaluronidase formulation caused severe necrosis with trastuzumab emtansine, while TACT prevented macroscopic injury and matched the intravenous antitumor efficacy. In non-human primates, subcutaneous TACT delivered seventy-eight percent relative bioavailability for total trastuzumab while cutting peak free-DM-one exposure more than sevenfold, with only transient cutaneous reactions. They show it works across trastuzumab emtansine, trastuzumab deruxtecan, a custom monomethyl auristatin E series, and enfortumab vedotin. Depot-mediated confinement looks like the right answer for subcutaneous ADCs.

That's the brief. Four reasons today to be optimistic about how chemistry, biocatalysis, and biomaterials are reshaping target discovery and modality engineering. Until tomorrow.
