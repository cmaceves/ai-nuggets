# Scripps Biomedical Brief — 2026-05-31

## Script

Good morning. Today's brief: a small-molecule pharmacological chaperone that re-folds destabilized mutant V-H-L in renal cancer cells, a non-catalytic inhibitor series targeting the H-DAC-six zinc-finger ubiquitin-binding domain, a chemogenetically activatable K-two-P potassium channel that opens on demand in response to rapamycin, and a machine-learning framework for predicting how small molecules partition selectively across distinct biomolecular condensates.

First, from John Karanicolas and Erica Golemis at Fox Chase Cancer Center, an unprecedented modality concept for renal cell carcinoma. Sporadic clear cell renal cell carcinoma is driven by somatic loss of V-H-L combined with loss of heterozygosity, and the team hypothesized that a substantial subset of V-H-L missense mutations actually destabilize the folded protein, shifting the unfolded-to-folded ratio and abolishing function rather than disrupting catalysis. They used computational structure-based virtual screening against the folded native conformation of p-V-H-L, then validated hits with biophysical binding assays and cellular function readouts. The campaign converged on a compound called C-P four-point-two-nine that selectively stabilizes the native fold of mutant p-V-H-L and restores wild-type p-V-H-L activity inside cells that carry the mutation. The conceptual contribution is the explicit pharmacological-chaperone framework for tumor suppressors — most prior chaperone programs targeted lysosomal storage enzymes, and almost none have rescued a folded-state tumor suppressor in a cancer context. If the V-H-L pocket holds up in optimization, the same playbook becomes tractable for the long tail of destabilizing missense mutations across other tumor suppressors so far considered undruggable.

Second, from George Burslem at the University of Pennsylvania, a chemical biology campaign that targets H-DAC-six through its second, underexplored protein interface. H-DAC-six carries two catalytic deacetylase domains and a C-terminal zinc-finger ubiquitin-binding domain. Essentially every clinical-stage H-DAC-six inhibitor targets the catalytic domains, but the zinc-finger ubiquitin-binding domain mediates the protein-protein interactions that couple deacetylation to ubiquitin-dependent degradation — and that interaction is functionally separable from deacetylase activity. The team starts from a known quinazolinone scaffold, runs a modeling-guided scaffold hop into a phthalazinone series with better physicochemical properties, and shows that machine-learning co-folding affinity predictions reproduce the experimental potency rank order of the resulting analogs. The therapeutic logic is to dissect ubiquitin-binding pharmacology from deacetylase pharmacology so that the two halves of H-DAC-six biology can be drugged independently — relevant across cell-fate and proteostasis programs where blunt catalytic inhibition has been disappointing.

Third, from Paul Riegelhaupt at Weill Cornell Medicine, a chemogenetically activatable potassium channel for direct manipulation of cellular membrane potential. The two-pore domain K-two-P potassium channels set resting membrane potentials across diverse cell types, but the available pharmacological toolkit has very few high-affinity, subtype-selective modulators. The team inserts the F-R-B domain of m-TOR into a short cytoplasmic loop of the TREK-one K-two-P channel; rapamycin or non-immunomodulatory rapamycin analogs then recruit endogenous F-K-B-P, and the F-R-B-rapamycin-F-K-B-P ternary complex assembles right inside the channel core. Cryo-electron microscopy structures show that this complex rigidifies the loop between transmembrane segments two and three into an active channel conformation, and electrophysiology shows nanomolar rapamycin produces clean activation that generalizes across multiple K-two-P isoforms. The take-home is a chemogenetic platform for direct potassium-channel-based control of membrane potential, built on an orally bioavailable approved-drug scaffold — useful both as a research tool and as a starting point for cell-type-restricted electrical-state engineering in therapeutics.

Fourth, from Jeetain Mittal at Texas A and M University, the first interpretable machine-learning framework for predicting how small molecules partition across distinct biomolecular condensates. Selective enrichment of small molecules inside condensates has emerged as a route to condensate-specific chemical probes and therapeutics, but the absence of a generalizable structure-to-partitioning model has slowed rational design. The team takes experimental partition coefficients for approved drugs and metabolites across four different condensate systems, trains a graph-based model with multi-task pretraining and per-condensate fine-tuning, and adds evidential uncertainty quantification and atom-level attribution. The model outperforms descriptor-based baselines and reveals that partitioning is not governed by a universal rule — the same chemical scaffold can be read very differently by different condensate microenvironments. The team then scores roughly one-point-seven million ChEMBL drug-like molecules and maps regions of chemical space where the model is confident versus where new wet-lab measurements would be most informative. For groups working on condensate-targeted drug discovery, this turns partitioning into a chemically learnable property and gives a useful prioritization layer over commercial screening libraries.

That's the brief. Four reasons today to be optimistic about pharmacological chaperones for tumor suppressors, non-catalytic H-DAC-six pharmacology, chemogenetic ion-channel control, and the early shape of medicinal chemistry inside biomolecular condensates. Until tomorrow.
