# Scripps Biomedical Brief — 2026-06-02

## Script

Good morning. Today's brief: a transferrin-receptor-targeting single-domain antibody that carries small-interfering RNAs across the blood-brain barrier into deep brain regions at clinically relevant doses, a generalizable binder system that finally lets peptide-major-histocompatibility-complex immunotherapy cross human-leukocyte-antigen polymorphism boundaries, a synthetic morphogenesis platform showing that adding cadherin-driven cell sorting to a reaction-diffusion circuit is what makes self-organized patterns robust, and a systematic finding that conventional codon optimization causes pervasive missplicing of heterologous genes, plus a splice-aware optimization algorithm to fix it.

First, from the Vect-Horus group with Michel Khrestchatisky at Aix-Marseille University, a delivery platform that addresses the single biggest barrier to nucleic-acid therapeutics for the central nervous system. Despite real progress on antisense oligonucleotides and small-interfering RNAs, deep brain regions remain essentially inaccessible to systemic dosing at clinically viable concentrations. The team uses camelid-derived single-domain antibodies — V-H-Hs — against transferrin receptor one for receptor-mediated transcytosis across the blood-brain barrier, conjugated one-to-one to tool small-interfering RNAs. The conjugates show rapid intracellular uptake and potent RNA-interference activity at low-nanomolar concentrations in neural cells. In wild-type mice, human-transferrin-receptor-one transgenic mice, and non-human primates, systemic dosing produced rapid receptor-dependent distributional clearance and efficient functional uptake in deep brain structures, with durable target knockdown of fifty to eighty percent at both messenger-RNA and protein levels and an effective dose for fifty-percent knockdown below one milligram per kilogram. That dose envelope is the news — at sub-clinical doses you can get half-maximal target knockdown in deep brain, across species, with a transferrin-receptor shuttle that has already had monoclonal-antibody and enzyme analogs translated. Real candidate platform for systemic CNS oligonucleotide therapeutics.

Second, from Nikolaos Sgourakis at Children's Hospital of Philadelphia, with Carl June at Penn and Possu Huang at Stanford, a generalizable framework for antigenic-peptide targeting across human-leukocyte-antigen polymorphism. T-cell receptors and T-cell-receptor-mimicking antibodies recognize peptides in the context of one specific human-leukocyte-antigen allotype, and the extreme polymorphism of the H-L-A locus restricts which patients any given binder can treat. The team combines two pieces: PepPred, a peptide-conformational-prediction tool, and TRACeR-I, a cross-H-L-A binding-protein engineering system, into a workflow they call x-TRACeRs that develops binders compatible across multiple H-L-A allotypes while keeping high specificity for the peptide. They validated this against established peptide antigens across five common H-L-A-A, -B, and -C supertypes. Cryo-electron-microscopy structures of x-TRACeR–peptide-H-L-A complexes for the oncofetal antigen P-R-A-M-E and the neuroblastoma-specific antigen P-H-O-X-two-B revealed how the binders accommodate polymorphic surface residues while making extensive interactions with the peptide itself. They then dropped two of these x-TRACeRs into chimeric antigen receptors and got potent, specific killing in vitro. The implication for development: if peptide-H-L-A immunotherapy can be engineered to cover supertypes rather than single alleles, the addressable patient population for any given peptide antigen expands by an order of magnitude.

Third, from Wendell Lim at UCSF's Cell Design Institute, a synthetic morphogenesis result that nails an old design principle in self-organized pattern formation. Reaction-diffusion circuits — local self-activation plus long-range inhibition — have been proposed since Turing as sufficient to produce cellular spots and stripes, but synthetic implementations have stayed fragile. The team builds the circuit explicitly: local positive feedback through juxtacrine synthetic Notch interactions, and long-range inhibition through diffusible competitor molecules. As predicted theoretically, this produced more spatially heterogeneous cell states but no robust patterns. The intervention that changes the picture is mechanical: when they also have the circuit induce expression of cadherins that drive local cell adhesion and cell sorting, well-defined patterns emerge. Theoretical analysis shows reaction-diffusion alone has a thin parameter window where patterns form; coupling the circuit to mechanical sorting dramatically expands that window. The transferable lesson is that evolved morphogenetic systems probably do not rely on biochemical signaling alone — they integrate signaling with mechanics, and synthetic morphogenesis programs aimed at tissue engineering or organoid design should plan to do the same.

Fourth, from Grzegorz Kudla at the University of Edinburgh, a finding that has implications for essentially every group doing heterologous gene expression — which is to say, for most of the biotech sector. Codon optimization is the default tool for boosting protein yields from transgenes, but the team shows that conventional codon optimization commonly produces pervasive missplicing of the resulting transcript in human cells. Across systematically designed libraries of more than five thousand synthetic reporter genes and natural human complementary-DNAs, most variants showed at least partial splicing, and in many the spliced isoform dominated, reducing protein output or abolishing expression entirely. Sequence-determinant analysis across multiple cell lines revealed a hierarchical architecture: G-C content sets baseline messenger-RNA level, local sequence features control splicing, and tissue-specific codon adaptation to transfer-RNA pools fine-tunes translation efficiency. From that they built predictive models for expression and splicing, benchmarked existing optimization tools — which often perform poorly on the splicing axis — and designed a splice-aware optimization algorithm that substantially improves transgene performance. For groups doing messenger-RNA therapeutics, viral-vector gene therapy, cell engineering, or recombinant-protein production, this reframes a quality-control problem you may not have realized you had, and provides an algorithmic fix.

That's the brief. Four reasons today to be optimistic about systemic delivery of small-interfering RNAs to deep brain regions, antigenic-peptide immunotherapy that crosses human-leukocyte-antigen supertypes, mechanically coupled synthetic morphogenesis, and splice-aware optimization of transgenes. Until tomorrow.
