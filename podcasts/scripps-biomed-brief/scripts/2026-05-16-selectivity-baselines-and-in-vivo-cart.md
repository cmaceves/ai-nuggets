## Script

Good morning, Pete. This is your Scripps Biomedical Brief for Saturday, May sixteenth. Four short stories today, focused on selectivity, baselines, and what cell engineering looks like when you do it in vivo.

First, a chemical biology paper from Neel Shah's group at Columbia, posted to bioRxiv on Thursday, takes direct aim at a perennial problem with kinase drugs. Every approved tyrosine kinase inhibitor binds the same conserved ATP pocket, which makes selectivity a never-ending arms race against off-target effects and resistance mutations. Shah's solution is to abandon the ATP pocket entirely. The group designs an optimized substrate peptide for a target kinase, then positions an electrophilic warhead on the peptide so it covalently captures a non-conserved cysteine on the kinase surface. They demonstrate it for Src and for fibroblast growth factor receptor one, including a covalent peptide that distinguishes an oncogenic FGFR1 mutant from the wild-type enzyme, and another that retains activity against a clinically relevant resistance mutation. This is a fundamentally different binding mode from every approved kinase inhibitor. It expands the covalent playbook from warhead-on-small-molecule to warhead-on-substrate-mimic, and it gives medicinal chemistry a new lever specifically for the kinases and mutants that small molecules struggle with.

Second, a methodology paper from the Shoichet lab at UCSF, also on bioRxiv this week, asks an embarrassingly basic question the field has never properly answered: what does a good hit-to-lead campaign actually look like compared to random luck. The authors took eighteen lead molecules across six targets and systematically introduced single-atom modifications — two hundred and fifty-seven random analogs in total. Roughly eleven percent of those random changes improved potency by ten-fold or more. That is a sobering baseline, because most medicinal chemistry campaigns report similar hit rates as evidence of skill. Worse, the more potent analogs had systematically worse pharmacokinetics — lower metabolic stability, lower plasma free fraction — so the potency gain often does not survive to the in vivo experiment. The paper does not solve the problem. It quantifies it. The Scripps and Calibr discovery teams now have a public benchmark to evaluate whether an active hit-to-lead campaign is actually beating background.

Third, BioAge Labs walked Fierce Biotech through its lead asset on Thursday, and the strategic picture is more aggressive than it has been. BGE-one-oh-two is an oral, brain-penetrant NLRP3 inhibitor with a novel chemotype and a non-canonical binding site, and BioAge is pushing it across cardiovascular disease, diabetic macular edema, and CNS indications from a single molecule. Phase one hit median high-sensitivity C-reactive protein reductions of eighty-five percent or more at the sixty and one-hundred-twenty milligram daily doses, with brain exposure above the target threshold. A dose-ranging Phase two cardiovascular proof-of-concept starts in the first half; a Phase one-b two-a in diabetic macular edema starts mid-year. The story to watch is not whether NLRP3 inhibition reduces inflammatory markers — that bar was cleared. It is whether one molecule with the right tissue distribution can credibly chase inflammation across three organ systems. If it does, it changes how the field thinks about platform value for an inflammasome program.

And fourth, the late-breaker at the American Society of Gene and Cell Therapy yesterday came from Azalea Therapeutics, which presented first-in-primate data for in vivo CAR T-cell engineering with site-specific integration at the TRAC locus — the same locus ex vivo allogeneic CAR-T programs use. The platform is a dual vector: a cell-selective enveloped delivery vector pairs with an AAV homology template, so the CAR cassette lands precisely where it is supposed to. In six rhesus macaques receiving a single intravenous dose with no lymphodepletion, they generated CAR T cells in vivo and achieved complete B-cell depletion across peripheral blood, lymph nodes, and bone marrow. This pairs nicely with the Tessera data we covered yesterday. Tessera showed permanent random integration of a CAR transgene from a lipid nanoparticle; Azalea shows targeted integration at the canonical engineered-T-cell locus from a viral platform. Two architectures, both delivered to T cells in vivo, both at ASGCT this week. The site-specific story is likely the one that matters most for safety, regulatory positioning, and durability.

That is your brief for Saturday. We will be back tomorrow.
