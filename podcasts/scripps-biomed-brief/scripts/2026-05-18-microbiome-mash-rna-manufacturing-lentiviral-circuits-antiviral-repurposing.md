## Script

Good morning, Pete. This is your Scripps Biomedical Brief for Monday, May eighteenth. Four bioRxiv stories today on the manufacturing and modality side of drug discovery — a microbiome-targeted small molecule for fatty liver disease, an engineered RNA polymerase for industrial messenger RNA synthesis, a lentiviral design framework for compact gene circuits, and an antiviral repurposing strategy for gut decolonization of Klebsiella.

First, from Bernd Schnabl's group at UC San Diego, a microbiome-targeted small molecule for metabolic dysfunction-associated steatohepatitis. The compound, called GR-seven, is a non-absorbable inhibitor of microbial bile salt hydrolase — a bacterial enzyme that deconjugates host bile acids in the gut. By restricting its activity to the lumen of the intestine, the molecule reprograms cecal bile acid pools without exposing the host systemically. In mouse MASH models, early intervention suppressed liver fibrosis and late-stage dosing reduced hepatic steatosis and inflammatory markers; the mechanism runs through reduced unconjugated primary and secondary bile acids, restored gut barrier function, and rewired hepatic bile acid metabolism. With the existing MASH field crowded around FGF twenty-one analogs, thyroid hormone receptor beta agonists, and GLP one glucagon co-agonists that all act on host receptors directly, this is a structurally different proposition — a non-systemic small molecule that drugs a bacterial enzyme to indirectly modulate FXR and TGR five signaling in the host. Worth watching how the chemistry translates from rodent gut to human.

Second, an enabling-technology paper for messenger RNA therapeutics from Pingjing Zhang's group at the Shanghai Cell Therapy Group. The bottleneck in industrial RNA manufacturing has been the trade-off between yield, transcript integrity, and double-stranded RNA byproducts that drive innate immune reactogenicity. The authors built chimeric T-seven RNA polymerases by fusing mutant catalytic domains to thermostable DNA-binding domains — Sso seven-d and MC one — and engineered salt tolerance up to two-hundred-seventy millimolar sodium chloride for in vitro transcription. The result is double-stranded RNA contamination below one one-thousandth of a percent of the product, and for circular RNA synthesis a fifty percent yield improvement reaching fifteen milligrams per milliliter in simple batch transcription. For circular RNA programs in particular — where double-stranded byproducts have been a persistent reactogenicity problem — this is a manufacturing-side fix that compounds with therapeutic-design improvements.

Third, from Kate Galloway's group at MIT, a design framework for lentiviral vectors that carry RNA-based gene circuits. Lentivirus packages around ten kilobases, which is enough for a multi-component circuit, but RNA regulatory devices like ribozymes and splicing switches have a way of degrading vector titer because they act on the viral RNA genome during production rather than waiting until after integration. The authors systematically dissected how genetic organization and component choice affect both titer and downstream expression, and arrived at design rules that lift titer over thirty-fold compared to naive configurations. The output is a usable two-gene lentiviral vector platform with embedded RNA-level control — directly relevant for any cell therapy program that wants ligand-responsive activation, feedback regulation, or context-dependent expression without abandoning lentivirus for harder delivery modalities.

And fourth, a repurposing story from Jay Vornhagen's group at Indiana University on Klebsiella pneumoniae — the antibiotic-resistant pathogen for which there is no approved gut decolonization therapy despite gut colonization being the precursor to most bacteremic, urinary, and pulmonary infections. The authors screened an antiviral compound library and identified six approved antiviral drugs with previously uncharacterized antibacterial activity against Klebsiella, with strain-specific potency across clinical isolates and context-dependent activity under simulated gut conditions. The mechanism remains to be worked out, but as a near-term clinical lever this is exactly the phenotype-first repurposing pattern that can move from preprint to investigator-initiated trial faster than de novo antibiotic development, given the existing safety data on the parent compounds.

That is your brief for Monday. We will be back tomorrow.
