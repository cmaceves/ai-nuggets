## Script

Good morning, Pete. This is your Scripps Biomedical Brief for Tuesday, May nineteenth. Four bioRxiv stories today, all on the target and modality side — an evolutionarily conserved longevity hormone axis downstream of mTOR, cryo-EM structures of a promiscuous bitter taste receptor with a new orthosteric site, a delivery-optimized suppressor transfer RNA strategy that rescues premature stop codons in mammalian brain, and a two-hour click-chemistry stain for active fibrosis.

First, from Adam Antebi's group at the Max Planck Institute for Biology of Ageing, a new node in the mTOR longevity pathway with a clear drug-target structure. mTOR downregulation extends life span across species, but the systemic mechanism — how a cellular nutrient sensor reaches across an organism to slow aging — has been thin on molecular detail. They show in C. elegans that deletion of the TORC one regulator raga-one drives production of dafachronic acid, a bile-acid-like steroid hormone, and the resulting life-span extension depends on the worm nuclear receptor DAF twelve, the homolog of mammalian farnesoid X receptor. A functional genomic screen identifies the short-chain dehydrogenase DHS twenty-six, whose mammalian ortholog DHRS-one, as a previously uncharacterized downstream effector of the axis. Mouse DHRS-one is also mTOR- and FXR-regulated, indicating the axis is conserved. The drug-discovery hook is the modality stack — FXR is already a clinical target across hepatology and metabolic disease, and an mTOR-DHRS-one-FXR longevity axis gives a pharmacological route to mTOR-like life-span effects without direct mTOR inhibition. Worth watching whether DHRS-one becomes a tractable target on its own.

Second, structural pharmacology of a bitter taste receptor from Anna Qiao's group at Tianjin University, with three cryo-EM structures of TAS-two-R-fourteen complexes — one of the most promiscuous GPCRs known, hit by hundreds of structurally diverse ligands. They solved the receptor bound to diiodosalicylic acid, flufenamic acid, and aristolochic acid at sub-two-and-a-half angstrom resolution, with a clear efficacy hierarchy across the three. Transmembrane helix six emerges as the central regulatory hub — agonist binding triggers a twelve-angstrom lever-like rearrangement that reshapes the G-protein interface and tunes coupling strength. The translational piece is the surprise hit. They identify cholesterol hemisuccinate as a new orthosteric agonist that selectively potentiates the diiodosalicylic acid signal about fivefold without affecting the other two ligands — a clean experimental demonstration of orthosteric-allosteric coupling that depends on which ligand is in the pocket. For drug discovery this matters because bitter taste receptors are increasingly being explored as drug targets beyond the tongue — there are credible programs in airway smooth muscle relaxation for asthma and in adipocyte browning — and a structural framework for ligand-specific allosteric modulation gives a path to selective tool compounds where every prior small molecule has been promiscuous.

Third, from Christopher Ahern's group at the University of Iowa, the cleanest delivery optimization yet for suppressor transfer RNAs in mammalian brain. Premature termination codons created by UGA stop mutations are the single most common class of rare-variant pathogenic mutation in neurodevelopmental disease, and suppressor tRNA therapy has been a promising modality stuck on delivery. They built a luciferase-UGA reporter mouse so payload activity can be read non-invasively from transcranial bioluminescence, then ran systematic optimization. Three results matter. First, the standard U-six promoter and the AAV-two-nine capsid both underperform — the field has been carrying the wrong configuration. Second, self-complementary AAV-two-nine with the suppressor tRNA in a minimal hundred-base-pair genomic context produces broad and efficacious stop-codon rescue across brain. Third, multiplexing the suppressor and including its native intron sustain efficacy at lower viral titers — the dose-sparing finding is exactly what's needed to move tRNA modalities into safe human dosing. Crucially, sequencing shows no perturbation of endogenous tRNA pools or their charging, which has been a recurring safety question for this approach. This is a credible delivery framework for any UGA-driven neurodevelopmental disorder.

And fourth, a chemical biology probe out of Mariane Le Fur's lab at the Martinos Center at Mass General, focused on a fibrosis problem that's slowed every anti-fibrotic drug development program. Lysyl oxidase catalyzes the lysine-to-allysine oxidation that crosslinks collagen and elastin and is the catalytic step driving aberrant matrix deposition in fibrotic disease and many tumors. There has not been a fast, robust histological assay for spatial lysyl oxidase activity in tissue — so target engagement in animal and human samples has been hard to verify. They built a bioorthogonal substrate plus a turn-on fluorophore that becomes fluorescent only after the click reaction with the enzyme's product. The protocol uses two commercial reagents, runs in under two hours, and is compatible with second harmonic generation and immunofluorescence on the same section. They validate it across healthy and fibrotic mouse and human tissue. For any anti-fibrotic program — and for the lysyl oxidase inhibitor pipelines that have struggled with target engagement readouts — this is a routine assay that closes a real gap.

That is your brief for Tuesday. We will be back tomorrow.
