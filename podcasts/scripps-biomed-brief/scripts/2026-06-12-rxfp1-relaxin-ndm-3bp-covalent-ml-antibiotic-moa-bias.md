# Scripps Biomedical Brief — 2026-06-12

## Script

Good morning. Today's brief: three preprints, all on bioRxiv this morning. A quick note up front — bioRxiv's full-text endpoints are gated by a Cloudflare challenge from this network this morning, so the takes below are grounded in the published abstracts and metadata. With that caveat, here are today's three.

First, from Joshua Osei-Owusu, Tristan Girbau and colleagues with Andrew Kruse at Harvard Medical School, divergent activation mechanisms for the relaxin receptor R-X-F-P-one resolved by cryo-electron microscopy. R-X-F-P-one is an unusual G-protein-coupled receptor with a long ectodomain that mediates the physiological adaptations of pregnancy and that is being clinically pursued in fibrosis and heart failure, where both protein and small-molecule agonists are now in mid-stage trials. The Kruse group reports three structures — ligand-free, bound to the native peptide hormone relaxin-two, and bound to the small-molecule drug candidate A-Z-D-five-four-six-two — and shows that these two ligand classes engage the receptor in completely different ways. Relaxin-two binds the ectodomain and induces a conformational reorganization of the linker domain into a helical secondary structure that propagates activation into the transmembrane bundle. A-Z-D-five-four-six-two, by contrast, binds inside the transmembrane domain itself and stabilizes a unique active conformation that selectively engages beta-arrestin. Hydrogen-deuterium-exchange mass spectrometry backs up the structural model. Critically, neither mechanism matches the so-called push-pull activation that has been characterized for the glycoprotein hormone receptors that R-X-F-P-one is structurally related to. The drug-discovery payoff is that medicinal chemistry around R-X-F-P-one now has an explicit structural framework for designing biased agonists at a target that, until recently, was effectively peptide-only.

Second, from Jacob Bradley, Karen Calvopina Tapia and colleagues with Adam Roberts at the Liverpool School of Tropical Medicine, and Chris Schofield's group at Oxford, a covalent strategy for inhibiting the New Delhi metallo-beta-lactamases N-D-M-one and N-D-M-five. Resistance to carbapenems, a clinical last-line antibiotic class, is largely driven by metallo-beta-lactamases, and no metallo-beta-lactamase inhibitors are approved for clinical use. The vast majority of reported inhibitors are zinc-ion chelators that act at the active site or in solution, and that strategy has historically struggled with selectivity and toxicity. The Roberts and Schofield groups show that the small-molecule three-bromopyruvate selectively restores the antimicrobial activity of meropenem against carbapenem-resistant clinical and environmental isolates of E. coli, Klebsiella pneumoniae and Acinetobacter baumannii from Tanzania and Malawi, but only in strains that carry N-D-M genes — not strains whose resistance is driven by serine beta-lactamases. Mass-spectrometry data on the purified enzymes support a mechanism in which three-bromopyruvate forms a covalent bond with an active-site cysteine. The bigger picture: this is the most direct evidence to date that a conserved active-site cysteine is a viable covalent handle on the metallo-beta-lactamase family. The molecule itself isn't a clinical candidate, but the result legitimizes covalent reactivity as a discovery strategy for metallo-beta-lactamases, the same playbook that produced the successful serine-beta-lactamase inhibitors.

Third, from Christopher Chain, Sara Ghaffari and colleagues with Zemer Gitai at Princeton, a thoughtful and somewhat sobering analysis of why machine-learning-driven antibiotic discovery keeps producing membrane-active hits and not compounds with truly new protein targets. The team built a machine-learning pipeline that, instead of post-hoc filtering by Tanimoto similarity, uses an X-G-Boost-based mechanism-of-action classifier up front to prioritize compounds predicted to have mechanisms distinct from known antibiotic classes, combined with graph neural networks for activity and toxicity prediction. Applied to the Zinc-twenty database, it did find non-toxic antibacterial compounds structurally distinct from approved antibiotics, and most of them were membrane-active with selectivity for bacterial over mammalian cells. None hit a novel protein target. The authors then go further: systematic analysis shows that the bottleneck is mechanistic bias in the training data — known antibacterials are dominated by a few mechanism classes, including membrane-targeting compounds, and the activity model preferentially learns to score lookalikes of those classes. Substantial changes to model architecture and to training data did not overcome the constraint. The implication for drug-discovery organizations betting on machine learning for antibiotics is that the gating problem isn't the model — it's curation. Investments in diverse, mechanistically-annotated chemical biology to expand the training set are likely a prerequisite for ML pipelines to deliver mechanistically novel antibacterials.

That's the brief. Three preprints pointing in three different directions: a structural framework for biased small-molecule agonism at the relaxin receptor, a covalent active-site cysteine handle on metallo-beta-lactamases as a route around the zinc-chelation problem, and a candid diagnosis of why machine-learning antibiotic pipelines keep yielding membrane disruptors and what would have to change to get past that. Until tomorrow.
