# Scripps Biomedical Brief — 2026-06-03

## Script

Good morning. Today's brief: an antibody-anchor add-on that solves the long-standing liver-tropism problem for lipid nanoparticles in a two-pipetting-step procedure, the first direct biochemical link between a tricarboxylic-acid-cycle metabolite and the active sites of Jumonji-domain histone demethylases, an engineered ribosome exit tunnel from the Schepartz and Cate groups that lets the translation machinery handle consecutive non-natural monomers, and a pathology-targeted prostaglandin-E-receptor-four agonist that reverses fibrotic muscle degeneration in a rat model of Duchenne muscular dystrophy.

First, from Benjamin Carter and colleagues at Hone Bio in Bristol, a platform called NanoPilot that takes on what is arguably the single largest bottleneck in extrahepatic delivery of lipid-nanoparticle-formulated nucleic acids — the liver consumes almost everything you put into systemic circulation. The mechanism has been understood for years: apolipoprotein E adsorbs onto the lipid-nanoparticle surface, the apolipoprotein-E-coated particle binds the low-density-lipoprotein receptor on hepatocytes, and the particle is internalized in the liver. NanoPilot is a modular fusion protein — an antibody against the target cell type at one end and an anchor that blocks the apolipoprotein-E binding site of low-density-lipoprotein receptor at the other. You add it to a preformulated lipid nanoparticle in ten minutes with two pipetting steps. With an anti-C-D-three-epsilon antibody version on human peripheral-blood mononuclear cells in vitro, they got a forty-fold increase in T-cell transfection and a ten-fold reduction in monocyte transfection. In immunocompetent mice, the same construct produced thirty to forty percent T-cell transfection in spleen and liver while bulk liver accumulation dropped three-fold. An anti-c-Kit version enhanced delivery to a hematopoietic-stem-cell-like line in co-culture. Functionally this is a generalizable retargeting layer that does not require reformulating the lipid nanoparticle itself, which means cell-therapy programs and in-vivo CAR-T or hematopoietic-stem-cell editing programs get a plug-in tool for cell-specific delivery.

Second, from Simone Sidoli's group with Julio Maianti at Albert Einstein College of Medicine, a result that connects metabolism directly to chromatin chemistry. We have known for a while that succinate accumulates in cancer cells with succinate-dehydrogenase mutations and in cells under metabolic stress, and that histone lysine residues get succinylated, but the active-site consequences of that mark were not well defined. Using quantitative proteomics and peptide pull-down, they identified Jumonji-domain-containing histone demethylases as direct interactors of succinyl-lysine peptides. The succinyl-lysine peptides bind and inhibit demethylase catalytic activity dose-dependently, including K-D-M-four-D and K-D-M-six-B, which remove the repressive H3-K9 and H3-K27 di- and trimethylation marks. Treating HepG2 hepatoma cells with supraphysiological succinate raised histone succinylation, raised the repressive methylation marks, and reduced transcriptional output, and combined CUT-and-Tag and chromatin-immunoprecipitation mass spectrometry confirmed that the succinylation marks co-occupy the same loci as the repressive methylation. The clean punch line is that a single metabolite acts as a direct demethylase ligand to stabilize heterochromatin, which gives the cancer-metabolism field a defined biochemical handle on why succinate-dehydrogenase loss alters the epigenome, and gives Jumonji-demethylase chemical-biology programs a new endogenous ligand class to design around.

Third, from Alanna Schepartz's and Jamie Cate's groups at Berkeley, a chemical-biology platform piece. The wild-type ribosome can incorporate many non-natural monomers, but it stalls when asked to incorporate runs of structurally challenging backbones — extended or constrained monomers that are exactly what you need to build the next generation of orally bioavailable peptide therapeutics and non-canonical biopolymers. The group engineered the ribosome exit tunnel itself and showed substantially improved consecutive incorporation of these challenging monomers. The implication for any group doing genetic-code-reprogramming or ribosomal display is direct: the bottleneck has been the tunnel, not the catalytic peptidyl-transferase center, and the tunnel is now a tunable handle.

Fourth, from Fabio Rossi at the University of British Columbia, with collaborators including Urs Häfeli and Paul Kostenuik, a target-and-delivery story for Duchenne muscular dystrophy. Adolescent patients hit a therapeutic gap that current interventions do not address: fibrotic and fatty replacement of muscle is well underway, the regenerative niche is depleted, and structural damage is not reversed by the standard of care. The team built a pathology-targeted prostaglandin-E-receptor-four agonist that homes to damaged muscle, and showed in a rat model of Duchenne muscular dystrophy that it reverses established fibrosis. The combination of a fibrotic-tissue-homing carrier with an E-P-four agonist as the payload is a clean architectural answer to a problem that pure systemic agonism could not solve, and it nominates E-P-four agonism as a target axis for the broader fibrotic-disease space where structural pathology has outpaced regenerative capacity.

That's the brief. Four reasons today to be optimistic about cell-specific extrahepatic delivery of lipid nanoparticles, a new metabolite-to-chromatin chemistry axis, ribosome engineering for non-natural polymer chemistries, and pathology-targeted reversal of muscle fibrosis. Until tomorrow.
