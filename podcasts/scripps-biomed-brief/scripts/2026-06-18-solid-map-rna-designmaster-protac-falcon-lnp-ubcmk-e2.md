# Scripps Biomedical Brief — 2026-06-18

## Script

Good morning. Today's brief is four bioRxiv preprints — a photoproximity-labeling chemistry that maps where small molecules bind on RNA, a diffusion model that finally bakes linker-length and physicochemical constraints into PROTAC generation, a closed-loop active-learning pipeline for lipid nanoparticles that delivers an eighty-four-fold improvement in cell-type selectivity, and the first standalone activity-based probe for E2 ubiquitin-conjugating enzymes.

First, from Zeshi Li's group at Utrecht University, SOLiD-MaP — short for singlet oxygen footprinting on RNA in a ligand-directed manner for mutational profiling. RNA-targeted small molecules are an emerging therapeutic modality, but defining where on the RNA a ligand actually binds has been hard, because existing crosslinking and covalent-capture chemistries depend on ligand-specific probe design, warhead compatibility, and local reaction geometry — every new compound class requires a new chemistry. SOLiD-MaP decouples the warhead from the binding event: the ligand carries a photosensitizer that generates singlet oxygen locally, and aniline acts as an efficient nucleophile that captures it on nearby ribonucleotides. Crucially, a soluble quencher constrains the diffusion radius of singlet oxygen, giving the user a dial for spatial resolution rather than a fixed footprint. A pairwise reverse-transcription-stop assay and mutational profiling with next-generation sequencing then read out ligand-proximal positions across the RNA. The team validated the system on the Mango-II aptamer with thiazole-orange-derived photosensitizers. The take is that this is an orthogonal, ligand-class-agnostic chemistry for RNA mechanism-of-action work — the kind of platform that lets a chemical biology program iterate on RNA-targeting chemotypes without rebuilding the assay each time.

Second, from Fuyi Li and colleagues at the University of Adelaide, DesignMaster — a diffusion-based generative framework for PROTAC design that explicitly conditions on linker length and physicochemical properties. The PROTAC linker is where most published AI design tools have hit a wall: structure-based ternary modeling and fragment-based linker generators both tend to ignore the length control and drug-like-property envelope that determines whether a ternary complex will actually form in cells. DesignMaster uses an E(3)-equivariant graph transformer with a gated multi-condition fusion module that injects linker length and physicochemical constraints throughout the diffusion trajectory, not just at the end. On PROTAC-DB version 2 and version 3, the model beats state-of-the-art baselines with a three-point-two percent improvement in validity and a thirty-four-point-four percent improvement in recovery, and in a case study targeting the structure designated 6W7O it cut linker RMSD against the reference PROTAC BCPyr by roughly half. For a degrader program, the practical implication is that the generator now respects the medicinal-chemistry constraints that PROTAC chemists already enforce by hand — a step toward AI linker design that is actually drop-in.

Third, from Hai-Quan Mao's group at Johns Hopkins, FALCON — a closed-loop active-learning pipeline that combines iterative wet-lab screening, surrogate modeling, and multi-objective optimization for lipid nanoparticle composition. The status quo in LNP design is brute-force factorial screening focused on one target tissue at a time. FALCON treats LNP optimization as a Bayesian-style closed loop where each round of in-vivo screening updates the surrogate and selects the next batch. In B-cell-targeted validation experiments, FALCON-optimized particles achieved a one-point-eight-fold increase in splenic B-cell transfection in vivo over reference compositions. When the objective was switched to selectivity rather than raw expression, FALCON-optimized particles delivered an eighty-four-fold improvement in selective transfection of splenic B cells over off-target liver — and the spleen-tropic behavior held across factorial panels of varying ionizable and helper lipid chemistries. In vaccine studies these particles drove higher IgG2c titers and a Th-1-biased profile. The system was also redeployed to optimize for myeloid-cell-selective delivery and recovered enhanced selectivity both across and within spleen and liver compartments. This is a credible replacement for the brute-force screening loop that has defined LNP development for the past decade.

Fourth, from Wenshe Liu's lab at Texas A&M, the first standalone activity-based probe for E2 ubiquitin-conjugating enzymes. E2s sit at the center of ubiquitin signaling but their catalytic cysteines are weakly nucleophilic with elevated pKa values, and existing ubiquitin probes mostly target deubiquitinases — the one reported E2-targeting probe requires E1-dependent activation and captures only a limited panel. Density-functional-theory calculations identified chloromethylketone as an unusually electrophilic warhead with a low activation barrier specifically for weakly nucleophilic thiolates. The team synthesized ubiquitin-chloromethylketone by activated-cysteine-based protein ligation, and activity-based protein profiling in HEK293T lysates enriched a broad set of E2 enzymes including many that had been inaccessible to prior probes. The probe also engages E1s and deubiquitinases, giving a single chemical tool that covers most of the ubiquitin-proteasome system, and the team used it to quantify endogenous E2 mobilization across oxidative, proteotoxic, inflammatory, metabolic, lipid-oxidative, and genotoxic stress. For a targeted-protein-degradation program that wants to interrogate E2 selectivity directly rather than inferring it from downstream readouts, this is the chemical handle the field has been missing.
