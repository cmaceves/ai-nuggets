# Scripps Biomedical Brief — 2026-06-10

## Script

Good morning. Today's brief: a paired-paper proof that the myeloid and neuroimmune checkpoint I-L-T-three — long pursued as an antibody target — is in fact small-molecule druggable, from Moustafa Gabr's group at Weill Cornell Medicine, with one chemistry validated for Alzheimer's disease and a second chemistry validated for tumor immunity; a ubiquitin-independent proteasome activation feedback loop running through Z-F-A-N-D-five and six and constraining N-F-kappa-B inflammatory signaling, from Paul Lehner's group at Cambridge; a striking demonstration from Alexander Kros's group at Leiden that the stereochemistry of the ionizable lipid in a lipid nanoparticle reshapes the particle's internal lipid phase and the protein expression of its m-R-N-A payload; and a single-molecule blood-based liquid biopsy from Cosomil and the University of Tokyo that profiles circulating pancreatic enzyme activity to detect pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma at stage one with seventy-five percent sensitivity.

First, from Sherif Abdel-Rahman, Nirmal Murugan and Moustafa Gabr at Weill Cornell Medicine, two back-to-back preprints establishing that I-L-T-three — also known as L-I-L-R-B-four — is a tractable small-molecule target across two very different disease contexts. I-L-T-three is an inhibitory myeloid receptor with a confirmed role both as a tumor immune checkpoint that restrains anti-tumor immunity and, more recently, as a neuroimmune checkpoint in Alzheimer's disease, where it restricts microglial activation and amyloid clearance through ApoE-dependent signaling. To date it has been pursued exclusively as an antibody target. The Gabr lab uses two orthogonal chemical-biology platforms to find the first direct-binding small molecules. In the first paper, affinity selection mass spectrometry against recombinant I-L-T-three identifies the chemotype designated L-T-twelve, with nanomolar binding confirmed by microscale thermophoresis and surface plasmon resonance, a discrete binding pocket mapped by computational modeling and mutagenesis, and disruption of the I-L-T-three to ApoE interaction. In human i-P-S-C-derived microglia, L-T-twelve attenuates S-H-P-one and S-H-P-two signaling, suppresses N-F-kappa-B activation, lowers interleukin-one-beta secretion, and restores amyloid-beta uptake; in 5-x-F-A-D mice it improves cognition and reduces amyloid burden. In the second paper, a Dianthus temperature-related intensity change screen of an eight-thousand-nine-hundred-sixty-one-member Enamine library yields a separate chemotype designated I-C-B-seven, binding in a hydrophobic D-two-domain pocket, disrupting the I-L-T-three to S-C-G-two interaction, and inhibiting downstream S-H-P-one, S-H-P-two, and S-T-A-T-three signaling. In patient-derived colorectal carcinoma and acute myeloid leukemia co-cultures, I-C-B-seven enhances cytotoxic T-cell killing; in the C-T-twenty-six syngeneic colorectal model it shows favorable pharmacokinetics and meaningful anti-tumor efficacy. Two takeaways: I-L-T-three is now a small-molecule target with chemical matter from two independent screening platforms, and a single myeloid checkpoint has rigorous preclinical proof-of-concept in both an inflammation-driven C-N-S degenerative disease and in solid tumor immunotherapy.

Second, from Sebastian Watson, James Williamson and colleagues with Paul Lehner at Cambridge, a previously hidden regulatory loop in the proteasome activation pathway that links targeted protein degradation, E-two enzyme abundance, and innate inflammatory signaling. Using pulsed S-I-L-A-C proteomics, the team systematically maps proteins that the proteasome degrades without first being ubiquitinated — a process called ubiquitin-independent proteasomal degradation. Among the substrates they identify the paralogous proteasome activation factors Z-F-A-N-D-five and Z-F-A-N-D-six. Z-F-A-N-D-five carries a ubiquitin-independent degron that drives its own rapid turnover by the very proteasome it allosterically activates, coupling activation and activator destruction in a single feedback circuit. The same factors, in concert with the autophagy scaffold p-sixty-two, restrain N-F-kappa-B activation downstream of T-L-R-four, and the team traces that restraint to the degradation of U-B-C-H-five-c, an abundant E-two ubiquitin-conjugating enzyme that primes ubiquitin chain synthesis. For a drug-discovery audience, this matters in two ways: the catalogue of proteins susceptible to ubiquitin-independent proteasomal degradation is larger than appreciated and intersects directly with the substrate space for targeted protein degradation, and inflammatory signaling sits on top of an E-two enzyme whose abundance is tunable through proteasome activator dynamics — an entry point for indirect modulation of innate immunity.

Third, from Daniel Aschmann, Renske Knol and colleagues with Alexander Kros at Leiden University, a clean dissection of how the stereochemistry of an ionizable lipid reshapes the supramolecular architecture of a lipid nanoparticle and, with it, the protein expression of its m-R-N-A cargo. The ionizable lipid c-K-K-E-twelve is normally used as a stereoisomer mixture. The Kros team resolves it into its trans form — R-S and S-R — and its cis form — R-R and S-S — and finds that L-N-Ps assembled with the cis isomer have superior physicochemical properties, improved stability, and higher protein expression. By then systematically varying the stereochemistry of the ionizable lipid, the phospholipid, and cholesterol, they show that stereochemistry-dependent differences in cellular uptake and protein expression hold across six cell lines, in zebrafish embryos, and in mice. A-I-assisted cryo-electron microscopy and small-angle X-ray scattering trace the functional differences to a stereochemistry-controlled selection between lamellar and inverse hexagonal internal lipid phases, with attendant changes in sample uniformity. The practical message for the R-N-A delivery field is that the chiral identity of every lipid in the assembly — not just the ionizable lipid headgroup — is a previously underexploited engineering handle on internal architecture and translation output.

Fourth, from Shuhei Sakamoto, Hiroshi Hiraide and colleagues with Yu Kagami at Cosomil and collaborators across the University of Tokyo and multiple Japanese hospitals, a blood-based liquid biopsy that detects pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma at early stage using single-molecule profiling of pancreatic enzyme activity. The platform — single-molecule enzyme activity profiling, or S-E-A-P — detects proteoform-level alterations in circulating pancreatic digestive enzymes at single-molecule resolution, bypassing the abundance and noise problems that limit protein-marker assays for this disease. A tissue-centric biomarker discovery strategy identified activity signatures of pancreas-specific digestive enzymes associated with P-D-A-C, and a combinatorial classifier was evaluated across six-hundred-ninety blood samples from multiple hospitals and biobanks. It detected P-D-A-C across stages one through four at ninety-five-point-five percent specificity and seventy-five percent sensitivity, with stage one and stage two sensitivity at seventy-four-point-two percent. In an independent validation cohort enriched for early-stage disease, it called positive on fifty-four percent of stage one-A, sixty-four percent of stage one-B, and twenty-one percent of stage zero pancreatic intraepithelial lesions. P-D-A-C survival is overwhelmingly determined by stage at detection, and a single-molecule activity readout that engages with the disease's earliest stages from a blood draw is the kind of platform that, if it holds up in prospective trials, would change how this cancer is found.

That's the brief. Four reasons today to be optimistic about the chemical-biology and drug-discovery toolkit: a paired-paper validation that a long-pursued myeloid and neuroimmune checkpoint is small-molecule druggable across both Alzheimer's disease and tumor immunity, a hidden ubiquitin-independent proteasome activation feedback circuit that connects degrader biology to inflammatory signaling, a stereochemistry-controlled engineering handle for lipid nanoparticle delivery, and a single-molecule pancreatic enzyme activity assay that detects pancreatic cancer at stage one from a blood draw. Until tomorrow.
