## Script

Good morning, Pete. This is your Scripps Biomedical Brief for Sunday, May seventeenth. Four short stories from a quiet Sunday cycle — three bioRxiv preprints and one agentic-AI tooling paper — covering cell engineering, drug repurposing, structural biology, and AI-driven target nomination.

First, Kyle Daniels' group at Stanford posted a synthetic biology paper this week that may matter for the next generation of cell therapies. The field has spent a decade engineering T cells with chimeric antigen receptors, but macrophages — which dominate the tumor microenvironment — have remained much harder to control. The Daniels lab built a library of synthetic cytokine receptors with programmable extracellular ligand-binding domains paired with custom intracellular signaling modules, then used them to polarize primary human macrophages into user-defined functional states on demand. They validated a two-state mathematical model that predicts polarization outcomes from receptor composition, and they showed a thirty-fold reduction in tumor burden in vivo. This is what programmable cell therapy looks like at the macrophage level, and unlike CAR-T, it sidesteps the antigen-escape and persistence problems that plague T cell programs in solid tumors.

Second, a drug repurposing story for a rare vascular disease, from a collaboration centered at the Institut Pasteur de Montevideo with Patrick Marambaud. Hereditary hemorrhagic telangiectasia is driven by loss of function in the BMP-nine, ALK-one endothelial signaling pathway, and standard of care today is largely VEGF inhibitors aimed at consequence rather than cause. The authors screened for activators of the upstream cascade and identified nitazoxanide — a long-approved oral antiparasitic with decades of clinical safety data — as a SMAD one, five, and eight activator that restores ID-one expression in patient endothelial cells carrying HHT mutations and reduces vascular malformations in mouse models. In parallel it suppresses m-tor activity, which the authors argue corrects a counterproductive metabolic cascade in the diseased endothelium. The implication for translational timelines is significant — this is a generic-approved oral drug, not a new chemical entity, so investigator-initiated trials could move in months, not years.

Third, a cryo-EM paper from Lorena Saelices at UT Southwestern that should reframe how the field thinks about central nervous system ATTR amyloidosis. As tafamidis, acoramidis, and the TTR silencers have prolonged peripheral survival, ATTR deposits are increasingly appearing in the brains of long-surviving patients — and we have been treating them with stabilizer pharmacology designed against peripheral fibril architecture. The Saelices group solved brain-derived ATTR fibril structures from V-thirty-M and V-thirty-G patients and finds they adopt folds that are distinct from the peripheral fibrils we have characterized for the last decade. Local tissue context shapes amyloid architecture. The mechanistic question is whether the existing stabilizers — which bind tetrameric TTR before fibril formation — translate to the CNS, and the structural question is whether brain-fold-specific small molecules or antibodies are needed for clearance. Either way, the next wave of ATTR programs needs to design against brain fibrils on their own terms.

And fourth, an agentic-AI tooling paper from Tomas Helikar's group at Nebraska that fits where the AI-for-drug-discovery field is heading. The system, called MechAInistic, is an LLM-guided multi-agent layer over genome-scale constraint-based metabolic models — natural-language questions get converted into executable workflows that run perturbation analysis, pathway comparison, and target nomination against the model. The case studies are immune cell metabolic models: the system identifies mitochondrial rewiring in rheumatoid arthritis and nominates candidate drug targets for multiple sclerosis. This is not generative chemistry and it is not a foundation model — it is a careful, model-grounded reasoning layer over the kind of mechanistic systems biology that has been sitting underused for years. Worth tracking as a template for how agentic AI gets paired with constraint-based mechanistic models rather than purely black-box statistical ones.

That is your brief for Sunday. We will be back tomorrow.
