## Script

Welcome to The Genome Brief for Friday, July twenty-fourth, twenty twenty-six.

Today: a ferret study from the Kawaoka lab that asks the biggest question about the ongoing US dairy H five N one outbreak — how close is this virus to picking up the mammalian adaptations it would need for efficient airborne spread — and comes back with a genuinely useful answer. A new preprint from Yunlong Cao's group in Beijing on the SARS cov two variants that are taking over the Asia-Pacific right now, showing a strange but classical evolutionary trade-off in the way they're evading immunity. GSK gets an early FDA nod for its first lung-cancer drug, courtesy of a company it bought only weeks ago. A microbiome foundation model out of China that tries to do for gut bugs and wastewater what protein language models did for proteins — and shows that it works for exactly the surveillance problem we keep talking about on this show. And in the Gulf of California, someone finally filmed a group of orcas performing a hunting move that involves ramming a very large fish so hard it explodes.

Let's start with pathogens.

Some background before the first story. Highly pathogenic avian influenza — clade two point three point four point four B, subtype H five N one — has been circulating in US dairy cattle since early twenty twenty-four. The lineage that infected the cows is now catalogued as genotype B three point one three, and it has spread across hundreds of herds in multiple states, with sporadic human infections in farm workers. The great worry is not what H five N one is doing to cows today. The worry is that every time this virus replicates in a mammal, it gets another shot at accumulating the mutations it would need to spread efficiently between mammals — which is the threshold that separates a scary agricultural outbreak from a human pandemic. The specific adaptations people watch for are things like changes in the polymerase gene P B two, changes in the hemagglutinin protein that shift receptor preference from bird-type to human-type sialic acid, and changes that raise the pH at which the viral membrane fuses with a host cell — all textbook signatures of a flu virus tuning itself to a mammalian host.

The question this new bioRxiv preprint from Thomas Friedrich, Yoshihiro Kawaoka, and colleagues at the University of Wisconsin actually asks is not "does H five N one eventually adapt in mammals" — everyone knows the answer to that is yes if you give it enough tries. The question is "how likely is that adaptation to happen in a single acute ferret infection, or during one round of ferret-to-ferret transmission." Ferrets are the standard mammalian model for influenza because their respiratory tract is anatomically and immunologically closer to a human's than a mouse or a rat, and because ferrets show pathology and transmission dynamics that map reasonably well to human flu.

They infected ferrets with two different bovine-derived B three point one three H five N ones and, importantly, they didn't just look at whether the ferrets got sick or transmitted. They deep-sequenced viral populations from every ferret at every time point they could sample. Deep sequencing means you don't just get the consensus sequence — the average sequence of the virus in that animal. You get the full spectrum of genetic variants circulating within each animal, including rare mutations that only a small fraction of viruses in the population carry. Those rare within-host variants are the raw material of adaptation. If a mammalian-adapting mutation is going to arise, this is the pool it has to come from.

Two findings are important. First, within any single ferret, viral genetic diversity was low, and the mutations that did show up were subject to weak purifying selection — a term that means, roughly, "most new mutations are neutral or slightly harmful, and there's no obvious signal of the virus being pushed toward specific mammalian-favored changes." No smoking-gun advantageous mutation appears and sweeps to high frequency. Second — and this is the more striking one — when transmission from one ferret to another does happen, it happens through what virologists call a stringent bottleneck. Only a tiny number of viral particles from the donor animal successfully colonize the recipient. That bottleneck is a randomizing filter: whatever rare adaptive variants might be accumulating in the donor almost never make it through. Transmission actively erases within-host diversity rather than preserving the fittest lineages.

Put those together and you get a mechanistic reason for a pattern that had been sitting in the epidemiological data without a good explanation. Bovine H five N ones replicate well in ferrets. But they transmit inefficiently between ferrets, and this study says the reason is not just some biophysical block on the receptor side — it's also that the evolutionary machinery inside a single infection isn't being pushed hard toward mammalian adaptation, and each transmission event effectively resets the game. The authors frame this as an evolutionary constraint or ceiling.

Now — the appropriate way to hold this finding. It is not a reason to relax. The paper is explicit that continued circulation of clade two point three point four point four B viruses gives rare-but-consequential events repeated chances to happen. What it does do is tell you why the past eighteen months have not already produced a pandemic-capable strain, and it points to what to watch for. If you start seeing bovine H five N ones with expanded within-host diversity, or wider transmission bottlenecks, or specific selection signals in the polymerase or hemagglutinin, that's your warning sign. And that's the kind of signal that only shows up if you're deep-sequencing the animal cases, not just typing them for surveillance. Which is another argument, on top of all the others, for keeping molecular surveillance on the dairy outbreak up and running.

Second pathogen story. In the last three months, a specific pair of SARS cov two sub-lineages has quietly gone from noise to dominance across the Asia-Pacific — one called P Q dot sixteen dot one dot one, the other called R K dot one. They descend from the variant NB dot one dot eight dot one that Genome Brief listeners will remember from earlier this year, and they carry — independently, in both lineages — the same new mutation. The mutation is D four hundred twenty N, in the receptor-binding domain of the spike protein. That's the part of spike that plugs into the human A C E two receptor to open the door into a cell. The fact that two separate sub-lineages have hit on the same amino acid change at the same position is what evolutionary biologists call convergent evolution, and it's usually the strongest possible signal that whatever the mutation is doing is being actively selected for.

Yunlong Cao's group at Peking University, which has been the most consistent producer of variant-characterization papers throughout the pandemic, posted a preprint this week that maps out the trade-off underneath D four hundred twenty N. Using surface plasmon resonance, which is a biophysical technique that measures how tightly two proteins stick to each other in real time, and using pseudovirus assays, which are non-replicating viral particles engineered to display the variant's spike, they measure two things. How tightly does the variant spike bind A C E two? And how well does it evade neutralizing antibodies from people who've been vaccinated or previously infected?

The answer is the classical viral trade-off. D four hundred twenty N reduces how tightly spike grips A C E two — that's a fitness cost, because it makes each infection event slightly less efficient. But it does that in a very targeted way: the position of the mutation lies in the epitope where Class one neutralizing antibodies bind — those are the most abundant and most potent antibodies produced against the original Wuhan spike. So plasma from people originally exposed to Wuhan-Hu-one-derived vaccines has much less neutralizing activity against these variants. Effectively, the virus has paid a small cost in raw receptor binding to buy itself a big discount on population-level immunity. Convergent evolution finding this trade-off in two lineages at once says the discount is worth it in the current immune landscape.

The near-term implication for anyone thinking about the coming respiratory-virus season is that P Q dot sixteen dot one dot one and R K dot one are almost certainly going to spread out of the Asia-Pacific into Europe and North America over the next couple of months, and the D four hundred twenty N-carrying background is what an updated vaccine formulation should be trying to cover.

On to biotech.

On Wednesday, the FDA approved a drug called Jideytro — the generic name is zidesamtinib — for adults with locally advanced or metastatic ROS one-positive non-small cell lung cancer whose disease has progressed after a prior ROS one inhibitor. This is worth explaining because it's an unusually clean case of targeted oncology working as designed, and it's tangled up in a very large corporate deal.

The biology. Non-small cell lung cancer is a heterogeneous group of tumors, and in a small subset — roughly one to two percent — the driver mutation is a chromosomal rearrangement that fuses the ROS one gene, which normally codes for a receptor tyrosine kinase, to some other partner gene. The fusion protein is constitutively active — it's a kinase that's always on — and it drives uncontrolled cell growth. ROS one-positive lung cancers are typically diagnosed in younger patients, often never-smokers, and the tumors have a strong tendency to spread to the brain. That last point matters a lot in a moment.

There are already several approved ROS one inhibitors — drugs that specifically block the ROS one kinase. But cancer being cancer, resistance eventually emerges. The dominant resistance mechanism is a point mutation in the ROS one kinase domain called G two thousand thirty-two R — a single amino-acid change that changes the shape of the drug-binding pocket enough that the earlier ROS one inhibitors no longer fit. Once G two thousand thirty-two R shows up, or once the cancer moves into the brain and the drug can't reach it, the previous line of therapy stops working. That was the unmet need.

Zidesamtinib was engineered for exactly that gap. It's designed to be a selective ROS one inhibitor with two specific properties. First, it retains activity against the G two thousand thirty-two R resistance mutant — meaning the drug still fits into the mutated ATP-binding pocket. Second, it crosses the blood-brain barrier, so it reaches brain metastases at concentrations that matter therapeutically. In the Phase one two trial called Arrows one, in a hundred seventeen patients who had already progressed on a prior ROS one inhibitor, the overall response rate was forty-four percent, with responses lasting a median well over a year. That's a striking result in a heavily pre-treated population, and it's the reason the FDA gave zidesamtinib both Breakthrough Therapy and Orphan Drug designations and approved it about eight weeks ahead of its target action date.

The corporate context is that zidesamtinib was not developed by GSK. It was developed by Nuvalent — a small Boston biotech founded specifically to build next-generation kinase inhibitors that handle resistance mutations. GSK acquired Nuvalent for about ten point six billion dollars, a deal that closed a few weeks ago. So the drug that got approved on Wednesday is the first approved medicine to come out of the Nuvalent acquisition, and it becomes GSK's first approved lung-cancer medicine full stop. The market for a next-generation ROS one inhibitor is small on paper — maybe fifty thousand ROS one-positive lung-cancer patients diagnosed worldwide per year — but the value proposition is high per patient, because the current option is basically no option, and Nuvalent also has a similar next-generation ALK inhibitor in late-stage development targeting a much larger patient population using the same engineering playbook. That's the shape of the bet GSK is making.

Now to A-I in biomedicine.

There's a preprint out of Kang Ning's group at Huazhong University of Science and Technology in Wuhan that got posted this week and that will interest anyone who works on microbiomes or wastewater. The paper introduces a model called M G M two, and the framing is that the way we've been analyzing microbiome data for the last twenty years is now the bottleneck.

The setup. A microbiome study — whether it's the gut of a patient population, or a soil sample, or effluent from a wastewater treatment plant — starts by sequencing the ribosomal RNA genes present in that sample. What you get out of the sequencer is millions of short DNA sequences. The standard workflow clusters these into what are called operational taxonomic units, or O T Us — essentially, DNA fingerprints assumed to correspond to distinct microbes — and then reports the abundance of each O T U in the sample. That gets you an abundance table. From there, almost every downstream analysis in the field — is this microbiome healthy, is this patient going to respond to a fecal transplant, is this wastewater catchment about to see an outbreak — is done on top of the abundance table. The problem is that this format throws away almost all the sequence information. It reduces a biologically rich sample to a spreadsheet of "how much of taxon X" columns, and every study is stuck reinventing its own statistical model on its own cohort.

M G M two is a foundation model that tries to change that. Same idea that powered protein language models a few years ago and biomedical text foundation models more recently: you pretrain a very large neural network on an enormous unlabeled dataset, get it to learn a general representation of the domain, and then downstream tasks are formulated as fine-tuning problems on top of that representation. The authors pretrained M G M two on one point eight million samples from the MicrobeAtlas database, covering roughly two hundred twenty-five thousand O T Us. It combines the sequence of each O T U with its abundance in the community, and it learns representations that are aware of both what a microbe is and what community it's sitting in.

The proof that this actually helps comes from comparing frozen M G M two representations — meaning representations the model produces without any task-specific retraining — against DeepPhylo, a previous state-of-the-art. Across five different microbiome classification tasks — from source-tracking, where you predict what environment a sample came from, down to fine-grained taxonomic labels — M G M two beat DeepPhylo by between six and twenty-one points of macro area-under-the-curve. It performed best exactly where classical methods perform worst, which is on rare labels and fine-grained distinctions. On predicting whether a patient would respond to fecal microbiota transplantation, it hit an area-under-the-curve of point seven-nine, and it forecast species-level trends across twenty-four wastewater treatment plants over time.

Why this matters for a show that talks about surveillance most days. Wastewater surveillance has become one of the most important early-warning systems for respiratory viruses, but for bacterial pathogens and antimicrobial resistance in wastewater the data is much richer and much less analyzed. The reason is that bacterial community data is noisy, high-dimensional, and cohort-specific in ways that classical statistics struggle with. A foundation model that has already seen a million and a half microbiome samples and knows what "normal" looks like across ecological contexts is much better positioned to flag anomalies — an antibiotic-resistance gene expanding in a specific catchment, an unusual species emerging in a specific city's sewers. Whether M G M two specifically becomes the model that's used, or whether it gets superseded by the next one in six months, the direction is real: microbiome analysis is going to move from abundance tables to learned representations, in the same way protein biology moved from sequence alignments to embeddings.

And finally, animals.

A study published yesterday in Frontiers in Ethology, led by Kathryn Ayres of the non-profit Beneath the Waves, describes a behavior in orcas that no one had formally documented before. In two separate encounters in the Gulf of California — one in July twenty twenty-four, one in September twenty twenty-five — a group of orcas including a juvenile were filmed doing the following. One adult orca picks up a sharp-tail sunfish by the tail fin, and holds it in place. A second adult orca then accelerates and rams the sunfish head-first at high speed, hard enough that the force of the collision breaks the fish's tissue apart, sending fragments dispersing through the water column. The juvenile orca then eats the smaller pieces while the adults take the larger portions.

A little context on the target. Sharp-tail sunfish are relatives of the giant ocean sunfish, and they are enormous — several hundred kilograms, disc-shaped, one of the heaviest bony fishes in the sea, with a thick layer of tough, gelatinous connective tissue that most predators can't easily bite through. Getting to the softer inner tissue of a sunfish is a real engineering problem for a marine mammal that doesn't have hands or tools. The paper's authors christen this new behavior "ram-to-fragment predation."

Now the interpretive question. Two events, over about a year, is a very small sample, and there are several possible reasons this behavior might exist. The most straightforward is mechanical. The sunfish is too big and too tough for a juvenile to reasonably eat whole, and ramming it apart is a way for the adults to portion the carcass into calf-sized pieces. That would put ram-to-fragment in the same category as other documented orca behaviors that seem to explicitly teach or provision juveniles. A second possibility is nutritional or microbiome-related — some interior parts of the sunfish that would otherwise be out of reach get exposed by the impact, and that includes gut microbiota the calf might benefit from ingesting. And the third possibility, which the paper cannot rule out and clearly enjoys not ruling out, is that some fraction of what these orcas are doing is play. Ramming is not previously a documented orca hunting strategy. But orcas have documented cultural behaviors, they clearly transmit them, and it wouldn't be the first time a marine mammal invented a novel activity that spread through a group essentially because it seemed like fun.

Whichever of those turns out to be right, the fact remains that in twenty twenty-six the scientific literature now contains a peer-reviewed paper describing a group of orcas exploding an eight-hundred-pound fish for reasons that are not entirely clear.
