## Script

Good morning. Welcome to The Genome Brief. It's Tuesday, July fourteenth, twenty twenty-six. Four stories today, plus a spider that isn't a spider.

On the pathogen front, a very large TB study out of Vietnam makes an uncomfortable argument: the standard rapid test that most of the world uses to diagnose tuberculosis is, in a real sense, manufacturing multi-drug-resistant TB. We'll walk through the data, and then check in on the Bundibugyo Ebola outbreak in the Democratic Republic of the Congo, which is still growing, and a new modelling paper that asks what would actually cut the death toll.

On the business side, Eisai presented Phase 1b/2 data at the big Alzheimer's conference on a drug called etalanetug. This one targets tau tangles rather than amyloid plaques, and the biomarker numbers are striking — up to a ninety percent drop in the tau species they were measuring.

On the AI front, a systematic third-party audit of AlphaFold3, the DeepMind protein structure model, is out from the Beltrao lab at ETH Zurich. The finding, in short, is that the extension of AlphaFold from proteins to everything else — RNA, lipids, small molecules, antibodies — is much more uneven than the marketing suggests.

And to close: harvestmen. The spider-like arachnids you have almost certainly seen and probably called daddy-longlegs, though they are not spiders and not even that closely related. It turns out that they are running away with the arthropod-fatherhood championship, and a big chunk of the new evidence came from people uploading photos to iNaturalist.

Let's start in Ho Chi Minh City.

TB. Tuberculosis kills more people every year than any other single infectious disease. About one point two three million deaths in twenty twenty-four alone. And the version of TB that everyone in public health worries about most is rifampin-resistant TB — often called RR-TB, or when it is also resistant to isoniazid, MDR-TB, multi-drug-resistant TB. Rifampin is one of the two drugs that anchor the standard first-line TB regimen. If your bacterium is resistant to rifampin, that regimen no longer works reliably, and you get moved to a longer, more toxic, more expensive second-line treatment.

Here is the diagnostic reality on the ground. The World Health Organization has, since around twenty eleven, recommended a rapid molecular test for TB. The most common one is called Xpert MTB/RIF. You spit into a cartridge, the machine spins for a couple of hours, and it tells you two things. One, do you have TB. Two, does that TB carry a mutation that makes it resistant to rifampin. That is it. It does not test for resistance to any other drug in the regimen. In particular, it does not test for resistance to isoniazid, the other backbone drug.

That is a design choice. Rifampin resistance is the single biggest clinical decision point, so it's what the test was built for. But it means that a large group of patients — the ones whose TB is resistant to isoniazid but still susceptible to rifampin — get treated as if they have a fully drug-susceptible infection. They get the standard four-drug regimen. That regimen contains isoniazid, which is basically inert in their infection, and rifampin, which is doing most of the work. That situation — one drug carrying most of the load, with weaker companions around it — is the textbook recipe for evolving resistance to the drug that is doing the work.

The new paper posted to medRxiv this weekend, from a group at Oxford University Clinical Research Unit in Ho Chi Minh City, is the largest, cleanest test of that hypothesis we have. Between twenty twenty and twenty twenty-four, they enrolled essentially all adults diagnosed with rifampin-susceptible pulmonary TB in Ho Chi Minh City — nearly forty-three thousand people in total. They banked more than thirty-three thousand sputum samples. Then they waited. They tracked whether patients came back with a second TB episode, and if they did, they whole-genome-sequenced both the first and the second isolate.

That whole-genome-sequencing step is important. Whole-genome sequencing means reading the entire genetic code of the bacterium. If you sequence the strain from a patient's first infection and the strain from their second infection, you can compare them base by base. If the two are near-identical, this is the same infection coming back — a relapse. If the two are wildly different, the patient caught a new infection from someone else — a reinfection. That distinction is critical, because if the second, resistant strain was caught fresh from another person, the resistance didn't evolve in this patient. But if the two strains are near-identical except for a new resistance mutation, then that mutation appeared under treatment. That is de novo resistance, evolution in real time, inside one person.

Here is the finding. Of the eight hundred seventy-three participants with complete data, fifty-one — about six percent — came back with a genuinely acquired rifampin resistance mutation. And of those fifty-one, forty-nine had undetected isoniazid resistance in their original strain at the time of first diagnosis. Ninety-six percent. Almost every case of new rifampin resistance emerged in a patient whose TB was already isoniazid resistant, and where the standard rapid test had missed it.

They then quantified the risk. After all the statistical adjustments for age, sex, HIV, diabetes, and TB lineage, the estimated risk of acquiring rifampin resistance was about three percent in patients with undetected isoniazid-resistant TB — and essentially zero, three-hundredths of one percent, in patients whose TB was fully susceptible from the start. That is a hundred-fold difference. The relative risk ratio came out at one hundred five.

To translate: if you diagnose TB with a test that only looks for rifampin resistance, and you happen to miss that the strain is already isoniazid-resistant, that patient has roughly a one-in-thirty-three chance of walking out of your clinic with rifampin-susceptible TB and coming back later with the multi-drug-resistant version. In someone diagnosed with fully susceptible TB, that risk is negligible.

They also built a transmission model. In Vietnam, if you rolled out universal isoniazid-susceptibility testing and appropriate treatment for the isoniazid-resistant strains, they project a forty-six percent reduction in new multi-drug-resistant TB cases over ten years. Even in a setting where isoniazid resistance is much rarer — five percent of TB, for the sake of argument — the projected reduction was still around twenty-six percent.

Why this matters. First, TB kills over a million people a year, and the multi-drug-resistant version is much harder and much more expensive to treat, with worse survival. If a substantial fraction of that MDR burden is being generated inside clinics by our own diagnostic blind spot, that is fixable. Second, the argument for a two-drug-target rapid test — one that reads out both rifampin and isoniazid resistance from the same cartridge — has now moved from "nice to have" to "the standard of care is actively harmful without it." Tests with that capability already exist, and are more expensive; this paper is going to shift that cost-effectiveness calculation. Third, this is a great example of why treating TB with a diagnostic-plus-drug regimen tuned to only the biggest bug is a false economy. The unmeasured resistance is where the next generation of superbugs comes from.

That is the Vietnam paper. It is a preprint, but the design is prospective, the numbers are enormous, and the mechanism — treatment failure under an inert companion drug — is straightforward biology.

Staying on the pathogen front but moving to viral surveillance: an update from the Democratic Republic of the Congo. We've been following the Bundibugyo Ebola outbreak in Ituri province for a few weeks now, and the numbers this week are worse.

Bundibugyo Ebola is one of the species of ebolavirus — the same genus as the Zaire ebolavirus responsible for the enormous West African outbreak in twenty fourteen and twenty fifteen. Bundibugyo is generally described as less lethal than Zaire, with historical case fatality rates around thirty to forty percent. In this outbreak, the case fatality is running higher.

As of the WHO update dated July twelfth, the DRC has reported one thousand nine hundred twenty-six confirmed cases, and seven hundred two deaths. That is up from about seventeen hundred cases and five hundred eighty deaths when we last checked in on July seventh. Roughly a hundred and twenty new deaths in five days. Ituri province is the epicenter with one thousand seven hundred forty-five of the cases and about six hundred of the deaths, spread across twenty-six of the province's thirty-six health zones. In Uganda, cross-border cases are holding steady at twenty confirmed and two deaths — no new cases since June twenty-first, which is genuinely good news on that side. The DRC also has more than seven hundred patients hospitalized in isolation right now, and more than ten thousand contacts under monitoring. And U.S. entry restrictions on travelers from affected areas, issued last month, are due to expire on July twenty-first.

Alongside that, a modelling paper posted to medRxiv this weekend, from a group led by Isaac Bogoch at the University of Toronto with Canadian collaborators, asks a policy-relevant question: given what we know about this outbreak, what would actually save the most lives?

The context you need. There is one Ebola vaccine that has been used at large scale — a live-virus vaccine called rVSV-ZEBOV, sometimes called Ervebo. It is a genetically engineered vesicular stomatitis virus that carries the surface protein of Zaire ebolavirus on the outside. Your immune system learns to recognize that protein, and if you later encounter actual Zaire ebolavirus, you're protected. It works extraordinarily well against Zaire — trials during past outbreaks showed roughly ninety-seven percent efficacy — and it is the vaccine that gets deployed in Ebola outbreak response using a strategy called ring vaccination. Ring vaccination means: someone tests positive, you identify their contacts, and their contacts' contacts, and you vaccinate that ring of people around the case to contain forward spread.

The problem here is that this outbreak is Bundibugyo, not Zaire. The surface protein of Bundibugyo virus is different enough that we don't actually know how well rVSV-ZEBOV cross-protects against it. There is limited animal data suggesting some level of cross-protection, but nothing like the ninety-seven percent efficacy figure that applies to Zaire. So a lot rides on that assumption.

The Toronto group built a stochastic network model — meaning a computer simulation of individuals connected by household and community contacts, with random variation between simulation runs, calibrated to the actual DRC outbreak data through July fifth. And they asked: under different assumptions about vaccine efficacy, contact tracing quality, and vaccination strategy, what happens?

The headline result. The single largest lever they found is not the vaccine. It's the operations. Improving case detection from thirty percent to seventy percent — meaning finding more sick people faster — combined with improving contact tracing from thirty percent to eighty percent, reduced deaths by roughly sixty percent all on its own. No vaccine required.

Then they added vaccination. If you assume that rVSV-ZEBOV gives forty-five percent efficacy against Bundibugyo — a middle-of-the-road guess — reactive ring vaccination, on top of already-improved operations, added another fourteen percent reduction in deaths. Modest but real.

Community vaccination — meaning you don't just vaccinate the contacts of known cases, you vaccinate everyone in the affected geography — was much more powerful. At fifty percent community coverage, they projected mortality reductions between fifty-eight and eighty-six percent, depending on how fast the rollout happens after the outbreak is declared. Every additional two weeks of delay ate meaningfully into that benefit.

The takeaway from that model — which lines up with what field epidemiologists have been saying for a while — is that in an Ebola outbreak, the boring, unglamorous stuff wins. Finding cases fast. Tracing contacts. Isolating the infectious. Vaccine helps, especially if you can get ahead of exposure by vaccinating a whole community rather than reacting case by case. But the vaccine can't rescue a slow, leaky surveillance system. And with an outbreak now past nineteen hundred cases and seven hundred deaths, the incremental gains from operations are real and immediate. Worth reading if you work anywhere near outbreak response.

On the business side, one of the bigger scientific stories from the Alzheimer's Association International Conference, which is running in London right now, dropped yesterday. Eisai — the Japanese company that co-developed lecanemab, brand name Leqembi, the anti-amyloid antibody that got a lot of headlines a couple of years ago — presented Phase 1b/2 data on a very different drug called etalanetug. Trial name Study 103.

To understand why this matters, a quick refresher on the biology. Alzheimer's disease has two hallmark pathologies. One is amyloid plaques — extracellular clumps of a peptide called amyloid-beta. The other is tau tangles — intracellular fibrils of a protein called tau. For thirty years, the pharmaceutical industry mostly bet on amyloid. Every approved disease-modifying drug for Alzheimer's — including lecanemab and donanemab — targets amyloid. The clinical benefit of those drugs, once they finally started working, is real but modest. They slow cognitive decline by around thirty percent over eighteen months. Which is meaningful, but not the transformation people had hoped for.

The observation that has always nagged at the field is that tau, not amyloid, correlates better with the timing and severity of cognitive symptoms. Amyloid appears to fall first, sometimes decades before symptoms. Tau spreads later, from region to region in the brain, and tau spread lines up much more closely with when a person actually starts declining. So the working theory is that you probably need to hit tau too. And drugs that hit tau have been the great white whale of Alzheimer's therapeutics. Most of them have failed.

Etalanetug is Eisai's take on this. It is a monoclonal antibody. But unlike the previous generation of anti-tau antibodies, which mostly went after the N-terminal end of the tau protein — which turned out to not be the part that spreads pathology — etalanetug targets the microtubule binding region, or MTBR. That's the middle domain of tau. It's the part of the protein that, when it gets clipped and misfolded, forms the seeds that go on to template new tangles in neighboring cells. Neutralize those seeds, the theory goes, and you slow the region-to-region spread.

Now, this is a Phase 1b/2 trial in a rare and unusually severe population: dominantly inherited Alzheimer's disease. These are patients who carry a mutation in one of three genes — presenilin 1, presenilin 2, or amyloid precursor protein — that essentially guarantee they'll develop Alzheimer's, typically in their forties or fifties. They have been studied intensively as a model population because the disease timing in these patients is predictable, and because their pathology is very floridly the classical amyloid-plus-tau picture. This particular study is being run in collaboration with the DIAN network — the Dominantly Inherited Alzheimer Network out of Washington University in St. Louis.

The data they showed. They tracked a specific biomarker, a fragment of tau called MTBR-tau243, that reflects the pool of pathological tau seeds circulating in cerebrospinal fluid and blood. At three months of dosing, plasma MTBR-tau243 was down seventy-eight percent. At nine months, it was down more than ninety percent. In cerebrospinal fluid, which more directly reflects what's happening in the brain, it was down sixty-two percent at three months and eighty-nine percent at nine months. Several other tau species — p-tau217, p-tau181, p-tau231, p-tau205, and total tau — also dropped. The p-tau205 reduction, they note, is the first time an anti-tau therapy has moved that particular marker in dominantly inherited AD.

This is a target-engagement result, not a clinical outcome result. It shows the drug is doing what it was designed to do — clearing the pool of misfolded tau. Whether that translates into slower cognitive decline is a different question, and it is being tested in two ongoing programs: the Tau NexGen trial in dominantly inherited AD in combination with lecanemab, and a Phase 2 study in early sporadic Alzheimer's. Fast Track designation from the FDA was granted last September. So the regulatory path is active.

Why this matters. If anti-tau approaches produce clinical benefit on top of what amyloid drugs do — and the working assumption in the field is that combination therapy will be the eventual answer — then this class of drug is potentially quite valuable. Etalanetug has the strongest biomarker package of any anti-tau antibody to date. The efficacy readout is still years away, but on target engagement, this is as clean as anti-tau has ever looked.

On the AI front. A paper posted this weekend to bioRxiv from the Pedro Beltrao lab at ETH Zurich, with collaborators, does something that had been overdue: a systematic, third-party audit of AlphaFold3.

Quick catch-up on what AlphaFold3 is. AlphaFold, from Google DeepMind, is the protein structure prediction model that in twenty twenty and twenty twenty-one essentially solved the single-protein folding problem — you feed it an amino acid sequence, it gives you an accurate three-dimensional structure. It was a legitimate transformation of structural biology. AlphaFold3, released last year, was pitched as the next step: same architecture idea, but expanded from folding single proteins to modeling entire complexes — proteins bound to other proteins, proteins bound to DNA and RNA, proteins bound to small molecule drugs, proteins with post-translational modifications like ubiquitination and phosphorylation. All-atom, one model. The premise was that AlphaFold3 would do for drug design, antibody design, and RNA biology what AlphaFold2 did for protein biology.

The new audit paper — from a group that includes Océane Follonier and Pedro Beltrao at ETH, with collaborators including Julien Racle and David Gfeller — takes AlphaFold3 and stress-tests it across five representative application areas. Ubiquitinated protein structures. T-cell receptor binding to antigenic peptides — the fundamental event in adaptive immunity. Antibody-antigen complexes — which matters for vaccine design and biologics. Protein-RNA interactions. Protein-lipid interactions.

Their headline finding, in plain language: AlphaFold3 is good in some settings and bad in others, and the pattern of when it is good and when it is bad is not what a naive user would guess. Two specific concerns.

The first is training-set contamination. AlphaFold3 is trained on the Protein Data Bank, the archive of solved structures. For some application areas — the ones where there are lots of examples of similar complexes already in the PDB — the model performs well. For areas where the target is genuinely novel — a class of complex it has not seen much of during training — performance drops off. This is not a shocking finding in isolation. It is the standard machine-learning failure mode. But it means that for the applications the community most wants — modeling something truly new — the model's performance is not as reliable as its aggregate benchmarks suggest.

The second is the confidence problem. AlphaFold models emit their own confidence scores — internal estimates of how good the model thinks its prediction is. In AlphaFold2, those confidence scores were unusually well-calibrated. When the model said it was confident, it was usually right. When it said it wasn't, it usually wasn't. That was one of the things that made it so usable in practice. In this new audit, for RNA-target predictions in particular, the AlphaFold3 confidence scores failed to distinguish real interaction partners from decoy interaction partners. That is a serious problem, because in real drug and biology applications, users are constantly making decisions based on whether the model thinks a predicted binding is real or spurious. If the confidence signal is unreliable, you can't triage.

The overall tone of the paper is measured. AlphaFold3 is still a powerful hypothesis-generation tool. But it is not, they argue, the general-purpose all-biomolecule oracle that some of the launch commentary suggested. It is a model whose reliability varies enormously by application, and whose confidence outputs need to be interpreted with respect to what the model has actually seen during training. The benchmark suite they built is being released so the community has something concrete to test future versions against.

Why this matters. There are billion-dollar drug-discovery programs — including from Google's own DeepMind spin-out Isomorphic — that lean heavily on AlphaFold3 for exactly the kinds of tasks this paper flags as unreliable, particularly antibody-antigen prediction and small-molecule binding. It doesn't mean those programs are wrong. It means the field now has a rigorous, external benchmark to check them against. In a scientific culture that has, for the last several years, been maybe a little too willing to treat DeepMind model outputs as ground truth, this kind of external audit is healthy. It arrived slightly late, but it arrived.

And finally, animals.

Harvestmen. You have seen these. They are the arachnids that look like a tiny compact body suspended in the middle of eight enormously long, thread-thin legs, wandering across your walls and porches. In parts of the English-speaking world they are called daddy longlegs. That name is unfortunate, because harvestmen are not spiders. They are in a different order of arachnids called Opiliones. They do not spin webs. They do not have venom. Their body plan is a single fused unit rather than a spider's two-part structure. If you have been told your whole life that daddy longlegs are the most venomous spiders in the world but their fangs are too small to bite you, that is not just wrong, it is wrong on both counts. They are not that spider. They have no venom.

Here is what harvestmen do have. According to a new paper this week in the Zoological Journal of the Linnean Society, led by a group at the University of São Paulo, harvestmen appear to account for more than half of the independently evolved cases of paternal care known across the entire arthropod phylum. Male harvestmen — in dozens of species — sit on the eggs. They guard clutches. They ventilate them. They protect them from predators and mold. In some species they will incubate the eggs of multiple females at once.

The paper does two things. First, it takes the classical literature — everything published about parental care in harvestmen from nineteen thirty-six to the present, which turned out to be about eighty cases — and reviews it systematically. Then it adds a genuinely novel data source: iNaturalist. iNaturalist is the citizen-science photo-sharing platform. Ordinary people, everywhere in the world, upload photos of the animals they see. The São Paulo group spent about two days scanning the harvestmen photos, looking for males sitting on eggs. In that two-day search, they found sixty-two new documented cases in species that had never been recorded doing paternal care before. Two days of citizen-science browsing more than doubled ninety years of formal literature. That is a story about the incredible latent value of platforms like iNaturalist for basic natural history — most of which we still, honestly, do not know.

The evolutionary story they piece together is even nicer. In insects, parental care almost always starts with the mother. In harvestmen, they find that paternal care evolved either directly from no-care ancestors, or, unusually, from ancestors where the female was already caring. The mechanism they propose is what evolutionary biologists call the sexy-dad hypothesis — the observation, first developed for fish, that in some species, females actively prefer to mate with males who are already guarding eggs from a previous female. If female harvestmen prefer devoted dads, then a male who takes on the eggs and guards them well gets more matings, and paternal care spreads through the population as a sexually selected trait. Not the mother relinquishing care. The father muscling in on it.

So the next time you see one of these creatures skittering around, consider that there is a decent chance you are looking at what is, by some counts, the most paternally invested lineage in the entire arthropod world. And that a big chunk of what we now know about that came from ordinary people uploading photos.
