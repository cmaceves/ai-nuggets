## Script

Welcome to The Genome Brief for Tuesday, July 21st, 2026.

Today: a new preprint explains why the current dominant flu strain has been quietly breaking the assay that global flu surveillance relies on — and why that matters heading into the next Northern Hemisphere season. The first large genomic survey of Valley fever fungus in the United States shows that its populations are expanding right now, with implications for where clinicians should start expecting cases. Bristol Myers Squibb becomes the first pharma company to buy in on NVIDIA's next-generation Vera Rubin supercomputer, and explains what that actually means for drug discovery workflows. And in Argentina and Brazil, biomechanical modeling shows that some giant sauropod dinosaurs really could rear up on their hind legs — but only while they were still young.

Let's start with pathogens.

The first story is about a genuinely strange finding that has implications for how the world tracks seasonal flu. H3N2 is one of the two influenza A subtypes that cause most human seasonal flu. Since 2024, a lineage called subclade K — technically known as J-point-2-point-4-point-1 — has been steadily displacing everything else. In the Northern Hemisphere 2025 to 2026 season, subclade K has been the predominant flu A virus in circulation. A new bioRxiv preprint from a group at Utrecht University explains why subclade K has also been quietly breaking a foundational surveillance assay that public health labs have been running the same way for decades.

To understand the problem, you need a bit of background on how flu vaccines get updated each year. Every strain of influenza is coated in copies of a spike-like protein called hemagglutinin — abbreviated HA. Hemagglutinin is what the virus uses to latch onto sialic acid sugars on the surface of your airway cells. Because it is the front-facing part of the virus, hemagglutinin is also the primary target of neutralizing antibodies from vaccines or prior infection. That is why flu vaccines have to be updated every year — hemagglutinin evolves, antibodies stop recognizing it, and the vaccine needs to be redesigned to match whatever is currently circulating.

To decide how well a candidate vaccine matches circulating viruses, labs run something called the hemagglutination inhibition assay, or HAI. The trick that makes it work goes back to the 1940s. Influenza virus, because it binds sialic acid, will stick to the surface of red blood cells — which are also coated in sialic acid — and physically glue them together into a visible clump at the bottom of a well. That clumping is hemagglutination. If you add antibodies that block hemagglutinin from binding, the red cells stop clumping and instead settle into a neat dot. The point at which they stop clumping tells you how well those antibodies recognize that virus. Every strain-selection decision the WHO makes for the annual flu vaccine relies on this assay. The workhorse red blood cells globally are from turkeys, because their surface sialic acids present the receptors the way most flu strains historically prefer to see them.

Here is the problem. Subclade K viruses have acquired substitutions in the receptor-binding site of hemagglutinin — the Utrecht team calls out changes at positions 140, 192, and 223. Those substitutions do not just move the antigenic surface around; they shift what shape of sugar the virus actually likes to bind. Human sialic acids are attached to a scaffold of underlying sugars that varies in length and branching. The subclade K hemagglutinin now prefers what the researchers call elongated glycans — longer, more complex sugar chains. Turkey erythrocytes do not display those elongated glycans in appreciable amounts.

The result: subclade K viruses do not efficiently agglutinate turkey red blood cells. Which means the standard hemagglutination inhibition assay — the one used to characterize the antigenic properties of circulating viruses and inform vaccine composition — either does not run properly or gives noisy, hard-to-interpret results for the very lineage that is currently dominating the season. The vaccine-selection pipeline is being asked to compare candidates against a target it cannot cleanly measure.

The paper offers a fix. The same group previously developed what they call glyco-remodeled erythrocytes: red blood cells whose surface sugars have been engineered to display the elongated glycans that subclade K prefers. Those glyco-remodeled cells do get bound efficiently by subclade K viruses, restoring the assay signal. The bigger picture is that human influenza is evolving toward binding preferences that laboratory reagents from a completely different species can no longer represent, and surveillance tools have to evolve with them or become progressively blind. Expect this to show up in the next round of WHO vaccine strain selection meetings.

Now to a different kind of surveillance gap — this one in a fungus.

Valley fever is the everyday name for coccidioidomycosis, an infection caused by two closely related fungal species: Coccidioides immitis and Coccidioides posadasii. The fungi live in dry soils across the arid Americas. When soil is disturbed by wind, construction, wildfires, or agriculture, tiny spores get thrown into the air, get inhaled, and cause a pneumonia that can range from mild to severe. In the American Southwest — especially Arizona, California, Texas, and increasingly the Pacific Northwest — Valley fever is a leading cause of community-acquired pneumonia. Cases in the United States have been climbing steeply, and climate change is expected to push suitable soil habitat into new regions.

Until this week, though, no one had published a large-scale genomic look at how Coccidioides populations in the U.S. are actually structured, where they came from, and whether they are expanding. A preprint posted this week to medRxiv from a group at the University of Utah, in partnership with a national diagnostic laboratory, provides exactly that. They collected 186 Coccidioides-positive clinical isolates prospectively and combined them with 126 previously sequenced genomes for a final dataset of 312 whole fungal genomes — 139 from C. immitis and 173 from C. posadasii.

They then used a set of standard population genomics tools to answer a few questions. Genomic clustering identifies how many genetically distinct groups exist. Time-calibrated phylogenetic reconstruction estimates how long ago those groups shared a common ancestor. Ancestral area reconstruction infers where each group probably originated geographically. Mating-type assignment reveals whether the fungus is reproducing sexually — which requires two different mating types — or spreading clonally. And demographic inference reads the shape of the genealogy to tell you whether a population has been growing, shrinking, or steady.

The findings paint a coherent picture. Each species contains three major genetic populations. C. immitis breaks into two California-centered populations and one Pacific Northwest population. C. posadasii breaks into two Arizona-centered populations and one Texas-centered population. The most recent common ancestor of C. immitis dates to roughly 127,000 years ago; C. posadasii is older, roughly 234,000 years. And critically — every one of these populations shows evidence of recent demographic expansion. Not steady state. Not decline. Recent growth.

The direction of movement is also informative. The team reconstructed dispersal as largely asymmetric — most spread in C. immitis originates from California and moves outward, while C. posadasii moves out from Arizona and Texas. So the Pacific Northwest population is not an ancient sister lineage; it is more consistent with a recent expansion out of California, likely riding shifts in climate and land use. Most populations contained both mating types, meaning sexual recombination is happening, but a couple — including a Brazilian subgroup and one C. immitis population — appear to be spreading clonally, which is a red flag for potential drug-resistance sweeps in the future.

The practical implication for a working clinician is this: if a patient with community-acquired pneumonia has a recent travel history to the Pacific Northwest, that is now a place to consider Valley fever in the differential — not just California, Arizona, and Texas. And the genomic surveillance framework the paper lays out gives public health an actual toolkit for detecting shifts in Coccidioides risk as climate change reshapes soil moisture and dust patterns.

On the AI-in-biomedicine side.

Bristol Myers Squibb announced yesterday that it is expanding its AI infrastructure by purchasing an NVIDIA DGX SuperPOD based on Vera Rubin — NVIDIA's newest data-center architecture, unveiled earlier this year and successor to the Blackwell family. BMS will be the first life sciences company to deploy the Vera Rubin-based SuperPOD. That headline sounds like a corporate press release, and partly it is. But there is a substantive claim underneath that is worth unpacking, because it says something about where pharma computing is going.

Some background. A DGX SuperPOD is a purpose-built cluster of NVIDIA GPU servers wired together with high-bandwidth interconnects and preconfigured software — sold as a turnkey supercomputer rather than a set of parts. Vera Rubin is the underlying chip architecture; each Vera Rubin system, in the flagship NVL72 configuration, is a rack that packages 72 GPUs and connects them with fiber and switching such that the whole rack behaves, for the purposes of a training job, more like one giant accelerator than seventy-two individual ones. The reason to want that is that modern large foundation models — including the ones being trained on biological and chemical data — do not fit inside one GPU. They have to be split across dozens or hundreds of GPUs that talk constantly during training. If the interconnect is slow, you spend most of your time waiting on communication instead of computing.

The claimed jump matters for a specific reason. Vera Rubin delivers roughly ten times more compute per watt than the previous generation. That is a big deal because the constraint on scaling AI infrastructure inside pharma is increasingly not the ability to buy GPUs — it is finding electrical capacity to run them. If you can do ten times as much work per megawatt drawn from the grid, you get to run larger models and more experiments inside the same power envelope you already have.

What is BMS actually going to do with it? Two things. First, train foundation models on Bristol Myers Squibb's own proprietary data — internal small-molecule libraries, biologics data, clinical data — which is data that no external model has ever seen. Second, run what BMS is calling agentic workflows across oncology, hematology, cardiovascular, immunology, and neuroscience programs. Agentic here means AI systems that can chain multiple steps together — for instance, propose a candidate compound, predict its properties, retrieve prior related experiments from the internal database, design the next round of variants — with less human intervention at each step. BMS's own chief research officer said the point is to move from evaluating roughly ten candidate compounds early in a program to dozens.

BMS also disclosed a specific claim that is worth naming: one experimental sickle cell disease treatment currently in its pipeline, they say, would not have been discovered without AI-enabled research. Take that with the usual grain of salt reserved for corporate attributions, but it is a concrete anchor for a set of assertions that pharma companies increasingly make and rarely quantify. Financial terms of the deal were not disclosed.

The through-line here is that the internal computing footprint of large pharma is starting to look genuinely different from what it looked like five years ago. Big companies are building in-house AI infrastructure at a scale that was previously the domain of hyperscalers, on the bet that models trained on their own proprietary molecular and clinical data will be worth more than models rented from anyone else. Whether that bet pays off in shortened development timelines is what everyone will actually be watching.

And finally, animals.

If you have ever watched an old paleoart illustration, you have probably seen sauropods — the group of long-necked, long-tailed, four-legged dinosaurs that includes Brachiosaurus and Argentinosaurus — occasionally posed rearing up on their hind legs, standing tall like giant swans, browsing high canopies or squaring off with predators. That pose has been the subject of a running argument in paleontology for decades. The animals were enormous — some over sixty feet long, weighing tens of tons. Could their skeletons actually take that load, or was rearing up a bit of paleoartist wishful thinking?

A paper this week in the journal Palaeontology, from an international team led by researchers at São Paulo State University and the University of Tübingen, takes the question to biomechanics. They picked two South American sauropods that lived about sixty-six million years ago, at the very end of the age of dinosaurs. The first is Uberabatitan ribeiroi from Brazil — a sauropod named after the town of Uberaba, near which its fossils were found — reaching adult lengths up to about eighty-five feet. The second is Neuquensaurus australis from Argentina, named for the Neuquén River. Both belong to a group called titanosaurs, which contains most of the truly large sauropods.

The team ran what is called finite element analysis, or FEA. This is a standard engineering technique: you take a virtual model of a structure — in this case, a digital reconstruction of the femur, the thigh bone — and you divide it into thousands of small connected pieces. Then you apply loads: the animal's body weight, and the muscle forces required to hold the pose. The software calculates how stress and strain distribute across every piece of the bone. If you see stresses climbing above what real bone material can tolerate, the animal probably could not hold that pose without breaking a leg.

Two simulations were run for each animal: one to model the effect of gravity and body weight on the femur while standing on two legs, and one to model the effect of the muscle forces needed to actually hold the rearing pose. The finding was that these particular sauropods had unusually robust femurs — stouter, chunkier in cross-section relative to their length than in other giant sauropods — and those robust femurs handled the calculated loads well.

But there is a catch. Younger individuals handled it well. As the animals grew — and titanosaurs grew a lot over their lifetime — body mass increased faster than the femur's ability to distribute stress. So the same animal that could rear up as a young adult probably could not do it once fully grown. Which means, if paleoart is going to show a sauropod standing on its hind legs, the accurate version is a teenager. Fully grown Uberabatitan probably kept all four feet on the ground.

There is something appealing about learning that biomechanics does not just tell us what dinosaurs could do — it tells us what they could do when they were young enough to still get away with it.
