## Script

Good morning. This is The Genome Brief for Friday, July seventeenth, twenty twenty-six. Today: an Ebola outbreak in eastern Congo that the WHO is now calling the fastest-growing it has ever managed, a new molecular explanation for why chloroquine-resistant malaria in South America looks genetically different from every other continent, the first cholesterol pill to actually match the potency of the injectable antibodies, Eli Lilly writing a nearly four-billion-dollar check for a psychedelic depression drug, an independent audit of the Evo two genomic foundation model on viral genomes that comes with some sharp caveats, and a new monkey species with orange lips.

Let's start with pathogen watch.

The Bundibugyo Ebola outbreak in the Democratic Republic of the Congo has continued to intensify. The latest WHO Disease Outbreak News, released this week, puts the total at roughly two thousand confirmed cases and more than seven hundred deaths across five provinces, up from about fourteen hundred cases and four hundred fifty deaths two weeks ago. Ituri province is still the epicenter — most cases are there, spread across twenty-six of thirty-six health zones — but the virus has now been picked up in Haut-Uele and Tshopo as well, meaning it is no longer contained to the initial cluster. Uganda's outbreak remains at twenty cases and two deaths with no new cases since late June. The US citizen medically evacuated from DRC to Germany on July thirteenth is being treated there.

The number that matters more than the raw case count comes from the WHO's Chikwe Ihekweazu, who said this week that eighty percent of new confirmed cases are people who were not on any contact list — meaning they were infected through chains of transmission the outbreak response never saw. He also said this is the fastest single-month growth WHO has managed in any Ebola outbreak on record. To put that in plain terms: an Ebola response works by tracing every contact of every case, watching them for twenty-one days, and vaccinating or isolating the ones who develop symptoms. When only one in five new cases comes from that surveillance net, it means the outbreak is running on parallel invisible transmission chains — the tracing is fighting the last wave, not the current one. Ituri has been a hard place to run any public-health operation for years because of armed conflict and community mistrust, both of which have been documented back to the 2018–2020 outbreak. The concerning trajectory here is that the outbreak has crossed a threshold: it's now growing at a rate that likely can't be stopped by contact tracing alone, and would need very high-coverage community vaccination, which is exactly the intervention the modelling paper we covered on Tuesday — Bogoch and colleagues out of Toronto — found could avert most deaths if delivered fast enough. We'll keep watching whether WHO and the DRC Ministry of Health shift toward that strategy in the next update.

Item two, staying with pathogens but shifting to malaria. A team led by Julian Rayner at Cambridge, with colleagues in Colombia and Cambridge's Deane lab, posted a bioRxiv preprint this week that reopens a question people thought was mostly settled — why chloroquine-resistant malaria in South America is a genetically distinct beast. Some background. Chloroquine was the workhorse antimalarial for most of the twentieth century. Resistance emerged independently on three continents — Southeast Asia, Africa, and South America — and the primary driver in all cases is a mutation in a Plasmodium falciparum transporter protein called PfCRT. But PfCRT alone doesn't explain the full resistance phenotype; there's a second gene called PfAAT1 — a putative amino acid transporter — that plays a modulatory role. Until now, the field has looked mostly at PfAAT1 mutations found in African and Asian parasites. South America has always looked like it has its own unusual pattern of PfAAT1 changes, but nobody had shown they were functionally important.

Here's what this team did. They used AlphaFold — the AI protein structure prediction system — to build a structural model of PfAAT1, and they noticed that it looks quite a lot like a human transporter called SLC38A9, which sits in lysosomes and moves arginine. That structural homology matters, because SLC38A9 has been thoroughly characterized: we know how it opens, we know which residues line the pore. The team then mapped South-American-specific PfAAT1 mutations onto their PfAAT1 model and asked which of those mutations sit at the mouth of the channel — the point where the substrate would enter. Two mutations stood out: a residue at position two thirty-one, where a valine is changed to an aspartate — call it V two thirty-one D — and a residue at position two forty-eight, where an isoleucine is changed to a threonine — I two forty-eight T. Both sit right at the pore entrance.

Then they did the actual functional experiment. They took 7G8, a well-known South American parasite strain that carries both mutations, and used CRISPR-Cas9 to revert V two thirty-one D back to the wildtype valine. When they did that, the parasite's chloroquine resistance dropped significantly. That's the first functional evidence that this specific South American mutation contributes to resistance. In a separate experiment, they engineered the Colombia-specific I two forty-eight T into a parasite that didn't have it, and the parasite's growth rate went up — suggesting this mutation isn't just tuning drug sensitivity, it's also easing a fitness cost that the parasite was paying somewhere else.

Why this matters. Two things. First, molecular surveillance for chloroquine resistance — which several countries in Central and South America still do because chloroquine is used for Plasmodium vivax, the other major human malaria species that shares the same continent — has been calibrated mostly to African and Asian mutations. This paper says: if you're screening PfAAT1 in the Americas and you're only looking at the mutations everyone else looks at, you will underestimate resistance. Second, chloroquine sensitivity in some parts of Africa has been slowly returning as chloroquine was withdrawn — the field has been debating whether reintroducing chloroquine, or its cousins like piperaquine, could work in combinations. Understanding that South American resistance is molecularly distinct means the calculus for that reintroduction has to be region-specific.

On the business side.

The big story is the FDA approval on Thursday, July sixteenth, of a drug called Lipfendra — generic name enlicitide — from Merck. Lipfendra is the first once-daily oral PCSK9 inhibitor ever approved. Let me explain why that phrase is the whole story.

PCSK9 stands for Proprotein Convertase Subtilisin Kexin type nine. It's a protein made by the liver that binds to LDL receptors on the surface of liver cells and targets them for destruction. LDL receptors are what pull LDL cholesterol — bad cholesterol — out of the bloodstream and into cells to be broken down. So if you block PCSK9, more LDL receptors survive, and blood LDL drops. This mechanism is well-validated: about a decade ago the field got two injectable monoclonal antibody drugs — Amgen's Repatha and the Regeneron/Sanofi drug Praluent — that target PCSK9. They cut LDL by about fifty to sixty percent on top of statins, and outcome trials showed they reduce heart attacks and strokes. Great drugs, big problem: they're monoclonal antibodies given by injection every two to four weeks, they're expensive, and adoption in the real world has been disappointing because a lot of high-risk patients simply don't stay on injections year after year.

The dream, ever since those antibodies were approved, has been an oral small molecule that works as well. That's genuinely hard, because PCSK9 is a protein-protein interaction target — the surface where PCSK9 grabs the LDL receptor is big and flat, exactly the kind of interface small molecules struggle with. Merck's answer is enlicitide, which is not really a small molecule and not quite a biologic — it's what medicinal chemists call a macrocyclic peptide. That means it's a chain of amino acids held together in a ring — big enough to make contact across a broad protein interface like an antibody would, but small and rigid enough to survive the stomach and be absorbed as a pill. In the Phase 3 CORALreef Lipids trial in about twenty-nine hundred patients with high cholesterol, enlicitide reduced LDL-C by roughly fifty-six percent versus placebo at twenty-four weeks, on top of a statin. In the CORALreef HeFH trial in patients with heterozygous familial hypercholesterolemia — the inherited high-cholesterol disorder — it cut LDL by fifty-nine percent. Those numbers are essentially the same as the injectables.

Why does this matter, beyond the obvious that pills are more convenient than shots? Because the barrier to actually driving down cardiovascular events at the population level has never been the biology of PCSK9 — it's been adherence. A twenty-milligram once-daily tablet in a class where the current standard is a subcutaneous injection every few weeks changes the shape of the market. It probably becomes eligible for step-therapy positioning after statins, ahead of the antibodies, in a lot of insurance formularies. The one thing enlicitide doesn't have yet is a cardiovascular outcomes trial — the ongoing study looking at whether it reduces heart attacks and strokes hasn't read out. If that outcomes trial hits, this becomes one of the more important cardiovascular drug launches of the decade. If it misses, the injectables still have a niche in the highest-risk patients. Either way, this is the first cholesterol pill to match the shots on LDL lowering, and that has been the field's holy grail for ten years.

Second business item, more briefly. Eli Lilly on Wednesday announced it is acquiring AtaiBeckley — the merged psychedelic-drug developer formed from atai Life Sciences and Beckley Psytech last year — for six dollars seventy-five cents per share upfront, or about two point eight billion dollars, plus a contingent value right of up to two dollars fifty cents per share, another roughly one billion, tied to development and regulatory milestones. Total potential value is close to three point eight billion. This is a real signal. Big pharma has been circling psychedelic depression drugs for years without buying one; Lilly is the first to actually write the check.

The asset they're really buying is BPL-003, an intranasal formulation of five-methoxy-N,N-dimethyltryptamine — that's five methoxy DMT, a short-acting serotonin-2A receptor agonist related to psilocybin and DMT but with a much shorter duration of action, on the order of twenty to forty minutes rather than the hours of a classic psilocybin session. In a Phase 2b trial in a hundred ninety-three patients with treatment-resistant depression, a single eight-milligram or twelve-milligram dose produced a mean drop of eleven to twelve points on the Montgomery-Åsberg Depression Rating Scale — the standard depression severity scale — at four weeks, versus roughly six points on a low-dose active control. The effect showed up within twenty-four hours and was sustained through the eight-week trial. Ninety-nine percent of adverse events were mild or moderate; no drug-related serious events or suicide-related safety signals. The open-label extension data reported response in sixty-three percent and remission in forty-eight percent of previously treated patients at week eight after a second dose. That's a stronger single-dose durability signal than any small-molecule antidepressant has produced in this population. What Lilly is betting on is that a fast-acting, single-dose psychedelic — sold and administered inside a health system that already runs ketamine clinics — becomes the dominant format for treatment-resistant depression. It's a big bet on the format changing, not just the molecule.

Onto AI in biomedicine.

A group in São Paulo, led by Deyvid Amgarten and colleagues at Hospital Israelita Albert Einstein, posted a bioRxiv preprint yesterday that is essentially an independent audit of the Evo two genomic foundation model — the twenty-billion-parameter DNA language model from the Arc Institute we talked about back on Ep twenty-one, when Goodfire and the Mayo Clinic used it to score human variants. This new benchmark is asking a different question: how well does Evo two handle *viral* genomes, and what happens when you try to use it for viral surveillance or design?

They built a pre-registered benchmark on nearly nineteen and a half thousand RefSeq viral genomes, organized by Baltimore class — that's the classical virus taxonomy scheme by genome type, so DNA-versus-RNA, single-versus-double-stranded, and so on — and by host domain. Then they tested three things. First, whether Evo two's embeddings can classify a viral genome's Baltimore class, host, and family from the model's own internal representation alone. Second, whether the embeddings encode functional genome architecture — things like gene density and how much of the genome is coding and how genes overlap. And third, generative behavior: can Evo two finish a fragmented viral genome coherently?

Two headlines came out. The good news: on Baltimore class and host domain, Evo two hit ninety-six and ninety-nine percent accuracy — clearly beyond a nucleotide-composition baseline, meaning the model is actually learning something about virus biology. Even more impressively, its embeddings decoded coding fraction, gene density, and gene overlap far better than a simple six-mer nucleotide-composition control could — meaning the model has genuinely learned genomic *architecture*, not just base-frequency patterns. That's a real result.

The bad news: on viral family classification, Evo two's embeddings barely beat the simple six-mer composition baseline. And in the generative test, perplexity — that's the model's measure of surprise on the next token, so lower is better — was one point one eight bits per nucleotide on bacteriophages but one point eight on eukaryote-infecting viruses that had been deliberately held out of training. That's a very large gap. What it means practically is that Evo two's ability to generate coherent viral sequence is highly dependent on how much of that virus type it saw during training. So for phages, which are heavily represented in metagenomic training data, it generates confidently. For animal or plant viruses it didn't see, it starts to guess.

Why this matters. There's real enthusiasm right now — including in the pathogen-surveillance and vaccine-design communities — for using foundation models like Evo two to design synthetic viral sequences, to score novel variant sequences, or to help build de novo antivirals. This benchmark says: use with care. The model knows what it was trained on. If you point it at a viral family it saw a lot of, you're getting real signal. If you point it at a family it didn't — say, an outbreak arbovirus, or a new coronavirus lineage — you're getting composition-flavored guesses. That is a message worth broadcasting before people start submitting Evo-two-generated designs to grant panels.

And finally, animals.

A team led by John Hart at the Lukuru Wildlife Research Foundation in the DRC, with Julia Arenson and Eric Sargis at Yale, Florida Atlantic University, and Hunter College, published in PLOS One on Tuesday what may be the highlight new-species paper of the year: an entirely new species of monkey, living in the high forest canopy of Lomami National Park in eastern DRC. Its scientific name is Colobus congoensis. Locally it's called Likweli. It is the fifth new African monkey species described in seventy-five years.

Colobus monkeys are the black-and-white long-tailed monkeys most people have seen in nature documentaries — they're arboreal, they eat leaves, they live in social groups high in the canopy. Likweli looks a little different. It's smaller, maybe fifteen pounds. Its coat is entirely black, but framed by long facial hair. The distinguishing feature — and this is the thing that seems to have caught the whole internet's attention — is a striking orange-cream patch around the mouth and nose. There are also white patches under the tail. Local conservationists first photographed one in 2008. Between twenty eighteen and twenty twenty-two, the team logged one hundred fourteen field observations across roughly seventeen hundred square kilometers of forest between three tributaries of the Congo River. Then they used physical measurements, genetics, and recordings of the roar — the loud territorial call these monkeys make — to confirm this is a species distinct from its closest relative, Colobus satanas. And here's the surprising evolutionary result: the two species share a most recent common ancestor somewhere between four point seven and five point eight million years ago. That's the deepest split within the entire Colobus genus, older than the split between humans and chimpanzees.

The authors are recommending an endangered designation. Population is small, range is limited, hunting pressure and habitat encroachment are rising. It's a strange and moving fact that we're describing a new large primate species — a lineage that has been quietly evolving for around five million years, right through the origin of our own — at essentially the moment its future is in doubt. And a reminder that the forests of the Congo basin, right where the Ebola outbreak we opened with is unfolding, are still turning up creatures we've never met.
