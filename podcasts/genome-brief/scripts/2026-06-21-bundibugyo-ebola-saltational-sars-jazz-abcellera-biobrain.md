## Script

Welcome to the Genome Brief for Sunday, June twenty-first. Big day for pathogen surveillance: WHO has refreshed the Bundibugyo-virus picture and the Bedford lab has dropped a four-million-genome read on where SARS cov two's strangest variants actually come from. We've also got a four-billion-dollar oncology pact, an agentic-microscopy preprint that quietly embarrasses frontier language models, and a spider in the Ecuadorian Amazon doing a deeply committed bit. On we go.

Pathogen watch first. The World Health Organization's June nineteenth update on the Bundibugyo-virus outbreak in the Democratic Republic of the Congo and Uganda is the biggest viral-surveillance story right now. DRC is reporting eight hundred ninety-six confirmed cases and two hundred thirty-two deaths as of June seventeenth; Uganda is at nineteen confirmed cases and two deaths as of June eighteenth, with one additional probable death. The PHEIC declaration is from mid-May. Two things make this one operationally hard in a way that doesn't show up in the case curve. First, the outbreak is unfolding in a conflict-affected zone with attacks on health facilities, so contact tracing and case ascertainment are partial — the real denominator is almost certainly larger. Second, Bundibugyo virus is a distinct Orthoebolavirus from Zaire ebolavirus, which means the licensed Zaire-specific vaccine and monoclonals don't directly apply; the field is leaning on cross-reactive countermeasures with weaker evidence. If you watch one situation report this week, watch this one.

Staying on viral genomics — Cécile Tran-Kiem, Trevor Bedford and colleagues at Fred Hutch posted a paper that systematically attacks one of the most uncomfortable questions in SARS cov two evolution: where do the highly mutated saltational variants actually come from? Across four-point-four million high-quality genomes, they detect saltational events at low but steady rates throughout the pandemic and post-pandemic period, in every region. The mutational signature of those events matches the signature seen in persistent human infections — and crucially, it does not match the signatures from mink or deer spillovers. So reverse zoonosis is largely off the hook, and chronic human infection is squarely on it. Most saltational lineages go nowhere, but the ones that do transmit tend to carry mutations that show up later in successful clades. The policy read is uncomfortable but clear: persistently infected immunocompromised hosts are the variant engine, and any preparedness plan that doesn't have a strategy for that population is missing the source.

On the business side. Jazz Pharmaceuticals and AbCellera announced a discovery and option-license collaboration on Tuesday for next-generation T-cell engaging multispecific antibodies, initially in GI and other solid tumors. Fifty-six million up front, with about seven hundred ninety-two million in option fees and milestones per program — and if a third program kicks off within twelve months, the headline total can clear four billion dollars. That makes it the largest licensing deal in Jazz's history. The strategic context worth flagging: T-cell engagers are finally working in solid tumors but the developability bottleneck — CD3 affinity tuning, half-life, on-target off-tumor toxicity — has been brutal, and AbCellera's antibody discovery platform is being asked to thread exactly that needle. Worth watching whether option-and-license structures like this become the dominant template for big-pharma access to next-gen TCE biology.

To AI in the biomedical sciences. A nice preprint posted yesterday from the Hatzakis group at Copenhagen and collaborators introduces BioBrain, a multi-agent framework that takes natural-language analytical goals and assembles validated microscopy-analysis pipelines, rather than generating analysis code from scratch. Every selected method and every inferred parameter is reported transparently, so the pipeline is traceable. The honest part of the paper is the benchmark: on two-channel total internal reflection fluorescence and three-D lattice light-sheet datasets, BioBrain reproduces expert results when parameters are specified and degrades predictably when they're not — while frontier large language models, asked to do the same analyses, produced large, model-dependent quantitative errors and completed without any warning that something had gone wrong. That last bit is the lesson. The risk in biology-and-LLM workflows isn't that models refuse to answer; it's that they confidently return the wrong number. Agentic systems built around validated tool calls, with traceability as a first-class property, are how that risk gets bounded.

We're going to skip the AI-tools segment today — nothing genuinely capability-jumping landed from the big labs in the last forty-eight hours that wasn't either an incremental update or a personnel headline.

And finally, animals. A team led by the Leibniz Institute and collaborators in Ecuador formally described a new Amazonian spider, Taczanowskia waska — informally, the Cordyceps spider — in Zootaxa earlier this month. The trick is that this spider mimics, in appearance and posture, the fruiting body of a Gibellula fungus, which is one of the parasitic fungi that hijacks and kills spiders from the inside. Field biologists kept walking past it because they thought it was a dead, fungus-infected spider. It hangs motionless on the undersides of leaves, exactly where the real fungus grows. So it's a spider pretending to be the corpse of a spider, to avoid being eaten and probably to ambush prey that has stopped paying attention to apparent corpses. As far as anyone can tell, this is the first known case of an animal mimicking the pathogen that would otherwise kill it. That's the brief. Have a good Sunday.
