## Script

Good morning and welcome to The Genome Brief for Thursday, July second, 2026. Four stories today.

A new bioRxiv preprint provides one of the first pieces of causal evidence that the classic Southeast Asian artemisinin resistance mutation can produce reduced drug response inside African malaria parasites — using CRISPR editing of parasites collected from Ethiopian patients. The FDA yesterday approved a first-of-its-kind cell therapy, built from a rare subset of donor immune cells, to reduce a major complication of bone marrow transplants in blood cancer patients. In the AI space, a research group has released an open-source rebuild of one of the leading computational antibody design pipelines — and along the way, they've delivered the first apples-to-apples benchmark of the two antibody language models the field has been quietly disagreeing about. And we close with pterosaurs and a question about what their wings actually looked like.

---

Pathogen watch.

Artemisinin is the backbone of modern malaria treatment. Every country in the world that treats malaria uses it, usually as part of an artemisinin-based combination therapy that pairs artemisinin or one of its derivatives with a longer-acting partner drug. It works fast, it clears parasites reliably, and until roughly fifteen years ago, resistance simply didn't exist.

That changed in the late 2000s in western Cambodia, where clinicians noticed patients taking longer than usual to clear their parasites. The genetic explanation turned out to be a mutation in a single Plasmodium falciparum gene called kelch13. One particular substitution — where the amino acid at position 580 changes from cysteine to tyrosine, written as C580Y — became the dominant marker of what's called artemisinin partial resistance. Within about a decade, C580Y had spread across the Greater Mekong Subregion, reaching near-fixation in some Cambodian and Vietnamese parasite populations.

Africa is the continent that bears roughly ninety-five percent of the global malaria death toll, and for years, C580Y stayed out. Other kelch13 mutations emerged in East Africa — different substitutions at different positions — but the specific C580Y variant, the one with the worst track record in Asia, was absent. That absence was one of the most closely watched facts in malaria surveillance.

Two years ago, that absence ended. Researchers reported two patients in northwestern Ethiopia, in Gondar Zuria district, carrying C580Y in their infections. It was the first confirmed detection of C580Y in the Horn of Africa. And here's where things got subtle. Simply finding the mutation isn't enough to know what it will do, because the same mutation can behave differently in different genetic backgrounds. When researchers have introduced kelch13 mutations from Uganda into standard Asian and African lab parasite lines, the resistance phenotype has been inconsistent — sometimes present, sometimes not. Sequence alone doesn't tell you the biology.

So a new preprint from a team at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill and collaborators in Ethiopia set out to answer that biological question directly, using parasites collected recently from Ethiopian patients. The team took two clinical isolates — one from Arba Minch in southern Ethiopia and one from Selekleka in Tigray, both collected in 2024 — and used CRISPR-Cas9 genome editing to introduce the C580Y mutation into their genomes.

Let me define a couple of pieces. CRISPR-Cas9 is a molecular scissors system. It uses a guide RNA to target a specific site in the genome and an enzyme called Cas9 to cut the DNA there; the cell then repairs the cut using a template the researchers supply, and the template encodes the desired change. In this study, they introduced C580Y into the parasites' kelch13 gene. Importantly, they also made control lines in which they introduced only silent, synonymous mutations at the same site — changes to the DNA that don't alter the protein sequence. That's the critical control: it shows that any resistance you see later is due to the C580Y protein change itself, not just some artifact of the editing.

Then they ran what's called a ring-stage survival assay. Ring stage is the earliest form of the parasite inside a red blood cell — it looks under the microscope like a thin ring of cytoplasm hugging one edge of the cell. Ring-stage parasites are the developmental window where artemisinin acts most strongly, and where kelch13 resistance mutations exert their effect. They exposed ring-stage parasites to a high, brief pulse of dihydroartemisinin — the active metabolite of artemisinin — for six hours, washed the drug away, and then measured how many parasites were still viable five days later.

The result was clear. In both Ethiopian parasite backgrounds, the C580Y-edited parasites survived significantly better than their isogenic controls. The differences were statistically significant and reproducible across biological replicates. In plain terms: C580Y is sufficient to confer artemisinin tolerance in contemporary Ethiopian parasite backgrounds, not just in Asian lab strains. This is one of the first pieces of direct causal evidence that this particular resistance mutation can actually work inside the African parasite genomes now circulating in patients.

Why does this matter for the public-health picture? It doesn't yet mean C580Y is spreading in Ethiopia — the initial detection was in only two patients, and whether the mutation persists and expands depends on whether it's compatible with the parasite's ability to grow and be transmitted by mosquitoes, questions the authors flag as the next thing to resolve. But it does close off one of the reassuring possibilities. It was previously plausible that C580Y might appear sporadically in Africa and then fail to establish itself because the local parasite background couldn't tolerate it. This work removes that reassurance. The mutation is functional here. Whether it spreads is now an epidemiological question, not a molecular one — which means artemisinin-combination-therapy surveillance in Ethiopia and the surrounding region needs to be treated as a live problem.

On the business side, a landmark cell therapy approval late Tuesday.

The Food and Drug Administration approved a therapy called Tregzi, developed by Orca Bio, for adults with blood cancers who are receiving an allogeneic stem cell transplant. It is the first regulatory-T-cell-based immunotherapy ever approved.

To understand what this therapy does, you need the setup. An allogeneic stem cell transplant is a curative option for blood cancers like acute myeloid leukemia, acute lymphoid leukemia, and myelodysplastic syndromes. The patient's own bone marrow is destroyed by high-dose chemotherapy or radiation, and then a matched donor provides stem cells that rebuild the patient's blood and immune system from scratch. That new immune system, built from donor cells, has the huge advantage of being unfamiliar with the cancer — it treats residual leukemia cells as foreign and attacks them. But it has an equally serious disadvantage: it also sees the patient's healthy tissue as foreign. When the donor immune cells attack the recipient's skin, gut, liver, and other organs, that's graft-versus-host disease. Chronic graft-versus-host disease is one of the leading causes of long-term morbidity and mortality after transplant, and the standard prevention regimen relies on suppressing the entire donor immune system with drugs, which brings its own side effects.

Tregzi is an attempt to prevent graft-versus-host disease with a more targeted biological tool: regulatory T cells, or Tregs. Tregs are a specialized subset of T cells whose job in a healthy body is to suppress inappropriate immune responses — the cells that damp things down when the immune system is doing too much. Roughly one in a hundred T cells in the bloodstream is a Treg, so isolating them in pure form is a technical challenge. Orca's platform sorts them out of a donor unit and separately purifies conventional T cells and blood stem cells, then infuses each population in defined ratios. The idea is that the stem cells rebuild the marrow, the conventional T cells provide the graft-versus-leukemia effect, and the Tregs specifically suppress the graft-versus-host attack on healthy tissue.

The Phase 3 data are strong. In an open-label trial comparing Tregzi to standard allogeneic transplant, seventy-eight percent of Tregzi recipients were alive at one year without chronic graft-versus-host disease, compared to thirty-eight point four percent in the standard-of-care arm. Overall one-year survival was ninety-four percent versus eighty-three percent. Those are large differences on the endpoint that matters most for long-term transplant outcomes.

The therapy is priced at four hundred twenty-eight thousand dollars per treatment, which sounds high in isolation but is within the range that cell therapies for hematologic malignancies have carved out. The regulatory precedent here matters as much as the drug itself: Tregzi is the first therapy to enter the market that is specifically built around Tregs as a defined therapeutic cell type. If the durability data hold up beyond one year, it opens a template that other developers are already following into autoimmune disease and solid organ transplant tolerance.

Now, AI in the biomedical sciences.

De novo antibody design — the idea that you could describe an epitope on a protein, hand it to a piece of software, and receive back an antibody sequence engineered to bind that specific target — has moved from science-fiction to publishable in about three years. The field's benchmark results in the last twelve months are genuinely striking. A method from Cornell and collaborators called Germinal, published earlier this year, showed that if you combine AlphaFold-Multimer with an antibody language model to guide the design process, you can test just forty-three to a hundred and one computational designs per target and reliably find binders with affinities in the nanomolar-to-low-micromolar range. That's a hit rate high enough that a normal academic lab, running standard biophysical assays, can find real antibodies without a screening pipeline the size of a large pharma company.

But Germinal, as originally published, has a practical problem. It depends on two pieces of software with restrictive licenses — PyRosetta, a molecular modeling toolkit that requires a commercial license outside academia, and IgLM, an antibody language model distributed under a non-commercial license. That combination makes it hard to actually use Germinal in industry or in a startup, and hard to reproduce.

A new preprint posted to bioRxiv this week from the University of Virginia introduces OpenGerminal, a fully open-source reimplementation of the pipeline. Every restrictively licensed component has been swapped out. PyRosetta is replaced by an open-source stack — OpenMM for molecular relaxation, FreeSASA for surface area calculations, Biopython for interface geometry, and a small custom module for shape complementarity. IgLM is replaced by AbLang1, a different antibody language model with an open license.

The result is more than just a licensing convenience. Because Germinal itself had never published head-to-head data comparing the two antibody language models — IgLM was the default in the original paper, and AbLang1 was later added as an option but never benchmarked — OpenGerminal ends up providing the first real comparison. The finding is not what most people would have guessed. Switching from IgLM to AbLang1 substantially increases the proportion of designed candidates that pass the pipeline's initial structural quality filter. On the PD-L1 target, the cofolding pass rate went from about eighteen percent to about thirty-four percent. On an interleukin-3 target, it went from eight percent to twenty-five percent. And the accepted designs from the open pipeline had modestly higher AlphaFold confidence metrics — better predicted local structure quality and better predicted interface quality — than the accepted designs from the original.

There's a cost. Trajectories run about one and a half times slower per candidate on the same GPU. And a few internal metrics that depended on PyRosetta's specific energy units are held at fixed placeholder values in the open version, one of which effectively disables a hydrogen-bond-count filter downstream.

The bigger significance is what this reveals about the field. Antibody design pipelines are stacks of interacting components — a structure predictor, a language model for sequence naturalness, a relaxation step, a filtering pass — and small choices deep in that stack can move the yield of the whole pipeline by factors of two or three. If you're planning to run one of these pipelines on your own target of interest, the choice of antibody language model is not a detail. It is arguably the single biggest lever on how many candidates make it through your filters. And now, thanks to this rebuild, that lever is measurable rather than opaque.

And finally, a story about wings.

Pterosaurs were the first vertebrates to evolve powered flight — appearing about two hundred and thirty million years ago and disappearing at the end of the Cretaceous, along with the non-avian dinosaurs. They ranged in size from small enough to perch on your hand to as large as a small plane. Quetzalcoatlus, the biggest known, had a wingspan of roughly ten to eleven meters. That's a hundred million years of evolution, in animal sizes spanning three orders of magnitude, in every environment from forests to open ocean.

Given that range, you'd expect their wings to look very different from one species to the next. Look at modern flying animals for comparison. Albatrosses have long, narrow wings for efficient soaring over open water. Sparrows have short, rounded wings for quick maneuvering through dense vegetation. Bats show similar variation across their species. Wing shape and flight style are tightly coupled.

But when researchers at the University of Bristol, in a paper published last month in the journal Palaeobiology, looked systematically at the pterosaur wings that appear in the scientific literature — from museum specimens, from artistic reconstructions, from published papers — they found something odd. Across nine well-known species spanning the entire range of pterosaur sizes and lifestyles, the wing shapes are all remarkably similar. Palm-sized pterosaurs and plane-sized pterosaurs are being reconstructed with essentially the same wing profile. That does not match what evolution has done in any other group of vertebrate fliers we can compare them to.

The reason, the authors argue, is not that pterosaur wings really were all alike — it's that we can't see them clearly. The bones of a pterosaur wing are the arm, the elongated fourth finger, and the shoulder girdle. Bones fossilize. But the actual flight surface — the membrane stretched between the finger, the body, and the legs — was soft tissue, and soft tissue almost never survives in the fossil record. The wing shapes in current reconstructions are extrapolations from the bones, filled in with information from a handful of exceptional fossils that preserve some soft-tissue outline. That approach imports the assumption that all pterosaurs were built the same way and biases every reconstruction toward the same silhouette.

The team mapped out mathematically what wing shapes pterosaurs could have had, given the physics of powered flight — a theoretical morphospace. Then they compared where existing reconstructions fall in that space. Existing reconstructions cluster together. The theoretical space allows for far more diversity, especially in aspect ratio — how long and narrow versus how short and broad the wing is. Pterosaurs likely explored much more of that space than we are currently giving them credit for. New imaging techniques — some using wavelengths of light outside the visible range — may recover soft-tissue outlines from fossils that look, to the naked eye, like they preserve only bone. If those techniques deliver, the pterosaur bestiary in fifty years may look substantially more varied than the one we grew up with.
