## Script

Welcome to The Genome Brief for Monday, July 20th, 2026.

Today: a new preprint shows that H five N one avian influenza can silently infect pigs through contaminated milk — a finding with real pandemic-preparedness implications. Malaria researchers in Bangladesh find a new route to artemisinin resistance that standard surveillance wouldn't catch. GSK walks away from a major chronic cough drug after its Phase 3 trials gave conflicting answers. And in Australia, paleontologists announce a lost order of marsupials that no one knew existed — one that survived for thirty-five million years before quietly disappearing twelve million years ago.

Let's start with pathogens.

H five N one — the highly pathogenic strain of avian influenza — has been spreading through U.S. dairy cattle herds since early 2024. You've heard about it infecting cows, farmworkers, and spreading to wild birds around the world. A new preprint posted this week to bioRxiv raises a different alarm: bovine H five N one can efficiently infect pigs — and it does so silently.

To understand why this matters, you need a bit of influenza biology. Flu viruses are unusual because their genetic material is split into eight separate segments, a bit like a genome spread across eight separate chromosomes. When two different influenza strains infect the same cell at the same time, they can swap those segments randomly, producing hybrid viruses. This process is called reassortment. It is how major pandemic strains emerge. The 2009 H one N one pandemic strain was a triple reassortant — it combined gene segments from human, swine, and avian flu lineages, and those components came together inside a pig.

Pigs are central to this story because they carry receptors in their airways that recognize both bird-adapted and human-adapted flu strains. Both types of virus can infect the same pig simultaneously, which makes pigs what virologists call mixing vessels — the biological arena where viral gene segments from very different influenza lineages can be shuffled into new combinations.

So the question researchers asked this week is: can bovine H five N one infect commercial pigs? They focused specifically on the mammary gland route, because since 2024 we have known that H five N one replicates at extremely high concentrations in infected cows' milk. The team, based at Ohio State University, took lactating sows whose vaccination histories were representative of U.S. commercial pig farms — meaning they had received standard swine influenza vaccines — and inoculated them directly through the mammary gland with bovine H five N one.

The result was productive infection. The sows showed sustained viral RNA shedding in milk, lesions in mammary tissue, viral antigen detectable by staining, development of neutralizing antibodies, and visible changes in milk color and consistency. The standard swine flu vaccines did not prevent infection. And critically, none of the sows showed any clinical signs — no fever, no respiratory symptoms, nothing that would alert a farmer or trigger the kind of routine surveillance that looks for sick animals. When infected sows nursed their one-week-old piglets, the piglets showed intermittent viral RNA in oral swabs, tracking the level of virus in the milk.

The concern here operates at two levels. First, there may already be subclinical H five N one circulating in commercial U.S. pig populations, completely invisible to animal health surveillance because standard surveillance is looking for sick animals. Second, if H five N one is silently present in pigs that are also seasonally infected with circulating human influenza strains — which happens every winter — the conditions for reassortment exist. The authors explicitly call for expanding H five N one surveillance to swine, something that currently is not happening at scale.

Moving to malaria — and a different kind of surveillance gap.

Artemisinin is the cornerstone drug for treating Plasmodium falciparum malaria, the deadliest malaria species. For years, drug resistance has been spreading outward from the Mekong region of Southeast Asia. The main driver of that resistance is mutations in a gene called pfkelch13 — that encodes a protein called Kelch13, which is involved in how the parasite takes up the contents of the red blood cell it lives inside. Standard global surveillance programs screen clinical isolates for pfkelch13 mutations to track resistance. That system has been valuable, but a new bioRxiv preprint this week shows it has a blind spot — at least in Bangladesh.

The study focused on the Chittagong Hill Tracts in southeastern Bangladesh, a region that borders Myanmar and still has persistent P. falciparum transmission. The researchers wanted to know whether artemisinin-resistant parasites could evolve locally in that geographic background, and what mutations would drive that resistance.

They used a technique called experimental evolution. They took a malaria parasite strain collected from a Chittagong patient in 2018 that was fully sensitive to artemisinin, then subjected it to escalating drug pressure over 18 months — 27 rounds of increasing artemisinin concentrations. They ran this independently three times to see which mutations would arise reproducibly across all three experiments.

What emerged was not mutations in pfkelch13. Instead, all three independent evolution experiments converged on loss-of-function mutations in a gene called pfkic1 — where KIC stands for Kelch13 Interacting Candidate. KIC1 is a protein that works in the same cellular compartment as Kelch13 — a structure called the endosome, part of the machinery the parasite uses to take in and digest hemoglobin from the red blood cell. The key connection to drug resistance is that artemisinin is chemically activated by heme, a molecule released during hemoglobin digestion. Disrupt the endocytic pathway, and you reduce the amount of heme-activated artemisinin that can damage the parasite. Knocking out KIC1 appears to do exactly this, bypassing the need for pfkelch13 mutations entirely.

To connect the lab finding to real patients, the team analyzed 22 field isolates from actual Chittagong patients treated with artemisinin-based drugs. All 22 had wild-type pfkelch13 — no conventional resistance mutations. But natural variation at the pfkic1 locus was statistically associated with both worse drug survival in the laboratory and slower parasite clearance in the actual patients who carried those parasites.

The practical implication is direct: if KIC1-based resistance is spreading in the Chittagong Hill Tracts, standard pfkelch13 surveillance would return clean results. Clinicians might see treatment delays attributed to other causes. The paper is an argument to add pfkic1 monitoring to resistance programs in Bangladesh and similar high-risk border regions before this pathway spreads further.

On the business side: GSK announced this week it is halting development of camlipixant, a drug for chronic refractory cough, after its two Phase 3 trials gave conflicting results.

Chronic refractory cough is a miserable condition — patients cough uncontrollably hundreds of times a day, with no effective treatment. The underlying mechanism is hypersensitivity of sensory nerves in the airways, which fire at signals they should not, triggering involuntary cough. Camlipixant targets a receptor called P2X3, which stands for purinergic receptor type 2, subunit 3. Those sensory neurons express P2X3, and they use it to detect ATP — adenosine triphosphate, the small molecule your cells use to store and transfer energy. When airway cells are irritated or damaged, they release ATP into the surrounding tissue. Released externally, ATP activates P2X3 on nearby sensory neurons, which triggers cough. Block P2X3 and you reduce that hypersensitivity.

GSK ran two large Phase 3 trials called CALM-1 and CALM-2. CALM-1 delivered a positive result at twelve weeks — patients on the fifty-milligram twice-daily dose significantly reduced their cough frequency versus placebo. But CALM-2, running to twenty-four weeks with the same dose, did not hit statistical significance. The lower twenty-five-milligram dose failed in both studies. Key patient-reported outcomes, including a chronic cough diary score, did not meet their target thresholds in either trial.

Two trials, same dose, opposite primary endpoint outcomes. GSK is ending the chronic cough program. The company originally licensed camlipixant as part of a two-billion-dollar deal, so this is a significant write-down.

It is worth noting that another P2X3 blocker — gefapixant, made by MSD — did receive FDA approval for refractory chronic cough. So the underlying mechanism is pharmacologically validated; the target is real. Camlipixant's failure is more likely a compound-specific story about dose, duration, or patient population than a verdict against the P2X3 approach. But for now, patients with chronic refractory cough have one approved option instead of two.

And finally, animals.

This one comes from deep time. In northwestern Queensland, Australia, there is a place called the Riversleigh World Heritage Area, where a series of ancient limestone deposits have been preserving fossils for somewhere between fifteen and twenty-five million years. It has been producing extraordinary specimens for decades. And this week, a paper published in the Journal of Paleontology describes three new marsupial species from those deposits — and proposes that they belong to an order that nobody knew existed.

Marsupials — the pouched mammals that include kangaroos, koalas, possums, and wombats in Australia, and opossums and others in the Americas — are divided into five recognized orders. An order is a broad taxonomic category: the grouping above family and below class. Think of it as the level that separates primates from rodents, or carnivores from hoofed mammals. The five marsupial orders account for all the major body plans and ecological niches marsupials have evolved into across their eighty-million-year history.

Now there is a sixth. Based on the distinctive dental morphology of three tiny new species from Riversleigh — ranging from the size of a shrew to the size of a mouse, living roughly eighteen million years ago — researchers at the University of New South Wales are proposing a new order they call Keeunamorphia. The teeth of these animals are geometrically distinct from all five existing marsupial orders in ways that indicate a deep evolutionary divergence. The analysis places the origin of this lineage more than fifty million years ago, during the period when Australia and Antarctica were still joined as part of the ancient supercontinent Gondwana.

And then this lineage persisted quietly in Australia for roughly thirty-five million years — through the epochs that saw the continent dry out and cool, through the evolution of the giant wombats and other Miocene megafauna — before disappearing in the Middle Miocene about twelve million years ago. Thirty-five million years is a time span longer than the entire evolutionary history of primates from their origins to today.

We found out about it from a handful of tooth fossils in a limestone outcrop in Queensland. The history of life is still full of chapters we have not read.
