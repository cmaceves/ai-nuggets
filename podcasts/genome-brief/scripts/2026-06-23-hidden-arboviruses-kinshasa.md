## Script

Welcome to The Genome Brief. Today is Tuesday, June twenty-third, twenty twenty-six, and this is Episode 1. I'm your host. We're back with a tighter, deeper format — fewer stories, more time on each one, more time spent actually explaining what the work shows and why it matters.

Today's lineup: a sero-survey out of Kinshasa that says the Democratic Republic of the Congo has been quietly sitting on a lot more dengue and chikungunya than anyone has been measuring. Then on the business side, Sanofi quietly betting that an AI vendor can dig undiagnosed alpha-one antitrypsin deficiency patients out of routine electronic health records. Then a really interesting interpretability paper that asks, in mechanistic detail, what a chemical language model actually knows about molecules — by taking it apart with sparse autoencoders. And to close, a beautiful bird-genomics story out of Japan: a single rare warbler that genomics has now split into two.

Let's start with pathogen watch.

A team led out of UNC and the Kinshasa School of Public Health posted a medRxiv preprint yesterday — June twenty-second — measuring how much dengue and chikungunya are actually circulating in Kinshasa Province, the capital region of the Democratic Republic of the Congo. And the answer is: a lot more than the surveillance system would tell you.

Quick context. Dengue and chikungunya are both arboviruses — that is, viruses transmitted by arthropods, in this case mainly Aedes mosquitoes, the same family that carries Zika and yellow fever. They cause acute febrile illness — high fever, joint pain, in dengue's case sometimes severe hemorrhagic disease — and they've exploded across the tropics over the last two decades. But sub-Saharan Africa is a strange blind spot. The mosquito vectors are there, the climate is right, and yet most countries in the region report almost no dengue or chikungunya cases. The question has always been: is that real, or is the surveillance just not catching it?

This study tries to answer that for the DRC by measuring antibodies instead of cases. The team took dried blood spots collected in twenty twenty-one from twelve hundred and fifty people across Kinshasa Province — a mix of the city center, a peri-urban site called Kimpoko, and a rural site called Bu — and ran an antigen-capture E-L-I-S-A to measure IgG antibodies to both viruses. IgG is the long-lasting class of antibody you make after an infection has run its course, so an IgG-positive sample means the person was infected at some point in the past. It's the standard way to ask, "Has this virus been here, even if we never diagnosed anyone?"

Here's what they found. For dengue: thirty-eight percent of participants were IgG-positive. That number rose with age, which is what you'd expect from a virus that's been quietly circulating for decades — every year you live, you have another chance to catch it. And it was highest in the peri-urban Kimpoko site, where fifty-five percent of people had antibodies. For chikungunya: twenty-four percent overall, also rising with age. Roughly thirteen percent of people had antibodies to both. Then they fit a Bayesian model — what's called a catalytic force-of-infection model. The idea there is simple: if you know the age distribution of who's been infected, you can back out how fast people get infected per year. The best-fitting version had transmission that varied over time and across space — meaning these aren't ancient antibodies from a one-off outbreak decades ago, they're being added in waves.

Why this matters. The DRC reports almost no clinical dengue and chikungunya in its routine system, but roughly two in five adults in Kinshasa have already been infected with dengue, and one in four with chikungunya. That tells you that the symptomatic cases — the fevers, the joint pain — are very likely being absorbed into the catch-all bucket of "fever of unknown origin" or misdiagnosed as malaria. There are millions of febrile patients across the DRC every year. Even a few percent of those being arboviral instead of parasitic is a meaningfully different clinical picture, with different management. And as urbanization, Aedes range expansion, and climate keep changing the map of mosquito-borne disease, this kind of sero-survey is the early warning that the textbook geography is wrong. We've heard this same story for Senegal and Burkina Faso over the last few years; the DRC fits the same pattern. Heads up: I'm going off the abstract on this one — the full text of the preprint is behind bioRxiv's Cloudflare wall and our usual workarounds couldn't get through this morning.

On the business side now.

Sanofi announced a partnership this morning with a company called Pangaea Data to use AI to find patients with alpha-one antitrypsin deficiency — A-A-T-D — across US health systems. The headline is dull. The motivation is interesting.

A-A-T-D is a rare inherited disorder where the body doesn't make enough alpha-one antitrypsin, a protein whose job is to protect the lungs from being chewed up by an enzyme called neutrophil elastase. Neutrophils are immune cells that arrive at sites of inflammation and release elastase to break down tissue. Normally alpha-one antitrypsin sits in the lung and neutralizes it. Without enough alpha-one antitrypsin, that elastase runs unchecked, and over years it dissolves the elastic tissue of the lung. Patients get early-onset emphysema, often misdiagnosed as ordinary smoker's C-O-P-D. By the time they get a correct diagnosis, the average patient has been symptomatic for five to eight years. Roughly ninety percent of people in the US with this condition are undiagnosed.

Pangaea's pitch is a kind of structured-plus-unstructured E-H-R mining. Their platform reads not just lab values and ICD codes but the free-text physician notes — and then surfaces patients whose pattern of pulmonary symptoms, family history, age, and prior visits matches the clinical guideline for A-A-T-D testing. It pushes those candidates back into the clinician's workflow with a recommendation to order the specific blood test. Sanofi gets to deploy this across large health systems and community clinics, and Pangaea gets one of the bigger pharma deployments of clinical-guideline-configured AI.

Now, why does Sanofi care? They have a drug called efdoralprin alfa — an engineered alpha-one antitrypsin protein replacement — that hit all primary and key secondary endpoints in a Phase 2 emphysema readout last October. It came in through their inhaled-X buyout, and it's now their lead late-stage asset in a market where the existing standard of care is augmentation therapy from human plasma. If the drug is going to support a real franchise, Sanofi needs to dramatically expand the diagnosed population — and the cleanest way to do that is to mine the E-H-R for the patients who already have the disease and don't know it. No financial terms were disclosed, and the press release notably did not include any prior validation data — sensitivity, specificity, positive predictive value, how well this platform actually identifies real A-A-T-D when independently checked. That's the number to watch as this rolls out.

It's also a useful template for the next decade of rare-disease drug commercialization. Diagnosis bottlenecks are now an explicit revenue lever, and pharma is willing to pay AI vendors to break them.

On to AI in biomedicine.

Researchers posted a paper on arXiv yesterday with the title, "What does a chemical language model know about molecules?" — and they answered it by reaching in with a tool from large-language-model interpretability called a sparse autoencoder.

Let me unpack what that means, because it's lovely.

A chemical language model is the same architecture as ChatGPT or Claude, but instead of being trained on English text it's trained on SMILES strings. SMILES — that's S-M-I-L-E-S, the Simplified Molecular Input Line Entry System — is a text format for representing molecules. Every atom and bond gets a character; rings, branches, stereochemistry, all encoded as text. So caffeine becomes a thirty-character string. If you train a transformer on tens of millions of these SMILES strings to predict the next token, what you get back is a model that has, in some sense, learned chemistry from text. The specific model in this paper is called MolFormer, an encoder-only model trained on a billion SMILES strings, and it's widely used in drug-discovery pipelines for things like predicting solubility or toxicity.

The big open question with these models has always been: is the model actually learning chemistry — atoms, functional groups, scaffolds — or is it just learning that the character "C" usually follows "C-1" inside a benzene ring? In other words, syntax versus semantics. That distinction matters a lot, because if the model is genuinely representing chemical concepts, then everything you ask it — solubility, binding, ADMET — has a chance of generalizing to molecules it has never seen. If it's just memorizing string patterns, you're going to get burned at the edges of chemical space.

Sparse autoencoders are the new way to answer that question for any neural network. The intuition is this: a transformer layer represents the input as a dense vector of, say, a thousand numbers. Each neuron isn't dedicated to a single concept — it's a smear of many concepts at once, what the field calls superposition. A sparse autoencoder is a small two-layer network you train on top of that vector. Its only job is to re-encode the same vector into a much wider space — say sixteen thousand features — under a strict constraint that only a handful of those features can be non-zero for any given input. So you force the model to disentangle. Once trained, you can ask: which inputs activate feature number five thousand and twelve? And often, what comes back is one specific human-interpretable concept — in chemistry, that might be "phenol ring" or "the carbonyl alpha to a nitrogen" or "the Lipinski rule-of-five violator pattern." This technique has been transformative for understanding what's inside language models like Claude, and now it's being applied to chemical models.

What the authors found is a clear layer-by-layer division of labor inside MolFormer. The early layers are doing what they call grammar parsing — features that track position within the SMILES string, that know "we are now inside a ring," "we are now in a branch." This is essentially low-level syntax. The later layers, deeper in the network, light up features that correspond to actual chemistry — atoms in specific substructures, pharmacologically meaningful fragments. So the network really has learned chemistry, but it's stacked on top of a stage where it's mostly just learning to read SMILES.

Two practical bites. First, they showed that if you take a valid molecule and rewrite its SMILES in a non-canonical way — same molecule, different string — the model's internal representation gets more disrupted than if you feed it an outright invalid SMILES. Because the position-tracking machinery breaks. That tells you canonical input formatting is doing more lifting than people realized. Second, they shipped a tool, InterMol, that lets a chemist click on a molecule and see which SAE features are active and what they correspond to. It's a step toward debugging chemical models the way we now debug language models — by looking inside.

Why care, beyond the technical pleasure of it. Drug discovery is increasingly betting on foundation models as the front end — generate candidates, score candidates, predict properties — and the field has had no real way to interrogate why a model recommends a molecule, or to catch the failure mode where the recommendation is just an artifact of how the input string was written. Mechanistic interpretability tools like SAEs are how you start to build that confidence.

And finally, animals.

Genomics found a new bird species in Japan — by literally listening to the DNA of one that had been hiding in plain sight for forty years.

Per Alström at Uppsala and colleagues — Alström's lab has spent careers on cryptic species in Old World warblers — published in PNAS Nexus, with a fresh round of coverage this past Saturday. The paper looks at Ijima's leaf warbler, Phylloscopus ijimae, a small olive-green songbird known from a few islands off Japan. To the eye, it's one species. The team sequenced birds from the Izu Islands and from the Tokara Islands — looked at thousands of genome-wide markers plus mitochondrial DNA — and the two populations are genetically more different than many recognized bird species are from each other. Mitochondrial DNA puts the split at roughly three million years ago. There is essentially no gene flow between them, even though both populations migrate and could in principle meet. And when the team recorded their songs, the songs were diagnostically different — a trained ear can tell them apart, but a museum drawer of skins can't.

So they've named the Tokara population a new species: Phylloscopus tokaraensis, the Tokara leaf warbler. It's Japan's first new bird species described in more than four decades.

The conservation footnote is heavier than it sounds. Tokaraensis breeds, as far as anyone has confirmed, on exactly one island — Nakanoshima — and the genomics indicate low diversity and a history of population decline. A species you didn't know existed can't be on any country's protected list. And this is the broader point: as low-cost genome sequencing rolls through systematic biology, we're going to keep discovering that things we thought were single species are actually two or three or five — and some of those splits are going to land on populations that are already in trouble, just nobody knew to count them separately. The leaf warbler is a small, charismatic example of that pattern. The genomics is, in a real sense, doing conservation triage.
