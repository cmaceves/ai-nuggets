## Script

Welcome to the Genome Brief for Tuesday, June twenty-third. Today: the Bundibugyo Ebola outbreak in the Democratic Republic of the Congo has cleared one thousand confirmed cases and is now the second-largest Ebola outbreak on record; AbbVie commits almost eleven billion dollars to a once-or-twice-a-year eczema antibody; an AI agent runs a full thousand-genome dengue epidemiology in under a day; and we close with a butterfly that doesn't seem to age. Let's go.

Pathogen watch. The Bundibugyo virus disease outbreak we covered on Sunday has continued to escalate. The DRC Ministry of Health and WHO now report one thousand and three confirmed cases and two hundred and fifty-four confirmed deaths in the DRC alone as of June twenty-first, with Uganda holding at nineteen confirmed cases and two deaths. Ituri remains the epicenter, with nine hundred and sixteen cases across twenty-two health zones. Case growth is now steeper than in any prior Ebola outbreak, this one is fighting without an approved vaccine or therapeutic, and the WHO Director-General convened a virtual meeting of African heads of state on the response last week. A timely medRxiv preprint posted yesterday from Okingo, Iroungou and Valdano takes the next analytic step — they quantify both introduction risk and sustained-transmission potential across every DRC health zone, including zones not currently affected, to flag where rapid amplification could follow an importation. The full text isn't rendered yet, so we're going off the abstract here, but the framing matters: response prioritization right now is being driven by where cases are, not where the next sustained chain is most likely. If their map holds up under peer review, it changes where you pre-stage trial vaccine and clinical staff before the next jump.

Staying on pathogen genomics — there's a Mozambique severe-malaria preprint also posted yesterday from a Hospital Central de Maputo cohort. One hundred and forty-nine non-pregnant adults admitted with severe Plasmodium falciparum malaria, of whom about forty percent were HIV-coinfected, with CD4 counts and parasite whole-genome sequencing. Two things stand out. First, in-hospital and thirty-day mortality were both significantly higher in the HIV-coinfected arm, and mortality tracked inversely with CD4 count — a reminder that severe malaria management still has to treat coinfection as a distinct clinical phenotype, not a checkbox. Second, on the resistance side: the canonical chloroquine and antifolate marker loads were not differentially elevated by HIV status, but the K-one-eight-nine-T mutation in pfkelch — the gene family that holds the artemisinin partial-resistance markers — was enriched in HIV-positive patients, twenty-one percent versus less than three. That's a small cohort and a single mutation, but it's exactly the kind of signal an Africa-wide artemisinin resistance surveillance network needs to be watching for. Africa cannot afford a kelch-driven resistance wave to settle in undetected.

On the business side. The big deal of the day: AbbVie announced an agreement to acquire Apogee Therapeutics for one hundred thirty-five dollars and eleven cents per share in cash, valuing Apogee at about ten point nine billion dollars. This is AbbVie's biggest acquisition since its sixty-three-billion-dollar Allergan buy in 2019, and the second-largest biotech buyout of 2026 so far. The headline asset is zumilokibart — a half-life-extended monoclonal antibody against I-L-thirteen, currently in Phase 3 for atopic dermatitis. The strategic point is the dosing interval. Sanofi and Regeneron's Dupixent, the dominant atopic dermatitis biologic, requires injection every two weeks. Zumilokibart, on Phase 2 data, looks like it can sustain efficacy with two to four injections per year. For a chronic disease, a quarterly or semi-annual maintenance dose is a different product on the patient-experience axis, and AbbVie has a well-rehearsed commercial playbook for immunology biologics. Closing expected Q3.

A second AI-pharma deal in the same news cycle worth flagging: Insilico Medicine signed an up-to-two-and-a-half-billion-dollar collaboration with SK Biopharmaceuticals on neuroimmune central nervous system targets, announced at the BIO 2026 conference. The structure is heavily back-loaded — eighteen million dollars in near-term payments, the rest in development, regulatory and commercial milestones, plus single-digit royalties — so this is partly a vote of confidence in Insilico's Pharma-dot-A-I platform on a notoriously hard therapeutic area, and partly SK Biopharm getting an AI-discovery-shaped pipeline at low entry cost.

To AI in the biomedical sciences. There's an interesting preprint posted today on bioRxiv from the Malavige group at the Genetech Research Institute in Sri Lanka, with collaborators from a small company called Advanced Molecular Works — the first two authors are the company's founders, which is worth flagging upfront. The system is called biomeStat, and the framing is a deliberate response to the "L-L-Ms hallucinate in biology" problem. Instead of asking the model to reason scientifically, the agent writes code that calls existing deterministic bioinformatics tools — IQ-TREE for trees, TreeTime, BEAST2 running on an NVIDIA H two-hundred GPU for phylodynamics, HyPhy for selection analysis, PyMOL for structure — and orchestrates them inside a sandboxed environment. The demonstration ran end-to-end on one thousand dengue virus genomes from sixteen Asian countries spanning 2000 to 2025, in under twenty-four hours of wall-clock time. Two outputs are worth noting. They identify nearly nineteen hundred candidate immune-escape sites that structurally co-localize with known B-cell and T-cell epitopes, and they confirm that the resistance-relevant residues for the two emerging dengue antivirals — JNJ-eighteen-oh-two and NITD-six-eighty-eight — are absolutely conserved across all four serotypes. The architectural pattern this paper pushes is the right one for high-stakes biological domains: don't have the L-L-M generate scientific conclusions, have it orchestrate deterministic tools that already work. That's probably the most defensible posture for "A-I scientist" claims for the next year or two.

We'll skip the AI tools segment again — there's nothing in the last two days from the major labs that clears the bar for this show.

And finally, animals. A nice paper out of Jessica Foley's group at Bristol with the Smithsonian Tropical Research Institute, published in Nature Communications last week, looked at lifespan and aging across the genus Heliconius — the long-winged, pollen-feeding rainforest butterflies of Central and South America. Heliconius hewitsoni, on their numbers, can live up to three hundred forty-eight days as an adult. The nearest relatives in the family — Dione juno, for instance — live about fourteen. That is a twenty-five-fold difference between genera that diverged not all that long ago. And in at least one species, Heliconius hecale, grip strength stays essentially flat with age while it declines steeply in the comparison species. The story isn't fully "they live longer because they eat pollen as adults" — that helps, but evolutionary adaptations contribute independently. The headline for the longevity field is that there's a clade of butterflies quietly evolving slow aging in plain sight, and the underlying biology hasn't been worked out yet. That's the brief.
