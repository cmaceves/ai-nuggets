## Script

Welcome to the Genome Brief for Monday, June twenty-second. Today's brief: an mRNA flu vaccine just cleared its FDA advisory committee nine to zero, an Australian state's twenty-year genomic record shows Shigella sonnei moving from sensitive to extensively drug-resistant in front of a public-health lab, a DNA language model paper offers an honest negative result about how much you can get out of pretraining, and T. rex apparently spent forty years growing up. On we go.

Pathogen watch first. There's a quietly important preprint out of the Microbiological Diagnostic Unit Public Health Laboratory in Melbourne — Norelle Sherry, Danielle Ingle, Ben Howden and colleagues. They sequenced thirteen hundred and five Shigella sonnei isolates from the state of Victoria, every cultured case from January 2002 through December 2024, with surveillance-grade metadata on travel and sexual exposure attached. What you see when you line up twenty years of population data is a stepwise march. The community moves from drug-sensitive Shigella to multi-drug-resistant lineages, and then to extensively drug-resistant lineages, with most of the escalation concentrated in men who have sex with men, plus a major point-source outbreak overlay. Two distinct X-D-R sub-lineages are now established in Victoria. The clinical-management consequence is real — over the period the local recommendation has had to shift through azithromycin and toward carbapenems, which is exactly where you don't want to be for a community-transmitted enteric pathogen. The broader point is that this kind of result is what genomic surveillance is for: not catching a single dramatic spillover, but watching a slow ratchet, sub-lineage by sub-lineage, and feeding that back into prescribing in close to real time.

On the business side. The biggest story is Moderna's mRNA flu vaccine — mFlusiva, code name mRNA ten-ten — clearing the FDA's vaccines advisory committee last Thursday with two unanimous nine-to-zero votes, one for adults fifty to sixty-four and one for sixty-five and over. The headline efficacy point is a roughly twenty-seven percent relative reduction in influenza illness compared with standard-dose flu vaccine in adults fifty and up. That number is interesting, but it isn't the strategic story. The strategic story is timing: an mRNA platform compresses the strain-selection-to-shipping window from about six months down to two or three. For seasonal flu, where the WHO strain pick and the actual circulating viruses routinely diverge, two months of additional matching window is potentially a much bigger health effect than the headline efficacy delta. The vote is also notable because the FDA had previously declined to even review the application, citing inadequate study controls, and reversed that position after public pushback — so this is the first new vaccine recommendation out of this committee since 2023. PDUFA date is August fifth. Worth watching for the broader signal about whether the mRNA platform stays usable for non-COVID vaccines.

To AI in the biomedical sciences. There's a careful preprint posted yesterday on fine-tuned DNA language models for ab initio gene annotation — the system is called GENATATORs. The setup is the most fashionable possible setup: take a pretrained DNA language model, freeze it, use the embeddings as features for downstream tasks. What they actually find is a sharp negative result. Pretrained DNA-LM embeddings on their own do not capture the features you need for accurate gene segmentation — the boundaries between exons, introns, and untranslated regions — and task-specific fine-tuning isn't a nice-to-have, it's essential. With fine-tuning, the models do beat existing annotation tools and, importantly, generalize across species separated by hundreds of millions of years from anything in training. There's a secondary lesson in the paper worth flagging: the standard per-token and per-sequence accuracy metrics that gene-annotation benchmarks have leaned on don't actually capture annotation quality the way a working genomicist would judge it. They propose biologically grounded alternatives. Both of these — fine-tuning isn't optional, and benchmark metrics need to track what biologists care about — are messages the foundation-model-in-biology field should be hearing more loudly than it is.

We're going to skip the AI-tools segment again today — the most discussed item out of the big labs in the last seventy-two hours was John Jumper leaving Google DeepMind for Anthropic, which is a serious personnel move but not a capability release, and there's nothing else in the window that clears the bar.

And finally, animals. Holly Woodward at Oklahoma State, with Nathan Myhrvold and Jack Horner, published a paper in PeerJ today that takes the question of how fast Tyrannosaurus rex actually grew and answers it with the largest T. rex bone-histology dataset ever assembled — seventeen specimens, growth rings read under polarized light using statistical reconstructions that recover rings the older methods couldn't see. The old textbook number was that T. rex stopped growing around age twenty-five. The new estimate is closer to forty. So an apex predator that we'd been thinking of as a fast-living, fast-growing animal was actually closer in life-history pace to a long-lived crocodilian or a large modern bird. There's also a side finding that some of the most famous "small T. rex" specimens in the literature — including Jane and Petey, which the field has gone back and forth on for years — may not be T. rex at all but a separate, smaller tyrannosaur. Not a bad way to start a Monday: the most famous dinosaur we know took as long to grow up as we do. That's the brief.
