## Script

Good morning. Today we have five stories.

First, a preprint from groups in Chile and Argentina shows that an emerging variant of one of the most feared antibiotic resistance enzymes is being packaged into little vesicles and shed into the environment around the bacteria that produce it — where it can degrade carbapenems and even cefiderocol, one of the last-line antibiotics, and protect bystander bacteria that should otherwise be killed by those drugs.

Then, a Cornell-led group has analyzed every confirmed chikungunya case in Brazil over the last eleven years — more than one point two million of them — and found something with immediate consequences for how Brazil rolls out its new chikungunya vaccine: the places driving epidemic spread and the places where people are dying are not the same places.

On the business side, Ipsen has agreed to buy Kartos Therapeutics in a deal worth up to one point seven five billion dollars for a single drug — an oral MDM2 inhibitor that tries to reactivate the most famous tumor suppressor in biology in a blood cancer that, despite a decade of available targeted therapy, still kills most patients.

In AI for biomedicine, a UCSF group has built a tool to extract the structure of how large language models actually reason through a clinical case — and used it to show that the models can get the right diagnosis without using the same reasoning schema twice.

And finally, mountain lions have moved into a two-square-kilometer patch of land south of San Francisco, and after a decade the entire food web has rearranged itself.

Let's get into it.

---

Pathogen watch.

First, the antibiotic resistance story, and it's a striking one. The preprint went up on bioRxiv on Monday from groups at Universidad del Desarrollo in Chile, the Houston Methodist Research Institute, and the Instituto de Biología Molecular y Celular de Rosario in Argentina.

To explain why this is a big deal, we need to back up a minute. The carbapenems — drugs like imipenem and meropenem — have been the workhorse antibiotics for the worst gram-negative infections for decades. When a bacterium becomes carbapenem-resistant, the options narrow dramatically and mortality rises. *Klebsiella pneumoniae* is one of the bugs the WHO lists as a critical-priority pathogen for exactly this reason. The two main resistance enzymes that get carbapenem-resistant *Klebsiella* into the news are called KPC and NDM. They are both beta-lactamases — enzymes that chew up the ring structure at the heart of carbapenem antibiotics and inactivate them — but they are mechanistically and structurally different. KPC is a serine beta-lactamase: it uses a serine amino acid to attack the drug. NDM is a metallo-beta-lactamase, often abbreviated MBL: it uses two zinc ions in its active site instead. And critically for today's story, KPC floats freely in the periplasm — the space just outside the bacterial inner membrane — while NDM is physically anchored to the outer membrane of the cell.

That anchoring detail turns out to matter a lot, because of a structure called the outer membrane vesicle, or OMV. Gram-negative bacteria, including *Klebsiella*, naturally pinch off little bubbles of their outer membrane and release them into the environment around them. These bubbles are real, lipid-bilayer nanoparticles, and they carry whatever happened to be in the patch of membrane they came from — including, potentially, any proteins anchored to that membrane. OMVs have been recognized as a kind of bacterial postal system for a while: they carry virulence factors, signaling molecules, and pieces of the cell wall out into the environment and to other cells.

What this paper shows, cleanly, is that NDM hitchhikes on this system, KPC does not, and the consequences are real. The team took clinical *Klebsiella pneumoniae* isolates carrying either NDM or KPC, isolated the outer membrane vesicles they released, and probed them for enzyme content. NDM enzyme was present in the vesicles and was catalytically active — it could chew up carbapenems sitting outside the bacterium. KPC was confined to the bacterial cells themselves and did not show up in vesicles at all. The mechanistic interpretation is straightforward: because NDM is membrane-anchored, when the outer membrane pinches off into a vesicle, NDM goes with it. KPC, floating in the periplasm, doesn't get loaded.

Then they did the experiment that matters most clinically. They took susceptible *Klebsiella pneumoniae* — bacteria that should be killed by imipenem — added the NDM-loaded vesicles from the resistant strains, and challenged them with the antibiotic. The susceptible bacteria survived. The vesicles were doing the work in the surrounding medium, degrading the imipenem before it could reach the cells, and effectively shielding bystander bacteria that had no resistance gene of their own.

Then they pushed further and tested cefiderocol. Cefiderocol is worth a moment, because it sits very near the bottom of the antibiotic well. It is a siderophore cephalosporin — meaning the drug is attached to a molecule that mimics iron-scavenging compounds bacteria need to survive, allowing the antibiotic to be smuggled into the cell through iron-uptake channels. It was approved specifically because it could get past the kinds of resistance mechanisms — including metallo-beta-lactamases like NDM — that defeat the older drugs. NDM-loaded vesicles partially restored growth of susceptible *Klebsiella* in cefiderocol. The shielding effect leaked into a drug that was developed specifically for these patients.

And the effect was more pronounced for NDM-7 — the variant in the title — than for the older NDM-1. NDM-7 has only two amino acid changes from NDM-1, but it is increasingly common in clinical isolates worldwide, and the suggestion here is that part of why it is spreading may be that it both degrades drugs more efficiently and is exported more efficiently into vesicles where it can also protect its neighbors.

The reason this is conceptually important is that antibiotic resistance has, until now, mostly been thought of as a cell-autonomous property: this cell carries this gene, so this cell survives this drug. Resistance moves between bacteria primarily through horizontal gene transfer — plasmids and similar mobile elements — on time scales of bacterial generations. What this work suggests is that resistance can also act at a distance, in real time, without any gene transfer happening at all. A resistant *Klebsiella* in a wound or a urinary tract or a bloodstream can be exporting enzymes that protect every susceptible bug in its neighborhood for as long as it keeps making vesicles. For clinicians, that means a polymicrobial infection might be harder to clear with a carbapenem than the susceptibility report on any single isolate would predict — because the dominant resistant organism is metabolically defending the others. This is a preprint, not yet peer-reviewed, and it remains to be shown whether vesicle-mediated protection happens at the concentrations that occur in real infected tissue. But it's the kind of mechanism that, if it holds up, changes how you think about a culture report.

Still on pathogens, also a preprint from Monday, this one in medRxiv from a Cornell-led group with collaborators in Brazil — and it is one of the most policy-actionable pieces of work I've seen this month.

The background is that chikungunya — a mosquito-borne alphavirus that causes high fever and an arthritis that can last months or years — has been hammering Brazil since it arrived in 2014. The country has logged more than one point two million confirmed cases, and the 2024 to 2025 wave pushed further south than any previous outbreak. There is now a vaccine, called IXCHIQ, made by a French biotech, that uses a live-attenuated virus to generate immunity. But the rollout has been complicated. In August 2025 the US FDA suspended the IXCHIQ biologics license over serious adverse events in adults aged sixty and older. The European, Brazilian, and Canadian regulators kept the vaccine on the market with revised guidance, asking prescribers to do individual benefit-risk assessments. In February of this year, Brazil began deploying it through SUS — Sistema Único de Saúde, the universal public health system. The unanswered question was: in a country of two hundred million people, who should be vaccinated, and where?

The Cornell group, led by Ana Bento, took every confirmed chikungunya case logged in Brazil's national notifiable-disease system from 2015 through 2025 — one point two three five million in total, across all five thousand five hundred and seventy Brazilian municipalities — and built a Bayesian hierarchical model on top of it. A Bayesian hierarchical model is just a statistical framework that lets you estimate things like transmission intensity at the municipality level while sharing information across neighboring municipalities and across years, so that small places with sparse data don't dominate the estimates. They paired that with survival models — Cox regression — to study how fast people who get hospitalized progress to either recovery or death.

The findings are striking on two axes. The first is geographic: the epicenter of chikungunya transmission in Brazil has shifted. In 2016 and 2017 it was concentrated in the Northeast — the historical entry point. By 2024 and 2025, the Central-West of the country was carrying disproportionate burden, with peak municipalities running incidence rate ratios more than two point seven times the national median. The disease has been moving across the country over a decade.

The second axis is demographic, and this is where things get interesting. The bulk of confirmed cases are in adults between twenty-five and fifty-five. But the mortality, when people do die, is concentrated in older adults — the hazard ratio for death after hospitalization is more than ten times higher for people aged eighty and over compared to those in their twenties. So Brazil has, in effect, two different chikungunya problems happening in two different places. The Central-West is the engine of transmission, but its population skews younger and its case fatality is lower. The Southeast and South have less transmission but older populations and substantially higher case fatality among those who do get hospitalized — nearly five percent versus three and a half percent in the Central-West.

The authors take the next step and convert this into a vaccination allocation framework. They classify all five thousand five hundred and seventy municipalities into three categories. Transmission-control priority municipalities — eight hundred and thirty-two of them — are high transmission with younger populations, mostly in the Central-West. Vaccinating young adults there breaks chains of transmission. Clinical-preparedness priority — another eight hundred and thirty-two — are lower transmission but older populations, mostly Southeast and South. The right move there is to vaccinate older adults to prevent severe disease when transmission does arrive. And four hundred and sixty-one combined-priority municipalities, mostly in the Northeast and Southeast, need both.

The headline number is sobering. If Brazil had used a simple age-based vaccination rule — vaccinate the elderly, full stop, the way many countries default to for vaccines they think of as protecting against severe disease — that rule would leave eighty-two percent of the 2024–25 reported cases in municipalities the rule would not have prioritized for vaccination. That's not a small mismatch. That's most of the active outbreak being missed. The argument is that for an arboviral disease in a country with sharp regional heterogeneity, you have to allocate the vaccine on both transmission and demographic axes simultaneously — or you'll spend doses in the wrong places.

On the business side of biomedicine.

Yesterday morning, the French biopharma Ipsen announced that it has agreed to acquire Kartos Therapeutics, a privately held California oncology company, in a deal worth up to one point seven five billion dollars — four hundred and fifty million up front and up to one point three billion in milestone payments tied to regulatory approvals and sales. The asset Ipsen is buying is essentially one drug: navtemadlin, an oral MDM2 inhibitor in Phase 3 development for myelofibrosis.

Myelofibrosis is a chronic blood cancer — one of a family of diseases called myeloproliferative neoplasms, which are clonal disorders of the bone marrow stem cells. In myelofibrosis specifically, the marrow gets progressively replaced by scar tissue, the spleen swells dramatically as it tries to take over blood production, patients become anemic and chronically symptomatic, and the median survival is measured in years. For more than a decade the standard treatment has been ruxolitinib, sold as Jakafi, which blocks the JAK-STAT signaling pathway that drives the abnormal cell proliferation. Ruxolitinib shrinks the spleen, improves symptoms, and extends life — but it is not a cure, and most patients eventually have a suboptimal response: the disease keeps progressing under the drug.

Navtemadlin attacks the problem from a completely different angle. MDM2 is a protein whose job, under normal conditions, is to keep the tumor suppressor p53 in check by tagging it for destruction. P53 is the single most famous tumor suppressor in human biology — it senses cellular damage and either pauses cell division or triggers apoptosis, the cellular self-destruct program. Cancer cells very often disable p53, either by mutating it directly or by overexpressing MDM2 to drag it down. Myelofibrosis is unusual in that more than ninety-five percent of patients have an intact, wild-type *TP53* gene — meaning the tumor suppressor is genetically fine, it's just being suppressed by MDM2. Blocking MDM2 with a small-molecule inhibitor like navtemadlin releases p53, which then activates its normal cellular damage response program inside the malignant clone — which translates to apoptosis and reduction of the leukemic burden.

In the earlier Phase Ib/II data, navtemadlin combined with ruxolitinib produced spleen volume reductions and symptom improvements in patients who had stopped responding adequately to ruxolitinib alone, and — more interestingly — produced reductions in driver-mutation allele frequency, which is a molecular signal that the drug is actually killing the malignant clone rather than just suppressing symptoms. The pivotal Phase 3 trial, POIESIS, is now enrolling more than six hundred patients across two hundred and fifty sites, randomized to ruxolitinib plus navtemadlin versus ruxolitinib alone, with top-line data expected next year.

The competitive context matters here. The MDM2 inhibitor class has had a long and painful history — multiple compounds, including some from Roche and Boehringer Ingelheim, have failed in trials because of dose-limiting toxicities, especially to platelets and to the gastrointestinal tract. Kartos owns several patents around once-daily oral dosing of navtemadlin that they argue keeps p53 activation pulsatile rather than continuous, which is meant to reduce these on-target toxicities. Whether that bears out in Phase 3 is the central scientific bet of the deal. The financial logic, though, is what's driving Ipsen: they project navtemadlin could launch as soon as 2028 and become accretive to their core operating income by 2029. For a mid-sized European pharma trying to deepen its hematology pipeline, this is a single, late-stage, focused acquisition rather than a sprawling bolt-on — and it's structured so that most of the price tag is contingent on actually getting the drug approved.

In AI for biomedicine.

This is from a preprint posted yesterday to arXiv by Nisarg Patel at UC San Francisco, and it does something I think is becoming necessary: it goes after the gap between whether a large language model can give the right answer to a medical question and whether it can actually reason consistently to get there.

The setup is this. The author takes five different large language models — the paper compares five contemporary frontier models — and gives them fifty clinical case studies, each under three different prompting conditions. So that's seven hundred and fifty diagnostic traces in total. For each trace, the model is asked not just for a diagnosis but for the explanation of how it got there.

The interesting move is what they do with those explanations. Rather than just grade the final diagnosis as right or wrong, they extract a structured representation from each one — what they call a clinical reasoning graph. The graph captures the steps of reasoning as nodes — pieces of evidence, candidate hypotheses, ruling-out steps, decisions — connected by typed edges. Five node types and seven edge types, defined ahead of time. The idea is that two diagnoses that arrive at the same answer through the same chain of reasoning should produce graphs that look very similar; two diagnoses that happen to land on the same answer for unrelated reasons should produce dissimilar graphs.

Now they pair up cases. Take two clinical scenarios that are similar — say, two presentations of the same underlying disease — and compare the model's reasoning graphs across them. If the model has a stable reasoning schema for the disease, those two graphs should look very alike. Then take two scenarios that are clinically dissimilar — different diseases — and compare those. Those graphs should look much less alike.

What the paper actually finds is that across all five models, graph similarity for clinically similar cases is essentially indistinguishable from graph similarity for clinically dissimilar cases. The numbers are quoted as 0.488 for both-correct paired cases versus 0.484 for both-incorrect paired cases — basically identical. The models hit sixty to seventy percent diagnostic accuracy, but they are not arriving at the right answer by recognizing case structure and applying a consistent reasoning template. The reasoning trace looks roughly equally similar regardless of whether the cases share an underlying clinical structure.

The framing the author lands on is "competence without consistency." A model that knows enough medicine to often get the diagnosis right is not the same thing as a model that reasons about clinical cases the way a clinician would. And the deeper claim is that final-answer accuracy is an inadequate evaluation metric: a clinician you trusted because they were right two-thirds of the time, but who came to that answer through a different chain of inference every time, would not be a clinician you'd want as your colleague. The framework they built — the graph schema and extraction pipeline — they have released publicly, so other groups can use the same evaluation on their own models. As clinical LLMs move toward actual deployment in support of diagnosis, this kind of process-level evaluation is going to start mattering more.

And finally.

About forty-five miles south of San Francisco, in the foothills of the Santa Cruz Mountains, sits a Stanford-owned biological preserve called Jasper Ridge. It is small — under five square kilometers — and surrounded on all sides by roads and residential neighborhoods. It is not the kind of place that ought to be supporting top predators. And then, starting around 2015, mountain lions started showing up.

In a paper out this month in *Ecology and Evolution*, a team led by Stanford doctoral student Chinmay Sonawane and senior author Liz Hadly worked through nine years of motion-activated camera-trap data and vegetation surveys at Jasper Ridge to see what happened in the ecosystem after the lions arrived. The answer is that essentially everything below them in the food web changed.

Mountain lions are not living year-round in the preserve — it is far too small to support a resident lion. But they range through it from the larger Santa Cruz Mountains, and they kill deer when they do. Deer activity dropped substantially after the lions arrived. With fewer deer browsing, woody plant density — young oaks especially, the kind of saplings deer normally chew right back into the dirt — increased dramatically. That's a classic trophic cascade: top predator down to herbivore down to plants. It's the same kind of cascade that famously played out at Yellowstone with wolves and elk.

But the more interesting effect happened among the mesopredators — the medium-sized carnivores. When the lions started coming through, coyote and bobcat activity went down. They are large enough to be killed or harassed by a lion, and they apparently decided not to be there. With the coyotes and bobcats gone, foxes — which are too small for a lion to bother with but are normally pushed around by coyotes — became more active. And the brush rabbits — which the foxes hunt — became more active in the early morning, shifting their schedule in response to the new predator regime.

The take-home is that you don't need a Yellowstone-scale wilderness for top predators to remodel an ecosystem. A four-square-kilometer suburban preserve, embedded in a residential matrix and connected to a larger range, is enough — provided the predator is willing to commute. This matters because, in the United States, more than eighty percent of protected areas are smaller than five square kilometers. The conventional view has been that those small fragments can't really do the ecological work of a true wilderness. Jasper Ridge suggests that, with the right connectivity, they can.

That's it for today. An emerging carbapenemase that travels in vesicles and protects the bugs around it. A million-case Brazilian dataset that says vaccinating only the elderly would miss four out of five chikungunya cases. A one point seven five billion dollar bet on reactivating p53 in myelofibrosis. A new way to grade not whether a model gets the answer right but whether it reasons the same way twice. And mountain lions, quietly rearranging a suburban California ecosystem from the top down.
