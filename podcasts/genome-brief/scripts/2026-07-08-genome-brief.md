We'll start on the pathogen watch with an update on the Bundibugyo Ebola outbreak in the Democratic Republic of the Congo, and a WHO decision from last week that quietly changes what the response looks like on the ground.

A brief refresher, because Ebola-the-species and Ebola-the-disease are not the same thing. There are four filoviruses that cause what we call Ebola virus disease in humans — Zaire ebolavirus, Sudan ebolavirus, Taï Forest ebolavirus, and Bundibugyo ebolavirus. Zaire is the one most people mean when they say "Ebola" — it drove the West Africa outbreak of 2014 to 2016 and has the highest historical case fatality rate. Bundibugyo, in contrast, has been the most obscure of the four. Since its discovery in 2007, there had been only a couple of small, localized outbreaks along the Uganda-DRC border, each with a few dozen cases. The current one, which the WHO Director-General declared a Public Health Emergency of International Concern on May seventeenth, is by far the largest ever — and it's still growing.

As of July fifth, the DRC has reported one thousand five hundred and sixty-one laboratory-confirmed cases and five hundred and six deaths, concentrated in Ituri Province. Ituri alone accounts for about ninety percent of the confirmed cases across roughly two-thirds of its health zones. Uganda has confirmed twenty cases and two deaths — most of them imports linked to Kampala, and importantly no new cases in Uganda since June fifth. The response is being run in a region with active armed conflict, which restricts where surveillance teams can go and how fast samples can be moved.

Here is where diagnostics become the story. For a disease with an incubation period of two to twenty-one days, and clinical presentation that overlaps with malaria, typhoid, and every other tropical fever in the region, you cannot control an outbreak without a fast, specific molecular test. And for Bundibugyo virus specifically, until this month, there was no diagnostic test that had cleared any regulatory pathway. Tests designed for Zaire ebolavirus don't reliably pick up Bundibugyo — the two viruses share only about sixty to seventy percent nucleic acid identity depending on the region of the genome. In practical terms, that mismatch means false negatives at the field level.

At the start of this outbreak, testing capacity was two to three laboratories in the affected region, with combined throughput of two to four hundred tests per day. That is a very small window into an outbreak now generating dozens of new confirmed cases daily.

On July second, WHO added the first-ever molecular diagnostic test for Bundibugyo virus to its Emergency Use Listing. The Emergency Use Listing, or EUL, is WHO's fast-track authorization pathway for medical products during public health emergencies — it is not full pre-qualification, but it is enough regulatory cover for donors, ministries of health, and multilateral procurers to buy and deploy the product at scale. The listed test, from Shanghai ZJ Bio-Tech, is an RT-qPCR assay — meaning it uses reverse transcription to convert the virus's RNA genome into DNA, then quantitative PCR to detect and amplify a specific Bundibugyo genetic signature. That specificity matters: an RT-qPCR designed with primers matched to Bundibugyo sequences avoids the cross-reactivity problems of pan-filovirus tests. Three more Bundibugyo diagnostics are under active review.

The listing has already translated into capacity. From two or three sites doing a few hundred tests a day, the response network is now ten laboratories across the affected provinces with capacity above two thousand tests per day. That is roughly an order of magnitude more surveillance, which changes what containment looks like: fewer suspected cases sitting in isolation for days waiting for a sample to reach a lab, faster contact tracing, and — critically — an earlier warning if the virus reaches a new geography. This is the kind of quiet infrastructure win that doesn't make outbreak headlines but is often the difference between an outbreak that gets contained and one that doesn't.

On the business side, AstraZeneca is buying its way into the newest respiratory drug class with a Chinese-developed asset. The deal was announced today.

The disease is chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, or COPD — the umbrella term for smoking- and pollution-related lung damage that combines the airway inflammation of chronic bronchitis with the alveolar destruction of emphysema. It affects around four hundred million people globally, kills about three million people a year, and until last year had gone more than two decades without a genuinely new class of maintenance drug. The workhorses have been long-acting bronchodilators — beta agonists and muscarinic antagonists that open the airways — and inhaled corticosteroids for the inflammation. Useful, but not disease-modifying.

In June 2024, the FDA approved Ohtuvayre, generic name ensifentrine, from a small U.K. biotech called Verona Pharma. Ohtuvayre is the first inhaled dual PDE3 slash PDE4 inhibitor, and the mechanism is elegant. PDE3 and PDE4 are phosphodiesterases — enzymes that break down cyclic AMP, or cAMP, a small molecule that acts as a second messenger inside cells. When you block a phosphodiesterase, cAMP levels rise. In airway smooth muscle, high cAMP causes the muscle to relax, which is bronchodilation — the same effect you get from an albuterol inhaler, but through a different pathway. In immune cells, high cAMP dampens pro-inflammatory signaling, tamping down cytokines like TNF-alpha and IL-8 that drive the chronic airway inflammation of COPD. So a single molecule that inhibits both PDE3 and PDE4 delivers bronchodilation and anti-inflammation together, without the systemic side effects of oral steroids. Merck saw the potential and last year acquired Verona Pharma to bring Ohtuvayre into its portfolio.

Enter TQC3721. Same mechanism — inhaled dual PDE3-slash-PDE4 inhibitor — developed by Chia Tai Tianqing, a subsidiary of the Hong Kong-listed Sino Biopharmaceutical. It's currently in Phase 3 registration trials in China as a nebulized inhalation suspension, and in Phase 2 as a dry powder inhaler formulation. The dry powder version is potentially the bigger prize: nebulizers are cumbersome and require a machine and about ten minutes of use per dose, while a dry powder inhaler is pocket-sized and takes seconds. If TQC3721 can match Ohtuvayre's efficacy in a dry powder formulation, it becomes a much more portable version of the same class.

The deal announced today gives AstraZeneca global rights outside of China. Sino Biopharm gets two hundred million dollars up front, up to one point nine billion dollars in milestone payments tied to development, regulatory, and sales targets, and tiered double-digit royalties on future net sales. Total headline value: about two point one billion dollars. Sino Biopharm keeps its home market. This is Sino Biopharm's second major out-licensing deal of the year — a Sanofi collaboration signed in February was the first — and it fits a broader pattern of Chinese biopharma companies increasingly serving as sources of clinical-stage assets that Western majors license for global development. For AstraZeneca specifically, it plants the company directly in the same drug class as its rival Merck, at roughly one-fifth the acquisition cost Merck paid for Verona.

Moving to AI in biomedicine, a preprint posted this week from Brian Pierce's group at the University of Maryland asks a very practical question about protein structure prediction: how good are AlphaFold and its cousins at modeling one of the hardest and most useful classes of protein complex — the way antibodies and T cell receptors recognize their targets — and what should you actually do if you're trying to use these tools for vaccine or antibody design.

Some context on what these models do and why immune recognition is such a hard case. AlphaFold, from DeepMind, famously solved the single-protein structure prediction problem — you feed it an amino acid sequence and it tells you the three-dimensional shape. AlphaFold2 opened this up to protein complexes; AlphaFold3, released in 2024, extended the accuracy and added support for small molecules and nucleic acids. For most protein-protein interactions, AlphaFold3 works remarkably well. But two categories of complex remain stubborn. First, antibody-antigen interactions — where you have a Y-shaped immunoglobulin molecule, and its two variable loops at the tips are the part that grips onto a specific target. The variable loops are highly diverse across antibodies and don't follow strong evolutionary conservation, which is precisely what AlphaFold uses as an information source. Second, T cell receptors, or TCRs, recognizing peptide-MHC complexes — where a small piece of a viral or tumor protein sits inside a cleft on the MHC molecule and the TCR reaches in to feel it. Same problem: the recognition interface is hyper-diverse.

The Maryland group did a systematic head-to-head. They benchmarked AlphaFold2, AlphaFold3, versions of each with increased sampling — meaning the model is asked to generate many candidate structures rather than one — and several related deep-learning methods, across three classes of complex: antibody-protein, antibody-peptide, and TCR-peptide-MHC. A few results are worth pulling out. AlphaFold3 and increased sampling both improve accuracy over the AlphaFold2 defaults, as you'd expect. But the size of the improvement varies wildly by complex class — antibody-peptide is stubbornly hard even though the antigen is tiny. And critically, different methods succeed on different cases. When they pooled predictions across methods and took the best-scoring structure for each complex, near-native success rate for antibody-peptide complexes jumped from forty-one percent for the best single method to fifty-nine percent for the pooled approach.

Why this matters for a working scientist. Vaccine and monoclonal-antibody design pipelines increasingly rely on computational structure prediction as a starting point — you predict the antibody-antigen interface, then engineer improvements. If you're using AlphaFold3 alone, you're leaving accuracy on the table. Model pooling is a well-known trick in machine learning generally, but the paper puts a specific number on how much it buys you in immune complex modeling, and identifies which subclasses of interaction genuinely need it. For anyone designing antibodies against a new pathogen or a new tumor antigen, that is directly actionable.

And finally, animals — with a piece of behavioral biology that is genuinely strange and, in its way, also a story about the built environment.

On summer nights between 2021 and 2025, an amateur naturalist named Eviatar Itzkovich in the Golan Heights region of northern Israel started noticing something extraordinary under streetlights: thousands of woodlice, walking together in enormous slow-moving circles. Woodlice — sometimes called pill bugs or roly-polies — are terrestrial isopods, small crustaceans that have made the unusual evolutionary leap from ocean to dry land. They are solitary, nocturnal, and generally spend their lives in leaf litter or under stones, avoiding light entirely. Yet here they were, in single-species processions of more than five thousand individuals, silently circling a spot on the pavement, all going the same direction.

A team led by PhD student Idan Sheizaf at the Hebrew University of Jerusalem documented five of these events over the four years, and published their analysis in the journal Ecology and Evolution on July sixth. The species involved is Armadillo sordidus, a Middle Eastern isopod, and most of the individuals in the processions were females carrying eggs — a striking observation because these are exactly the animals that should most be avoiding an exposed, dangerous behavior.

They experimentally reconstructed the setup and found that the specific ingredient is a streetlight shining down from above. The light creates a circular illuminated patch on the ground. Woodlice are drawn to the boundary of that illuminated zone, and once enough of them are present, the collective walks the perimeter — the kind of self-sustaining group behavior that also produces the famous army-ant death spirals. Magnetic fields, ultraviolet content of the light, and other candidates were ruled out through controlled trials. The geometry of the illuminated boundary is what drives the phenomenon.

The likely explanation is that streetlights are recruiting these animals into a behavior they never encountered in their evolutionary history — walking a circular perimeter in group densities that would never occur naturally — and doing so at a physiological cost. Woodlice lose water rapidly when exposed, and hours of circling under a streetlight is a real fitness hit, especially for egg-bearing females. It is one of the more direct demonstrations that light pollution alters not just what nocturnal animals see, but what they actually do — even down at the invertebrate scale, where we usually don't think of behavior as being that plastic.

Twenty thousand woodlice, walking under a lamp in Northern Israel, is not a headline. But it is the kind of observation that shifts how ecologists think about the reach of the built environment into the rest of the living world.
