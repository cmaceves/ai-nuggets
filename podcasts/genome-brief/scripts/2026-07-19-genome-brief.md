## Script

Good morning. This is The Genome Brief for Sunday, July nineteenth, twenty twenty-six. Today: a preprint that explains why treating meningococcal sepsis can paradoxically worsen vascular damage — and what to give alongside the first dose of antibiotics to stop it; the first mRNA vaccine attempt against drug-resistant gonorrhea, which creates antibodies but not protection, and what that gap reveals about this pathogen; a new genomic map of the only virus in the hantavirus family known to spread between people, published days after the cruise ship outbreak that dramatized why this matters; and a DNA study that rewrites the origin story of the koala — turns out the species nearly vanished sixty thousand years before the first human arrived in Australia. Let's start with pathogen watch.

Neisseria meningitidis — the meningococcal bacterium — is one of medicine's most feared pathogens. It can turn a healthy teenager into a critically ill patient in under twenty-four hours. The disease has a distinctive and devastating clinical picture: bacteria invade the bloodstream, reach the walls of blood vessels, and drive widespread clotting and hemorrhage. The signature is the petechial rash — small spots of blood under the skin — and in the most severe cases, purpura fulminans, where large areas of skin and underlying tissue lose their blood supply entirely and undergo necrosis. Children with purpura fulminans can lose fingers, hands, or feet even after surviving the infection.

The treatment is straightforward: intravenous ceftriaxone, started immediately, kills the bacteria within hours. But here is a clinical paradox that has frustrated infectious-disease physicians and pediatric intensivists for decades. In many patients, vascular injury continues or accelerates after the bacteria are gone. New purpuric lesions develop. Patients require amputations. Some die from coagulopathy — blood that stops flowing correctly — even after the cultures have cleared. Something continues to attack the blood vessels after the bacteria are dead.

The dominant explanation has been endotoxin — the component of the meningococcal outer membrane called lipopolysaccharide, or L-P-S. Endotoxin is released as bacteria die and drives potent immune activation. But anti-endotoxin therapies have consistently failed in clinical trials, suggesting endotoxin is not the whole story.

A bioRxiv preprint posted this week from a large multicenter UK team — investigators from Glasgow, Liverpool, and London, with Dutch collaborators — identifies what the rest of the story is. The culprits are a class of bacterial proteins called histone-like proteins, or H-L-Ps, released from dying bacteria, which then attack the walls of blood vessels. And crucially, the study shows the damage can be blocked.

A brief detour into cell biology. Your cells package DNA in a nucleus, wrapped around protein scaffolds called histones. Bacteria don't have a nucleus or true histones. Instead, they use their own simpler DNA-binding proteins to compact their chromosomes. These are the histone-like proteins. They're positively charged, they bind bacterial DNA tightly, and in a healthy bacterium they stay inside the cell. When antibiotics kill bacteria, the cells lyse, and these proteins spill into the environment.

The team's core experiment was to treat meningococcal bacteria with ceftriaxone in culture, then apply what the dying bacteria released to primary human endothelial cells — the cells that line blood vessel walls — and measure the effect in real time using an electrical impedance sensor. The endothelial barrier collapsed within three hours. Then they ruled out endotoxin as the driver using a specially engineered strain of the bacteria that completely lacks L-P-S. The LPS-null strain, killed with ceftriaxone, caused exactly the same degree of barrier collapse as the wild-type strain. L-P-S amplifies inflammation, but it does not breach the vessel wall. Histone-like proteins do.

The team generated the individual HLP proteins in the lab and tested each one. Each caused dose-dependent endothelial barrier disruption. The mechanism: VE-cadherin — the protein that holds adjacent endothelial cells tightly together like molecular zippers — was disrupted and shed from cell junctions. And a receptor called EPCR, endothelial protein C receptor, which keeps blood in a non-clotting state near the vessel wall, was also stripped from the cell surface. Lose VE-cadherin and blood leaks out of vessels; lose EPCR and the local chemistry tips toward clotting. Together, those changes reproduce the vascular catastrophe of purpura fulminans.

Then the team looked at actual patients. From skin punch biopsies taken at the edges of purpuric lesions in children with severe meningococcal disease, they stained for histone-like proteins and found them — outside of bacteria, in the extracellular space, concentrated at sites where fibrinogen had leaked out of blood vessels. Fibrinogen normally stays inside vessels. When it appears in tissue outside a vessel, that marks a breach. The histone-like proteins were there, at the breach, in the patients.

The therapeutic finding is the most significant part of the paper. Histone-like proteins are highly positively charged. Heparin — the anticoagulant — is highly negatively charged and works partly by binding positively charged molecules. Non-anticoagulant heparin, a chemically modified version that retains the charge-binding properties but eliminates the anticoagulant effect, has been developed and tested in other contexts. The team applied a panel of non-anticoagulant heparins to their system and found they significantly reduced histone-like-protein-induced endothelial damage, both in cell culture and in a wax moth larva toxicity model where injection of the proteins otherwise killed the animals. A cocktail of antibodies raised against the histone-like proteins also completely reversed barrier disruption in culture.

The clinical picture this paints is this: treating meningococcal sepsis may require two simultaneous interventions — antibiotics to kill the bacteria, and something given at the same moment to neutralize what those dying bacteria release. The therapeutic window is precisely at the start of antibiotic treatment. Non-anticoagulant heparins are a rational first candidate: they have a defined chemical structure, and a version without anticoagulant activity could in principle be co-administered with the first dose of ceftriaxone in an emergency room. Randomized trials in pediatric sepsis would be the next step. And because histone-like proteins are conserved across bacteria — the team also showed that streptococcal and staphylococcal versions are toxic to endothelial cells — this mechanism may extend to bacterial sepsis more broadly, not just meningococcal disease.

Second item in the pathogen watch. A different story: a vaccine approach that took a first step and hit a first wall, and what that wall reveals.

Neisseria gonorrhoeae — the bacterium responsible for gonorrhea — is a World Health Organization priority resistant pathogen. The global spread of antibiotic resistance has been severe: a six-fold rise in azithromycin resistance documented over the past decade, ceftriaxone-resistant strains in US cities, and roughly eighty-two million new cases globally per year. The FDA approved two new antibiotics for gonorrhea in 2026 — gepotidacin and zoliflodacin — but new antibiotics are not long-term solutions if resistance continues to evolve. A vaccine is the more durable answer, but developing one has proved extremely difficult. Gonorrhea is one of the most immunologically sophisticated human pathogens. It constantly shuffles its surface proteins to avoid antibody recognition, recruits human complement proteins as a disguise, and actively suppresses the inflammatory response. A large 2026 trial of a meningococcal vaccine called 4CMenB, which was hoped to cross-protect against gonorrhea based on earlier epidemiological signals, found no protective efficacy.

A team from Oregon Health and Science University, Portland, and the Galveston National Laboratory posted the first evaluation of an mRNA vaccine platform against N. gonorrhoeae on bioRxiv this week. The strategy mirrors COVID and influenza mRNA vaccines: encode the instructions for a bacterial antigen in messenger RNA, package it in lipid nanoparticles — precisely engineered fat droplets that ferry the mRNA into cells — and let the immune system manufacture the antigen and build antibodies against it. The antigen they chose is BamA — the beta-barrel assembly machine, an outer membrane protein that is highly conserved across gonococcal strains, including drug-resistant ones, and is essential for outer membrane integrity and bacterial survival.

The mRNA-lipid nanoparticle formulation raised strong BamA-specific antibodies in mice, both in blood and in vaginal washes. The antibodies recognized diverse clinical isolates, including ceftriaxone-resistant strains. That's the positive result: mRNA-lipid nanoparticle technology can generate an antibody response against a gram-negative bacterial antigen delivered to the reproductive mucosal compartment, which is historically a difficult immunological context. This is also — to the authors' knowledge — the first evaluation of an mRNA vaccine against any gram-negative bacterium in an infection model.

But the protective result didn't follow. Neither intramuscular nor intranasal delivery accelerated bacterial clearance in a mouse vaginal infection model. And there was no serum bactericidal activity — meaning the antibodies could bind to gonorrhea bacteria but couldn't kill them. Bactericidal activity requires antibodies to bind in the right orientation to activate complement and punch holes in the bacterial outer membrane. These antibodies apparently can't execute that step.

This reveals something about the biology of gonorrhea's immune evasion. The bacterium presents a surface that generates antibodies but those antibodies can't kill it. BamA may be incompletely accessible on the bacterial surface during actual infection; the antibodies may not bind in configurations that efficiently fix complement; or gonorrhea's sophisticated immune evasion systems neutralize the complement response even when triggered. All plausible for a pathogen this well-adapted to evading human immunity.

The significance of this preprint is the platform establishment, not the negative efficacy result. Next iterations will test different antigens, adjuvants designed to push the response toward complement-fixing antibodies, and improved mucosal delivery approaches. Computational antigen discovery — using AI to predict which surface proteins are best positioned for complement-mediated killing — is one of the active threads in the field. The first mRNA bacterial vaccine experiment has been run, the data are in hand, and the gap to close is now clearly defined.

Third item. One more from the pathogen watch.

In late April of this year, a cluster of severe respiratory illness appeared among passengers on a Dutch expedition cruise ship called the M.V. Hondius. The cause was Andes virus — a hantavirus endemic to southern South America — and it was alarming enough for the World Health Organization to notify member states. Thirteen confirmed and probable cases. Three deaths. Passengers from twenty-three nationalities. The WHO declared the outbreak over on July second of this year.

What made that cluster frightening beyond its numbers is what Andes virus is. Every other member of the hantavirus family infects humans from rodent excreta — mice or rats shed the virus in urine and feces, you inhale dried aerosols, and that's how infection occurs. Human-to-human transmission in other hantaviruses essentially does not happen. Andes virus — circulating in Chile and Argentina in populations of long-tailed rice rats — is the exception. Multiple past outbreaks in the southern Andes have documented clear human-to-human spread through close respiratory contact. That's what happened on the cruise ship.

Published this week in The Lancet Microbe, a team from the Pontificia Universidad Católica de Chile presents the first nationwide full-genome characterization of Andes virus from human clinical samples — fifty-eight patients infected in Chile between 2011 and 2024, with whole-genome sequencing across all three genome segments of the virus. The Andes virus genome, like other hantaviruses, is segmented into three pieces encoding respectively the nucleocapsid protein, the two surface glycoproteins used for cell entry, and the RNA polymerase.

The team found that Chilean Andes virus divides into two major genomic lineages with distinct geographic distributions. Chi-North circulates in central Chile; ANDV-South circulates in southern Chile. The two lineages differ consistently across all three genome segments, shaped by regional isolation and drift over time, with no mixing between them in any of the fifty-eight patient samples.

The finding with the most clinical relevance is what the team did not find: no consistent genetic signature predicting which viral strains are capable of human-to-human transmission. They examined sequences from four patients with documented person-to-person transmission in past outbreaks. Those sequences did not cluster in one lineage, did not share consistent mutations distinguishing them from sequences in cases with no identified human source, and did not show any unique amino acid substitutions. The capacity for human-to-human spread does not appear to be readable from the viral genome.

That's a meaningful null result. Genomic surveillance of Andes virus infections will not yield an early warning about which cases carry higher risk of onward spread. You cannot sequence the virus and assign an elevated alert level based on what you find. Host and ecological factors — duration and proximity of exposure, the setting — appear to drive the difference between a self-limiting case and one that seeds a cluster. For vaccine design, there's also a practical implication: any candidate vaccine will need to demonstrate protection against both the Chi-North and ANDV-South lineages, which carry distinct amino acid profiles in their surface glycoproteins — the primary vaccine targets.

And finally, animals.

Koalas are among the most recognizable mammals in the world — the stocky, round-eared marsupials of Australia that spend nearly their entire lives wedged in eucalyptus branches, eating leaves so fibrous and nutritionally dilute that they sleep up to twenty-two hours a day to conserve energy. Today they are listed as vulnerable to extinction, the consequence of two centuries of habitat clearing, chlamydial disease, wildfire, and climate-driven loss of eucalyptus. Conservation campaigns frame this as something humans created. A new study published in Molecular Biology and Evolution this week finds that the koala's difficult relationship with extinction is considerably older than that.

A team led by researchers from the University of Sydney and Texas A&M University sequenced hundreds of koala genomes and, for the first time, calculated the mutation rate of the species — the pace at which the koala genome naturally accumulates random sequence changes across generations. That rate is essential to what the team did next: converting genetic distances between individual koalas into time. If two populations have diverged by a certain number of mutations, you need to know how fast mutations accumulate to know whether that divergence happened a thousand years ago or a hundred thousand years ago. With that calibration, the team reconstructed population history from the genomes, tracing how many koalas have existed at different points in time by reading the signatures of bottlenecks and expansions written in the diversity of the genome.

What they found is that the koala population began collapsing around one hundred thousand years ago and reached its worst point approximately sixty thousand years ago, at the height of the last glacial maximum. Australia at that time was colder, drier, and far less hospitable to the eucalyptus forests that koalas depend on. The population fell to what the data suggest was a small founding group of survivors. Then, as the planet warmed and forests returned between roughly sixteen thousand and six thousand years ago, koalas expanded and differentiated into the five distinct genetic populations found along Australia's east coast today.

The first human beings arrived in Australia approximately fifty-five thousand years ago. The great koala near-extinction was already forty-five thousand years underway by the time any human set foot on the continent. Climate drove the first crash, not people. Every koala alive today — in the wild, in any zoo, on any continent — descends from the animals who made it through that sixty-thousand-year-ago bottleneck.

This changes how we read the current conservation picture in two ways. First, it explains something that geneticists had noticed but struggled to interpret: contemporary koala populations have unusually low genetic diversity for a mammal of their size and geographic range. That narrowness was already baked in before European settlement. The nineteenth and twentieth century hunting that killed millions of koalas compressed diversity further, but the species was starting from a narrow baseline. Second, and more hopeful: the species has already demonstrated it can survive a bottleneck this severe and recover. That's biological evidence for resilience. The challenge now is that habitat fragmentation prevents the dispersal movements that drove the post-glacial recovery, and climate change is shifting the range of eucalyptus faster than koalas can follow.

The technique itself — calculating mutation rate de novo from genome sequences, without fossil-record anchors — is one that can now be applied to other marsupials and other vulnerable species whose demographic history is invisible in the fossil record. The koala now has a fuller story.
