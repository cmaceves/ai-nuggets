## Script

Good morning, and welcome to The Genome Brief. It's Saturday, July eleventh, twenty twenty-six. Today: a new look at how different SARS cov two variants hit hospitalized children in South Africa, a big obesity drug lands in the New England Journal, an AI agent designed to help Kenya's public-health authorities decide when an outbreak is really an outbreak, OpenAI ships its next family of models, and, to close, the goblin shark finally gets caught on camera in its own home. Let's get into it.

First, pathogen watch. A group at Stellenbosch University and Tygerberg Hospital in Cape Town posted a retrospective cohort study on medRxiv this week — I'm going off the abstract here since the preprint is only two days old and the body isn't rendered yet — but the finding is worth flagging. They looked at three hundred and fifty-four children hospitalized at Tygerberg with confirmed SARS cov two infection between March twenty twenty and June twenty twenty-two. That window covers three distinct chapters of the pandemic: the ancestral virus, then the pre-Omicron waves driven by Beta and Delta, and finally the Omicron era.

A bit of context on why this matters. Early in the pandemic, the consensus was that COVID mostly spared kids. That was true in high-income settings but noticeably less true in low- and middle-income countries, where children carry a much heavier baseline burden of severe respiratory disease. Malnutrition, HIV exposure, tuberculosis co-infection, patchy vaccination coverage, and thin access to care all pile risk onto the same kids. So the question the South African group set out to answer is: within that already-vulnerable population, did the different variants of concern behave differently in children?

Their answer is yes. Severity was not flat across the three waves. The heaviest hit came during the pre-Omicron period — the Beta and Delta waves. Not the ancestral virus, and not Omicron. That mirrors what we saw in adults: Delta especially was a nasty variant clinically, and the Omicron family, while more transmissible, tended to sit higher in the airway and cause less severe lower-respiratory disease. The finding here is that the same pattern shows up in pediatric hospitalization data from a resource-limited setting.

Two other things fell out of the analysis. Younger age was independently associated with more severe disease, which is consistent with what pediatric respiratory infections generally do — infants have narrower airways and a less-developed immune response. And elevated C-reactive protein, or CRP — a general blood marker that goes up when the body is running a strong inflammatory response — flagged the sicker kids. That's not a mechanistic finding so much as a useful bedside signal: in a setting where you can't easily sequence the virus in real time, CRP at admission can help triage who's likely to deteriorate.

Why it matters: the take-home is not "COVID is over for kids." It's that variant identity really does shift the pediatric risk profile, and public-health responses in low- and middle-income countries need surveillance systems that can actually see which variant is circulating right now. A response tuned to Omicron looks very different from a response tuned to Delta, even in the same hospital.

On the business side, Rhythm Pharmaceuticals had a big week. The New England Journal of Medicine published the full Phase three results for their drug setmelanotide in a condition called acquired hypothalamic obesity. The FDA already approved the drug for this indication back in March, so the regulatory decision is not new — but the peer-reviewed data landing in NEJM is what the clinical community has been waiting for.

Let me unpack what this drug does, because the biology is genuinely interesting. Acquired hypothalamic obesity is a rare disease where the hypothalamus — the small region at the base of the brain that regulates hunger, temperature, and hormones — gets damaged. Most often the damage comes from a craniopharyngioma, a benign tumor near the pituitary, or from the surgery and radiation used to treat it. Once the hypothalamus is injured, patients gain weight quickly and can't stop feeling hungry, no matter how much they eat. It's a devastating condition and it doesn't respond well to diet, exercise, or standard obesity drugs.

The mechanism the drug targets is a signaling pathway called the melanocortin four receptor, or MC four R, pathway. In a healthy brain, when you've had enough to eat, hypothalamic neurons release a molecule called alpha M S H that binds MC four R and tells the body: satiety, stop eating, burn calories. Hypothalamic injury breaks that signal — alpha M S H drops, MC four R goes quiet, and the appetite-and-metabolism circuit that would normally push back against weight gain is essentially offline. Setmelanotide is a synthetic replacement: it binds MC four R directly and turns the pathway back on.

The trial itself, called TRANSCEND, randomized one hundred and twenty adults and children age four and up two-to-one against placebo, for fifty-two weeks. At the end of a year, patients on the drug had a mean BMI drop of sixteen and a half percent. Placebo patients gained three and a half percent. Eighty percent of the treated group hit at least a five percent BMI reduction, and patients reported that the constant hunger dropped meaningfully too.

Why it matters: this is one of the first cases where an obesity drug is actually replacing a broken biological signal instead of just suppressing appetite generally. The GLP one drugs get all the attention, but they work through a totally different mechanism. Setmelanotide is proof that if you can identify the specific pathway that's broken, you can build a drug that fixes it. That framing — obesity as a heterogeneous set of pathway failures rather than a single disease — is probably where a lot of the field is heading.

On to AI in biomedicine. A group centered at the University of Michigan working with Kenya's Ministry of Health posted a medRxiv paper describing something called the DMT-PHE AI Agent. Again — I'm summarizing from the abstract because the preprint is only a day old. But it's an interesting piece of work.

Here's the setup. When an unusual health event happens somewhere in Kenya — a cluster of fevers, a suspected mpox case, a spike in miscarriage among livestock that might hint at Rift Valley fever in humans — someone at the district or national level has to decide: is this a routine event, or does it need to be escalated, notified up the chain, or trigger an international alert under the WHO's rules? Kenya has a validated framework for making that call, called the Decision-Making Tool for Public Health Emergencies, and it works — but it's fragmented across documents, and different officers interpret the criteria differently under pressure.

What the team built is a retrieval-augmented generation agent — or RAG agent — sitting on top of that framework. Retrieval augmented generation just means: instead of asking a language model to answer from memory, you give it a curated knowledge base — in this case, the framework plus the associated public-health guidance — and it retrieves the relevant passages before it answers. That does two things. It anchors the model to authoritative text rather than letting it hallucinate, and it lets you show the source of every recommendation, which matters a lot when a real outbreak decision is on the line.

They piloted the agent with eleven Kenyan public-health professionals across three simulated outbreak scenarios: an event of unknown cause, a Rift Valley fever suspicion, and an mpox cluster. Compared against expert gold-standard responses, the agent's overall concordance score was zero point nine two — a rough proxy for "the AI agreed with the expert on the right call about ninety percent of the time." Almost eighty percent of its recommendations came with a supporting citation, and none of the citations were fabricated. Users rated the system highly on usability, trust, and, notably, time saved.

Why it matters: this is a very grounded use case for agentic AI in public health. Nobody is claiming the agent replaces the epidemiologist. It's decision support — the human still owns the call. But in the window between "we're seeing something weird" and "we're activating the response," faster and more consistent classification saves lives. And the design principle — take an already-validated national framework and use the language model to make it more usable, rather than trying to invent public-health judgment from scratch — is a much more responsible way to deploy this technology than what we usually see.

On the AI-tools front, OpenAI opened up its GPT five point six family to everyone on Thursday. Three models: Sol at the top for complex reasoning and agents, Terra as the balanced middle tier, and Luna as the fast, cheap workhorse. All three come with a one-million-token context window, a knowledge cutoff of mid-February this year, and a set of API features aimed at building agents — programmatic tool calling, native multi-agent orchestration, explicit prompt-cache breakpoints.

The interesting benchmark is one OpenAI calls Agents' Last Exam, which tests how well a model executes long professional workflows. Sol scored fifty-three; Anthropic's Fable five scored forty on the same test. Even the small Luna model beat Fable at a fraction of the price. But on SWE-Bench Pro, which measures software-engineering ability, Fable still runs ahead by a comfortable margin — eighty percent to Sol's sixty-five. So the picture is less "one model won" and more "the leading labs are specializing." OpenAI is optimizing hard for agentic professional work at aggressive prices. Anthropic is holding onto the coding lead. For biomedical readers, the relevant question is which of these will end up powering the lab-automation and literature-mining agents you rely on. Probably the answer is: whichever is cheapest per successful task, and Luna at a dollar per million input tokens is going to be very hard to argue against for high-volume pipelines.

And finally, animals. This one is delightful. A team led by the University of Hawaii Manoa and the Minderoo-U W A Deep-Sea Research Centre published in the Journal of Fish Biology this week the first-ever footage of a goblin shark alive in its natural habitat.

If you have not seen a goblin shark, please look up a picture after this episode. They are the last surviving members of a shark family that stretches back a hundred and twenty-five million years, and they have a jaw that shoots forward out of the face like a mousetrap. Until now, every single video we had of a live goblin shark was recorded after one had been accidentally hauled up on a fishing line. Nobody had ever caught one behaving naturally in the deep.

The team got two clips. The first was in twenty nineteen, from a remotely operated vehicle at a seamount near Jarvis Island in the central Pacific, about twelve hundred meters down. The second was in twenty twenty-four, from a baited camera on the Tonga Trench slope at nearly two thousand meters — seven hundred meters deeper than any goblin shark had ever been documented, and now the deepest confirmed sighting of any shark in the entire mackerel-shark order. The lead author put it plainly: there is still so much left to explore in the deep ocean.
