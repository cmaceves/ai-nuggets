## Script

Welcome to The Genome Brief for Thursday, July twenty-third, twenty twenty-six.

Today: a genomic surveillance study from Somalia that fills in one of the last blank spots on the map for artemisinin-resistant malaria in the Horn of Africa — and finds the resistance mutation everyone was looking for. A large sequencing project in Nepal that asks what actually happened to typhoid after the country rolled out its new conjugate vaccine, and gets a nuanced answer with real policy consequences. Agios walks away from a next-generation sickle cell drug after a placebo-controlled trial that came back essentially flat, and the mechanism behind that failure is worth understanding. Anthropic — the company behind Claude — puts real money and infrastructure behind rare genetic diseases, in a way that says something about how AI companies are choosing to enter drug discovery. And in Düsseldorf, a group publishes a study that identifies the specific brain circuit that tells a honeybee whether to be a nurse or a forager — and shows they can flip an old bee back into a nurse just by silencing it.

Let's start with pathogens.

Some background you need to follow the first story. Artemisinin is the most important antimalarial drug in the world. Every first-line treatment for uncomplicated Plasmodium falciparum malaria — Plasmodium falciparum being the deadliest of the malaria parasites — is now an artemisinin-based combination therapy. The whole global malaria control program leans on it. Resistance to artemisinin first emerged in Southeast Asia around 2008 and slowly spread through the Mekong region, and the great fear for the last decade has been that it would establish itself in Africa, where the malaria burden is by far the highest. In the last few years that fear has started to materialize — but not through the Southeast Asian resistance lineage moving. It's happened independently. Local African parasites have generated their own artemisinin-resistance mutations, in the same gene, but different specific changes.

The gene is called kelch-thirteen, or K-thirteen. It's a component of the parasite's machinery for dealing with cellular damage. Certain point mutations in K-thirteen let the parasite tolerate the burst of oxidative stress that artemisinin normally uses to kill it. In East Africa, the dominant emerging mutation is called R-six-two-two-I — a single amino-acid change at position six hundred twenty-two of the K-thirteen protein. Over the past four to five years, R-six-two-two-I has been detected in Rwanda, Uganda, Eritrea, Ethiopia, and Sudan, and in northern Ethiopia it's now been documented at more than forty percent of P. falciparum infections in some regions. It's spreading rapidly in the Horn of Africa, and there was one very obvious hole in the surveillance map — Somalia. Somalia sits in the middle of that geography, has heavy malaria transmission, and until this week nobody had published systematic molecular data on whether R-six-two-two-I was there or not.

A new medRxiv preprint from a team led by researchers in Mogadishu working with the WHO and Kings College London closes that gap. Between May and October of twenty twenty-three, they collected roughly seventy-one hundred dried blood spots from patients across eight health facilities in seven regions of Somalia, and did molecular sequencing on the K-thirteen gene. They found what everyone feared and no one had confirmed: at least one infection, from Dolow, in the Gedo region, carried the R-six-two-two-I mutation. This is the first confirmed detection of a validated artemisinin partial-resistance marker in Somalia. It's a single isolate, and the study wasn't powered to give a prevalence estimate. But the geography — Dolow sits right against the Ethiopian border in a corridor of high cross-border movement — is exactly what you would predict if this mutation is spreading regionally by human movement.

The same study also mapped a different problem in the same samples — one that is not about drug resistance but about diagnostic escape. Most rapid malaria diagnostics in Africa detect P. falciparum by targeting a parasite protein called histidine-rich protein two, or H-R-P-two. If a parasite lineage has deleted the gene for H-R-P-two, the rapid test comes back falsely negative. There's a related gene, H-R-P-three, which produces a similar protein and provides some backup cross-reactivity. Both can be deleted. In Somalia, the Somalia study found H-R-P-two deletions in about four percent of confirmed infections, which is right at the World Health Organization's five-percent threshold for considering a national switch to a different type of diagnostic — a level that indicates the country is on the edge, but not clearly across it. But H-R-P-three deletions are much more common, thirty-seven percent, which means the diagnostic redundancy that's supposed to buffer against H-R-P-two loss is already compromised in a third of Somali P. falciparum infections. In practice most current tests still detect these parasites through a second antigen called pan-L-D-H — pan-lactate-dehydrogenase — so the near-term clinical damage is limited. But the population-level picture is that Somalia now has both problems that undermine malaria control in the Horn of Africa: emerging artemisinin partial resistance, and eroding diagnostic markers. And they are not independent. Work in northern Ethiopia has shown that parasites carrying R-six-two-two-I are significantly more likely to also carry H-R-P-two deletions — the two are traveling together for reasons the field is still working out.

The practical takeaway is not that Somalia's frontline treatment is failing today. Artemisinin partial resistance means slower parasite clearance, not treatment failure, and the combination-therapy partner drug is doing most of the work. The takeaway is that Somalia joins the list of countries where the surveillance regime has to change: routine molecular monitoring, not just microscopy and rapid tests, is the only way to catch what's actually happening in the parasite population before it becomes clinical failure. And for the countries around Somalia, this study fills in a piece of the puzzle they've been working blind on.

Second pathogen story, staying with genomic surveillance but shifting to a different pathogen and a different question. Nepal in twenty twenty-two became one of the first low- and middle-income countries to introduce a national typhoid conjugate vaccine — usually shortened to T-C-V — into its routine childhood immunization schedule. Typhoid, caused primarily by the bacterium Salmonella enterica serovar Typhi, is one of the classic diseases of contaminated water and poor sanitation. Nepal reports one of the highest rates of typhoid in the world, and it also has an unusually high burden of a related infection, paratyphoid, caused by Salmonella Paratyphi A — which is clinically almost identical to typhoid but is not covered by any currently deployed vaccine. So Nepal's twenty twenty-two vaccine rollout was a natural experiment. A team led by Jason Andrews at Stanford, with collaborators in Nepal, sequenced three hundred fifty Typhi genomes and one hundred fourteen Paratyphi A genomes collected between twenty eighteen and twenty twenty-four, added them to almost eighteen hundred previously published genomes, and asked what the vaccine — and, incidentally, the COVID pandemic that hit right before the rollout — did to the population structure of these bugs.

Two things they were looking for. First, is the vaccine reducing transmission — that is, contracting the effective population size of Typhi? Second, is the vaccine driving what evolutionary biologists call selection for escape — the emergence of strains that don't carry the surface molecule the vaccine targets? T-C-V works by making antibodies against Vi, a sugary capsule coating Typhi. In principle, Typhi lineages that don't produce Vi could escape vaccine immunity and take over.

The answers are interesting because they diverge across lineages. Overall, both S. Typhi and S. Paratyphi A populations shrank during this period. But when the team split it up by lineage and by time period, the pattern got specific. One S. Typhi lineage called H-fifty-eight — which had dominated typhoid globally for years and carries most of the drug resistance — contracted sharply during the pandemic itself, most likely because pandemic-era interventions such as lockdowns, hand hygiene, and reduced restaurant dining reduced person-to-person and food-borne transmission. A different lineage, three-point-three, contracted specifically after the vaccine came in — that's the lineage-level signature you would expect from a vaccine effect. And Paratyphi A — which the vaccine does not target — had only a mild pandemic dip and no vaccine-era contraction, which is exactly what should happen if the vaccine is doing what it says on the tin.

Crucially, they saw no evidence of Typhi variants escaping vaccine immunity by losing Vi. That's a real and reassuring finding — the failure mode most feared for T-C-V rollouts hasn't happened in Nepal.

The concerning finding is on drug resistance. Multi-drug-resistant Typhi has actually declined, which is good. But fluoroquinolone reduced susceptibility — the class of antibiotics that includes ciprofloxacin, and one of the workhorses for treating typhoid in adults — is now present in about seventy percent of Typhi isolates and ninety-nine percent of Paratyphi A isolates in this cohort. Fluoroquinolone resistance in Paratyphi A is essentially universal now in Nepal. Because Paratyphi A is not covered by the current vaccine, this creates a very specific policy situation: the pathogen you are not vaccinating against is the one where the antibiotic pipeline is most compromised. It's a clear argument for developing bivalent Typhi-Paratyphi conjugate vaccines, which several manufacturers have in early trials.

The big-picture takeaway is that the vaccine is working the way we would hope it would work — contracting transmission, not driving escape — but that the AMR landscape is being reshaped rather than solved, and Paratyphi A is now the more troubling half of the enteric-fever problem in Nepal. Genomic surveillance is what makes that distinction visible; case counts alone couldn't do it.

On to biotech.

On Tuesday, Agios Pharmaceuticals announced it was ending development of a drug called tebapivat for sickle cell disease after a Phase two trial came back essentially flat. This is a real, mid-sized company shelving a real program, so it's worth understanding what happened and what it says about sickle cell drug development more broadly.

Some background. Sickle cell disease is caused by a single amino-acid substitution in the beta-globin chain of hemoglobin, the oxygen-carrying protein in red blood cells. That change makes hemoglobin polymerize into stiff, sickle-shaped rods when oxygen levels drop — the deformed red cells are fragile, they lyse in circulation, they clog small vessels, and patients suffer chronic anemia and episodic vaso-occlusive pain crises. Curative approaches — bone marrow transplant, and now the CRISPR-based gene therapy Casgevy — exist but are extremely expensive and available to few. Most patients are still managed with hydroxyurea and, when needed, transfusion. There is enormous room for a well-tolerated oral pill that reduces hemolysis and raises hemoglobin.

Enter the pyruvate kinase activators. Red blood cells rely almost entirely on the glycolytic pathway — the enzymatic chain that breaks down glucose — to produce A-T-P, the cell's energy currency, and the last step of glycolysis is catalyzed by an enzyme called pyruvate kinase, or P-K. Boosting P-K activity means more A-T-P available to the red cell, and, importantly, less accumulation of a downstream metabolite called two-three-D-P-G, which lowers hemoglobin's affinity for oxygen. In sickle cell, less two-three-D-P-G means hemoglobin holds onto oxygen a little longer, which reduces the fraction of the cell's hemoglobin sitting in the deoxygenated state that polymerizes into sickles. So a pyruvate kinase activator should, in principle, reduce sickling and reduce hemolysis. That's the biological rationale.

Agios has been the pioneer here. Their first molecule, mitapivat, is already approved for a different red-cell disorder called P-K deficiency, and Agios has a supplemental application pending with the F-D-A for mitapivat in sickle cell, with a target decision date of November first, twenty twenty-six. Tebapivat was intended to be a next-generation P-K activator: same mechanism, potentially better pharmacokinetics, potentially a wider therapeutic window. Agios ran a placebo-controlled Phase two, randomizing fifty-nine sickle cell patients across three doses of tebapivat and a placebo arm. The primary endpoint was the proportion of patients whose average hemoglobin went up by at least one gram per deciliter compared to their pre-treatment baseline.

The actual numbers explain why the program is being cut. In the low-dose arm, forty-four percent of patients met the response criterion. In the middle-dose arm, forty-seven percent. In the high-dose arm, twenty-nine percent. In the placebo arm, thirty-three percent. Any experienced trialist will recognize that pattern instantly: there's no consistent dose response, one of the tebapivat arms is actually worse than placebo, and the numerical separation from placebo is much smaller than the Phase-two effect size Agios saw with mitapivat. The safety profile was consistent with prior sickle cell trials, so this wasn't a tolerability failure — it was a clinical differentiation failure. Which means Agios looked at these numbers and concluded that even if tebapivat is doing something biologically, it isn't doing enough more than mitapivat to justify carrying a second molecule with the same mechanism through Phase three.

The strategic implication is that Agios is now betting everything on getting mitapivat approved for sickle cell in November, which is where its investor pitch already lives, and this failure lets them focus resources instead of splitting them. The scientific implication is a little more interesting. Small placebo-controlled trials in sickle cell often show large placebo responses — better clinic engagement, more consistent care, more hydration, more attention to triggers. A thirty-three-percent hemoglobin response in the placebo arm is not small, and it's probably a real ceiling problem for any oral drug in this space: some fraction of what looks like drug effect in single-arm studies is really the effect of joining a trial. Tebapivat may not be a bad drug so much as a drug whose modest benefit disappeared once a rigorous placebo arm was added — which is a lesson for the whole P-K activator class.

Now to A-I in biomedicine, which today overlaps with A-I tools, so we'll roll them together.

On Monday, Anthropic — the company that makes the Claude family of large language models — did something that a year ago would have surprised people, and today looks like the model of how a foundation-model company enters biomedicine. The announcement had two parts. The first is that Anthropic will run its own preclinical drug-discovery programs targeting rare genetic diseases. The second is a formal grant program that gives outside researchers up to fifty thousand dollars in Claude usage credits over six months, split into a basic-research track and a biotech track.

Some context on why this is interesting. Rare diseases — conditions affecting fewer than around two hundred thousand patients in the U-S — are collectively enormous: something like seven thousand disorders, tens of millions of people, and most of them have no approved therapy. Individually, they are commercially unattractive to traditional pharma because the return on a Phase three trial in a two-thousand-patient indication is dismal. But rare diseases have a scientific property that is very well-suited to the current wave of A-I tools: most of them stem from a single well-characterized genetic lesion — one gene, one variant, one clear mechanistic story — which is exactly the setting where computational reasoning over the medical literature, variant databases, and pathway knowledge can plausibly add value.

Anthropic's grant program is explicit about who they're partnering with, and this is where it gets specific. Named partners include the Monarch Initiative, a long-running international consortium that maintains the Mondo Disease Ontology and the Monarch Knowledge Graph — essentially structured, machine-readable representations of what is known about human diseases and how they relate to each other. The other partners are Every Cure, which is trying to systematically identify drug-repurposing opportunities across all approved drugs and all rare diseases; the Centre for Population Genomics, which is building a Claude-based system for classifying variants in undiagnosed rare-disease patients; and the Violet Research Institute, using Claude for regulatory work.

The pattern here is worth naming. Anthropic is not trying to build a wet lab. It's positioning Claude as the reasoning layer that sits on top of already-curated biomedical knowledge — ontologies, knowledge graphs, variant databases — and adding two capabilities that traditional bioinformatics pipelines don't have: reading unstructured text like case reports and clinical notes, and generating natural-language hypotheses that a human can check. Both of those are things large language models are actually good at. The failure modes are also worth naming. Language models hallucinate, they can invent plausible-sounding gene-disease relationships that don't exist, and rare-disease research is exactly the setting where a hallucinated variant classification could send a patient down a wrong path. Anthropic's structure — grants routed through established rare-disease consortia rather than direct-to-patient tools — is doing some of that safety work by keeping expert humans in the review loop.

The other reason this matters is competitive positioning. Google DeepMind and Isomorphic Labs, which we covered last week, framed their biomedical work through pandemic preparedness — bioresilience. OpenAI has partnered with Retro Biosciences on aging and longevity, and previously announced work with the Broad Institute. Anthropic is choosing rare disease and drug repurposing. Each of the three frontier labs has now planted a flag in a different corner of biomedicine, and the shape of A-I-driven drug discovery over the next five years is probably going to be determined by which of those bets actually produces a molecule that reaches the clinic.

And finally, animals.

This week, a group at Heinrich Heine University in Düsseldorf, working with collaborators in Cologne and Frankfurt, published a paper in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences that answers a question people have been asking about honeybees for decades. How does a colony of tens of thousands of bees, with no central planner, decide who does which job?

A honeybee colony has castes and job types. The queen lays eggs. Workers, all female and all sisters, do everything else. And within worker bees there is a well-known age-based progression: for the first two to three weeks of her adult life a worker performs in-hive tasks — cleaning, feeding larvae, tending the queen, building comb — and then transitions to being a forager, leaving the hive to gather nectar and pollen. This age polyethism has been known for a century. What has not been known is what actually flips the switch. Is it purely developmental? Is it responsive to colony demand? What in the brain is different between a nurse and a forager?

The Düsseldorf group focused on a gene called doublesex. Doublesex is a very old, very evolutionarily conserved gene in insects — it plays a role in sex determination and in creating dimorphism between males and females across essentially every insect species. In bees, doublesex is expressed only in a very specific subset of neurons in the brain. The group engineered honeybees so that these doublesex-expressing neurons could be selectively silenced — inactivated on demand — by feeding the bees a chemical trigger. They could then look at what those bees did afterwards, and compare them to control bees whose doublesex neurons were still firing normally.

What they found is that when you silence the doublesex neurons in older worker bees — bees that in a normal colony would have already transitioned to foraging — those bees don't behave like foragers anymore. They revert to nurse behavior. They go back to caring for the queen and the brood. And when the silencing is removed and the neurons are allowed to fire again, the normal age-appropriate behavior comes back. The circuit isn't controlling the whole bee — it's controlling the age-linked behavioral phase.

Why this is more than a bee-brain curiosity. Division of labor in bees is one of the most-cited examples of self-organizing biological systems — it's used in engineering textbooks about swarm robotics, and it's a canonical case for how complex group behavior can emerge without hierarchy. This study says that the "no central planner" story is right at the level of the colony, but underneath it there is a very specific cellular mechanism: a small population of doublesex-expressing neurons acts as a switch inside each individual bee, and the switch is what implements the age-based transition. That's an unusually clean mapping from gene, to circuit, to behavior, to colony-level phenomenon. The same doublesex gene is present in almost every insect, including a lot of species — like mosquitoes and agricultural pests — where understanding what makes an individual do what it does at what age is not just academic.

For today, though, the lovely thing is that when the German team silenced the switch, the older bees turned into younger bees, and went back to taking care of their queen.
