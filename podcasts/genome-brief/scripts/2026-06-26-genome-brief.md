## Script

Good morning. Today we have six stories, and the one leading the episode is an Ebola outbreak that has now crossed two borders and prompted the World Health Organization to declare a global public health emergency — the first such declaration since the COVID era. We'll also look at three seasons of real-world data on infant RSV protection, the first oral carbapenem antibiotic ever approved, a ten-billion-dollar immunology acquisition, an agentic AI system that ran a complete dengue genomic analysis overnight, a historic U.S. government export control that suspended access to a frontier AI model, and a spider in the Ecuadorian Amazon that has evolved perhaps the most unsettling disguise in the natural world. Let's get into it.

---

Pathogen watch.

The active Ebola outbreak in the Democratic Republic of the Congo has become the most serious international disease emergency of 2026. As of this week, the outbreak — which is caused by the Bundibugyo species of Ebola — has produced more than eleven hundred confirmed cases in the DRC, with roughly 291 deaths, and has crossed the border into Uganda, where nineteen confirmed cases have been reported in the Kampala metropolitan area. On May 17th, the World Health Organization declared it a Public Health Emergency of International Concern, which is the highest level of international health alert the agency can issue.

To understand why this outbreak is particularly difficult to manage, it helps to understand what Bundibugyo virus is and how it differs from the Ebola strains most of us know from past news coverage. Ebola is not a single virus — it's a genus of six or seven related viruses, all of them filoviruses, meaning they are long, filamentous RNA viruses that cause hemorrhagic fever. The most familiar is Ebola virus proper, the Zaire strain, which has caused the largest outbreaks historically, including the devastating 2014 West Africa epidemic. Zaire Ebola can kill up to 90 percent of those infected when untreated, and critically, there are now licensed vaccines and approved monoclonal antibody treatments for it.

Bundibugyo is different. It was first identified in 2007 in western Uganda, in a district called Bundibugyo, and it causes disease that is clinically similar — fever, hemorrhage, organ failure — but with a somewhat lower case fatality rate, typically in the 25 to 35 percent range. The key issue is that there is no licensed vaccine and no approved specific treatment for Bundibugyo Ebola. The interventions used against Zaire Ebola — the rVSV vaccine, the monoclonal antibodies like atoltivimab — were designed against the Zaire strain and do not work against Bundibugyo. So the clinical team is managing this outbreak with supportive care alone: fluids, electrolyte correction, antibiotics for secondary bacterial infections, blood pressure support. Supportive care can absolutely save lives — it's why Zaire Ebola survival rates improved dramatically even before the specific treatments arrived — but it is labor-intensive and it requires trained healthcare workers and supply chains that are hard to sustain in conflict-affected regions.

The outbreak is concentrated in Ituri Province, which has been one of the most conflict-affected parts of the DRC for decades. More than ninety percent of the confirmed cases are in Ituri, with the rest spread across North Kivu and South Kivu — provinces that are themselves areas of ongoing insecurity. That context matters because it shapes everything: healthcare workers evacuating facilities, supply chains disrupted, contact tracing difficult when populations are displaced. The cross-border cases in Uganda are all imported — contacts of DRC cases who traveled to Kampala — and so far Uganda has not reported community transmission, which is encouraging. But with 56 percent of the importation probability modeled in one recent paper, Uganda's public health system is under real pressure.

On June 17th, right in the middle of this outbreak, the World Health Organization published its first-ever comprehensive clinical management guidelines for filovirus disease — covering Ebola and Marburg, all strains. This had not existed before. The guidelines lay out sixteen evidence-based recommendations. They say: monitor specific lab parameters to catch treatable complications early, particularly low blood glucose and electrolyte imbalances. They specify fluid resuscitation protocols — oral rehydration when patients can drink, intravenous when they cannot. They address septic shock management. They call for empirical antibiotics for secondary bacterial infections, which are common and sometimes the proximate cause of death even in viral hemorrhagic fever. And they establish structured follow-up protocols for survivors, because filovirus persistence in immune-privileged sites — the eyes, the testes — causes long-term complications in a significant fraction of survivors.

Why does it matter that this guidance has been codified now? Because previously "supportive care" was often implemented inconsistently across outbreak response teams. These guidelines operationalize exactly what good supportive care looks like, which should improve survival outcomes at the current outbreak and future ones.

For the moment, global risk remains low. Bundibugyo does not spread easily — it requires direct contact with bodily fluids of sick patients or the deceased. It is not airborne. The concern is containment in an active conflict zone, where healthcare infrastructure is fragile and surveillance is hard to sustain.

Still on pathogens: a new study from the Yale-New Haven Health System followed nearly eighteen thousand infants and young children tested for RSV over three consecutive respiratory virus seasons — from the fall of 2023 through the spring of 2026 — to assess how well nirsevimab holds up over time.

Just briefly: RSV stands for respiratory syncytial virus, which is the leading cause of infant hospitalization in the United States. Nirsevimab is not a vaccine — it's a monoclonal antibody, meaning it's a pre-manufactured protein that mimics an antibody your immune system would make, delivered by injection. The distinction from a vaccine matters here: a vaccine trains your immune system to generate its own antibodies over time; nirsevimab provides immediate but temporary passive immunity, more like getting a blood transfusion of a single useful antibody.

The headline finding is that nirsevimab is effective, but the protection wanes faster than we might hope. Across all three seasons, it reduced hospitalizations due to RSV by about 89 percent. That is strong protection. But when the researchers looked at how effectiveness changed with time since the shot, they found that protection against hospitalization started at 92 percent at one month, dropped to 77 percent at six months, and was down to about 40 percent at twelve months. The study also found that later-than-expected RSV seasons — which has been a consistent trend post-COVID — were associated with lower observed effectiveness, because by the time the virus was circulating, the protection had already begun to wane.

This does not mean nirsevimab is failing. The current U.S. recommendation is to give it once per season, ideally before RSV season begins. What this data clarifies is that the timing of administration relative to when the virus actually circulates in your local community matters a lot. As RSV seasonality continues to shift — later and more irregular than the predictable winter peaks of the past — health systems will need to adapt when they give these doses and potentially how long before the season they administer them.

On the business side.

Two pieces of news, each historically significant in its own domain.

First, on June 17th, the FDA approved tebipenem pivoxil — brand name Utebzi, developed by Spero Therapeutics and now marketed by GSK — for complicated urinary tract infections in adults. This is the first oral carbapenem antibiotic ever approved in the United States, and that is a milestone worth pausing on.

Carbapenems are among the most powerful antibiotics we have. They're broad-spectrum, meaning they kill a wide range of bacteria, and they're specifically active against many bacteria that have developed resistance to the antibiotics typically tried first. Because of their potency and their importance as a last line of defense, carbapenems are classified as critically important antibiotics by the WHO — they're meant to be reserved for cases where other drugs have failed. Until now, every carbapenem approved in the U.S. required intravenous administration, which meant hospitalization or at minimum a home-IV setup.

Having an oral form matters enormously for complicated UTIs caused by drug-resistant organisms. These are infections where, right now, a patient who has failed oral antibiotics typically needs to be hospitalized just to receive IV treatment. With Utebzi, some of those patients can be treated as outpatients. In the pivotal Phase 3 PIVOT-PO trial, oral tebipenem was non-inferior to intravenous imipenem-cilastatin in treating complicated UTIs, with about 58 percent treatment success versus 60 percent for the IV drug.

The obvious concern with any new carbapenem is antimicrobial resistance. Carbapenems are last-resort agents. Making one pill-sized and convenient risks expanding use beyond the infections that genuinely need it, which will accelerate resistance emergence. Antibiotic stewardship programs — the systems hospitals and outpatient clinics use to make sure antibiotics are prescribed appropriately — will be critical here. The clinical reality is that an oral carbapenem will be transformative for the right patients in the right settings, but it needs to be deployed thoughtfully.

The second business story: on June 22nd, AbbVie announced it will acquire Apogee Therapeutics for approximately $10.9 billion. The main asset is a monoclonal antibody called zumilokibart, which blocks a signaling molecule called interleukin-13 — also written as IL-13, and pronounced interleukin thirteen. IL-13 is a cytokine, meaning a small protein that cells use to communicate with each other. In inflammatory skin conditions like atopic dermatitis — which most people know as eczema — IL-13 is one of the key drivers of the itch-scratch-inflammation cycle. Blocking it can quiet the disease. In Apogee's Phase 2 trial, about two-thirds of patients on zumilokibart achieved significant skin clearance at week 16.

AbbVie's strategic reason to pay this premium is pretty clear. The company built its fortunes on Humira, the adalimumab antibody that was the world's best-selling drug for years. As Humira's patents expired and biosimilars entered the market, AbbVie has been diversifying aggressively. Atopic dermatitis is one of the largest markets in dermatology globally — it affects roughly one in ten adults — and it's a space where IL-13 blockade is already validated. Dupilumab, which targets both IL-4 and IL-13, is currently the market leader. Zumilokibart, which targets IL-13 alone and has been engineered with a half-life extension so it lasts longer in the body and can potentially be dosed less frequently, is a potential competitor. Whether that half-life extension translates into a clinical or convenience advantage in a head-to-head comparison with existing drugs will be the critical question in Phase 3.

AI in biomedicine.

A preprint posted this week describes biomeStat, a system that reframes how agentic AI gets applied to genomic epidemiology. Rather than using an AI model to interpret or summarize data, biomeStat uses AI as a strict deterministic orchestrator: it writes code on the fly, executes established bioinformatics tools in sandboxed environments, provisions computing resources as needed, and ensures reproducibility by treating natural language inputs as instructions to be converted into deterministic computational pipelines.

To test the platform, the developers ran a fully autonomous end-to-end analysis of one thousand dengue virus genomes sampled from sixteen Asian countries over a twenty-five year period. The system orchestrated phylogenetic tree building, Bayesian phylodynamics on a GPU-accelerated cluster, selection pressure analysis, and three-dimensional structural modeling of the viral envelope. The full analysis completed in under twenty-four hours of wall-clock time. The biological findings were themselves interesting — the dengue epidemic across Asia appears to be in endemic equilibrium, with an effective reproduction number near one, and the system identified 1,869 candidate immune escape sites on the viral surface as well as 176 highly conserved drug target residues across all four dengue serotypes — but the methodological point is the one that generalizes. This kind of workflow previously required weeks of expert bioinformatician time, careful tool chaining, and institutional computing access. If systems like biomeStat hold up in broader testing, they could genuinely democratize sophisticated genomic epidemiology for public health labs that lack dedicated bioinformatics infrastructure.

AI tools.

On June 9th, Anthropic launched Fable 5, part of a family of advanced models. It immediately topped the SWE-bench Pro benchmark for software engineering tasks, with a score of 80.3 percent. Three days later, on June 12th, the U.S. Commerce Department issued an export control directive requiring Anthropic to suspend all access to Fable 5 and Mythos 5. The directive was issued by Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick. Anthropic confirmed it is complying.

The specific reasoning behind the directive has not been fully disclosed, as is typical with export control actions. What's notable about this is the precedent it sets. This appears to be the first time a major commercial AI model has been suspended via export control authority — a legal instrument that was developed for physical goods like advanced semiconductors and aerospace technology, and has historically been used to prevent proliferation of those capabilities to adversaries. Applying it to an AI model suggests regulators are beginning to treat frontier model capabilities as similarly exportable and controllable. Claude Opus 4.8, released in late May and currently leading the Artificial Analysis Intelligence Index at 69.2% on SWE-bench Pro, remains fully available.

Whether this was the right call for Fable 5 specifically is unknowable from the outside. But as a policy signal, it confirms that the U.S. government is building out the regulatory machinery to intervene in AI model access, not just in AI development. Anyone building systems that depend on frontier model availability should probably be thinking about what resilient architectures look like in a world where model access can be administratively suspended.

And finally, animals.

In the canopy of Ecuador's Llanganates-Sangay Corridor, one of the most biodiverse regions on the planet, researchers have described a spider with what may be the most surreal disguise in the animal kingdom. It does not mimic a leaf, or a twig, or another animal. It mimics a fungus — specifically the fruiting body of Gibellula, a genus of parasitic fungi that kills spiders.

Here's how Gibellula works: the fungal spores infect a living spider, grow inside it, and ultimately kill it. The dead spider remains attached to a leaf or branch while the fungus sprouts elongated stalks from the corpse, releasing more spores. If you've ever heard of the zombie-ant fungus from tropical documentaries, this is the spider equivalent.

The new spider, named Taczanowskia waska, has evolved to look almost exactly like a spider that has been killed and colonized by Gibellula. Its abdomen is pale and covered with elongated projections that closely resemble fungal stalks. It holds its body still in a posture that matches a dead infected spider. The mimicry covers both appearance and behavior.

The discovery came by accident. A biologist conducting a nighttime survey in the Ecuadorian Amazon photographed what appeared to be a mushroom growing on vegetation. When the photo was later posted to iNaturalist, the citizen science platform, other users recognized it as a spider. Only then did the team go back and realize they had found something entirely new.

What makes this remarkable from an evolutionary standpoint is the nature of the model being mimicked. Most animal mimicry involves copying something threatening, or something another predator doesn't want to eat. This spider is copying something that predators have learned to avoid for a completely different reason: not because it's dangerous, but because it looks diseased and dead. If you were a bird or a lizard that had ever seen what Gibellula does to a spider, you would probably want nothing to do with anything that looked like it was already losing that battle. Taczanowskia waska has, apparently, figured this out.
