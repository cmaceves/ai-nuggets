## Script

Good morning and welcome to The Genome Brief for Friday, July third, 2026. Four stories today.

The World Health Organization announced yesterday that the first patient has been enrolled in a scientific trial designed to identify effective treatments for Bundibugyo Ebola virus disease — the strain now driving a large outbreak in the eastern Democratic Republic of Congo. This is the first-ever randomized trial for this Ebola species. On the business side, the French pharma Ipsen is buying a Swiss biotech called Memo Therapeutics for up to seven hundred million euros to take control of the first monoclonal antibody targeting BK polyomavirus, a virus that quietly ruins a large fraction of kidney transplants. In the AI space, a group at MIT and collaborators have shown that an AI method called FragFold can design short protein fragments that turn off — or on — the ability of a target protein to form the internal droplets that cells use to organize themselves. And we close with a study from Canadian national parks that answers, definitively, which large mammal is the most dangerous — and it is not the bear.

---

Pathogen watch.

Bundibugyo virus is one of the six known members of the ebolavirus family. When people say "Ebola," they usually mean the Zaire species, which has caused every one of the very large West and Central African outbreaks in the last thirty years, and for which a licensed vaccine and two licensed monoclonal antibody treatments now exist. Bundibugyo is a different animal. It was first identified in western Uganda in 2007, in an outbreak that killed about a third of the people it infected, and until this year it had only appeared in small, brief flare-ups. There is no approved vaccine for it. There is no approved treatment. And critically, because Bundibugyo differs from Zaire virus by about a third of its genome — including large stretches of the surface glycoprotein that antibodies have to recognize — the licensed antibody drugs for Zaire Ebola do not reliably cross-neutralize it. It has been, from a therapeutic standpoint, a blank.

That blank became urgent this spring. Since May, Ituri Province in the eastern Democratic Republic of Congo has been dealing with the largest Bundibugyo outbreak ever recorded. By this week, more than fourteen hundred confirmed cases and over four hundred forty deaths have been reported in the DRC, with a spillover cluster into western Uganda. The average has been around thirty-eight new confirmed cases a day for the past two weeks. Health-care workers have been attacked. A treatment center in Ituri was raided, and two people were killed. The response conditions are, by any measure, extremely difficult.

Against that backdrop, on Wednesday, July second, the WHO announced that the first patient had been enrolled in a trial called PARTNERS. That is short for Platform Adaptive Randomised Trial for New and Repurposed Filovirus TreatmentS. Let me unpack what each piece of that name means, because the design matters. "Platform" and "adaptive" refer to the trial's structure: rather than testing one drug against a placebo in a fixed protocol, PARTNERS is set up as a scaffold that can hold multiple treatment arms in parallel, and drop the arms that are clearly not working while adding new ones over time. That structure is important for a disease like Bundibugyo, where you do not know in advance which of a small number of candidate drugs is going to help and you cannot afford to run four separate slow trials in sequence. "Randomised" means patients are assigned to arms by chance, which is the only way to get a reliable answer about whether a treatment is better than the current standard of care.

The two drugs entering the trial first are both borrowed from other filovirus programs. The first is MBP134, a two-antibody cocktail made by Mapp Biopharmaceutical — the same company whose earlier antibody cocktail ZMapp became well-known during the 2014 West Africa Ebola epidemic. MBP134 was engineered to cross-react across multiple ebolavirus species, so it targets sites on the surface glycoprotein that are conserved between Zaire, Sudan, and Bundibugyo viruses. It has been shown to protect non-human primates against all three species in preclinical studies, but before this trial it had never been given to Bundibugyo patients under trial conditions. The second drug is remdesivir, a broad-spectrum antiviral originally developed by Gilead. Remdesivir is best known now as a COVID-19 drug, but it was actually originally developed against Ebola — it just did not perform as well as antibodies in the Zaire Ebola trials in 2019. It inhibits the viral RNA-dependent RNA polymerase, which is the enzyme every RNA virus needs to copy its genome. Because that enzyme is highly conserved across the ebolavirus family, remdesivir is expected to work against Bundibugyo, but nobody has proven it in humans.

PARTNERS will test each drug alone, both drugs together, and the current standard of intensive supportive care as controls. The primary endpoint the WHO has flagged is survival. Patients are followed for at least twenty-eight days. The trial is sponsored by the WHO and coordinated by the Institut National de Recherche Biomédicale in Kinshasa, the Institute of Tropical Medicine in Antwerp, and the University of Oxford — the same coalition that ran the four-arm Ebola treatment trial during the 2018 to 2020 outbreak that established the two currently approved Zaire Ebola antibodies.

Why does this matter? A few reasons. First, if PARTNERS produces a positive signal, this could become the first approved treatment for Bundibugyo Ebola, ever. Second, the trial is a real-world test of the platform-trial design being deployed inside an active, violent outbreak. The 2018 Zaire Ebola trial showed this can be done, but each time is different, and the enrollment rate over the coming months will tell you a lot about how well the field has learned. And third, the combination arm — MBP134 plus remdesivir — is a bet on a specific hypothesis, that antibodies and small-molecule antivirals hit the virus at complementary steps and should be additive. That hypothesis has been tested in animals but never in humans for Ebola. Watch for interim results probably later this year or early next; a platform trial does not wait for full enrollment before releasing what it knows.

On the business side, a two-hundred-million-euro deal for an obscure but important virus.

The French pharmaceutical company Ipsen announced yesterday that it is buying Memo Therapeutics, a Swiss biotech spinout of ETH Zurich, for two hundred million euros up front and up to seven hundred million euros in total including milestone payments. The prize is a single drug — a monoclonal antibody called potravitug — that is the first drug of its class for a virus most people, including most working scientists, have never heard of: BK polyomavirus.

BK polyomavirus is a small, non-enveloped DNA virus that almost every human being on the planet catches in early childhood, usually without noticing. After that first infection, the virus does what many DNA viruses do — it retreats into a latent state, sitting quietly in the kidney's tubular cells and the urinary tract for the rest of your life. In a healthy immune system, this latent infection is invisible. It never comes out.

The trouble starts when the immune system is suppressed on purpose. And the population where that most commonly happens is kidney transplant recipients. To keep the recipient's body from rejecting the donated kidney, transplant patients are placed on a lifetime cocktail of immunosuppressive drugs — calcineurin inhibitors like tacrolimus, mycophenolate, and corticosteroids. Those drugs work, but they also lower the immune surveillance that normally keeps latent viruses in check. And so in about thirty percent of kidney transplant recipients in the first year after transplant, BK polyomavirus wakes up in the graft and begins to replicate. In some of those patients, the reactivation progresses to what is called BK polyomavirus nephropathy — a direct viral infection of the transplanted kidney's tubular cells that causes measurable damage. Around ten percent of transplant recipients end up losing the graft or having permanent function loss because of it.

The current standard of care for BK reactivation is unsatisfying: doctors reduce the dose of the immunosuppressive drugs. That gives the immune system more room to clear the virus, but it also raises the risk that the immune system attacks the transplanted kidney itself. Clinicians are essentially threading a needle without a targeted tool.

Potravitug is that targeted tool. It is a monoclonal antibody that binds the BK virus's VP1 capsid protein — the outer shell that the virus uses to attach to host cells. By coating VP1, the antibody prevents the virus from entering new cells and continuing its replication cycle. Because the antibody is neutralizing the virus directly, in principle you can use it without pulling back on the anti-rejection drugs.

The trial that made Ipsen willing to spend two hundred million euros up front is called SAFE KIDNEY II. It enrolled ninety-five patients across twenty-two U.S. sites, and gave each one either potravitug or placebo on top of standard management. The primary endpoint — driving the BK virus in the blood down below the detection limit by the end of the treatment window — was technically missed on statistical significance. That would normally be a warning sign. But the secondary endpoints told a different story. In patients who came into the trial with biopsy-proven kidney damage from the virus, the histological picture on repeat biopsy improved dramatically. The fraction of patients with biopsy-proven nephropathy dropped from about fifty-one percent at baseline to about thirty-two percent in the treated group, while the placebo group barely budged. And the fraction of patients who had a large drop in their viral load — more than a hundred-fold — was substantially higher in the potravitug group. The FDA granted potravitug fast-track designation back in 2023, and Memo is planning a pivotal Phase 2/3 trial to start later this year, which is what Ipsen has bought into.

The interesting piece for anyone watching pharma strategy is that this is not a big-market drug. BK polyomavirus nephropathy is a niche, though a growing one as transplant volumes grow. Ipsen is paying for a foothold in the transplant complications market — a space where the addressable population is small but the unmet need is nearly total. Expect other companies to look harder at latent-virus reactivations in the immunosuppressed as a distinct therapeutic category.

Now, AI in the biomedical sciences.

An updated preprint posted yesterday to bioRxiv from a group at MIT and collaborators takes an AI method that was originally built to design short protein fragments that inhibit other proteins, and applies it to a fundamentally different problem — controlling something called biomolecular condensates.

Let me define that term, because it is the whole point of the paper. If you look inside a cell, you will find the classical membrane-bound organelles you learned about in high school — the nucleus, the mitochondria, the Golgi. But over the past decade, biologists have realized that a lot of cellular organization is done without membranes at all. Instead, certain proteins can spontaneously demix from the surrounding cytoplasm and form little liquid droplets — the way oil droplets separate out of vinaigrette. Those droplets are called biomolecular condensates. They concentrate specific molecules, they are dynamic, and they show up in essentially every cellular process — the stress response, immune signaling, gene regulation, viral replication. When condensates go wrong, they get implicated in neurodegeneration, in cancer, and in viral disease.

The way most condensates form is through what is called multivalent interaction networks. Imagine a protein that has several sticky patches on its surface, each of which can bind to a matching patch on another copy of the same protein. Now put a lot of copies together in solution. They will start to cross-link into a mesh — a droplet held together by many weak interactions in parallel. That is a condensate. And here is the intuition behind the new work: if you could design a small piece of that protein — a fragment — that has just one of the sticky patches on it, but not the others, that fragment would bind to its parent protein and cap one of the interaction sites, terminating the mesh at that point. In chemistry terms, it acts like a chain terminator. If a fragment instead bridges two proteins together without blocking their other interfaces, it enhances the mesh and makes condensates form more readily.

That is exactly what the FragFold method — built on top of AlphaFold2 with a custom scoring function — was used to do. The group screened, in silico, over two thousand possible protein fragments across four condensate-forming proteins, and picked eighteen candidates to actually synthesize. The four proteins they targeted span the therapeutic map: G3BP1, which nucleates stress granules; the SARS-CoV-2 nucleocapsid protein, which forms condensates that concentrate viral RNA during replication; TDP-43, whose aggregation is a hallmark of amyotrophic lateral sclerosis and frontotemporal dementia; and focal adhesion kinase, which is often overexpressed in cancer.

Nine of the eighteen fragments worked — a fifty-percent hit rate — and every one of the four proteins had at least one successful fragment. Most acted as inhibitors, as chain terminators dissolving condensates. In FAK, they got both effects: one fragment that shuts condensates off, and one that binds in a bridging mode and makes condensates larger and more frequent.

The reason this is interesting is threefold. First, it is generalizable. The same computational pipeline worked on four unrelated proteins with different structures and different biology, using only the parent protein sequence as input — no prior knowledge of which surfaces were involved in condensate formation. Second, the successful fragments doubled as biological probes. In focal adhesion kinase, the inhibitory fragment pointed at an interaction between the FERM domain and the kinase domain as the key mesh-forming interaction — and when the group deleted the kinase domain and repeated the experiment, condensates vanished, confirming the model. So the AI method was not just producing molecules; it was producing hypotheses about how the biology works, and those hypotheses could be tested. Third, one of the fragments — the inhibitor for FAK — was transfected into living mouse cells and reduced condensates there in a dose-dependent manner, which is a much harder result than working in a test tube.

Condensate-targeted drugs are an actively watched frontier — the first small-molecule condensate modulator went into cancer trials only a couple of years ago. What this preprint suggests is that peptide-length fragments derived directly from the target protein itself may be a much more systematic starting point than either small molecules or de novo designed binders. That is worth watching.

And finally, a question about wildlife in national parks.

Everyone who has been through the safety orientation at a national park in North America has heard the standard warnings: watch for bears, especially grizzlies. Do not run. Do not surprise them. Carry bear spray. The mental model that comes out of that is that the wild animal to fear in a park is a large carnivore. A new study published yesterday in Frontiers in Conservation Science from Holly Landles and colleagues at the University of York in the U.K., using thirteen years of incident data from Parks Canada, suggests that mental model is almost entirely wrong.

The team looked at every reported human-wildlife aggressive encounter in Canadian national parks between 2010 and 2023 — nearly thirty-five hundred incidents in total — involving elk, black bears, grizzly bears, coyotes, and mule deer. The animal responsible for the largest share of aggressive encounters, by a huge margin, was the elk. Sixty-two percent of all reported incidents. For comparison, grizzlies were fourteen percent, black bears thirteen percent, mule deer seven, and coyotes just three.

There are a few reasons for this that the paper picks apart. The first is that elk are herbivores, and people do not perceive herbivores as threats the way they perceive carnivores. So visitors get close. They stop for photos. They walk between a cow and her calf. Elk are also large — a mature bull can weigh well over three hundred kilograms — and they defend a personal space that most people do not know exists. The second is that elk have learned something clever, which is that human presence deters predators. Bull elk have been documented using areas near tourist infrastructure as refuges during the rut. So townsites are exactly where you get the most human-elk overlap. In the data, over seventy-three percent of townsite encounters involved elk, and elk also dominated encounters during adventure sports like mountain biking and skiing.

Bears, by contrast, mostly hurt people during quiet activities — hiking, birdwatching, wildlife photography. And the reason is the mirror image of the elk pattern: a quiet person walking through dense vegetation can genuinely startle a bear, and a startled bear reacts. The old advice about talking loudly and traveling in groups is not folklore. In this dataset, forty-five percent of grizzly incidents and forty-three percent of black bear incidents happened during those low-noise low-impact activities.

The public-facing takeaway is a little counterintuitive: if you are walking through the woods, being noisy is protective. If you are walking through the town square in the same park, being noisy does not help you at all, and the animal on the golf course is the one you actually need to keep a respectful distance from.
