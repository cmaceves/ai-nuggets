## Script

Good morning. Today we have four stories.

First, bird flu has reached Australia — the last continent on Earth without a confirmed mainland case of H five N one. We'll walk through what this means for wildlife and agriculture at the bottom of the world.

Then, a six-year retrospective from the University of Washington shows that nearly half of all herpes simplex virus samples sent for acyclovir resistance testing came back positive, with twenty-three resistance mutations that nobody had ever characterized before.

On the business side: the FDA has accepted — for a third time — a biologics license application for an engineered herpes virus designed to kill cancer cells from the inside, this time paired with a fresh analysis that tries to answer the agency's persistent question about whether the virus is actually earning its place.

And finally, there's a spider in tropical Queensland that has invented a catapult. Just for ants.

Let's get into it.

---

Pathogen watch.

H five N one — the highly pathogenic avian influenza strain — reached mainland Australia last week, confirmed June twentieth by the CSIRO Australian Centre for Disease Preparedness. The infected bird was a brown skua found dead or dying at Cape Le Grand National Park, about seven hundred kilometers southeast of Perth. A giant petrel in the same area also died of suspected infection. For the purposes of global H five N one tracking, this marks the moment the virus has touched every inhabited continent on Earth.

To understand why this matters, it helps to know what has been happening with H five N one globally over the past four years.

H five N one has been circulating in birds since 1996, when a strain called goose-Guangdong first emerged in domestic geese in southern China. But what happened starting in 2021 was qualitatively different from everything before. The predominant clade — a clade is just a group of viruses that share a common ancestor and a set of defining mutations that trace back to that ancestor — is called two point three point four point four B. That clade spread explosively through wild waterfowl across Europe in 2021 and 2022, then crossed into North America and, for the first time in history, into South America, devastating seabird colonies that had no prior exposure to this virus. The scale is staggering: hundreds of millions of birds have been killed globally, and tens of thousands of marine mammals — sea lions, elephant seals, fur seals, dolphins — died in mass mortality events stretching from Argentina to Alaska. The virus had essentially redrawn its own ecological map.

What made Australia unusual until now was a combination of geography and bird migration ecology. The Southern Ocean species — penguins, albatrosses, petrels, skuas — were the most plausible vectors for a southern approach, and Australian biosecurity authorities had been watching this pathway. In August 2025, H five N one was detected on Heard Island, a remote sub-Antarctic Australian territory about four thousand kilometers from the mainland. That detection signaled the virus was in the neighborhood.

Brown skuas are large, predatory seabirds that breed across the Southern Ocean and sub-Antarctic islands and range widely for food. They are aggressive scavengers and, critically, they can carry H five N one over long oceanic distances. The brown skua at Cape Le Grand was the first confirmed arrival on the Australian continent proper.

What happens next depends heavily on species ecology. Brown skuas and giant petrels are marine birds; they do not ordinarily mix with domestic poultry or with freshwater waterfowl under normal circumstances. The scenario that most concerns wildlife biologists is a cascade: a sick skua is scavenged by a native wedge-tailed eagle or a raven, which then contacts other species, and the virus eventually reaches migratory shorebirds and duck populations that do interface with poultry and agricultural settings. Once it enters duck populations — which travel over long distances and mix freely with domestic animals — the threat to Australia's agricultural bird industry becomes acute.

Australia's federal government has pledged a hundred and thirteen million dollars for preparedness: tightened biosecurity at poultry farms, active surveillance of shorebirds and raptors, and vaccination of captive individuals of critically vulnerable endemic species. Researchers at the Doherty Institute note there is still a real possibility that the virus doesn't establish itself — that it burns out in the isolated coastal population where it appeared without jumping to the broader bird community.

The human risk from this specific detection is very low. Skuas and giant petrels are not species that regularly contact farmworkers or the public. But the event is a reminder that H five N one's current global expansion is not a temporary emergency. It is an ongoing redistribution of a pandemic-capable virus into ecosystems that were previously untouched, and the window for any region to remain an exception is closing.

Still on pathogens, a preprint posted this week to bioRxiv from the University of Washington Clinical Virology Laboratory offers a detailed five-year look at how herpes simplex virus acyclovir resistance behaves in clinical practice — and it raises some genuine concerns.

Most people know herpes simplex virus as the cause of cold sores, driven by HSV-1, or genital herpes, driven by HSV-2. In people with a normal immune system, herpes is bothersome but manageable: outbreaks recur but are usually self-limited. In people who are immunocompromised — organ transplant recipients on long-term immunosuppression, patients with HIV who have low CD4 counts, people on chemotherapy — the picture is very different. HSV can cause severe and spreading mucocutaneous disease. In the most serious cases, it can disseminate to the liver, lungs, or brain.

Acyclovir has been the backbone of herpes treatment since the early 1980s. It is a nucleoside analog — meaning it is a synthetic molecule that closely resembles guanosine, one of the four standard building blocks of DNA. The critical trick is that acyclovir requires a viral enzyme called thymidine kinase to be converted into its active form. Healthy human cells do not convert it efficiently. HSV-infected cells do. So the drug preferentially kills cells that harbor the virus and largely leaves uninfected cells alone.

Resistance emerges when the virus mutates its thymidine kinase gene — called UL23 in the HSV genome — in ways that prevent the enzyme from activating acyclovir. In immunocompromised patients on prolonged therapy, resistance rates can reach thirty percent, because the virus has both the selection pressure of continuous drug exposure and the replication time of extended infections to evolve around the drug.

The traditional test for resistance was phenotypic: you culture the patient's virus in cells, expose it to drug, and measure whether replication slows. This works, but it takes weeks and requires live virus culture. In 2020, the University of Washington launched what they describe as the first clinical genotypic resistance test in the United States. Rather than culturing virus, you sequence the UL23 gene directly from patient samples and look for mutations already associated with resistance. Turnaround drops from weeks to roughly ten days.

This study reports on a hundred and thirty-six samples tested at UW between January 2020 and November 2025. Of those, forty-seven point eight percent harbored acyclovir resistance mutations. The authors are careful to note that this is not a population prevalence rate — only clinically suspicious cases get sent for resistance testing, so the denominator is already enriched for patients who are failing therapy. Still, nearly half of tested samples being resistant is a stark number.

The mutation landscape is also informative. Frameshift mutations — insertions or deletions in the UL23 coding sequence that shift how the gene is read and often destroy the enzyme entirely — appeared in forty-five samples. And among the full set of mutations detected, twenty-three had never been characterized before. These novel variants are a genuine clinical problem: without biochemical testing to confirm they actually abolish thymidine kinase activity, clinicians don't know whether a patient's virus is truly resistant or whether the mutation is harmless.

This matters because when acyclovir resistance is confirmed, the treatment shift is to foscarnet or cidofovir — drugs that attack the viral DNA polymerase rather than thymidine kinase, bypassing the resistance mechanism. Both are more toxic than acyclovir and require intravenous administration. Knowing promptly and accurately whether a patient has genuine resistance, versus a variant of unknown significance, has real stakes for how aggressively you treat and what side effects you're willing to inflict. The UW dataset maps out the twenty-three novel variants that now need biochemical follow-up to be usefully interpretable in the clinic.

On the business side of biomedicine.

The FDA has accepted, for the third time, a biologics license application for Replimune's RP1 — technically known as vusolimogene oderparepvec — combined with nivolumab, for patients with advanced melanoma. The agency set a decision date of August second and expects to convene an advisory committee meeting in late July. This is not a routine regulatory story, and it is worth understanding why this particular application keeps coming back.

Melanoma is a skin cancer that arises from melanocytes, the pigment-producing cells. Caught early, surgery is often curative. When it metastasizes — when it spreads to lymph nodes, lungs, liver, or brain — it has historically been one of the harder solid tumors to manage. That changed with immune checkpoint inhibitors. Nivolumab is a drug that blocks PD-1, a molecular brake that tumors use to suppress immune surveillance. PD-1 blockade has transformed outcomes in advanced melanoma; for patients who respond, it can produce durable remissions measured in years.

The problem is that roughly half of patients with advanced melanoma don't respond to initial PD-1 therapy, and many who do respond eventually develop resistance. For those patients, options narrow considerably.

RP1 is an oncolytic virus — a virus engineered to kill cancer cells from the inside. It is built on a modified strain of herpes simplex virus type one. Three key changes were made. First, the engineers deleted a gene called ICP34.5, which normally helps HSV replicate in neurons without triggering the innate immune response. That deletion makes RP1 unable to cause neurological disease. Second, they deleted ICP47, which normally helps HSV hide infected cells from cytotoxic T cells. That deletion makes RP1-infected tumor cells more visible to immune attack. Third, they inserted the gene for human GM-CSF, a cytokine that recruits and activates dendritic cells — the antigen-presenting cells that train T cells to recognize and kill a specific target.

When RP1 is injected directly into a tumor, it preferentially replicates in the cancer cells, which often have defects in innate immune signaling that healthy cells would use to block viral infection. As the tumor cells are killed by the virus, they die in a way called immunogenic cell death — releasing their contents, including tumor-specific antigens, into the local environment. The goal is to use the tumor as its own vaccine factory: local antigen loading combined with activated dendritic cells creates a T cell response that theoretically attacks cancer cells elsewhere in the body, including tumors the injection never reached. Paired with nivolumab, which releases the PD-1 brake on those T cells, the combination is meant to amplify both local destruction and systemic immune targeting.

The clinical results from the IGNYTE trial were striking. In a hundred and forty patients who had already failed prior PD-1 therapy, the combination produced a thirty-four percent objective response rate — tumors shrinking by at least thirty percent — with a median duration of response of nearly thirty-four months and two-year overall survival of sixty-three percent. For a population defined by having already failed the drug class the study is now adding back, those numbers are notable.

The FDA's concern — raised twice now, in complete response letters from July 2025 and April 2026 — is essentially this: when you rechallenge a patient with nivolumab after a period off-drug, some of them respond again, without any contribution from RP1. Without a nivolumab-alone control arm in the trial, the agency cannot determine how much of the thirty-four percent response rate came from the oncolytic virus versus the nivolumab rechallenge itself. The company has now submitted additional analyses — subgroup comparisons, biological endpoint data, and cross-trial benchmarking — aimed at demonstrating RP1's independent contribution. Whether those analyses satisfy the advisory committee and the FDA will be clear by August.

If RP1 ultimately earns approval, it would join a very small class. The only oncolytic virus with current FDA approval is T-VEC, a different modified HSV approved in 2015 for injectable melanoma lesions, which struggled commercially in part because it worked primarily on injected tumors and was difficult to position in an era of highly active systemic checkpoint inhibitors. Replimune's argument is that RP1, with its additional genetic modifications for improved immunogenicity, is better positioned to generate the systemic immune cascade needed to eliminate disease beyond the injection site. August second will tell whether the FDA is persuaded.

And finally.

In the tropical rainforests near Cooktown, in far north Queensland, a team from Macquarie University has described a spider that has essentially invented the catapult — and engineered it to hunt exactly one prey species. The paper appeared last week in Current Biology.

The spider, an unnamed member of the genus Propostira, has been nicknamed the ballista spider after the ancient Roman siege weapon that used tensioned ropes or sinew to fling heavy projectiles. Here is what it does.

The spider constructs a cone-shaped structure near the ground from fifteen to sixty strands of silk, bundled together under tension like a compressed spring and anchored to the substrate at a single point. The spider positions itself nearby. The prey — exclusively the green tree ant, a large, aggressive species common in the Cape York Peninsula rainforest — approaches the cone. The researchers suspect the spider adds a chemical lure during final construction, something that induces the ants to attack the cone directly. The ant bites the cone. That bite detaches the anchor. The stored elastic energy in the silk is released in an instant, and the ant is launched more than thirty centimeters upward into the spider's main web overhead.

The biomechanics are genuinely remarkable. The ant experiences a peak acceleration exceeding thirteen hundred meters per second squared — about a hundred and forty times the force of gravity. The instantaneous power density of the mechanism peaks at nearly twelve megawatts per kilogram. To put that in context, this is three to four orders of magnitude beyond what muscle tissue can produce. The silk functions as a biological spring capable of storing and releasing energy at rates that exceed any known muscular system.

What makes this spider's solution so elegant is the problem it was solving. Green tree ants are dangerous prey. They bite hard with serrated mandibles, spray formic acid, and when one is threatened, it rapidly recruits backup. A spider attempting a direct ambush faces a coordinated, chemically-armed counterattack. By tricking the ant into triggering its own ejection — from the ground into an inescapable web above — the spider eliminates the risk almost entirely.

The spider is also, as far as anyone knows, the only arachnid that specializes exclusively on a single prey species. Every feature of the trap — the cone dimensions, the tensioning, the likely chemical lure — appears to be specifically tuned to green tree ant behavior. The researchers suggest this hyperspecialization evolved because green tree ants are extraordinarily abundant in that rainforest, making them a reliable and protein-rich prey source worth the architectural investment.

Professor Ajay Narendra and his team spent ten days and nights in the rainforest locating and filming these spiders, using high-speed and infrared cameras to capture the mechanism in action. It took ten nights in a tropical rainforest to document a trap that fires in less than a millisecond.

That's it for today. H five N one now has no empty continents left on its map. Half of clinically tested herpes samples are resistant to the standard treatment, and twenty-three mutations nobody has characterized yet are in the wild. A clever engineered herpesvirus faces its third try at FDA approval in August. And somewhere near Cooktown, a spider waits patiently beside a spring it has loaded with silk.
