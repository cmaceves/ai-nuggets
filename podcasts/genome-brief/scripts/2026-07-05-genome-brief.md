Good morning, and welcome to The Genome Brief for Sunday, July fifth. Today: a second filovirus surfaces in Uganda even as the Bundibugyo Ebola outbreak keeps growing; the first post-pandemic look at RSV genomes from Bangladesh brings some reassuring news for the new vaccines and antibodies; Roche's next-generation KRAS drug beats the incumbents in a head-to-head trial; and human laughter, it turns out, has been running on the same rhythm as great ape laughter for fifteen million years.

Let's start in Uganda, with a story that ties directly to what we covered on Thursday.

On the thirtieth of June, Ugandan health authorities notified the World Health Organization of a confirmed case of Marburg virus disease. Marburg is a filovirus — the same family as Ebola — and it causes an acute hemorrhagic fever with a case fatality rate that historically runs between twenty-four and eighty-eight percent, depending on the outbreak. There is no licensed vaccine and no approved treatment. Several candidates are in late clinical development, but the state of care for a Marburg patient today is still supportive.

The confirmed case is a child, one year and five months old, in Kyegegwa District in western Uganda. And here is the detail that matters most: the case was picked up through enhanced surveillance for Ebola. Uganda has been operating heightened screening because of the ongoing Bundibugyo Ebola outbreak just across the border in the Democratic Republic of Congo. That screening picked up hemorrhagic-fever symptoms that turned out, on laboratory testing, to be Marburg, not Ebola. Africa CDC and the WHO have described this as an isolated case so far. STAT News reported that two possible cases were under investigation as of the initial notification, but no identified contacts have developed symptoms and there is no confirmed onward transmission. Uganda's last Marburg outbreak was in 2017 and the country has had a total of four historical outbreaks; this appears, at least for now, to be a fifth.

The reason this is worth pausing on is what it says about surveillance. When you scale up filovirus screening for one pathogen, you also catch adjacent pathogens with overlapping symptoms. That has real public-health value. It is also a reminder that filovirus spillovers into Uganda are not rare — this region, with its cave-dwelling fruit bat reservoirs of Marburg, has produced multiple independent introductions over the last two decades.

Now, on the Bundibugyo Ebola outbreak itself, the WHO's July third update has some important numbers. Total confirmed cases across all three affected countries — the DRC, Uganda, and France — are now fourteen hundred and eighty-one, with four hundred and fifty-four confirmed deaths. That is a case fatality rate of about thirty-one percent, which is right in the historical range for Bundibugyo virus specifically. Bundibugyo tends to be less lethal than Zaire Ebola virus but more lethal than the average seasonal respiratory pathogen by a very large margin. Ituri Province in the DRC remains the epicenter, with over ninety percent of the cases. Uganda has reported twenty confirmed cases, but has not reported a new case since June twenty-first — a good sign that the enhanced surveillance and contact tracing are containing spillover into Uganda proper. The imported case in France is a physician who returned from a five-week deployment in Ituri, self-reported symptoms at Charles de Gaulle Airport on June twenty-third, and was immediately isolated. A hundred and seven contacts have been listed for follow-up in Kinshasa, and no secondary cases in France have been reported. So even though the outbreak continues to grow in the DRC, the international spread is being caught.

Zooming out: the trial we discussed on Thursday, PARTNERS, is now enrolling patients into the first-ever randomized study of Bundibugyo Ebola treatments. It cannot come soon enough.

On to a second pathogen genomics story, and this one is quieter but interesting.

A preprint from Bangladeshi researchers at ICDDR,B posted on bioRxiv this week presents the first post-pandemic genomic surveillance data for RSV — respiratory syncytial virus — in Bangladesh. Why does this matter? Because RSV kills a lot of children — over ninety-five percent of RSV deaths worldwide are in low- and middle-income countries — and yet virtually all the genomic surveillance data driving vaccine and antibody design has come from wealthy countries in Europe and North America. If the strains circulating in South Asia carry mutations that escape the new interventions, we would want to know before, not after, those tools are rolled out.

The team took fifty-nine RSV-positive samples with high viral loads from hospital patients, collected between August 2024 and December 2025, and sequenced them using Oxford Nanopore technology. They successfully assembled forty-nine complete genomes — an eighty-three percent success rate — and they deposited all of them into the public GISAID database.

The findings are largely reassuring. Ninety-four percent of the RSV they found was RSV subtype A — specifically the ON1 genotype, which is the dominant global lineage — with the remaining six percent being RSV-B of the BA9 genotype. Both of those are exactly what circulates in high-income settings. They looked hard at the F protein — that is the fusion glycoprotein on the surface of the virus that mediates entry into human cells, and it is the target of both the maternal vaccines and the long-acting monoclonal antibodies now being deployed for infants. They found two notable amino acid substitutions: S276N in about thirty-five percent of the RSV-A sequences, and S389P in every single RSV-B sequence they collected. Neither substitution sits in the epitopes — the specific regions on the F protein where the therapeutic antibodies actually bind. In other words, the Bangladeshi strains still match the targets of nirsevimab, of clesrovimab, and of the F-protein-based vaccines. There is nothing here that would compromise the new tools before they get to South Asia.

They did find something else worth noting: every single RSV-A virus in the dataset had picked up a new N-linked glycosylation site on the F protein — a spot where a sugar chain gets attached. Most RSV-B viruses had also gained one. Adding glycans to a viral surface protein can shield underlying epitopes from antibodies. The team says this does not yet compromise the current therapeutics, but glycan drift is one of the ways viruses gradually escape immune pressure, and it is worth watching.

The bigger point: this is exactly the kind of local sequencing infrastructure the field needs. Fifty genomes from Bangladesh sitting in GISAID is fifty more than existed a month ago, and it lays the foundation for the surveillance you would need if a variant did emerge.

On the business side, a genuinely significant clinical result out of Roche on Thursday.

Roche reported positive Phase 3 data from a trial called Krascendo 1, comparing their investigational drug divarasib head-to-head against the two approved first-generation KRAS G12C inhibitors — Amgen's sotorasib, brand name Lumakras, and Bristol Myers Squibb's adagrasib, brand name Krazati — in patients with previously-treated KRAS G12C-mutant non-small cell lung cancer.

Let me back up and explain why this matters. KRAS is one of the most famous oncogenes in cancer biology. It is a small signaling protein that acts like an on-off switch inside the cell — when it is on, it drives cell proliferation. In normal cells it flips off after each signal; in cancer cells with certain mutations, it gets stuck in the on position, and the cell divides uncontrollably. For decades KRAS was considered undruggable — the protein's surface was too smooth for small-molecule drugs to bind. Then in 2013, chemists identified a druggable pocket that opens up only in one specific mutant form: KRAS G12C, where the twelfth amino acid, normally glycine, has been swapped for cysteine. That cysteine gave chemists a chemical handle. And in 2021 and 2022, the FDA approved sotorasib and adagrasib, which covalently bind that mutant cysteine and lock the protein in its inactive off state. It was one of the most exciting drug-discovery stories of the decade.

But the first-generation drugs have limitations. Resistance emerges fairly quickly, response rates are modest, and progression-free survival is measured in months. So there has been a race for next-generation KRAS G12C inhibitors that bind more tightly, more selectively, and lock the protein down more completely.

Divarasib is Roche's entry. In preclinical studies it showed greater potency and selectivity than sotorasib and adagrasib. The Krascendo 1 trial randomized three hundred thirty-eight patients with previously treated advanced KRAS G12C lung cancer to either divarasib once daily or investigator's choice of the two approved drugs. The primary endpoint was progression-free survival, judged by blinded independent central review. The key secondary endpoint was overall survival.

Roche reported that divarasib beat the approved drugs on both. Progression-free survival was statistically significantly improved. And crucially, overall survival was also statistically significant at the interim analysis — meaning divarasib is not just delaying tumor growth on a scan, it is helping patients live longer than the current standard of care. No new safety signals. Roche did not release the specific hazard ratios or median survival numbers yet — those will be presented at an upcoming medical meeting — but a positive head-to-head OS win in a poor-prognosis oncology population is the kind of result that changes the standard of care.

Roche projects peak sales of one to two-and-a-half billion Swiss francs, and this trial should support a regulatory filing in second-line lung cancer. For patients with KRAS G12C tumors, this is unambiguously good news.

And finally, animals.

Human laughter, if you strip away language and consider it just as sound, is a curious thing — a rhythmic sequence of short breathy vocalizations. A new paper in Communications Biology from a University of Warwick group led by Chiara De Gregorio asks: is that rhythm uniquely human? Or do our closest relatives share it?

They recorded and analyzed a hundred and forty laughter sequences from four species of great apes — orangutans, gorillas, bonobos, and chimpanzees — plus humans, and they measured the timing between successive laugh sounds. Specifically, they looked at whether the intervals between one laugh burst and the next were evenly spaced — a property called isochrony, which is the same rhythmic feature that underlies music and speech.

Across all five species, the pattern held. Ape laughter is isochronous. The rhythm is regular. And because that structure shows up in orangutans, gorillas, bonobos, chimpanzees, and humans, the most parsimonious interpretation is that it was already present in the common ancestor of all great apes — roughly fifteen million years ago.

Now, humans have layered our own tweaks on top. Human laughter is faster than ape laughter, and we vary the tempo and pitch depending on context — a tickled child's laugh sounds different from a nervous laugh at a work meeting. That flexibility is uniquely ours. But the rhythmic scaffolding underneath is fifteen million years old.

The authors argue this matters for speech evolution. Producing rhythmic, temporally-controlled vocalizations is a prerequisite for speech, and this suggests our ancestors had that prerequisite long before language ever appeared. Speech, in other words, was built on top of laughter's rhythm — not the other way around.
