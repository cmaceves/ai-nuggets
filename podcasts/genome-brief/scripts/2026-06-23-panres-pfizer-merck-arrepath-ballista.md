## Script

Welcome back to the Genome Brief for Tuesday, June twenty-third — second edition. Today: a structure-aware antimicrobial resistance database adds a third of its resistome hits in European wastewater that conventional BLAST missed; two huge 2023 oncology buyouts split the day with one Phase 3 miss and one Phase 3 win; a real-world machine-learning antibiotic screen doubles its hit rate; and we close with an Australian spider that built itself a crossbow.

Pathogen watch. A bioinformatics preprint posted to bioRxiv yesterday from Munk and colleagues at the Technical University of Denmark introduces PanRes, a unified antimicrobial resistance database that does something none of the prevailing AMR resources do — it works in structure space, not just sequence space. They pull eleven thousand seven hundred resistance genes covering antibiotic, biocide, and metal resistance into a single ontology, predict representative protein structures, and cluster them into five hundred ninety-eight structurally conserved groups. From each cluster they build a hidden Markov model — so remote homologs that share the fold but have drifted past sequence similarity are still callable. The real test is the wastewater run: across metagenomes from seven European cities, the 3D-based H-M-Ms expanded detection beyond high-confidence BLAST by thirty-five-point-two percent, with the additional hits showing greater sequence divergence from known proteins. For genomic surveillance of resistance, this is exactly the gap. BLAST tells you what you already know is out there; structure-aware homology search lets you see drift you'd otherwise miss. Several beta-lactamases in their analysis kept the fold and the catalytic geometry despite weak sequence similarity — which is the regime in which new resistance emerges before anyone's database catches up.

Also worth flagging from medRxiv yesterday: a Kinshasa serosurvey, twelve hundred fifty participants, that puts dengue I-g-G prevalence at thirty-eight percent and chikungunya at twenty-four percent across the Democratic Republic of the Congo's capital. Force-of-infection modeling fits time-varying transmission. The DRC tracks malaria intensively and has Bundibugyo Ebola on its hands at the moment, but arboviral surveillance across much of central Africa is essentially blind. A third of adults seropositive for dengue in a major African capital is not a quiet baseline.

On the business side — a striking contrast on the day in oncology M-and-A return-on-investment. Pfizer announced this morning that sigvotatug vedotin, the first new antibody-drug conjugate to read out from its forty-three-billion-dollar Seagen acquisition, missed overall survival in the Phase 3 SigVie-002 trial in previously-treated metastatic non-squamous non-small-cell lung cancer. Seven hundred three patients, head-to-head versus docetaxel. There's a partial-credit subgroup — second-line-only patients, about two-thirds of the study, showed a stronger trend for both O-S and P-F-S — and the safety profile was clean and consistent with prior studies. Pfizer is keeping the program alive in a first-line combination Phase 3 with pembrolizumab in PD-L1-high patients. But this is a first-in-class integrin beta six A-D-C, the first major Seagen-derived asset to reach Phase 3, and missing your primary endpoint is missing your primary endpoint. The Seagen acquisition is going to need a Phase 3 win in the next twelve to eighteen months.

Now the contrast. Merck announced yesterday that tulisokibart, the anti-T-L-one-A monoclonal antibody it acquired in its ten-point-eight-billion-dollar Prometheus Biosciences buy in 2023, met its primary endpoint of week-twelve clinical remission in the Phase 3 ATLAS-U-C induction study in moderate-to-severe ulcerative colitis — and met its key secondary endpoints. Tulisokibart is the first anti-T-L-one-A in any indication to hit a Phase 3. T-L-one-A has been one of the most-watched immunology targets of the last three years, with multiple companies racing — Roivant, Sanofi, Pfizer — and Merck just got there first. Bigger picture: the Prometheus deal is now a structurally sound return, and the Seagen deal is one. The "Pfizer paid too much, Merck paid right" narrative has the receipts on the same news cycle. Crohn's, R-A, psoriatic arthritis Phase 2-b's for tulisokibart are already running.

AI in biomedicine. Sticking with the antibiotic-resistance thread — a preprint posted yesterday from Arrepath, an antibacterial discovery company, reports the real-world results from a machine-learning-guided high-throughput screen against E. coli. The framing matters because most M-L-for-antibiotic-discovery papers are retrospective on public datasets. This one ran the screen prospectively against a hyperpermeable l-p-t-D-minus mutant of E. coli, with full downstream hit confirmation, profiling, cytotoxicity counter-screen, and mechanism-of-action determination. Headline numbers: a doubled hit rate versus a conventional screen, and three-fold fewer toxic hits — meaning more activity, less time chasing compounds you'd never advance. The M-L models held up on cherry-picked structurally novel compounds outside the training distribution, which is the hard test for any chemistry M-L. The competing-interest disclosure is unavoidable — all authors are or were at Arrepath, and patents are filed — but the operational point is that someone is running a real antibiotic screen, with M-L in the loop, on a meaningful timescale. Given how thin the modern antibiotic pipeline is, that's news.

And finally, animals. A spider that built itself a ballista. A team led by Ajay Narendra at Macquarie University, working in rainforest near Cooktown in far north Queensland, has formally described a new genus and species in Current Biology yesterday — they're calling it the ballista spider, after the Roman siege weapon. It builds a silk snare suspended from fifteen to sixty tension lines, the whole thing under load — and when a green tree ant, *Oecophylla smaragdina*, walks across the trigger thread, the snare snaps shut and launches the ant upward over thirty centimeters, hitting an acceleration over thirteen hundred meters per second squared. That's about a hundred and forty g — fifteen times what a fighter pilot experiences in extreme maneuvers. The mechanism is unusual on two fronts. First, the prey, not the predator, fires the weapon. Second, the spider is so specialized it targets a single ant species — which the team calls the ultimate specialization. Ten days and nights of high-speed and infrared cameras in the rainforest to get the footage. That's the brief.
