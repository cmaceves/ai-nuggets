## Script

Good morning. This is The Genome Brief for Friday, July tenth, twenty twenty-six. Today: a new preprint lays out the molecular mechanism of a coronavirus drug candidate that has already been tested in humans, and it turns out the drug kills viral replication in two completely separate ways. Vertex Pharmaceuticals makes the largest acquisition in its history — ten billion dollars to expand into rare endocrine diseases. Roche announces it is walking away from tominersen, ending a decade-long attempt to slow Huntington's disease. An AI model for predicting drug-induced liver injury becomes the first artificial-intelligence tool ever accepted into the FDA's drug development qualification program. And to close: a tiny mouse that lives on the summits of Andean volcanoes, in temperatures down to negative sixty Celsius, where the air contains less than half the oxygen of sea level, turns out to have some genuinely strange tricks for pulling that off — and they were published in Science just yesterday.

Let's start with the pathogen watch.

A preprint posted this week on bioRxiv, from researchers at the University of Alberta, Leiden University, and the University of North Carolina, dissects the mechanism of an antiviral called CMX five twenty-one against the SARS cov two RNA polymerase. Let me explain what that means and why it matters.

Every RNA virus — coronaviruses, influenza viruses, noroviruses — has to copy its genome to replicate. But animal cells don't naturally have the machinery to copy RNA. So RNA viruses bring their own. The enzyme responsible is called the RNA-dependent RNA polymerase — meaning an RNA polymerase that uses RNA as its template. Because this enzyme is present in viruses but absent in human cells, it is an attractive drug target: you inhibit it in the virus without damaging the host. This is the same logic behind remdesivir, which was the first antiviral authorized for COVID-19. Remdesivir is a nucleoside analog — it mimics one of the four building blocks of RNA — and it works by getting incorporated into the growing RNA strand and then jamming the polymerase.

CMX five twenty-one is also a nucleoside analog. It mimics adenosine, one of the four RNA letters. It was originally developed as a broad-spectrum antiviral for caliciviruses — the family that includes norovirus, the stomach bug — and it turns out it also blocks coronaviruses. It has already been through Phase 1 clinical studies, meaning it has been given to humans and found to be safe at the doses tested.

What the preprint does is use a combination of biochemical assays and single-molecule magnetic tweezers — a technique that physically grabs a single RNA molecule and measures forces and distances at the level of individual chemical events — to watch, in real time, what happens when CMX five twenty-one meets the coronavirus polymerase.

There are two completely distinct mechanisms.

The first happens during normal replication. When the polymerase is building a new RNA strand, it can accidentally pick up a CMX five twenty-one molecule from the available pool and incorporate it. When that happens, the polymerase pauses for about nine seconds on average and then, in most cases, keeps going. Mechanism one slows things down but does not necessarily stop them.

Mechanism two is more powerful and works entirely differently. When the polymerase is reading the template strand — the original viral RNA it is copying — and encounters a CMX five twenty-one residue already embedded there, it stalls. It cannot properly place a new base across from the analog. And here is the striking part: when the viral helicase is also present — the helicase is the companion enzyme that unwinds the double-stranded RNA ahead of the polymerase — the stalled polymerase begins to copy backward, producing what are called copy-back RNAs. This backward synthesis is a second, completely separate inhibitory mechanism that does not just pause replication; it causes the copying machinery to run in reverse and generate aberrant products. When multiple CMX five twenty-one residues accumulate in the template strand, they completely block elongation entirely.

Two mechanisms is better than one in antivirals for the same reason wearing both a belt and suspenders is more reliable than either alone: it is harder for the virus to evolve resistance to an inhibitor that attacks from two independent angles simultaneously. The broader significance is that the drug works not just against SARS cov two but across coronaviruses as a class — which is exactly the kind of broad-spectrum tool the field has been saying it needs for pandemic preparedness. You do not want to be inventing the drug from scratch after the next novel coronavirus emerges.

On the business side, let's start with the largest deal in Vertex Pharmaceuticals' forty-year history. This week Vertex agreed to acquire San Diego-based Crinetics Pharmaceuticals for ten billion dollars in cash — a one hundred and two percent premium over where Crinetics shares were trading before the announcement. If you know Vertex, you know them primarily as the company that makes Trikafta, the combination therapy that transformed cystic fibrosis by correcting the misfolded protein that the most common disease mutation produces. Their cystic fibrosis franchise generates several billion dollars a year. This acquisition is a major push into entirely different territory: rare diseases of the hormonal glands.

There are two assets Vertex is buying.

The first is Palsonify, a drug already on the market, which treats a condition called acromegaly. Acromegaly happens when a benign tumor of the pituitary gland — a pea-sized gland that sits beneath the brain and serves as the master regulator of hormone production throughout the body — secretes too much growth hormone. Excess growth hormone causes bones and soft tissues to grow abnormally in adults: the hands, feet, and jaw enlarge; the heart and other organs can grow and dysfunction; vision and nerve function can be impaired. Untreated, it shortens life expectancy substantially. Palsonify is a somatostatin receptor agonist. Somatostatin is a natural hormone that normally tells the pituitary to suppress growth hormone release. Palsonify is a synthetic molecule that mimics it, turning down that hormonal tap.

The second asset is atumelnant, which is in late-stage clinical development for congenital adrenal hyperplasia, or CAH. CAH is a genetic disease — recessive, meaning you need two defective copies of the causative gene — in which the adrenal gland cannot properly make cortisol because an enzyme in the cortisol synthesis pathway is missing or defective. When cortisol is chronically low, the pituitary gland senses the deficit and secretes a stimulating hormone called ACTH — adrenocorticotropic hormone — which drives the adrenal gland to compensate. But because the cortisol pathway is blocked, that ACTH drive instead causes the adrenal gland to overproduce androgens, meaning male sex hormones. In females with CAH, this causes a range of effects beginning at birth, including abnormal genital development, disrupted puberty, irregular periods, and difficulties with fertility. Atumelnant targets a receptor in the pituitary that reduces ACTH output at the source, offering more precise control than the current standard of care, which is cortisol replacement with hydrocortisone — a drug requiring careful dosing because too much of it globally suppresses the stress hormone response. Vertex says the two Crinetics assets together could eventually bring in more than five billion dollars a year, and the deal reads as Vertex's declaration that it intends to be a multi-franchise rare disease company, not just a cystic fibrosis company.

And now to a much more difficult story. Also this week, Roche announced it is ending development of two drugs for Huntington's disease — tominersen and RG six four nine six. For those who follow this space, this is significant and painful news.

Huntington's disease is a dominantly inherited neurodegenerative condition, meaning you only need one defective copy of the gene to develop it, and each child of an affected parent has a fifty percent chance of inheriting it. The underlying mutation is an abnormal expansion of a three-letter DNA repeat — C, A, G — in the gene for a protein called huntingtin. The longer the repeat, the earlier the disease tends to start and the more severe its course. The mutant huntingtin protein misfolds and aggregates, killing neurons particularly in a brain region called the striatum, which coordinates voluntary movement. The clinical picture is involuntary, uncontrollable movements known as chorea, accompanied by progressive cognitive decline and psychiatric symptoms. It is always fatal. There is currently no drug that slows the disease.

Tominersen was one of the field's most serious attempts to change that. It is an antisense oligonucleotide — a short synthetic strand of nucleic acid designed to bind to the messenger RNA that carries the instructions for making huntingtin protein, causing it to be degraded before it can be translated. Less messenger RNA means less mutant huntingtin protein. The drug was delivered by injection directly into the spinal fluid, and it was developed over about a decade in partnership with Ionis Pharmaceuticals. Roche previously suspended a Phase 3 trial of tominersen in twenty twenty-one after a safety review, then redesigned the program targeting earlier and less-advanced patients, and then launched the GENERATION HD two Phase 2 trial with renewed hope for this revised approach.

That trial has now failed. Roche said tominersen did reduce levels of huntingtin protein in the spinal fluid — meaning the drug was doing what it was designed to do at the molecular level — but it did not improve functional outcomes. Patients on the drug did no better than those on placebo on the functional, cognitive, and physical measures that matter for how people with Huntington's actually live. Roche is discontinuing development entirely, with no plans for compassionate use.

At the same time, Roche is also discontinuing RG six four nine six, a more recent and more selective antisense oligonucleotide designed to reduce only the mutant copy of huntingtin, leaving the normal protein intact — a theoretically more elegant approach because the normal protein may have important cellular functions. That trial was halted after only three patients, not because it did not work in humans but because animal safety studies showed that chronic repeated dosing caused problems that made it unethical to continue.

What is left in the pipeline: the gene therapy company UniQure has a one-time surgical gene therapy called AMT one thirty delivered directly into the brain, which is furthest along among remaining programs. Wave Life Sciences has allele-selective antisense programs that selectively target only the mutant copy. Eli Lilly's pepinemab takes a different approach, targeting neuroinflammation rather than huntingtin protein directly.

The broader issue the field is now forced to grapple with is whether reducing huntingtin protein levels — even substantially, as tominersen demonstrably did in the spinal fluid — is sufficient to change the clinical course of the disease once neurodegeneration is already underway. The answer, at least for tominersen in this patient population, appears to be no. That is a hard molecular and clinical lesson, and it will shape how remaining programs are designed and when in the disease course they intervene.

Moving to AI in biomedicine. This week the FDA formally accepted Absentia Labs' Digital Liver Model into its Innovative Science and Technology Approaches for New Drugs program — a mouthful that is usually shortened to ISTAND. This is the first time an AI-based drug development tool has ever entered this qualification pathway.

The problem the model is designed to address is drug-induced liver injury, universally abbreviated as DILI. The liver is the body's primary organ for metabolizing and detoxifying foreign chemicals, including drugs. When a drug or its breakdown products are toxic to liver cells, that can manifest as elevated liver enzymes in the blood, and in severe cases as acute liver failure. DILI is one of the most common reasons drugs fail in clinical trials and one of the most common reasons approved drugs are eventually withdrawn from the market. The frustration is that animal models — rats, dogs, the species routinely used in preclinical safety testing — are notoriously poor at predicting which drugs will cause liver injury in humans, because human liver metabolism differs enough from these animals that compounds that are toxic in people often look fine in rodents, and vice versa.

Absentia Labs is a Boston startup founded in twenty twenty-four. They have built what they describe as a mechanistic AI model of liver biology — not a purely statistical tool trained to recognize patterns, but one that incorporates the known biochemistry of drug metabolism pathways and hepatocyte — meaning liver cell — toxicity, combined with drug-response data, to predict DILI risk before a drug goes into humans. Acceptance into the ISTAND pathway means the FDA is now formally evaluating the model as a potential regulatory-grade tool. Pharmaceutical companies could eventually be able to cite its predictions in drug applications. Formal qualification has not yet been granted; that evaluation process is ongoing. But it is the first time the agency has opened that door to an AI tool in this space, and it points toward a near-term future where some preclinical animal testing is supplemented or eventually replaced by validated computational models.

And finally, animals. Published in Science on July ninth — just yesterday — a team led by evolutionary biologist Jay Storz at the University of Nebraska-Lincoln reports on the biology of the Andean leaf-eared mouse, scientific name Phyllotis vaccarum, which holds the distinction of being the world's highest-dwelling mammal. This animal lives on the summits of Andean volcanoes in Chile and Argentina at altitudes above six thousand seven hundred meters — roughly the altitude of the death zone in mountaineering, where extended survival without supplemental oxygen is impossible for humans. Up there, temperatures drop to negative sixty degrees Celsius and the air contains only about forty-four percent of the oxygen available at sea level.

Storz's group studied populations of Phyllotis vaccarum across a broad elevational gradient — from sea level to the summits — comparing their physiology and genomics. The headline finding is that high-altitude mice have evolved dramatically enhanced capacity for heat production in their muscles. In small mammals in extreme cold, the primary source of metabolic heat is shivering — rapid, involuntary muscle contractions. What the team found is that the skeletal muscle mitochondria of high-altitude mice have significantly increased respiratory capacity, meaning they can burn oxygen more rapidly to generate energy and heat. Their muscles are evolved furnaces.

There is also evidence these mice manage their blood chemistry differently at altitude. When oxygen is scarce, the body's instinct is to breathe faster, but hyperventilating too much blows off carbon dioxide and changes blood pH in ways that are harmful — a problem called respiratory alkalosis. The mouse's physiology appears to buffer carbon dioxide in ways that reduce this risk.

But the most surprising finding is dietary. Population genomics showed that both the high-altitude and low-altitude populations carry genes for detoxifying poisonous plant compounds — an evolved biochemical toolkit for eating vegetation that would be lethal to most mammals. At six thousand meters, where food is nearly nonexistent and these mice eat whatever they can find, the ability to metabolize plant toxins appears to be just as critical to survival as the cold-and-hypoxia physiology. The same species, with the same genome, thrives from sea level to active volcano summits. That is the broadest altitudinal range of any mammal on Earth, and a remarkable demonstration of how far a single lineage can stretch when natural selection has enough time and enough pressure to work with.
