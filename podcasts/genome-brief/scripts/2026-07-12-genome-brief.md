## Script

Good morning. Welcome to The Genome Brief. It's Sunday, July twelfth, twenty twenty-six. Three stories today.

On the pathogen front: H five N one — the strain that has been running a global animal pandemic since 2021 — has now reached Australia. Last week it was confirmed in a local non-migratory seabird, completing the virus's sweep of every major landmass on the planet. We'll look at how it got there and what happens next.

On the business side: the FDA on Thursday expanded a major bladder cancer approval. A regimen combining an antibody-drug conjugate with a checkpoint inhibitor — which was previously approved for a subset of patients — can now be used across the full group of people with muscle-invasive bladder cancer heading into surgery. The trial numbers are striking.

And to close: a new spider species was just described in the Indian Himalayas, and it turns out this spider — discovered half a world away from Hawaii — independently evolved an identical smiley face. The biology here is genuinely delightful.

Let's start in Australia.

---

For five years, as H five N one carved through wildlife populations across Europe, Africa, the Americas, and even Antarctica, Australia remained the one major holdout. The island continent had, until this June, never recorded the strain that has dominated the global bird flu picture since 2021. That changed with the first detection in Western Australia last month, and last week the situation took a step that surveillance experts were watching for closely: confirmation of the virus in a non-migratory bird — a greater crested tern found dead on the South Australian coast near the town of Robe.

As of this weekend, Australia has thirteen confirmed detections spread across three states: seven in Western Australia, five in South Australia, and one in New South Wales. All are in seabirds. No poultry have tested positive. There are no reports of mass wildlife die-offs. Human health risk remains low.

So what is actually going on, and why does this matter?

Start with the virus itself. The strain is the clade two-three-four-four-b H five N one lineage. A quick word on nomenclature: influenza viruses are classified partly by their hemagglutinin protein — that's the H in H five N one, and hemagglutinin is the surface protein the virus uses to latch onto and enter host cells. The particular clade that's been circulating since 2021 has caused unprecedented damage to wildlife globally. In 2022 alone, somewhere between a third and nearly half of all adult northern gannets in the North Atlantic died from this strain. It has killed sea lions, seals, and Andean bears. Antarctica was breached in 2024. Until now, Australia had been spared.

Why had Australia avoided it so long? The simplest explanation is geography and migration patterns. The strain that has circulated through Asia since the 1990s never made the jump to Australia because there are no duck species that routinely migrate between the two regions. And migratory ducks are the key amplifying host. They can carry and shed the virus over long distances while showing few signs of illness, which is exactly the mechanism that repeatedly introduced H five N one into poultry operations and wildlife populations along European and Asian flyways.

What changed is that the global spread of this strain has moved beyond the Asian duck flyways. Marine birds — petrels, skuas, terns — that roam the Southern Ocean appear to be the bridge. The initial June detections were in brown skuas, transoceanic wanderers that could have picked up the virus from populations in Antarctica or the sub-Antarctic islands. The July tenth detection in a greater crested tern is the more significant development: that species breeds locally on Australian shores. It is not a long-range migrant. Finding the virus in a resident bird suggests the virus has already started circulating in the coastal environment.

The surveillance question that matters most now is what happens if this reaches Australia's local duck populations. The biology is important here. Ducks are efficient amplifying hosts — they can shed high viral loads while showing minimal signs of illness, and if the virus establishes itself in migratory or semi-migratory duck populations that overlap with poultry farms, containment becomes much harder. That is the epidemiological sequence — wild seabirds to coastal environment to ducks to poultry — that has caused the most serious damage in other countries. Australia is not there yet, but the first two links in that chain are now confirmed.

The Doherty Institute in Melbourne, which put out a public briefing this week, flagged one other dimension worth understanding: Australia has globally unique endemic bird species that have never encountered anything like this strain. Wherever clade two-three-four-four-b has reached new ecosystems, it has caused serious mortality among naive populations — birds with no prior immune experience with the virus. Australia's endemic fauna face the same risk.

The current advice for the public is unchanged: do not handle sick or dead birds, report unusual mortality to your state wildlife authority.

On the business side, the FDA on Thursday expanded the approved use of enfortumab vedotin plus pembrolizumab — sold under the brand names Padcev and Keytruda — for muscle-invasive bladder cancer. This is a significant expansion, and the trial data behind it is worth understanding.

First, the disease. Muscle-invasive bladder cancer means the tumor has grown through the bladder's inner lining into the muscle wall. That matters because once cancer reaches the muscle, it is at high risk of spreading beyond the bladder. The standard treatment is a two-stage process: first, neoadjuvant therapy before surgery to shrink the tumor and kill any cancer cells that may already be circulating in the body; then surgery to remove the bladder — a procedure called cystectomy — followed by adjuvant therapy afterward to reduce the risk of the cancer coming back. In the neoadjuvant step, the traditional chemotherapy regimen has been gemcitabine plus cisplatin, which has been standard for decades.

The Padcev plus Keytruda regimen was already approved for one group of these patients: those who couldn't safely receive cisplatin. Cisplatin is a powerful chemotherapy drug that can damage kidneys and cause hearing loss and nerve damage, and a substantial fraction of bladder cancer patients — perhaps thirty to forty percent — can't tolerate it safely because of pre-existing kidney problems or other vulnerabilities. For those patients, the ADC-plus-checkpoint-inhibitor combination became an option a couple of years ago.

What changed Thursday is that the FDA now says this combination can be used for all patients with muscle-invasive bladder cancer who are candidates for cystectomy — cisplatin-eligible or not — because the trial data showed it outperforms even the best existing chemotherapy.

Let me explain what these two drugs are and why they work well together.

Enfortumab vedotin is what's called an antibody-drug conjugate, or ADC. The basic concept: you take a monoclonal antibody — a protein engineered to recognize and bind a specific target on cancer cells — and you chemically attach a highly toxic drug to it, essentially using the antibody as a targeting delivery vehicle. The antibody finds the cancer cell, sticks to it, gets pulled inside, and releases the toxin directly into the cancer cell. Enfortumab vedotin targets a protein called Nectin-4, which is expressed at high levels on the cancer cells lining the bladder. Once the drug is delivered inside those cells, they die.

Pembrolizumab — Keytruda — is a checkpoint inhibitor targeting a receptor called PD-1. Here's how that works: T cells, the immune system's main cancer-killing cells, carry a molecular off-switch on their surface called PD-1. When PD-1 binds to a corresponding protein called PD-L1, the T cell is told to stand down. Many cancers exploit this mechanism — they express PD-L1 to essentially hide from the immune system. Pembrolizumab blocks PD-1, removing that brake so T cells can mount a sustained attack.

The reason these two drugs work particularly well together is a phenomenon called immunogenic cell death. When enfortumab vedotin kills a cancer cell from the inside, the cell doesn't die quietly — it releases molecular fragments that function as danger signals and tumor antigens. That stimulates a local immune response. The checkpoint inhibitor then ensures that the T cells responding to those signals aren't suppressed. The ADC creates the initial kill and triggers an immune alarm; the checkpoint inhibitor makes sure the alarm gets answered at full strength.

Two large trials supported Thursday's approval. The KEYNOTE-B15 trial enrolled over eight hundred patients who were eligible for cisplatin, and randomized them between standard gemcitabine-cisplatin chemotherapy and the Padcev-Keytruda combination. The key metric is something called pathologic complete response — the fraction of patients in whom surgeons find no viable cancer in the surgical specimen when the bladder is removed. In the ADC-immunotherapy arm, the pathologic complete response rate was 55.8 percent, compared to 32.5 percent with standard chemotherapy. More than half the patients had no detectable tumor left by the time of surgery. The hazard ratio for event-free survival — a measure comparing how much better or worse patients fared on one treatment versus the other — was 0.53, meaning roughly a 47 percent reduction in the risk of disease-related events.

The KEYNOTE-905 trial compared the regimen against immediate surgery with no neoadjuvant therapy — the approach used when patients can't tolerate any systemic treatment. There, pathologic complete response rates were 57.1 percent versus 8.6 percent. The event-free survival hazard ratio was 0.40.

A pathologic complete response rate above 55 percent in muscle-invasive bladder cancer is clinically remarkable. Achieving no detectable cancer at the time of surgery is one of the strongest available predictors of long-term cure. This approval will likely move this regimen toward becoming the new standard of care for this disease.

And finally, animals. A team from the Forest Research Institute and Regional Museum of Natural History in India, working in the mountains of Uttarakhand — a state in the northern Indian Himalayas — has described a new spider species. It wears a smile.

The species is named Theridion himalayana, and the genus name should mean something to anyone who has spent time in Hawaiian natural history. Because the Hawaiian islands are home to one of the most studied small arthropods in the world: Theridion grallator, the Hawaiian happy-face spider — a tiny creature that has a marking on its back that looks unmistakably like a red grinning face. Biologists have studied it for decades, partly because the variation in its markings across Hawaiian islands is striking, and partly because nobody has figured out why a spider would evolve to look like it is smiling.

Now there is a second smiling spider, half a world away in the Indian Himalayas at elevations above 2,000 meters. When the researchers compared the DNA of the two species, they found roughly 8.5 percent genetic divergence — enough to confirm these are distinct evolutionary lineages that are not closely related. The two happy-face spiders did not inherit their smiles from a shared ancestor. They evolved the same pattern independently, on opposite ends of the planet.

This is what biologists call convergent evolution: when different lineages independently arrive at the same trait, often because they face similar evolutionary pressures. The textbook examples are things like wings, which evolved independently in insects, bats, and birds; or camera-style eyes, which appear in dozens of separate animal lineages. In those cases, the function of the trait is obvious — wings are for flying, eyes are for seeing. A grinning pattern on the back of a spider the size of a pea? Nobody knows what it is for. Does it startle predators? Mimic something else? Attract mates? All of those ideas have been proposed for the Hawaiian species. None has been definitively confirmed.

What makes the Himalayan discovery stranger is one additional detail. Both the Hawaiian happy-face spider and the new Himalayan species prefer to live on plants in the genus Hedychium — ginger plants. This preference is not characteristic of the genus Theridion broadly. It is specific to these two smiling species, on opposite sides of the world, that are not closely related. Whether this parallel host preference is somehow connected to the parallel evolution of the smile — perhaps because ginger plants attract similar predators, or create similar visual environments — remains completely unknown.

The Himalayan population also shows extraordinary color variation: 32 different color morphs have already been documented, ranging from pale and unpatterned to fully red-faced. Only some individuals wear the full grinning pattern. That variation itself may eventually be part of the scientific story — the question of why some individuals have the full smile and some don't, and whether it makes any difference to their survival or reproduction.

It is a reminder that the natural world still has the capacity to produce, in the mountains of northern India, a spider that seems to be smiling at you, for reasons that two decades of evolutionary biology has not yet explained.

That is your Genome Brief for Sunday, July twelfth. Thanks for listening.
