## Script

Good morning. This is The Genome Brief for Thursday, July ninth, twenty twenty-six. Today: the Bundibugyo Ebola outbreak in the Democratic Republic of Congo is still intensifying, and the surveillance dragnet Uganda built around that outbreak has just caught something else entirely — an isolated Marburg case. Novartis is buying a small British biotech for up to one-point-five billion dollars, and the reason it matters is that Novartis is betting on a genuinely new way to poison a cancer cell. An open-source, six hundred fifty-five million parameter biomolecular foundation model from a company called Aureka just dropped under an Apache license, and it is worth understanding why an all-atom co-folding model is what the field has been waiting on. OpenAI's GPT five point six family opens up globally today. And to close, one of the strangest looking sharks on the planet has finally been filmed alive in its natural habitat, seven hundred meters deeper than we thought it could live.

Let's start with the pathogen watch.

The Bundibugyo Ebola outbreak in the eastern Democratic Republic of Congo is not settling down. As of Monday this week, the D R C had reported one thousand seven hundred and eight confirmed cases and five hundred and eighty deaths. Two days earlier that number was one thousand five hundred and sixty-one — so the outbreak added roughly a hundred and fifty confirmed cases in about three days. The WHO Regional Office for Africa is now saying explicitly that the outbreak "continues to intensify", driven by sustained transmission in the hotspot health zones of Ituri and North Kivu provinces, growing numbers of deaths that are being detected in the community rather than in treatment centers, and — and this is the surveillance-genomics detail that matters most — spread into previously unaffected health zones.

Here is the number that is really telling you what is happening. Twelve thousand four hundred contacts are under follow-up in the D R C. But only about a third — thirty-two point four percent — of newly confirmed cases were previously known as contacts. The other two-thirds are essentially arriving out of nowhere, from chains of transmission that the response never had eyes on. In outbreak epidemiology terms, that means transmission has largely escaped the tracing net. When your case-detection-through-contacts number drops below about half, the traditional ring approach — quarantine the contacts of every confirmed case, watch them for symptoms, break the chain — starts to fail structurally. You are always reacting to yesterday's exposure.

To recap the pathogen for anyone new: Bundibugyo virus is one of the six species of ebolavirus. It was first identified in two thousand seven in Uganda. It is genetically distinct from the more familiar Zaire ebolavirus that caused the twenty fourteen West Africa epidemic, which matters because most of the countermeasures we have — including the Ervebo vaccine — target Zaire, not Bundibugyo. We covered on Wednesday that the WHO has now added the first-ever molecular diagnostic test specifically for Bundibugyo to its Emergency Use Listing, and that response-network testing capacity has been scaling as a result. But you can see the tension in these numbers: better tests are coming online, and the outbreak is still outpacing the response.

Which brings us to Uganda. Uganda has been running enhanced disease surveillance for Ebola for months now, because it borders the affected DRC provinces and had a small imported Bundibugyo cluster earlier in the outbreak — twenty confirmed cases, two deaths, with the last confirmed Ebola case there identified on June twenty-first. Then, in the first days of July, that same enhanced surveillance system in Kyegegwa District, in western Uganda, picked up a very sick one-and-a-half-year-old child. The clinical picture looked like Bundibugyo Ebola. The samples were sequenced. And it was not Bundibugyo. It was Marburg.

If you have not encountered Marburg before, it is a filovirus — the same family as ebolaviruses, essentially a cousin. It causes a severe hemorrhagic fever with a case fatality rate that typically runs around fifty percent in outbreak settings. Uganda has seen it before — small outbreaks in two thousand seven, twenty twelve, twenty fourteen, and twenty seventeen. The reservoir is the Egyptian fruit bat, and human cases usually trace back either to a caves-and-mines exposure or to a household contact of a prior case. There is no licensed vaccine and no specific antiviral, though several vaccine candidates including a Sabin Vaccine Institute construct are in clinical trials.

The WHO Emergencies Director, Doctor Ihekweazu, put the surveillance point very plainly. He said, quote, "This was not a normal detection. This case was initially apparently thought to be Bundibugyo, but then confirmed to be Marburg", end quote. In other words, the reason Uganda caught this Marburg case at all is that they were already looking hard for Ebola. The Ebola net caught something the routine surveillance system would very likely have missed. As of the last update, contacts of the child are being monitored, none have developed symptoms, and Africa CDC is describing it as an isolated case with no confirmed additional infections. That is the good news. The genuinely uncomfortable news is that if there is one Marburg case in Kyegegwa, there was almost certainly a source — a bat exposure, an unrecognized cluster — that nobody knows about yet. The source investigation is ongoing.

The take: high-intensity outbreak response infrastructure produces surveillance dividends beyond the pathogen it was built for. This is what "enhanced surveillance" is actually supposed to do. It also means you should assume every non-Ebola febrile-illness signal in the surveillance zone will now get scrutinized more than it otherwise would, and expect more discoveries — some spurious, some, like this one, very real.

On the business side. Novartis announced this week that it is acquiring Myricx Bio, a small privately held UK spinout from Imperial College London, in a deal worth up to one point five billion dollars — one point one billion cash up front, and up to four hundred million more in milestone payments. Myricx is still preclinical. What Novartis is paying for is not clinical data. It is a novel antibody-drug conjugate payload class.

Some background on where antibody-drug conjugates sit in oncology right now. An antibody-drug conjugate is a monoclonal antibody with a cytotoxic drug — the "payload" — chemically stitched to it via a linker. The antibody binds a tumor-cell surface protein, the whole complex gets internalized, the linker gets cleaved, and the payload — which is generally too toxic to give as free drug — kills the cell from the inside. This is now a foundational class in oncology; Enhertu, the Daiichi-Sankyo HER2 ADC, is the biggest example, and Merck, AstraZeneca, Pfizer and Novartis have all been buying and building here for the last five years.

The catch is that essentially every antibody-drug conjugate on the market or in late-stage development uses one of two payload chemistries. Either a microtubule inhibitor — think of it as gluing the cell's structural scaffolding in place so it cannot divide — or a topoisomerase-one inhibitor, which snaps DNA strands in ways the cell cannot repair. Both work. Both have well-known toxicity ceilings — Enhertu in particular runs into a lot of dose reductions and treatment interruptions for interstitial lung disease and other off-tumor toxicities. And tumors are increasingly showing up in the clinic that are resistant to topoisomerase-one payloads, meaning the entire payload class fails against them.

Myricx has a completely different payload — an N-myristoyltransferase inhibitor. N-myristoyltransferase is an enzyme that does a very specific and slightly weird biochemical job. It attaches a fourteen-carbon fatty acid — myristic acid — to certain proteins, at a specific glycine residue at the amino terminus, right after those proteins are made. That lipid tag lets those proteins anchor themselves to the inner face of cell membranes, where a lot of essential signaling has to happen. When you inhibit that enzyme, dozens of client proteins that depend on that lipid tag never get to their correct location. Signaling pathways that cancer cells rely on for survival — proliferation signaling, apoptosis suppression, membrane trafficking — collapse simultaneously. It is a multi-pathway hit, not a single-target hit. Preclinically, Myricx has shown broad activity across solid tumor models, including — and this is the key selling point — models that are resistant to topoisomerase-one payload conjugates.

Myricx has two lead assets, both preclinical, aimed at established tumor antigens: B seven H three, and HER two. HER two is obviously already an Enhertu target — so this is directly a next-generation-payload play, betting that a new-payload antibody-drug conjugate aimed at HER two could work in Enhertu-resistant patients. Fiona Marshall, who runs biomedical research at Novartis, said the quiet part out loud: "ADCs have become an important part of cancer treatment, but there remains a clear need for new payload mechanisms to overcome resistance."

Why it matters: antibody-drug conjugate development for the last five years has looked like a lot of large deals for very similar payloads with different antibody warheads. This is one of the first large biopharma bets on a genuinely different chemistry of killing. If N-myristoyltransferase-inhibitor payloads deliver a differentiated toxicity profile and hit topoisomerase-one-resistant tumors, the next wave of antibody-drug conjugate clinical trials will look meaningfully different from the current one.

Moving to AI in biomedicine. On Monday, a company called Aureka — a US-China AI biotech based in Laguna Hills, California and Shanghai — released a model called OpenDDE, the Open Drug Discovery Engine, under the Apache two point zero license. It is a six hundred and fifty-five million parameter biomolecular foundation model, and Aureka says training it took roughly four hundred and fourteen thousand G P U hours — call it fifty-plus years on a single machine. Weights are on Hugging Face and GitHub, no gate.

To explain why this matters, remember what AlphaFold does. AlphaFold two and three, from Google DeepMind, take a protein sequence — sometimes with a partner molecule — and predict the three-dimensional structure. AlphaFold two was protein-only. AlphaFold three, released in twenty twenty-four, added the ability to co-fold a protein together with a small molecule ligand, a nucleic acid, or another protein — this is called co-folding — and that is a much more useful primitive for drug discovery, because a lot of drug discovery is about predicting how a small molecule sits in a binding pocket. The problem, from an open-science perspective, is that AlphaFold three's weights are not freely available for commercial use; you access it through DeepMind's server with usage restrictions, and full weights went to only academic non-commercial users under an agreement.

OpenDDE is basically an attempt to be an open, all-atom, co-folding model — the atomistic-reasoning core of a drug discovery pipeline. It models proteins, nucleic acids, small molecule ligands, and other biomolecular components all together in one atomic space. On the standard antibody-antigen co-folding benchmarks in the field, OpenDDE reports success rates of fifty-one percent, seventy percent, and sixty-six percent respectively under top-ranked selection, rising into the eighties under oracle selection. Those are respectable numbers for a fully open model, though — and this is important — direct head-to-head against AlphaFold three on identical splits is not yet published, so calibrate expectations accordingly.

What is worth stepping back for: over the last three years the field has consolidated around the pattern of AlphaFold-two style structure prediction being the entry point to everything downstream — de novo protein design with RFDiffusion, binder design, affinity prediction, molecular dynamics warm-starts. Most of the openly-usable models at that entry point are getting older. A six hundred fifty-five million parameter, Apache-two-zero, co-folding model is exactly the kind of open-science piece the biotech and academic community needs to keep building on. If OpenDDE holds up in third-party benchmarks, expect a wave of downstream tools that use it as the structural reasoning engine.

Quick AI tools note. OpenAI's GPT five point six family — three models called Sol, Terra, and Luna — moves from a limited government-vetted preview into broad public availability today, July ninth. Sol is the frontier model, priced at five dollars per million input tokens and thirty dollars per million output. Terra is a mid-tier at two dollars fifty and fifteen. Luna is the small, cheap tier at one dollar and six. OpenAI is describing Sol as its "strongest model yet for cybersecurity and biology" and reporting elevated scores on internal SecureBio biology evaluations and on ExploitBench for security tasks — which is the specific reason the initial rollout was limited to about twenty government-vetted partners for two weeks before the broader release. For working scientists, the practical question is whether Sol's biology capability jump is big enough to matter for tasks like literature synthesis, experimental protocol drafting, and biomolecular reasoning. Worth kicking the tires this week.

And finally, animals. A team led by Aaron Judah at the University of Hawai'i at Mānoa Deep-Sea Fish Ecology Lab, publishing in the Journal of Fish Biology, has reported the first-ever footage of a live goblin shark in its natural habitat. If you have never seen one, the goblin shark, Mitsukurina owstoni, is a pink, translucent, deep-sea shark with a long flattened snout and a jaw that slingshots outward when it strikes — genuinely one of the strangest-looking vertebrates on the planet. Until now, every confirmed video of a live goblin shark has come after the animal was accidentally caught on a fishing line and brought to the surface, where they usually did not survive. There has been essentially no footage of the species doing anything in its natural environment.

The team put together two sightings. The first was in twenty nineteen, on a seamount northwest of Jarvis Island in the central Pacific, captured by a remotely operated vehicle called Hercules operating from the exploration vessel Nautilus during a livestreamed dive — and then, incredibly, sitting archived in Ocean Exploration Trust footage for years before someone flagged the possible sighting and Judah's group formally identified it. The second was in twenty twenty-four, at the slope of the Tonga Trench, filmed with a baited camera on a bottom lander deployed from the research vessel Dagon by Alan Jamieson's Minderoo deep-sea research center in Western Australia. That Tonga Trench observation was about seven hundred meters deeper than the goblin shark had ever been known to live, which meaningfully rewrites the vertical range of the species.

Two footnotes worth flagging. First, this is a paleogenetic-ish story disguised as an animal-of-the-week story: goblin sharks are the last surviving members of a lineage — the family Mitsukurinidae — that goes back roughly a hundred and twenty-five million years. They are what evolutionary biologists call a living fossil. So the range extension we just got is not a minor addendum. It is the first meaningful ecological data on a lineage older than most modern shark families. And second, there is a delightful surveillance parallel to today's Ebola-and-Marburg story. Both observations were made possible by an unrelated data-collection operation. The twenty nineteen sighting was archived from a public livestreamed exploration. The twenty twenty-four sighting was collected by a lander doing broad deep-ocean sampling. In both cases, the goblin shark was serendipity — the by-catch of somebody looking at everything.
