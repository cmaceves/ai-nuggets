## Script

Good morning. This is The Genome Brief for Thursday, July 16, 2026. Today's episode: how a plant virus that just rides through the human gut could turn wastewater sequencing into a genuine quantitative surveillance tool, an unexpected structural rescue of a nearly forgotten TB antibiotic that still works against resistant polymerase, an FDA approval for a first-in-class four-target cancer drug that's been quietly stalking the PI3K pathway for a decade, four hundred million dollars for one of the more interesting AI drug design shops, and a study suggesting that chimpanzees are, in fact, just as into crystals as we are.

Let's start with pathogen watch.

Item one — and this is the one to sit with. A team led by Pardis Sabeti at the Broad, with collaborators at Stanford, Berkeley, and elsewhere, posted a preprint to medRxiv this week that quietly changes what wastewater sequencing can be used for. To back up a step: for the last several years, public-health agencies have been running two very different kinds of wastewater assays in parallel. One is targeted PCR — you pick a pathogen you're worried about, like SARS cov two or influenza, and you count copies. That works well. The other is metagenomic sequencing — you sequence everything in the sample and let the reads tell you what's there. That's exciting because it's pathogen-agnostic: one sample, one sequencing run, and in principle you catch anything from norovirus to a new coronavirus to an antibiotic-resistance gene. The catch is that metagenomic wastewater has always been useful for *detecting* things but bad at *quantifying* them. And here's the underlying reason, which is worth explaining, because it's a pretty deep problem in the whole field of sequencing.

When you sequence a wastewater sample, every read is a *relative* measurement. If SARS cov two makes up one in ten thousand reads today and one in five thousand tomorrow, you don't actually know whether SARS cov two doubled or whether everything else halved. Metagenomic data is what mathematicians call compositional — the fractions have to sum to one, so you can only see the ratios, not the absolute amounts. On top of that, wastewater is a wildly variable substrate: rainfall dilutes it, industrial discharge shifts the bacterial background, sample handling matters. The classic fix in PCR-land is to measure something you know is constant per person, and use it as a scaling denominator. The gold standard for PCR is a plant virus called pepper mild mottle virus — humans eat peppers, the virus survives the gut, and it ends up in stool at a fairly stable rate per person. Divide your pathogen signal by pepper mild mottle virus signal and you've corrected for how much human waste is actually in your sample.

The question this paper asks is: can you do the same thing for metagenomic data — and if so, what's the right denominator? The team retrospectively pulled fourteen hundred wastewater metagenomic samples from twenty-five US sites, from a consortium called CASPER, and benchmarked seven different normalization strategies against paired PCR data and against clinical case counts from Epic Cosmos and the CDC. The winner was tobamovirus counts — that's the broader genus containing pepper mild mottle virus, essentially the diet-derived plant viruses that reliably ride through the human gut. Normalizing metagenomic reads by tobamovirus reads improved concordance with the clinical ground truth in eighteen of nineteen pathogen-and-comparison combinations. The biggest gains were for year-round pathogens like SARS cov two and norovirus, where signal-versus-noise is the whole game. For sharply seasonal viruses like flu and RSV, gains were smaller — but that's because those signals were already easy to see. Crucially, tobamovirus normalization almost never made things *worse*: median gains were about five times larger than median losses.

Why does this matter? Because if this holds up, tobamovirus-normalized wastewater metagenomics is competitive with targeted PCR on *quantitative accuracy* while retaining the pathogen-agnostic detection PCR can't do. In practice, one metagenomic sample can give you a real trend line for known pathogens *and* an early warning for unknown ones. That's the surveillance holy grail the field has been chasing since COVID. Sabeti's group has been pushing on this for years; this feels like the paper that makes metagenomic wastewater a plausible operational tool for a public-health department, not a research-grade toy.

Item two, staying with pathogens. A structural biology group in Poland posted a preprint that solves a small mystery in an antibiotic family we've talked about a lot on this show — the rifamycins, the backbone of first-line TB treatment. Recall from earlier episodes that rifampicin — the R in the standard four-drug TB regimen — works by binding a pocket in the bacterial RNA polymerase, the enzyme that transcribes DNA into RNA. Block that enzyme, and Mycobacterium tuberculosis can't make protein, can't grow. The problem is that a single point mutation in that binding pocket — typically at a residue called S531 — knocks affinity down by orders of magnitude, and boom, you've got multidrug-resistant TB. That resistance mechanism is exactly what's driving the crisis we covered on Tuesday out of Vietnam.

Now, rifamycin B is the *original* rifamycin — the molecule that was discovered back in the fifties and that all the clinically useful derivatives, rifampicin included, were built from. It's been considered a dead end for decades: unstable in solution, weak antibacterial activity in cells, nobody bothers with it. But this new paper — the first crystal structure of rifamycin B ever solved, and the first structure of rifamycin B bound to a bacterial RNA polymerase — shows something unexpected. Rifamycin B has a chemical group at position four called an O-carboxymethyl. In the free molecule, that group folds back and interacts with itself. But when rifamycin B binds the polymerase, that group swings out and makes a *salt bridge* — a strong electrostatic contact — with a part of the enzyme called fork loop two, on the beta subunit. And here's the payoff: because rifamycin B is grabbing onto the enzyme with this extra contact that clinical rifampicin doesn't use, mutations in the classic rifampicin-binding pocket — the S531 mutations that make TB resistant — don't disrupt rifamycin B nearly as much. The old, ignored parent compound retains inhibitory activity against the very RNA polymerase variants that our first-line drug can't touch.

Nobody is going to put rifamycin B into a patient. It's chemically unstable, and its whole-cell activity is weak. But this is a proof of principle. The C-four position on the rifamycin scaffold — which medicinal chemists have basically left alone for seventy years — is a place where you can bolt on a chemical group that reaches out and grabs a *different* part of the enzyme, one that isn't a mutational hotspot. That's a real handle for designing rifamycins that hold up against rifampicin-resistant TB. In a field where the entire pipeline of new anti-TB drugs is thin, and the resistance data we covered on Tuesday says the problem is getting worse, structural insights like this are how you get out.

On the business side.

The FDA on Monday, July fourteenth, approved a drug called Revtorpyk — generic name gedatolisib — from a Minneapolis company called Celcuity, in combination with two existing breast-cancer drugs, palbociclib and fulvestrant. The indication is hormone-receptor-positive, HER2-negative advanced or metastatic breast cancer without a PIK3CA mutation, after progression on endocrine therapy. That's most of advanced hormone-driven breast cancer, so this is a big market. But what makes this drug interesting mechanistically is that it's the first FDA-approved therapy that inhibits all four class one PI3K isoforms — alpha, beta, delta, and gamma — *and* both mTOR complexes, mTORC1 and mTORC2. Let me explain what that means and why anyone should care.

The PI3K–AKT–mTOR pathway is basically the growth control system inside a cell. External signals — insulin, growth factors, hormone-receptor engagement — hit PI3K on the inner face of the membrane, PI3K phosphorylates a lipid called PIP two into PIP three, PIP three recruits AKT, and AKT turns on mTOR, which decides whether the cell grows, divides, or stops. In estrogen-receptor-positive breast cancer, when you block estrogen signaling with endocrine therapy, tumors escape by cranking up this exact pathway. So targeting PI3K or mTOR alongside hormone therapy is an old and obvious idea. The problem is that there are four different isoforms of class one PI3K, and when you knock out one, the tumor upregulates a different one and reroutes the signal. Drugs targeting only alpha — like alpelisib — hit only PIK3CA-mutant tumors, and even those tumors escape. Drugs targeting only mTOR — like everolimus — trigger feedback loops that reactivate the pathway upstream.

Revtorpyk is designed to shut all four PI3K isoforms and both mTOR complexes simultaneously — the whole vertical axis at once. In the Phase 3 VIKTORIA-1 trial, the triplet regimen — Revtorpyk plus palbociclib plus fulvestrant — gave a median progression-free survival of nine point three months, versus two months on fulvestrant alone. Objective response rate was thirty-two percent versus one percent. The hazard ratio was zero point two four — meaning a roughly seventy-six percent reduction in the risk of progression or death. Those are large numbers for post-endocrine-therapy breast cancer, and they're in the *PIK3CA-wildtype* population, which is the bulk of patients and has historically been the harder group to treat with PI3K pathway drugs. Celcuity plans to file a supplemental application for the PIK3CA-mutant population in the third quarter. The strategic question this raises for the field is whether pan-isoform inhibition — which people worried would be too toxic — actually turns out to be manageable, and whether that opens up other tumor types that have been resistant to isoform-selective drugs.

Onto AI in biomedicine, briefly.

Chai Discovery, one of the higher-profile AI drug design startups, closed a four-hundred-million-dollar Series C on Monday, tripling its valuation to three point eight billion dollars in seven months. Index Ventures led, with Sequoia, Kleiner Perkins, Dimension, and a large syndicate including Bain Capital Ventures, Battery, Baillie Gifford, and BDT and MSD; existing backers Thrive, OpenAI, and Menlo rolled over. What Chai actually does that's differentiating: they build all-atom molecular structure prediction models, essentially in the AlphaFold three lineage, but the pitch is that their models are integrated into an antibody design workflow used by Lilly, Pfizer, and Novartis on live discovery programs. The number worth watching is not the raise itself — AI-drug-discovery valuations have been frothy for two years — but the customer list. Three top-ten pharmas is a stronger signal than most models produce, because those pharmas have their own internal AI teams and don't pay for outside inference unless something's demonstrably faster or better than what they've got. If Chai's antibody design output holds up when the first pharma-partnered programs read out in the clinic, it becomes hard to argue that the current wave of protein-structure foundation models isn't materially changing early-stage drug discovery. If it doesn't, we get another datapoint on how thin the gap is between benchmark performance and real-world impact.

No major AI tool release from the big labs today worth pulling into the episode — the industry is coiled for Google's Gemini three point five Pro launch, reportedly tomorrow.

And finally, animals.

A team led by Juan Manuel García-Ruiz at the Donostia International Physics Center in San Sebastián published in Frontiers in Psychology this week what may be the most delightful primatology paper of the year: chimpanzees, it turns out, are into crystals. Enculturated chimps at the Rainfer Foundation in Spain — nine animals across two groups — were presented with mixed piles of ordinary pebbles and small quartz and calcite crystals. The chimps went straight for the crystals. They picked them up. They held them up to eye level and rotated them, apparently to see through them. They spotted small quartz crystals hidden among twenty pebbles within seconds. One female, Sandy, sorted her crystals into groups by type. The novelty response looked exactly like human novelty response — strongest at first, tapering off as the animal grew familiar. The paper's argument is that our human fascination with shiny transparent rocks — the sixty thousand years of collecting crystals we can document in the archaeological record — may sit on top of an attraction to transparency and geometric form that goes back at least six million years to our common ancestor with chimps. Which is either a lovely story about deep shared aesthetics, or a reminder that when you leave a chimp alone with a bag of quartz, the chimp is going to do what any of us would do. Both, probably.
