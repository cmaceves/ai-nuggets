Pathogen watch.

Let's start with H five N one in dairy cattle and a paper from Science Advances that I think finally closes the loop on one of the stranger chapters in recent influenza epidemiology.

To follow the finding, you need to remember what happened in early 2024. H five N one — highly pathogenic avian influenza — had been circulating in wild birds and causing outbreaks in farm poultry for years. But in March of 2024, dairy cattle in the United States started getting sick. Influenza in cows was unusual enough. But the way they got sick was the real puzzle. H five N one in every other mammal the world had seen it in — seals, mink, humans — causes severe respiratory disease: pneumonia, lung damage. That's what the virus is known for. In dairy cows, the dominant symptom was mastitis — a severe infection of the udder — with a sudden, dramatic drop in milk production. The lungs were largely spared.

Virologists were genuinely puzzled. Influenza tropism — meaning which tissues the virus infects — tracks with receptor distribution in the host. So what was going on in cow tissues that made H five N one a disease of the mammary gland and not the airway?

The University of Pittsburgh School of Public Health team, led by Suresh Kuchipudi, published the answer in Science Advances last Thursday. The key is a specific category of cell-surface receptor called N-linked sialic acid receptors. Let me explain what that means.

Sialic acid is a family of sugar molecules that coat the outer surface of your cells. Influenza viruses use a protein on their surface called hemagglutinin — that's the H in H five N one — to grab onto sialic acid and get pulled inside the cell. This binding specificity is one of the core principles of influenza host range. Avian flu viruses have evolved to prefer sialic acid linked in a particular geometry, called alpha-two-three linkage. Human respiratory cells tend to display the alpha-two-six version. That geometric mismatch is one reason most avian flu strains don't spread easily in people: wrong key, wrong lock.

But this paper is not about alpha-two-three versus alpha-two-six. It is about a different distinction entirely: whether the sialic acid is attached via an N-linked glycan or an O-linked glycan. Those terms refer to how the underlying sugar chain is anchored to the protein it hangs from. N-linked glycans attach through a nitrogen atom; O-linked glycans attach through an oxygen. It turns out H five N one specifically requires the N-linked version — and this requirement maps almost perfectly onto cow tissue type.

The Kuchipudi team used binding experiments, staining, and high-resolution imaging to map glycan receptor distribution across cattle tissues in fine detail. N-linked sialic acid receptors are densely present throughout the mammary epithelium — the cells lining the inside of the udder. In the cow's airway tissue? Virtually absent, in Kuchipudi's words — making udders a perfect breeding ground and airways a desert for this particular virus. The unusual pathology of 2024 follows directly from the receptor geography. H five N one did not mutate to become a mammary pathogen; it was always going to infect cow udders rather than cow lungs, because that is where its receptor lives.

This matters beyond explaining 2024. The Pittsburgh group argues that mapping N-linked sialic acid receptor distribution across tissues and species now gives us a predictive framework. Before an H five N one outbreak in a new host species, you could in principle run this receptor screen and forecast which tissues will be hit. If a virus variant jumped to a species whose airways were packed with N-linked sialic acid receptors, the picture would look very different. That's useful for surveillance and pandemic preparedness planning.

Still in pathogen watch, let's move to malaria. A preprint posted yesterday to bioRxiv from a group at KAUST — the King Abdullah University of Science and Technology in Saudi Arabia — and Indiana University School of Medicine maps a protein called PfAP2-HCR and shows it acts as the master control switch for one of the most remarkable biological programs in all of parasitology.

Quick background. Plasmodium falciparum, the parasite responsible for the most dangerous form of malaria, spends part of its life cycle inside human red blood cells. Red blood cells are, by design, stripped-down cells — no nucleus, minimal internal structure, essentially bags of hemoglobin. The parasite invades one of these cells and then does something audacious: it remodels the entire cell from within. It exports hundreds of its own proteins through the cell membrane and into the host cell's interior, restructures the red cell membrane, creates new internal compartments, and installs its own trafficking machinery. It essentially builds a second apartment inside someone else's apartment.

This remodeling is not optional. Without it, the parasite cannot access the nutrients it needs, cannot hide from the spleen's filtration systems, and cannot eventually rupture out and invade more red cells. The whole thing is essential for survival and for the disease pathology of malaria.

Two structures are central to this remodeling program. The first is called PTEX — the Plasmodium Translocon of Exported Proteins. PTEX is a protein pore embedded in the membrane that surrounds the parasite inside the red blood cell; it acts as a molecular pump that pushes the parasite's proteins through into the host cell's interior. The second set of structures are called Maurer's clefts — flat, disc-like membranous compartments that appear in the host cell's cytoplasm and serve as staging areas for proteins being trafficked onward to the red cell's outer surface.

What this KAUST preprint identifies is the transcription factor that switches the whole program on. Transcription factors are proteins that bind to DNA and control which genes get read. This one, PfAP2-HCR, belongs to a family called ApiAP2 proteins. Here is a fascinating evolutionary detail: the AP2 DNA-binding domain these proteins use is normally found in plants — plant transcription factors use it to respond to environmental stress. Somewhere in evolution, the ancestors of Plasmodium picked up this domain and repurposed it. The malaria parasite now has around twenty-seven of these ApiAP2 proteins, each one regulating a chunk of the parasite's gene expression at different life-cycle stages. PfAP2-HCR is the one that runs the host cell remodeling program during the ring stage — the earliest phase after the parasite invades the red blood cell.

The KAUST team engineered parasites carrying a conditional truncation: they could switch off part of the pfap2-hcr gene on demand. When they did, two things happened. Ring-stage development arrested — the parasite got stuck and could not mature. And the remodeling program collapsed: Maurer's clefts never assembled, the PTEX export apparatus did not form, and about thirty percent of the parasite's exported proteins were not produced at all, including twenty-seven proteins already known to be essential for virulence.

RNA sequencing and chromatin immunoprecipitation experiments revealed the network this protein sits inside. PfAP2-HCR directly or indirectly controls about ninety-three of the three hundred and five genes encoding exported proteins. It is also embedded in a larger chromatin regulatory complex alongside two other proteins — PfMORC and PfISWI — that physically open and close sections of the genome for transcription. One protein at the top of that network, loss of which takes out thirty percent of the essential exported proteome, found in multiple life-cycle stages including gametocytes and mosquito-stage sporozoites.

For drug development, that profile is attractive. A transcription factor sitting at the apex of an essential program, with no clear human counterpart, regulating targets we already know are required for parasitism — that is exactly the kind of hub that drug designers look for. The caveat is that transcription factors are generally difficult to drug directly; the work needed now is structural characterization of the protein and mapping of its protein-protein interactions to find tractable binding sites.

On the business side.

MoonLake Immunotherapeutics released full Week fifty-two data from their Phase three VELA program on Sunday — two large randomized trials of sonelokimab in hidradenitis suppurativa, or HS. This is data worth understanding because the one-year durability question was really the final piece, and because the disease itself is worth knowing about.

Hidradenitis suppurativa is a chronic, painful inflammatory condition of the skin that most people have never heard of, despite affecting roughly one percent of the population. It causes recurring abscesses and inflammatory nodules in skin-fold areas — armpits, groin, underneath the breasts — and in its more severe forms develops into tunnel-forming disease, where chronic infection carves interconnected tracts under the skin. Patients describe the pain as severe, the smell as distressing, and the social impact as devastating. It's also chronically underdiagnosed: average time from first symptom to correct diagnosis is around seven years.

The biology involves overactivated innate immune signaling at the hair follicle, with a key role for two related inflammatory proteins called interleukin-17A and interleukin-17F. These are cytokines — molecular signals that recruit immune cells and drive local tissue destruction. Blocking them is the mechanistic rationale behind several drugs now in development or recently approved for HS.

Sonelokimab is a nanobody. A nanobody is a single-domain antibody derived from the unusual immunoglobulin format found in camelid animals — camels, llamas, alpacas. In conventional human antibodies, the active binding region requires a heavy chain and a light chain working together. Camelids evolved antibodies that function with only a single heavy-chain domain. Biotechnology companies have learned to clone these domains, engineer them for human use, and use their small size to access tissue compartments that full-size antibodies can't reach easily. Sonelokimab hits all three active forms of IL-17: the A-slash-A homodimer, the A-slash-F heterodimer, and the F-slash-F homodimer — the three shapes in which these cytokines do damage. It also has a central domain engineered to bind albumin, the most abundant protein in blood, which extends the drug's half-life and may help concentrate it at inflamed sites.

The one-year data: sixty-seven percent of patients on sonelokimab achieved the primary endpoint, called HiSCR seventy-five. That score — the letters stand for Hidradenitis Suppurativa Clinical Response — defines success as at least a seventy-five percent reduction in the count of nodules and abscesses, without any worsening of the draining tunnels. A third of patients hit HiSCR one hundred, meaning complete resolution. And twenty-six percent achieved inflammatory remission by a more stringent measure. On the quality-of-life scale used in the trial, the average patient moved from "severe" to "mild" impact over twelve months.

MoonLake notes that their response rates exceed competitor IL-17 inhibitors in HS by around ten percentage points. Cross-trial comparisons carry caveats, and regulatory agencies will want head-to-head data before making strong claims about comparative efficacy. But one-year durability with a clean safety profile is a solid regulatory package. The company confirmed an investor day for today, which almost certainly means discussion of a regulatory filing timeline.

AI in biomedicine.

On Sunday, at the BIO International Convention in San Diego, Insilico Medicine and SK Biopharmaceuticals announced a research and development collaboration valued at up to two-point-five billion dollars. The upfront and near-term payments to Insilico are eighteen million; the rest of that headline number is contingent milestone payments tied to preclinical, clinical, regulatory, and commercial success across a portfolio of neuroimmune drug programs.

The deal is worth using as a lens on what an AI drug-design platform actually is, because the category description — artificial intelligence for drug discovery — covers a wide range of things that are very different in their ambition and their risk.

Insilico's platform, which they call Pharma dot AI, centers on two distinct capabilities. The first is target identification: using large-scale biological data — transcriptomics, proteomics, genetic association data, biomedical literature — to predict which proteins or biological pathways, if modulated, could treat a particular disease. The question here is, "what should we shoot at?" The second is de novo molecule design: using generative AI models to design entirely new small molecules from scratch that bind the identified targets with the right properties. Not searching a library of existing chemicals, but generating new chemical matter that has never existed, guided by models trained on what makes drugs work — their binding geometry, their solubility, their metabolic stability, their ability to get into cells.

The company's most prominent evidence that this works is ISM001, an oral small molecule for idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis — scarring of the lung tissue of unknown cause — that was nominated as a drug candidate entirely by AI and has advanced into Phase two clinical trials. That is still a proof-of-concept story: the molecule has not yet been approved, and the clinical outcomes are what will ultimately matter. But having a fully AI-designed molecule in mid-stage human trials is genuinely new.

The therapeutic area for the SK Biopharmaceuticals deal — neuroimmune CNS disorders — is historically among the most difficult in all of drug development. The brain is protected by the blood-brain barrier, which excludes most drugs. The disease mechanisms in neuroinflammatory, neurodegenerative, and rare neurological conditions are often poorly understood. Clinical trials are expensive, slow, and have notoriously high failure rates. The argument for AI here is that if the platform can identify non-obvious biological targets — proteins that traditional hypothesis-driven research would have missed — it might find ways into diseases where the field has been stuck. SK Biopharmaceuticals brings CNS clinical experience and a commercial presence in Asia.

The structure of the deal is typical for the category: the AI company takes the front-end scientific risk, and the pharma partner pays out as milestones are hit. If none of the programs succeed, SK Biopharmaceuticals has spent eighteen million dollars. If they all succeed, Insilico gets two-point-five billion. It is a contract that prices AI drug discovery as highly speculative — which is honest. The field is moving fast, but the clinical proof is still accumulating.

And finally, animals.

The kākāpō is the world's only completely flightless parrot — and also the world's heaviest parrot. It's found only in New Zealand, critically endangered, with a current global population of fewer than three hundred individuals. It is nocturnal, enormous, smells faintly of honey and flowers, and has a leisurely approach to reproduction that makes conservation genuinely difficult: females breed only every two to four years, during mast-seeding years when certain trees fruit heavily, and even then the nests are vulnerable on the ground. If you've seen any wildlife documentary in the last decade, you've probably encountered the kākāpō — it's the parrot that tries to mate with a wildlife cameraman's head. The defining fact about the kākāpō is that it can't fly.

A paper published this week in the journal Alcheringa, an Australian paleontology journal, reports the discovery of a million-year-old relative. A team led by Trevor Worthy at Flinders University excavated a cave near Waitomo on New Zealand's North Island and recovered fossils spanning roughly one-point-five to one million years before the present. This is an unusual time window for New Zealand paleontology — there is a fifteen-million-year gap in the country's terrestrial fossil record, and finds from this Pleistocene epoch window are rare.

The assemblage they recovered is remarkable: twelve bird species and four frog species, representing an entire ancient North Island vertebrate community that no one had ever documented. Between a third and half of those species had gone extinct in the subsequent million years, apparently driven by volcanic eruptions and climate shifts — natural forces reshaping the fauna long before any human being arrived in New Zealand.

The kākāpō connection: the team identified a new species, Strigops insulaborealis, belonging to the same genus as the modern kākāpō. Analysis of its leg bones found them proportionally weaker than a modern kākāpō's. The modern bird has massively developed leg musculature because it climbs trees using its powerful feet — the vestigial wings provide only balance on the ground. The ancient relative's weaker legs suggest it was less dependent on climbing, which implies it may have retained at least some capacity for flight. The kākāpō is so thoroughly grounded today in part because New Zealand has no native land mammal predators — it never needed to fly. A million years ago, under different ecological pressures, its ancestor may have still had the option.
