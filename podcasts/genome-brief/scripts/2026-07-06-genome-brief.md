Good morning, and welcome to The Genome Brief for Monday, July sixth. Today: researchers find a nutritional choke point in the malaria parasite's life cycle that could be exploited to block transmission; Novartis pays up to one and a half billion dollars for a UK biotech built around a completely new class of antibody-drug conjugate payload; a smart use of active learning quintuples the hit rate of a whole-cell antibiotic screen against tuberculosis and Lyme disease; and it turns out that the humble rat-tailed maggot has been secretly using its snorkel as an oar.

Let's start with a malaria transmission story out of Weill Cornell in New York.

To understand this one, we need to remember where malaria transmission actually happens. When a mosquito bites an infected person and sucks up blood, it picks up parasite forms that fuse in the mosquito gut to form what's called an oocyst. That oocyst then embeds itself under the lining of the mosquito's midgut and grows explosively over the next seven to ten days. Inside a single oocyst, one starting cell replicates into thousands of daughter cells called sporozoites. Those sporozoites migrate to the mosquito's salivary glands, and when the mosquito bites its next human, they're what actually get injected and cause a new malaria infection. If you can stop that oocyst from developing, you break the transmission cycle.

The mystery has always been: where does the parasite get the nutrients to do all that growing inside the mosquito? Ten days of exponential biomass expansion isn't free. And it turns out to depend on a very specific molecular tool.

The new preprint on bioRxiv from Kim Williamson's group at Weill Cornell, together with collaborators at Wageningen and the Radboud University Medical Center, identifies that tool. They focused on a protein called PfApiAT2 — a transporter protein that sits in the parasite's membrane and moves small molecules in and out. Human cells and yeast cells have hundreds of these transporters; Plasmodium falciparum, the parasite that causes the deadliest form of malaria, has only about a dozen. So each one is doing a lot of work. PfApiAT2 belongs to a family called the ApiAT transporters — the Apicomplexan Amino Acid Transporters — that other researchers had already flagged as candidates for moving amino acids into the parasite.

What the team showed is that PfApiAT2 is specifically a proline transporter, and that in the mosquito stage of the life cycle it becomes essential. When they knocked out PfApiAT2 in the parasite and let mosquitoes take an infected blood meal, oocysts started to form — but they stalled. They grew to a fraction of normal size and then degenerated. The knockout parasites produced no sporozoites at all. Sporogony — the whole process of dividing into thousands of daughter cells — was completely blocked.

That's a striking phenotype, because in the blood stage — the stage in the human body that actually causes disease — losing PfApiAT2 does essentially nothing. The parasites grow normally. So this transporter isn't required in the human host, but it's absolutely required in the mosquito.

And the team went a step further and asked: is this because the parasite can't scavenge enough proline from the mosquito hemolymph, the mosquito's version of blood? To test that, they fed the infected mosquitoes a second meal — a blood meal or even just a protein meal — a few days after infection. The extra nutrients partially rescued the defect. Then they fed the mosquitoes sugar water spiked with free amino acids, and that fully rescued the oocysts, at least in size. In other words, the parasite is failing because it can't get enough proline out of the standard mosquito environment on its own, and adding external proline patches over that failure.

Why does this matter? Two reasons. First, it identifies a genuine biological vulnerability in transmission. If you could develop a compound that shuts down PfApiAT2 specifically at the mosquito stage, you would have a transmission-blocking drug — a drug that a person takes not to cure their own malaria but to prevent them from being able to infect the next mosquito. That's a category of intervention the malaria field has been chasing for years. Second, PfApiAT2 also happens to be the target of an experimental antimalarial called halofuginone, which mimics proline and jams up the parasite's protein-making machinery. Halofuginone-resistant parasites carry mutations in this same transporter. So resistance to the human-stage drug and the ability to be transmitted by mosquitoes are linked at the same protein. That's a useful piece of leverage: it may mean that mutations that let the parasite escape halofuginone come at a cost to transmission.

There's a lot of work left before this becomes a drug, but the vulnerability is real and clean.

On to the business side, and a genuinely significant biotech deal announced this morning.

Novartis is buying Myricx Bio, a small UK company based in London, for up to one and a half billion dollars — one point one billion in cash upfront and up to four hundred million in milestones. Myricx has been in relatively quiet development mode. It was spun out of Imperial College London and the Francis Crick Institute, backed by Novo Holdings and Sofinnova, and it raised a series A of about a hundred and fourteen million dollars in 2024. What it has, and what Novartis is buying, is a completely new payload class for antibody-drug conjugates.

Let me back up and explain why that matters. An antibody-drug conjugate — ADC — is a hybrid molecule with three parts: an antibody that recognizes something on the surface of a cancer cell, a chemical linker, and a toxic payload attached to the linker. The antibody drives the whole thing to the tumor, the linker holds the toxin quietly in the bloodstream, and then once the antibody binds and gets internalized by the cancer cell, the linker releases the payload inside that cell and the payload kills it. ADCs have been one of the great biotech success stories of the last five years — Enhertu, Trodelvy, Adcetris — and the field has been hunting for new payloads because the existing ones, mostly microtubule-disrupting compounds like MMAE or DNA-damaging camptothecin analogues, all share a fairly narrow set of mechanisms. Cancers that develop resistance to one payload often resist the others.

Myricx's payload is a small-molecule inhibitor of an enzyme called N-myristoyltransferase, or NMT. NMT catalyzes the attachment of a fourteen-carbon fatty acid called myristate onto the front end of hundreds of different human proteins. That fatty-acid tag anchors those proteins to membranes and to other proteins, and many of the proteins that need it are proteins cancer cells depend on for survival — signaling components, membrane trafficking machinery, structural proteins that hold the cell together. Block NMT and a certain subset of cancers essentially collapse, because they can't maintain the membrane-associated protein networks they've become addicted to. Non-cancer cells tolerate NMT inhibition much better, so there's a therapeutic window. Myricx has also shown that the drug triggers something called the unfolded protein response — a cellular stress pathway — and reprograms tumor-associated macrophages away from a tumor-friendly state. So the mechanism is genuinely different from anything currently in an ADC.

The strategic play here is straightforward. Novartis has been rebuilding its oncology pipeline aggressively and specifically leaning into ADCs. Adding a first-in-class payload with two lead ADC programs already generating preclinical data gives them chemistry they can pair with their own antibody expertise across multiple tumor types. And it fits a broader industry pattern: pharma is willing to pay premium prices upfront for novel payload chemistry, because the returns on ADCs that work are enormous — Enhertu alone has been a nine-billion-dollar franchise for AstraZeneca and Daiichi Sankyo. Whether NMT-inhibitor ADCs turn into that kind of blockbuster is a clinical question that won't be answered for years, but the deal signals that Novartis thinks the answer is yes.

On the AI-in-biomedicine front, a preprint that connects nicely to Friday's tuberculosis story.

The team is led by Maha Farhat at Harvard, along with collaborators at Tufts and Texas A and M. Farhat is the same investigator whose group did the big tuberculosis drug-resistance benchmark we discussed on Friday, and this new paper is essentially about a different piece of the same problem — not diagnosing resistance, but discovering new antibiotics in the first place.

Here's the setup. When you look for a new antibiotic, one of the most powerful approaches is whole-cell screening: you take a live bacterium, grow it in tiny wells, dose each well with a different candidate compound, and see which compounds kill the bug. Whole-cell screens are great because they don't require you to know in advance what target you're hitting — they'll find any compound that works, by any mechanism. The problem is that they're expensive and slow. Screening a chemical library of a hundred thousand compounds means physically preparing a hundred thousand wells and reading a hundred thousand results, and the vast majority of those wells are going to be misses. Historical hit rates for whole-cell antibiotic screens run around one in a thousand.

The idea behind this preprint is that machine learning can steer the screen. Instead of running the entire library, you run a small starting batch, use the results to train a model that predicts activity from chemical structure, and then use the model to pick the next batch of compounds — biased toward ones the model thinks are likely to be hits. Then you screen those, retrain, pick the next batch, and keep looping. This is called active learning. The tricky part is how you balance two goals: exploitation, where you pick compounds similar to known hits to lock in a good chemical class, and exploration, where you pick compounds that look weird and unfamiliar so the model can generalize to chemistry it hasn't seen before.

The team's contribution is to systematically evaluate three different active-learning strategies for that balance, first in retrospective simulations on existing tuberculosis screening data, and then live in the lab on a Borrelia burgdorferi screen. Borrelia burgdorferi is the bacterium that causes Lyme disease, and there is a genuine unmet need for narrow-spectrum antibiotics that hit it without disrupting the rest of the gut microbiome the way broad-spectrum drugs do.

The results are striking. Their best strategy — one that mixes exploitation of predicted hits with a novelty bonus that pulls the model toward unfamiliar chemistry — took the experimental hit rate from 0.2 percent, which is the baseline they got by picking compounds by expert intuition, up to 1 percent. That's a five-fold increase, and it means the same amount of lab work generates five times as many leads. Even more impressive: when they took the trained model and used it to pick from a much larger, more chemically diverse library, then went and tested those picks in the lab, 11 percent of the model's predicted hits were confirmed active. Compared to the 0.2 percent baseline, that's a fifty-three-fold enrichment. And every one of those confirmed hits was selective for Borrelia — not a broad-spectrum killer.

The bigger lesson matters for the whole antibiotic-discovery pipeline. This is a domain where experimental data is scarce and expensive, and where models trained on limited data tend to fail hard when they encounter chemistry they haven't seen. Getting the exploration-versus-exploitation balance right turns those weaknesses into strengths. It's the kind of methods work that quietly compounds.

And finally, animals.

A short and slightly gross preprint from a team at the University of Tokyo posted on bioRxiv last week that gave me pure delight. The paper is about rat-tailed maggots — which, first, are a real thing. Rat-tailed maggots are the larvae of hoverflies in the subfamily Eristalinae, and they live in stagnant, oxygen-poor water: puddles, sewage, that sort of thing. They have a very distinctive feature, which is a long thin telescoping tube coming out of their rear end. That tube is a respiratory siphon — an air snorkel. The maggot lives down in the water where it feeds on rotting organic material, and it extends its snorkel up to the surface to breathe air. If it needs to reach deeper, the siphon extends. It can stretch several times the length of the body.

That much has been known for a long time. What Teruyuki Matsunaga and Akinao Nose did in this new paper is figure out how the snorkel actually deploys, and in the process they discovered it has a second job.

Using fluorescent labeling and staining for muscle fibers, they showed that the siphon isn't inflated by fluid pressure, the way you might expect a stretchable tube to work. Instead, it's built like a nautical folding rig. The tube is stored folded up inside itself, and a dense array of transversely-oriented muscle fibers — muscles that wrap around the tube like rings — controls the unfolding and re-folding. When those ring muscles relax on one side and contract on the other, the folded siphon telescopes out asymmetrically.

And that asymmetry is what caught their attention. Watching the maggots in the water, they realized that this ring-muscle system doesn't just push the siphon straight up; it can bend it side to side. And when the maggots need to move along the water's surface, they don't swim. They row. They flex the siphon left-right-left-right, and each stroke pushes the animal forward.

So a snorkel — an organ evolved to breathe — has been quietly moonlighting as a rowing oar. The authors point out this is a nice example of what evolutionary biologists call functional co-option: you evolve an organ for one job, and physics happens to let it do a second unrelated job at the same time, and if that second job is useful, selection preserves it. The rat-tailed maggot is now the proud owner of a multifunctional appendage that both breathes and swims.
