## Script

Good morning. This is The Genome Brief for Saturday, July eighteenth, twenty twenty-six. Today: a preprint that reads the DRC Ebola numbers we've been quoting for two weeks and argues the plateau isn't a plateau, it's a detection failure; the first nationwide genomic surveillance of mpox in the US and the two Clade one-b samples that showed up in it; the FDA approving the first drug to actually slow kidney decline in the most common form of primary kidney disease worldwide; Google DeepMind and Isomorphic Labs formally standing up a joint bioresilience program; another Gemini deadline slip and Meta reportedly writing a ten-billion-dollar compute check to Anthropic; and a sea worm with genuinely metallic teeth.

Let's start with pathogen watch.

We've been tracking the Bundibugyo Ebola outbreak in the DRC across most of the last month, and yesterday's episode covered the WHO's mid-July update — roughly two thousand cases, seven hundred deaths, and eighty percent of new cases outside any contact list. Overnight, a medRxiv preprint from Bouhentala and Kadir at Mohamed Khider University in Biskra took the same public WHO data and did the analysis nobody at the WHO briefings has been doing on-mic, which is: what do these numbers actually support?

Their answer is uncomfortable. Between June twenty-fifth and July thirteenth — that's eighteen consecutive days — confirmed daily cases held flat, between roughly forty and fifty-two per day. On the surface that looks like a transmission plateau, the sort of thing you would report as good news, the outbreak stabilizing. But over the same eighteen days, the death-to-case ratio inside each reporting interval rose sharply, from twenty-eight percent to fifty-eight percent. Those two things together are hard to explain if the outbreak is genuinely plateauing. If new infections were actually flat, you would expect deaths to roughly track cases, not to keep climbing as a fraction. A rising death-to-case ratio with flat detected cases is instead the classical signature of what epidemiologists call saturated case detection — the diagnostic system is finding roughly as many cases as it can find, and everyone else who is getting sick is entering the ledger only when they die.

The authors then did the interpretive work of reconstructing the symptom-onset curve from the reported counts using a technique called Richardson-Lucy deconvolution — which is just a mathematical way to shift the reporting-date curve back in time by the average delay between when someone gets sick and when they're counted, and to correct for the fact that people who got sick recently haven't been reported yet. When you do that and let the model use plausible onset-to-report delays of five to nine days, the reconstructed doubling time — how long it takes for the outbreak to double in real transmission terms — comes out at around ninety days, not the twenty-two-day doubling that WHO briefings have been quoting from the raw cumulative curve. And the current reproduction number, Rt — the number of new cases each case is generating right now — was about one point two eight in early July. That's still above one, meaning the outbreak is still growing, but it's fallen a long way from the two point nine estimate for mid-May.

The case fatality picture is also worse than the headline. The commonly reported thirty-seven and a half percent is the crude estimator, which just divides deaths by all reported cases including those still in active infection. If you correct for the outcome delay — meaning you compare deaths only to cases that have had time to reach a final outcome — the CFR is fifty percent. If you compute it from just the resolved cases, it's sixty-seven percent. And the provincial breakdown is stark: about thirty-five percent CFR in Ituri, fifty-eight percent in North Kivu, and eighty-one percent in the newly affected provinces. That last number, in provinces where the outbreak has arrived recently, is the pattern of a disease being caught late.

The takeaway the authors write into their conclusion — and it is the takeaway that matters — is that public reporting of doubling times computed from cumulative counts is currently misleading, and the reported case curve has substantially decoupled from the true transmission curve. What the surveillance system needs, and does not yet publish, are the denominators: how many tests are being run per day per health zone, and what fraction come back positive. Without those, we cannot presently distinguish an outbreak being brought under control from one that is running past the ability to see it. This is a small preprint from a small research group, but if it holds up under peer review — and its arithmetic is straightforward — it changes how we should be reading every WHO update from now until the outbreak resolves.

Item two on the pathogen side, and staying with genomic surveillance but shifting pathogens. A team at Labcorp posted a medRxiv preprint yesterday describing the first nationwide, integrated genomic surveillance system for mpox virus in the United States. Some background: mpox — formerly known as monkeypox — is a double-stranded DNA virus in the same family as smallpox. It has two major clades: Clade one, historically endemic in central Africa and generally the more severe of the two, and Clade two, the West African variant that drove the 2022 global outbreak. Clade one-b is a newer sublineage that emerged in the eastern Congo in 2023 and 2024 and has been driving the ongoing central-African outbreak. So when a US surveillance system detects Clade one-b in a US sample, that's a notable event: it means the sublineage causing sustained transmission on another continent has been imported.

The Labcorp team sequenced three hundred twenty-six full-length mpox genomes from residual clinical specimens collected between August 2024 and June 2025, across all ten US Department of Health and Human Services regions. Two of those genomes were Clade one-b — one from January 2025, one from June — confirming introduction events but not sustained transmission. The remaining Clade two-b diversity broke into thirteen distinct lineages, and they picked up something structurally new: multiple genomes with large deletions, ranging from about sixteen hundred bases to seventeen thousand six hundred bases, sitting near what are called the inverted terminal repeats — the ends of the mpox genome, which are known to be genetically unstable in orthopoxviruses.

The methodology is the piece worth explaining. Sequencing mpox from clinical samples is harder than sequencing SARS cov two, because it's a much bigger genome — about two hundred kilobases versus thirty kilobases — and mpox samples are often much less viral-rich than a good COVID nasal swab. The team used something called Molecular Loop probe capture combined with PacBio Sequel two long-read sequencing. In plain terms, Molecular Loop is a technique where you design short DNA baits that hybridize to specific regions across the genome and pull those regions out of the sample, enriching viral sequence relative to human background. Long-read PacBio sequencing then lets you resolve the terminal repeat regions and structural variants — the same reason you use long reads for TB or influenza when you want to see indels rather than just SNPs.

And the APOBEC three signal is worth pointing to. APOBEC three is a family of human enzymes that edit cytosine to uracil in single-stranded DNA and RNA. It's part of the human innate immune response — a way to mutate incoming viral genomes and inactivate them. The characteristic signature is a specific type of C-to-T substitution at particular sequence contexts. Every human host a virus passes through leaves this fingerprint. The Labcorp team saw substantial APOBEC three signal across both clades, meaning both Clade two-b and the two Clade one-b introductions were passing through humans, not sitting in animal reservoirs. That's the clearest evidence you can get, from sequence alone, of human-to-human transmission — including for the Clade one-b samples, which is the fact you'd want to know from a surveillance perspective. The takeaway: the same sequencing infrastructure that was built out for COVID is now doing double duty for mpox, and mpox Clade one-b is intermittently arriving in the US via humans, not import of animals.

On the business side.

The FDA on Thursday, July sixteenth, granted traditional approval to Fabhalta — generic name iptacopan — from Novartis for a new indication: slowing kidney function decline in adults with primary IgA nephropathy who are at risk of disease progression. This is the first and only complement inhibitor ever approved with a proven kidney-protection endpoint in IgA nephropathy, which is a big deal for a disease that has had almost nothing.

Some background. IgA nephropathy — usually just called IgAN, or Berger's disease — is the most common form of primary glomerular kidney disease worldwide. The mechanism is that patients produce a poorly-glycosylated form of immunoglobulin A — the antibody isotype that normally patrols mucosal surfaces — that circulates in blood, gets bound by other antibodies, and forms immune complexes that deposit in the glomeruli of the kidney. The glomerulus is the filtering unit of the kidney, and once these immune complexes settle in, they activate the complement system — specifically the alternative pathway — and drive chronic inflammation and scarring. Over years, that leads to progressive loss of kidney function; roughly a third of patients progress to kidney failure needing dialysis or transplant within ten to twenty years of diagnosis. Standard of care has been RAS-blockade — ACE inhibitors or ARBs to control proteinuria and blood pressure — plus, more recently, sparsentan and a targeted-release oral budesonide. But none of those directly target the underlying complement activation that IgAN pathologists have been staring at in kidney biopsies for four decades.

Fabhalta is different. It's an oral small-molecule inhibitor of complement factor B — the enzyme that assembles the alternative-pathway C3 convertase, the complex that amplifies complement activation. Block factor B and you shut off the alternative pathway at its amplification step, without knocking out the whole complement system the way older drugs like eculizumab do. The Phase 3 APPLAUSE-IgAN trial randomized patients on top of standard RAS-blockade and looked at the annualized rate of change in estimated glomerular filtration rate — eGFR, the standard measure of kidney function. Fabhalta patients lost kidney function at a rate of about three milliliters per minute per year, versus five point seven milliliters per minute per year in the placebo arm — a forty-eight percent slowing of decline. Proteinuria — the leakage of protein into urine, which is both a driver of kidney damage and the earliest biomarker of it — dropped as early as two weeks after starting the drug.

Two things to underline. First, the pathway-specific mechanism is a real advance: you can knock out complement amplification in the glomerulus while leaving the classical pathway — the one that fights bacterial infection — mostly intact. That matters because complement inhibition raises the risk of infection with encapsulated bacteria like meningococcus, and Fabhalta comes with a REMS — Risk Evaluation and Mitigation Strategy — program mandating vaccination against those organisms before starting the drug. It's a manageable safety trade, not an idle warning. Second, this is Novartis's third IgAN indication in eighteen months — they already have Vanrafia, an endothelin receptor antagonist for proteinuria, and are advancing zigakibart, an anti-APRIL antibody targeting the upstream B-cell factor that drives production of the pathogenic IgA in the first place. In other words, Novartis is trying to build a complete kidney-protection stack across the pathway — from B-cell IgA production, to glomerular pressure, to complement amplification. If they can layer those, IgAN could shift from a slowly progressive disease with no disease-modifying therapy to something that is genuinely modifiable. Fabhalta on its own is the first proof-of-concept.

Onto AI in biomedicine.

Google DeepMind and Isomorphic Labs on Thursday, July sixteenth, formally launched a joint program they're calling their approach to bioresilience. The word "bioresilience" is doing some work here — it's a re-framing meant to combine biosecurity, pandemic preparedness, and rapid medical countermeasure development into one integrated stance. The announcement is worth reading in its structure, because it tells you how the two most technically advanced life-science AI labs in the world are now thinking about how their models get used.

They're organizing the work under three pillars: prevent, detect, respond. Prevent, in their terminology, is the biosecurity piece — internal red-teaming of their foundation models to identify dual-use risks, extending SynthID, which is their invisible watermark for AI-generated text and images, to synthetic biological sequences. In other words, if you generated a DNA sequence using AlphaFold or a related model, DeepMind wants it to be technically detectable downstream. Detect is the surveillance piece — they've deployed AlphaEvolve, their evolutionary program-synthesis system, to optimize metagenomic sequencing algorithms, aiming to reduce the cost and speed of environmental sequencing. Metagenomic sequencing is when you sequence everything in a sample and computationally sort out what's there afterward, so any efficiency gain compounds across every wastewater or clinical panel that uses it. Respond is the drug-design piece — Isomorphic Labs, which is DeepMind's separately-incorporated drug design company, has stood up a dedicated bioresilience unit that will apply their drug design engine, IsoDDE, to design medical countermeasures — vaccines and therapeutics — for both natural pandemics and misuse scenarios. Trusted external researchers will get access.

What matters about this announcement is less any single product than the fact that AlphaFold, AlphaGenome, and IsoDDE are being formally positioned as biosecurity infrastructure. DeepMind is saying, in a governance move, that the same models capable of accelerating drug design can accelerate other things, and that the answer to that dual-use is not to lock the models down but to build a partnership infrastructure — they cite over fifteen partnerships with governments, biosecurity organizations, and research groups over the past year — that can rapidly deploy them for countermeasures. It's an ambitious framing, and one worth watching, because if it works it becomes a template other frontier AI labs will be pressured to follow.

Meta, notably, does not have an established business selling compute, so this would be a new-line-of-business move. And finally, animals.

A team led by Christian Hellmich at TU Vienna, working with colleagues at the University of Vienna, published a paper in Biophysics Reviews earlier this week describing something that is genuinely difficult to categorize. They looked at the jaws of a common Mediterranean marine bristle worm called Perinereis cultrifera — a segmented predator that catches and crushes prey with paired jaws it extends from its mouth — and they used a technique called nanoindentation, where you press a diamond tip into a material and measure how much it dents and how much it springs back. And they found the bristle worm's jaw behaves like a metal. Not "hard like a metal" as a metaphor — actually mechanically, at the nanoscale, like a metal.

Two properties in particular. The first is called the Nix-Gao size effect. In real metals, when you press smaller and smaller indenters into the surface, the material appears harder in proportion — because at small volumes the atomic dislocations that let metals deform under pressure get pinned up against each other, and there's less room for them to move. That effect is a fingerprint of crystalline metal deformation. The bristle worm's jaw shows it. The second is that the jaw shows size-dependent elasticity — how bouncy it is depends on the scale at which you measure it — and that particular combination is not what real crystalline copper or silver does, which is what tells the authors this is a new class of material. It isn't a metal in the classical sense. There's no lattice of metal atoms bonded to each other. What there is, is metal ions — most likely zinc — locked individually into a protein matrix, concentrated more heavily at the jaw tip where the biting happens. In other words, the worm has built a distributed metal by hanging single ions off a scaffold of protein, and that arrangement has enough of the physics of a real metal to reproduce these mechanical fingerprints, and enough of the physics of a soft biological material to differ where it needs to.

Hellmich's group is proposing a new material category, which they're calling bio-metals, defined by three joint properties: metal-like hardness, metal-like strain response, and this specific ion-and-protein composite architecture. The obvious question is whether we can copy this — grow synthetic composites where you distribute metal ions through an engineered protein scaffold to make cutting tools or coatings that are hard but not brittle. That's speculative. The more immediate point is that a small predatory sea worm has been chewing its way through the Mediterranean for millions of years using material physics that we're only now finding a category for.
